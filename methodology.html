1. Data Sources

All ESG Financial Quotient scores are derived exclusively from companies' own public regulatory disclosures. We do not use estimates, surveys, or third-party data providers. The primary sources are:

📄
SEBI BRSR Filings
Business Responsibility & Sustainability Reports filed by listed companies as part of their Annual Reports under SEBI's mandatory disclosure framework (top 1,000 companies by market cap, mandatory from FY 2022-23). Covers GHG emissions, water use, waste, energy, supply chain, governance and ESG targets.
🏭
CPCB EPR Portal (Public Data)
Publicly available data from the Central Pollution Control Board's Extended Producer Responsibility portals for Batteries, Plastics, E-Waste and Tyres. Used to determine EPR applicability and compliance obligations by sector and product type.
🏢
MCA Company Registry
Ministry of Corporate Affairs public registry for company identification, CIN numbers, and sector classification used to map BRSR filings to company profiles.
📋
Regulatory Notifications
Published notifications and circulars from SEBI, MoEFCC, BEE, CPCB and other Indian regulators, used to assess compliance risk levels and upcoming regulatory obligations by sector.
What we do not use: Real-time stock prices, NSE/BSE live market data, proprietary databases, or self-reported ESG questionnaires outside of BRSR filings.

2. Scoring Overview

The ESG Financial Quotient (EFQ) score is a composite 0–10 risk score. A higher score indicates greater financial exposure to ESG-related risks — not a lower ESG performance. This is a risk lens, not a ratings system.

Score interpretation: A score of 0 means the lowest measured ESG financial risk in the listed universe; 10 means the highest. Scores span the full range, with the median company around 4.3. Read the score alongside its sector-relative percentile and Disclosure Confidence (Section 5).

3. The Three ESG Pillars

Every dimension is scored 0–10 by its percentile rank within the listed universe, then rolled up into three pillar scores — Environmental, Social and Governance — that combine into the composite EFQ. Dimensions are grouped by pillar below.

🌍
Environmental
Pillar weight: 45%

Carbon intensity — real Scope 1+2 emissions per ₹ of revenue, re-extracted from BRSR XBRL (Principle 6). Energy transition — share of non-renewable energy in the mix. Water intensity — water use per ₹ of revenue, credited for Zero Liquid Discharge and treated discharge. Waste intensity — waste per ₹ of revenue, credited for documented recovery and recycling.

High: carbon / resource intensive, low renewables Low: efficient, high recovery & renewables
👷
Social
Pillar weight: 30%

Workforce safety — Lost-Time Injury Frequency Rate, fatalities and total recordable injuries (Principle 3), with credit for an occupational health & safety management system. Diversity — share of women on the board and in key management. Scored on disclosed figures, not policy yes/no answers.

High: injuries / fatalities, low diversity Low: strong safety record, diverse leadership
🏛️
Governance
Pillar weight: 25%

Pay equity — ratio of median board remuneration to median worker remuneration (Principle 5). Assurance — whether BRSR / BRSR-Core disclosures are independently assured. Business ethics — anti-corruption framework. Fines & penalties — monetary regulatory penalties relative to revenue.

High: wide pay gap, fines, no assurance Low: equitable pay, assured, clean record

4. Dimension Weights

Each dimension is first normalised to a 0–10 risk score by its percentile rank within the full listed universe (so a company is scored relative to where it actually sits, robust to the extreme spread of raw intensity figures). Sub-scores roll up into three pillar scores, which combine into the composite EFQ. Weighting leans Environmental because carbon, water and waste carry the most direct quantifiable financial exposure under Indian regulation (CCTS, PAT, EPR, CPCB), while Social and Governance add the workforce-safety, diversity and accountability signals BRSR now mandates.

Pillar Weight Dimensions within the pillar
Environmental (E) 45% Carbon intensity (Scope 1+2 ÷ revenue) · Energy transition (% renewable) · Water intensity · Waste intensity (credited for recovery/recycling & ZLD)
Social (S) 30% Workforce safety (LTIFR, fatalities, recordable injuries, OH&S system) · Board & management diversity (% female directors / KMP)
Governance (G) 25% Pay equity (board-to-worker remuneration ratio) · Independent BRSR assurance · Business ethics (anti-corruption) · Fines & penalties
Total 100%

The composite EFQ score is calculated as:

EFQ Score = (0.45 × Environmental) + (0.30 × Social) + (0.25 × Governance)
Missing data is never imputed to a midpoint. Each pillar is a weighted average over the dimensions a company actually discloses; weights re-normalise to the disclosed set. How much of the score rests on verified data is reported separately as Disclosure Confidence (Section 5) rather than silently inflating or deflating the risk score.

5. Sector-Relative Ranking & Disclosure Confidence

Absolute ESG intensity varies by orders of magnitude across sectors — a steelmaker's carbon footprint is not comparable to a bank's. So alongside the absolute EFQ score, every company carries a sector-relative percentile: its rank against true peers, grouped by the official Indian NIC industrial classification (2-digit division) declared in its own BRSR filing. A company is judged against steelmakers, banks against banks. This is the headline figure on company cards; the absolute tier (Section 6) provides the cross-sector reference.

How peers are formed: The 1,200+ companies resolve into ~20 NIC sector divisions. Where a division has at least 8 companies, the percentile is computed within that division; smaller divisions fall back to a whole-universe percentile so every company still gets a fair relative rank.

Disclosure Confidence

Because the score is built only from the dimensions a company actually discloses (never imputed to a midpoint), each company also carries a Disclosure Confidence figure (0–100%) — the share of scored dimensions backed by verified data, weighted toward independently-assured disclosures. A company can have a low risk score on thin data; Confidence tells you how much to trust it. This separates two things older single-number ratings conflate: how risky a company is and how well we can actually see it.

🟢
High Confidence (≥ 80%)
Most pillars backed by disclosed, often externally-assured BRSR data. The score is well-supported.
🟡
Partial Confidence (50–79%)
Some pillars rest on limited disclosure. Read the score alongside the disclosed-data coverage.
🔴
Thin Disclosure (< 50%)
Material gaps in the underlying filing. The score is directional and should be treated with caution.

6. Risk Tier Thresholds

Companies are classified into three tiers based on their composite EFQ score. Thresholds are calibrated to the actual distribution of the Indian listed universe (terciles), so each tier holds roughly a third of companies. The headline view on company cards is the sector-relative percentile (see Section 5); these absolute bands provide a cross-sector reference.

≥ 5.0 / 10
High Risk
Top tier of measured ESG financial risk in the listed universe — typically high carbon/resource intensity, weak workforce-safety or governance metrics, or a combination across pillars. Warrants attention from management and investors.
3.5 – 5.0 / 10
Medium Risk
Mid tier of measured risk. Mixed performance across the Environmental, Social and Governance pillars. Read alongside the Disclosure Confidence indicator, which shows how much of the score rests on verified data.
< 3.5 / 10
Low Risk
Lowest tier of measured ESG financial risk — low resource intensity and/or strong safety, diversity and governance metrics relative to the universe. Consistent with ESG leadership among Indian listed companies.

7. Financial Exposure Estimates

The Estimated Compliance Cost Band shown on company profiles (e.g., ₹10–38 crore) is a directional estimate of potential regulatory compliance costs, not an audited figure. It is derived from:

  • Published penalty structures under SEBI BRSR enforcement guidelines
  • CPCB EPR non-compliance penalty rates for Plastics, Batteries, E-Waste and Tyres
  • Sector-wide carbon pricing frameworks (₹600–900/tonne CO₂e under India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme)
  • Company revenue scale and waste/emissions intensity as a multiplier
Important: These are indicative ranges only. Actual compliance costs depend on company-specific circumstances, regulatory discretion, and remediation timelines. Do not use these figures for financial reporting, legal proceedings, or investment decisions.

8. Double Materiality Assessment

Following the ISSB IFRS S1/S2 and EU CSRD double materiality framework, each company is assessed on two axes:

Financial Materiality (→)

How ESG risks affect the company's own financial position, earnings, and access to capital. Derived from the composite EFQ score.

Impact Materiality (↑)

How the company's operations affect the environment and society. Derived from absolute GHG, water, and waste figures relative to sector peers — independent of disclosure completeness.

QuadrantFinancial MaterialityImpact MaterialityMeaning
Dual MaterialityHigh (≥5)High (≥5)ESG risks affect both company finances and the environment — highest priority for disclosure and action
Financially MaterialHigh (≥5)Low (<5)Strong regulatory/financial risk but relatively lower absolute environmental impact
Impact MaterialLow (<5)High (≥5)High environmental impact but lower near-term regulatory financial risk
Watch ListLow (<5)Low (<5)Currently lower risk on both axes — monitor for regulatory changes

9. Update Cycle

🔄
Company Scores
Updated annually when new BRSR filings are published (typically June–September for the previous financial year). Scores reflect the most recently available BRSR filing — the financial year is shown on each company profile.
📰
Regulatory Radar
Regulatory notifications, circulars and compliance deadlines are curated and reviewed periodically as new SEBI, CPCB, BEE and MoEFCC circulars are published. Always verify active compliance deadlines on official regulatory portals before relying on them for filings.
🏭
EPR Data
CPCB EPR portal public data is checked periodically and EPR exposure scores are updated when new registration data or compliance notices are published.

10. Limitations & Disclaimers

⚠️
Self-Reported Data

All scores are based on data companies disclose in their BRSR filings. We score only what is disclosed (never imputing missing data to a midpoint) and report coverage separately as Disclosure Confidence — so thin disclosure lowers confidence rather than silently distorting the score. We score what is disclosed — not what is true.

⚠️
Not Investment Advice

ESG Financial Quotient scores are analytical outputs for informational purposes only. They do not constitute investment advice, a buy/sell recommendation, or a securities rating. Green Curve is not a SEBI-registered Research Analyst or ESG Rating Provider.

⚠️
AI-Assisted Analysis

Company risk summaries are generated using AI analysis of BRSR data. While grounded in disclosed data, AI-generated summaries may contain inaccuracies or omissions. Always verify findings against the original company BRSR filing.

⚠️
Data Currency

Scores reflect the most recently available BRSR filing, which may be 12–18 months old at any given time. Company circumstances, regulatory compliance, and environmental performance may have changed since the filing date.

⚠️
Coverage Scope

The current dataset covers over 1,200 listed companies — a large subset of India's listed universe. Absence from our database does not imply lower or higher ESG risk. We are working to expand coverage further.

11. Frequently Asked Questions

Why does a higher score mean higher risk? Shouldn't a better company score higher?

The EFQ is a financial risk score, not an ESG performance rating. It measures how much ESG-related regulatory and financial risk a company is exposed to. A score of 10 means maximum identified risk — similar to how a credit risk score works. Think of it as: the higher the score, the more work the company needs to do to reduce its ESG-related financial exposure.

What does Disclosure Confidence mean, and how is it different from the risk score?

The risk score measures how much ESG financial risk a company carries; Disclosure Confidence (0–100%) measures how much of that score rests on verified data. Missing data is never imputed to a midpoint — each pillar is computed only over the dimensions a company actually discloses, and Confidence reports the coverage (weighted toward independently-assured figures). A low score on low confidence should be read with caution.

My company is listed here — can I request a correction?

Yes. If you believe your company's score reflects an error in reading your BRSR filing, please contact us with the specific BRSR page reference and we will review. We are committed to accuracy and will update scores when corrections are warranted.

Is this the same as SEBI's ESG Rating Provider framework?

No. SEBI's ERP framework regulates registered ESG rating agencies that issue formal ESG ratings used for regulatory and investment purposes. Green Curve is not a SEBI-registered ESG Rating Provider. Our scores are research-grade analytical outputs derived from public BRSR data — not formal regulatory ratings.

How is the ESG Financial Quotient different from MSCI, Sustainalytics or other ESG ratings?

Global ESG rating providers use proprietary datasets, surveys, and estimated data across global frameworks. The EFQ is designed specifically for the Indian regulatory environment — it focuses on financial risk from Indian laws (SEBI BRSR, EPR rules, CCTS) and uses only publicly disclosed Indian regulatory data. It is narrower in scope but more directly relevant to India-specific compliance risk.

Can I use this data in my research or reports?

Yes, with attribution. You may cite Green Curve ESG Financial Quotient scores in research, journalism, or analysis with a reference to greencurve.solutions and the data date. You may not reproduce the dataset wholesale or use it for commercial redistribution without written permission.
