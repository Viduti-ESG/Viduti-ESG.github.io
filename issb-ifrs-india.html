ISSB and IFRS S1 / S2: A Guide for Indian Companies

The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) has created a global baseline for sustainability and climate disclosure through IFRS S1 and IFRS S2. This guide explains what they require, how they relate to TCFD and BRSR, and what Indian companies should do now.

What is the ISSB?

The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) was established by the IFRS Foundation at COP26 in November 2021 to create a global baseline of sustainability disclosures for capital markets. It consolidated several earlier frameworks (TCFD, SASB, CDSB, Integrated Reporting) into one standard-setter.

IFRS S1 and IFRS S2

The ISSB issued its first two standards in June 2023, effective for annual periods beginning on or after 1 January 2024:

  • IFRS S1 — General Requirements: disclose all sustainability-related risks and opportunities that could affect a company's prospects, using the familiar governance / strategy / risk-management / metrics-and-targets structure.
  • IFRS S2 — Climate-related Disclosures: climate-specific requirements including Scope 1, 2 and 3 GHG emissions, transition plans, and scenario analysis.

How ISSB relates to TCFD

IFRS S2 fully incorporates the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD). From 2024 the ISSB took over responsibility for monitoring climate-disclosure progress from the TCFD. In practice, a company already aligned to TCFD has a strong head start on IFRS S2.

Why this matters for Indian companies

India has not mandated IFRS S1/S2 domestically — SEBI's BRSR remains the local requirement, and it already overlaps substantially with ISSB on climate and governance. But ISSB matters for Indian companies that:

  • are subsidiaries of multinational groups reporting under ISSB;
  • export to markets adopting ISSB-based rules;
  • seek global capital from investors using ISSB as the baseline.

Aligning BRSR data to ISSB concepts now reduces duplicate reporting later.
