EPR Registration in India: Plastic, E-Waste & Battery

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) makes producers, importers and brand owners responsible for the end-of-life of the products and packaging they put on the market. This guide covers EPR registration in India across plastic, e-waste and batteries — the CPCB portals, targets, certificates and penalties.

What is EPR?

Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) is a policy principle that shifts the responsibility for collecting and recycling waste back to the entities that introduce products into the market. In India it is administered by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) through dedicated online registration and reporting portals for each waste stream.

Who must register for EPR?

EPR obligations fall on Producers, Importers and Brand Owners (PIBOs) — and, in some streams, on recyclers and refurbishers. If your business manufactures, imports, or sells products under its brand in any of the regulated categories below, you almost certainly need to register.

Importers, note: EPR registration is increasingly a gateway for customs clearance. Missing registration can block consignments at the port.

The main EPR waste streams

StreamGoverning rulesWho registers
Plastic packagingPlastic Waste Management Rules, 2016 (amended 2022, with EPR guidelines and later amendments)Producers, importers, brand owners
E-wasteE-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 (effective 1 April 2023)Producers, manufacturers, recyclers, refurbishers
BatteriesBattery Waste Management Rules, 2022Producers and importers of batteries
Tyres / used oilHazardous & Other Wastes rules with EPR schedulesProducers and importers

How EPR registration works

The mechanics are broadly similar across streams:

  1. Register on the relevant CPCB EPR portal with your incorporation, PAN and GST documents.
  2. Declare the quantity and category of material you place on the market.
  3. Meet annual EPR targets — collect/recycle a prescribed percentage of that quantity.
  4. Buy or generate EPR certificates from registered recyclers to evidence fulfilment.
  5. File annual returns reconciling obligations against certificates.

Penalties for non-compliance

Shortfalls attract Environmental Compensation (EC) levied by CPCB under the Environment (Protection) Act — calculated on the unfulfilled obligation, and in serious cases accompanied by suspension of registration or blocked imports. EC is intended to be higher than the cost of compliance, so it is rarely cheaper to default.

How EPR connects to BRSR

EPR data — quantities placed on market, recycling achieved, EC paid — feeds directly into Principle 2 and Principle 6 of your BRSR. Treating EPR and BRSR as one data pipeline avoids duplicate effort and inconsistent numbers.
