Executive Summary

The Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD) released its beta framework for market testing, inviting companies and financial institutions to pilot nature-related risk assessment and disclosure practices ahead of the finalised framework. For Indian businesses — particularly those in agriculture, textiles, mining, pharmaceuticals, and financial services — early engagement with the TNFD beta process is not optional preparation; it is a strategic imperative. As global capital markets increasingly price nature-related risks alongside climate risks, companies that build disclosure capabilities now will hold a decisive advantage when mandatory or investor-driven reporting requirements arrive.

What Changed

The TNFD (Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures) is a global, market-led initiative that has developed a framework to help organisations identify, assess, manage, and disclose their dependencies and impacts on nature — covering biodiversity, freshwater, land, and ocean systems. Modelled structurally on the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD), the TNFD framework was released in iterative beta versions (v0.1 through v0.4) between 2022 and 2023, inviting businesses worldwide to stress-test the methodology in real operating environments before the final framework was published in September 2023. The beta testing phase was significant because it moved nature-related financial risk from a theoretical concern into a practical, structured disclosure discipline. Companies that participated in beta testing worked through the LEAP approach — Locate your interface with nature, Evaluate dependencies and impacts, Assess material risks and opportunities, and Prepare to respond and report. This iterative, open-testing model allowed the TNFD to refine sector-specific guidance, data requirements, and metrics based on real-world feedback from over 500 pilot organisations globally.

Who Is Affected

  • Agribusiness, food & beverage companies with land-use footprints and supply chain dependencies on ecosystems
  • Textile and apparel manufacturers reliant on water-intensive cotton and natural fibres
  • Mining and extractives companies with operations in or near biodiversity-sensitive zones
  • Pharmaceutical and biotechnology companies dependent on natural compounds and genetic resources
  • Infrastructure and real estate developers whose projects intersect with natural habitats
  • Banks, NBFCs, and institutional investors financing sectors with significant nature dependencies
  • Export-oriented businesses supplying to EU, UK, and US markets where nature disclosure is becoming a procurement and regulatory requirement
  • Large listed companies likely to face SEBI BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting) evolution toward nature metrics

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Familiarise leadership and sustainability teams with the final TNFD framework (published September 2023) and the sector-specific supplemental guidance available at tnfd.global
  • Conduct an initial LEAP assessment: map where your operations and supply chains interface with nature-sensitive areas using tools such as the TNFD's recommended IBAT (Integrated Biodiversity Assessment Tool) and WWF's STAR metric
  • Review SEBI's BRSR Core disclosures (mandatory for top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24) — nature-adjacent metrics on water, waste, and biodiversity are already embedded and will likely expand
  • Monitor the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS E4 — Biodiversity & Ecosystems), which will affect Indian companies with EU operations or large EU customer bases
  • Engage with pilot cohorts and industry associations (such as CII, FICCI sustainability councils) running TNFD familiarisation workshops to benchmark against Indian peers
  • Begin building internal data infrastructure for nature-related metrics — species impact, freshwater consumption by watershed, land-use change — as these will underpin future disclosures
  • No single statutory deadline in India as of now, but EU CSRD obligations for large non-EU subsidiaries begin phasing in from 2026; proactive alignment is advised immediately

Climate Transition Angle

Nature and climate are inseparable systems, and the TNFD framework explicitly recognises this interdependence. Physical climate risks — extreme heat, erratic monsoons, flooding, and groundwater depletion — are simultaneously nature risks for Indian businesses. A textile manufacturer in Gujarat facing water scarcity is experiencing both a climate physical risk and a nature dependency risk. The TNFD framework provides the analytical architecture to assess these compounding risks together, rather than in silos. Globally, the TNFD aligns with and complements the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) framework — particularly IFRS S2 on climate — and is being integrated into CSRD's ESRS standards. Indian companies reporting under ISSB-aligned standards (which SEBI is expected to progressively adopt) will find that nature disclosure is the logical next layer after climate disclosure. The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, agreed at COP15 in 2022, set a target of 30% protected land and oceans by 2030 and requires financial flows to be aligned with biodiversity goals — creating regulatory and reputational pressure that will flow through to corporate supply chains and investment mandates. Early TNFD adoption positions Indian businesses ahead of this tightening landscape.

What To Do Now

  • ASSIGN OWNERSHIP: Designate a cross-functional TNFD working group including sustainability, finance, risk, and procurement leads — nature risk is not solely a CSR function; it requires CFO and board-level visibility
  • RUN A MATERIALITY SCREENING: Use the TNFD's LEAP approach to conduct a rapid, high-level screening of your top three to five value chain nodes for nature dependencies and impacts — this scoping exercise can be completed in 6 to 8 weeks with existing data
  • AUDIT YOUR DATA GAPS: Identify which nature-related metrics (water withdrawal by source, land-use change, supplier geographic coordinates near protected areas) you currently do not capture, and initiate data collection protocols in your next reporting cycle
  • ENGAGE YOUR SUPPLY CHAIN: Nature risks are overwhelmingly concentrated in upstream supply chains — begin supplier engagement and questionnaires on land use, water stewardship, and proximity to Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs)
  • ALIGN WITH BRSR AND PLAN AHEAD: Map TNFD disclosure requirements against your current BRSR Core submissions to identify overlap, efficiency opportunities, and gaps — use this as your roadmap for the next two to three annual reporting cycles

Our Take

The TNFD beta framework testing phase was a quiet but consequential moment in the evolution of sustainable finance — it signalled that nature-related financial risk is transitioning from voluntary best practice to the foundational layer of corporate disclosure, sitting alongside climate as a non-negotiable lens for investors, lenders, and regulators. For Indian businesses, the urgency is real but the window to act proactively is still open. India's economy is among the most nature-dependent in the world: agriculture employs over 40% of the workforce, water stress affects major industrial corridors, and biodiversity loss threatens supply chains from pharmaceuticals to food. Companies that treat TNFD preparation as a compliance checkbox are missing the strategic point — those that genuinely integrate nature risk into capital allocation, procurement decisions, and long-term strategy will be better positioned for resilience, investor confidence, and access to green finance. The question is no longer whether nature disclosure will become mandatory for significant Indian enterprises; it is how quickly, and whether your organisation will be ready.
