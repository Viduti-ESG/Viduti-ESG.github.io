Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice directed at registered recyclers operating under India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, signalling heightened regulatory scrutiny of the extended producer responsibility (EPR) ecosystem. This notice is a clear indicator that CPCB is actively monitoring compliance performance — including recycling volumes, certificate issuance, and data accuracy — across the e-waste value chain. Producers, importers, brand owners, and their recycling partners must treat this as an immediate prompt to audit their EPR obligations and recycler relationships.

What Changed

India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 overhauled the previous 2016 framework by introducing a market-based EPR certificate trading system, mandatory online registration on the CPCB EPR portal, and clearly defined responsibilities for producers, dismantlers, and recyclers. Under this framework, registered recyclers are required to process e-waste collected through authorised channels, upload processing data on the centralised portal, and issue EPR certificates to producers as proof of fulfilment. The CPCB's urgent notice to registered recyclers suggests that the regulator has identified systemic issues — potentially including discrepancies in reported recycling quantities, non-submission of quarterly or annual returns, irregularities in EPR certificate generation, or failure to meet minimum infrastructure and environmental standards. While the full text of the notice was not publicly extracted, such urgent communications from CPCB typically serve as a final compliance warning before punitive action, which can include suspension or cancellation of recycler registration. For the broader EPR ecosystem, this matters enormously: if a recycler's registration is suspended, any EPR certificates previously purchased from them may be rendered invalid, directly exposing the producers and importers who relied on those certificates to non-compliance liability under the Rules.

Who Is Affected

  • Registered e-waste recyclers and dismantlers holding CPCB authorisation under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022
  • Producers of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) — including consumer electronics, IT and telecom equipment, and large/small household appliances — who have EPR obligations and have procured certificates from recyclers
  • Importers and brand owners of EEE who are registered on the CPCB EPR portal and rely on recycler certificates for annual compliance fulfilment
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) compliance managers and environmental officers within manufacturing companies
  • Third-party EPR compliance aggregators and intermediaries who facilitate certificate trading between producers and recyclers
  • Investors and lenders with exposure to e-waste recycling businesses or EEE manufacturers with significant EPR liabilities

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Registered recyclers must ensure all processing data — including quantities received, processed, and residue disposed — is accurately uploaded on the CPCB EPR portal without delay, as data gaps are a primary trigger for regulatory notices
  • Recyclers must verify that EPR certificates issued to producers accurately reflect actual e-waste processed and are not inflated or duplicated — certificate fraud is treated as a serious violation under the Rules
  • All recyclers must maintain valid consent-to-operate from their respective State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) and ensure facility-level standards (storage, processing, effluent management) are audit-ready at all times
  • Quarterly and annual returns must be filed on the CPCB portal within prescribed timelines — recyclers should immediately check for any overdue submissions and file pending reports
  • Producers and importers should urgently verify the active registration status of all recyclers from whom they have procured EPR certificates in the current compliance year, using the public CPCB EPR portal database
  • Any recycler that has received a direct notice from CPCB should seek legal and environmental compliance counsel immediately and respond within the stated notice period — typically 15 to 30 days — to avoid escalation to registration cancellation
  • Producers whose recycler partners are under scrutiny should proactively engage alternate authorised recyclers to ensure their annual EPR targets remain on track ahead of the March 31 financial year-end deadline

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste recycling is not merely a regulatory checkbox — it sits at the heart of India's circular economy and critical mineral security strategy. Electronic devices contain recoverable quantities of gold, silver, copper, cobalt, and rare earth elements, all of which are essential inputs for clean energy technologies including solar panels, EV batteries, and wind turbines. A well-functioning e-waste recycling sector directly reduces India's dependence on virgin mining and imported critical minerals, both of which carry substantial carbon and supply chain risk. From a global standards perspective, the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standard and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) increasingly require companies to account for Scope 3 emissions — which includes the end-of-life treatment of products sold. Companies that cannot demonstrate responsible, verified end-of-life recycling through credible partners risk both regulatory exposure under Indian law and reputational damage in export markets where customers and investors are demanding supply chain transparency. Physical climate risk is also relevant here: e-waste recycling facilities located in flood-prone or heat-stressed regions face operational disruptions that can cascade into compliance failures for the producers who depend on them. Companies should be mapping the climate vulnerability of their recycler networks as part of broader supply chain resilience planning — a practice that is becoming standard under TCFD-aligned disclosure frameworks increasingly referenced by SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) requirements.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your recycler roster immediately: Log into the CPCB EPR portal and verify the current registration status, certificate issuance history, and compliance standing of every recycler from whom your organisation has procured EPR certificates in FY 2024-25 — do not wait for official intimation of a problem
  • Cross-check EPR certificate volumes against actual recycling capacity: If your recyclers have issued certificates significantly in excess of their reported processing capacity or installed throughput, this is a red flag — conduct a desk-based due diligence review and consider physical verification visits
  • File all pending returns and close data gaps: Whether you are a recycler with overdue portal submissions or a producer with unreconciled EPR fulfilment data, use this notice as a trigger to bring all filings current before the financial year end
  • Diversify your recycler base and build contractual safeguards: Dependence on a single recycler for large volumes of EPR fulfilment is a concentration risk — identify and onboard backup authorised recyclers, and include contractual clauses requiring recyclers to notify you of any regulatory actions against them
  • Integrate e-waste EPR into your BRSR and ESG disclosures: Document your EPR compliance methodology, recycler due diligence process, and certificate verification practices — this strengthens your BRSR Core and Comprehensive disclosures and positions your organisation well for future alignment with ISSB S2 value chain disclosure expectations

Our Take

This CPCB notice is a significant signal that India's e-waste EPR regime is maturing from a paper-compliance framework into one with real enforcement teeth. The regulator has built the digital infrastructure — the centralised EPR portal, certificate trading mechanisms, and data dashboards — and is now clearly moving into an active verification and enforcement phase. We expect this to be followed by more targeted show-cause notices, potential registration cancellations of non-compliant recyclers, and possibly sector-wide audits in the coming months. For producers and importers, the lesson is unambiguous: your EPR compliance is only as strong as the weakest link in your recycler network. Companies that have been treating EPR certificate procurement as a procurement transaction rather than a compliance obligation will find themselves exposed. The time to build rigorous recycler due diligence, real-time portal monitoring, and contingency planning into your EPR programme is now — before CPCB's scrutiny reaches your doorstep.
