Executive Summary

The Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures (TNFD) has established a global framework that compels companies and financial institutions to assess, disclose, and act on their dependencies and impacts on nature — including biodiversity, freshwater, land, and ocean systems. For Indian businesses, which are deeply embedded in nature-dependent sectors such as agriculture, textiles, pharmaceuticals, and infrastructure, this is not a distant voluntary exercise — it is the next frontier of mandatory ESG accountability. As global investors and trading partners increasingly align with TNFD, Indian companies that fail to understand their nature footprint risk losing capital access, supply chain contracts, and social licence to operate.

What Changed

In September 2023, the TNFD released its final recommendations — a comprehensive, science-based framework for organisations to report on how their business activities affect and depend upon nature. Built on the architecture of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD), the TNFD framework introduces the concept of 'nature-related risks and opportunities' across four pillars: Governance, Strategy, Risk & Impact Management, and Metrics & Targets. Unlike climate disclosures, which focus primarily on greenhouse gas emissions and temperature pathways, nature-related disclosures require companies to map their interactions with ecosystems — think water consumption from stressed river basins, deforestation embedded in commodity supply chains, soil degradation from intensive farming, or habitat destruction from real estate and mining operations. The TNFD also introduces the LEAP approach (Locate, Evaluate, Assess, Prepare) — a practical methodology for identifying where a company's operations and supply chains intersect with sensitive or degraded ecosystems. Globally, over 400 organisations have already signed up as early adopters, committing to begin TNFD-aligned reporting by financial year 2024–25. Regulators in the EU, UK, and Singapore are actively exploring how TNFD recommendations will be incorporated into mandatory disclosure regimes. India's own SEBI Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework is increasingly converging with global standards, making TNFD alignment a near-term inevitability for listed Indian companies.

Who Is Affected

  • Large listed Indian companies (NSE/BSE) already subject to SEBI's BRSR Core disclosures, particularly those in high nature-impact sectors
  • Agribusiness, food & beverage, and FMCG companies with commodity supply chains touching forests, freshwater, or marine ecosystems
  • Textile and apparel manufacturers dependent on water-intensive cotton and synthetic fibre production
  • Pharmaceutical and chemical companies sourcing botanical or marine-derived inputs
  • Infrastructure developers, real estate firms, and mining companies operating near biodiversity-sensitive zones or protected areas
  • Financial institutions — banks, asset managers, and insurers — with portfolio exposure to nature-dependent sectors
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational corporations whose parent companies are TNFD early adopters or subject to EU CSRD requirements
  • Export-oriented businesses supplying to EU or UK markets, where nature-related due diligence obligations (e.g., EU Deforestation Regulation) are already in force

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • TNFD Early Adopter Commitment (Voluntary — FY2024-25 onwards): Companies that have signed up as TNFD early adopters are expected to begin producing TNFD-aligned disclosures in their annual or sustainability reports for financial years starting in 2024
  • EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) — Enforcement from December 2025 (delayed from 2024): Indian exporters of soy, palm oil, cattle, wood, cocoa, coffee, rubber, and derived products must demonstrate deforestation-free supply chains with geolocation data
  • EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — Phased from 2025: Indian companies that are subsidiaries or significant suppliers of EU-regulated entities will face nature-related disclosure expectations flowing down their value chains
  • SEBI BRSR Core — Mandatory for top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24, expanding to top 1,000 by FY2026-27: While not yet explicitly TNFD-aligned, BRSR's biodiversity, water, and land-use disclosures are foundational building blocks
  • Immediate action (now): Conduct an initial screening of operations and supply chains using the TNFD LEAP methodology to identify high-priority nature-related interfaces
  • Near-term (6–12 months): Establish internal governance ownership of nature risks at board or senior management level, mirroring existing climate risk governance structures

Climate Transition Angle

Nature and climate are inseparable. Approximately 55% of global GDP is moderately or highly dependent on nature, and the degradation of ecosystems directly amplifies physical climate risks. Deforestation reduces carbon sinks, increasing warming trajectories. Wetland destruction removes natural flood buffers, exposing assets and communities to intensifying monsoon events. Soil degradation reduces agricultural resilience to droughts — a mounting concern for India's food systems as temperatures rise. The TNFD framework is explicitly designed to work in tandem with the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate standard and the TCFD framework. As the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) signals its intention to develop a nature-related standard building on TNFD, Indian companies that invest in nature disclosure infrastructure now will be positioned ahead of the curve when ISSB biodiversity standards arrive — likely within the next two to three years. For Indian supply chains, the stakes are particularly high. India is one of the world's most biodiverse countries and simultaneously one of the most vulnerable to nature-related physical risks — water stress, land degradation, and biodiversity loss. Businesses that fail to account for these dependencies are carrying unpriced risk on their balance sheets, a fact that institutional investors and credit rating agencies are beginning to scrutinise with increasing rigour.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a TNFD LEAP screening: Use the TNFD's freely available LEAP (Locate, Evaluate, Assess, Prepare) methodology to map where your operations and tier-1 supply chain intersect with ecologically sensitive locations — start with IBAT (Integrated Biodiversity Assessment Tool) and global biodiversity footprint databases
  • Assign board-level ownership: Designate a board committee or senior executive (Chief Sustainability Officer or equivalent) as accountable for nature-related risk oversight, and ensure it is documented in your governance disclosures
  • Integrate nature into your existing ESG/BRSR reporting process: Audit your current BRSR disclosures for biodiversity, water, and land-use sections and identify gaps relative to TNFD's recommended metrics — treat this as a gap analysis, not a separate workstream
  • Engage your supply chain: Issue supplier questionnaires or updated code-of-conduct requirements covering deforestation commitments, water stewardship certifications (e.g., AWS, FSC, RSPO), and land-use practices — particularly for tier-1 suppliers in agriculture, forestry, and raw material extraction
  • Monitor regulatory developments actively: Track SEBI's evolving BRSR framework, the EU Deforestation Regulation's enforcement timeline, and ISSB's nature standard development — assign a team member to produce a quarterly regulatory watch brief so your compliance strategy stays ahead of mandatory deadlines

Our Take

The TNFD framework represents a structural shift in how capital markets will price nature-related risk — and for India, the implications are profound and proximate. This is not a framework designed for European companies alone; it is the scaffolding upon which global investors, banks, and regulators are building a new vocabulary of accountability for any business that touches land, water, or biodiversity. Indian corporates have navigated the climate disclosure learning curve with TCFD and BRSR — and those that moved early gained a measurable advantage in investor confidence and export market access. The nature disclosure curve will be steeper and more complex, because ecosystems are local, data infrastructure is nascent, and supply chain traceability in sectors like agriculture and textiles remains largely underdeveloped. But that complexity is precisely why the time to act is now, not when SEBI mandates it or when a European buyer sends a non-compliance notice. Companies that embed TNFD thinking into their strategy today will find it is not just a compliance exercise — it is a risk management and competitive differentiation opportunity that will define which Indian businesses remain investable in the decade ahead.
