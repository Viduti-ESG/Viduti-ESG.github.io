ESG & Climate Compliance Digest — 22 May 2026: EPR Enforcement Intensifies Across E-Waste, Plastics & Batteries

Executive Summary

Today's digest captures a significant regulatory surge across India's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) landscape — with urgent enforcement notices targeting e-waste recyclers and producers, a second amendment to Plastic Waste Management Rules, updated EPR guidelines for plastic packaging, two rounds of e-waste rule amendments, and back-to-back Battery Waste Management Rules in 2024 and 2025. The cumulative signal is unmistakable: India's Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) are closing compliance gaps and dramatically raising the cost of non-conformance. Producers, importers, brand owners, and waste processors operating in electronics, FMCG, consumer goods, EV/energy storage, and packaging-intensive sectors must treat EPR obligations as board-level risk — not a back-office checkbox.

What Changed

TODAY'S HEADLINE — EPR ENFORCEMENT NOTICES (E-WASTE): Two urgent notices issued by CPCB mark the sharpest escalation in e-waste enforcement to date. The first targets registered recyclers and refurbishers under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, flagging non-submission or under-reporting of processing data and threatening suspension or cancellation of EPR certificates. The second is directed at producers — including manufacturers and importers of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) — for failure to meet annual EPR targets, file returns, or maintain valid EPR authorisations. Both notices signal a shift from advisory to punitive enforcement. PLASTIC WASTE — 2ND AMENDMENT & EPR GUIDELINES: The 2nd Amendment to the Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2022 tightens definitions around single-use plastics, revises the categorisation of plastic packaging for EPR purposes, and introduces stricter producer accountability for multilayer plastics. Simultaneously, CPCB has released revised Guidelines on EPR for Plastic Packaging, which update the EPR certificate framework, clarify the role of PROs (Producer Responsibility Organisations), and set revised recycling/end-of-life targets by plastic category. Together, these updates close several loopholes that companies had used to reduce reported plastic footprints. E-WASTE RULE AMENDMENTS — 2023 & 2024: The E-Waste Second Amendment Rules (2023) introduced revised collection targets phased by producer turnover category and tightened the dismantling-to-recycling handoff documentation requirements. The Third Amendment Rules (2024) went further — introducing mandatory deposit-refund scheme pilots for select EEE categories, stricter penalties for non-compliant recyclers, and a new requirement for producers to publish annual EPR performance reports on their websites. Read together, these amendments represent a near-complete overhaul of the 2022 framework within just two years. BATTERY WASTE MANAGEMENT RULES — 2024 & 2025: The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2024 established India's first comprehensive EPR framework specifically for batteries — covering portable, automotive, industrial, and electric vehicle batteries. The 2025 update accelerates collection and recycling targets, introduces extended producer liability for battery-embedded products (including EVs and consumer electronics), and mandates traceability through a centralised Battery Waste Management Portal. This is particularly consequential for EV OEMs, energy storage system providers, and consumer electronics brands, all of whom now carry full lifecycle responsibility for battery waste.

Who Is Affected

  • Electronics & IT Hardware Manufacturers (laptops, phones, white goods, industrial EEE) — directly under e-waste producer EPR obligations
  • Importers & Brand Owners of EEE — including global brands selling through Indian subsidiaries or e-commerce channels
  • E-Waste Recyclers & Refurbishers — facing direct CPCB scrutiny on processing records and certificate validity
  • FMCG, Retail & Consumer Goods companies — under plastic packaging EPR rules, especially those using multilayer or non-recyclable packaging
  • EV Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) and Battery Manufacturers/Importers — battery waste EPR obligations now fully operational
  • Energy Storage System (ESS) Providers — industrial battery EPR targets now applicable
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) — compliance and reporting obligations tightened across all three waste streams
  • E-commerce platforms acting as marketplace operators for EEE or packaged goods — increasingly in regulatory scope as deemed producers
  • SMEs and MSMEs in manufacturing with annual turnover above threshold limits — phased targets now apply

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE (within 30 days of notice): E-waste producers and recyclers served with CPCB urgent notices must respond with compliance documentation, EPR certificate status, and corrective action plans — failure risks certificate suspension
  • Q1 FY2026-27 (by 30 June 2026): Annual EPR return filing deadline for e-waste producers for FY2025-26 on the CPCB EPR Portal — non-filing triggers automatic penalty proceedings
  • 30 June 2026: Plastic packaging EPR targets for FY2025-26 must be met and reported; revised guidelines require updated PRO agreements aligned to new recycling categories
  • 31 July 2026: Battery waste EPR targets (2025 Rules) — annual collection and recycling obligations for FY2025-26 must be submitted via the Battery Waste Management Portal
  • Ongoing — FY2026-27 Targets: E-waste Third Amendment (2024) requires producers to publish EPR performance reports on corporate websites by 30 September 2026 for the previous financial year
  • Ongoing: Recyclers must maintain real-time digital records of inward and outward material flows; CPCB has indicated surprise audits will increase in H2 2026
  • Ongoing: Multilayer plastic producers must demonstrate transition roadmaps to mono-material or recyclable alternatives under the revised PWM Amendment — no hard deadline yet, but regulatory guidance expected by Q3 2026
  • EV OEMs: Battery traceability registration on the Battery Waste Management Portal is now mandatory for all new vehicle models; retrospective registration for existing models required by 31 December 2026

Climate Transition Angle

India's escalating EPR enforcement is not occurring in isolation — it is structurally aligned with the global shift toward circular economy principles embedded in major international frameworks. The ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standard requires companies to assess physical and transition risks across their full value chain, which now unambiguously includes end-of-life waste liabilities. Companies with unresolved EPR non-compliance are, in effect, carrying unquantified transition risk on their balance sheets. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its accompanying European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) require disclosure on circular economy practices, waste generation, and resource use — meaning Indian subsidiaries or supply chain partners of EU-headquartered companies will face double-barrelled pressure: Indian regulatory compliance AND international disclosure obligations. The battery waste framework, in particular, connects directly to physical climate risk. As India's EV penetration grows — driven partly by climate policy and partly by energy security — the volume of lithium-ion battery waste will grow exponentially. Poorly managed battery waste creates both toxic contamination risk (a physical risk to communities and supply chains) and a critical minerals recovery opportunity. Companies that build closed-loop battery recovery systems now will have a strategic advantage in securing recycled lithium, cobalt, and nickel as virgin material costs rise. Finally, the tightening of plastic waste rules signals regulatory alignment with India's commitments under the UN Global Plastics Treaty negotiations. Companies that treat EPR as a minimum compliance floor rather than a strategic input risk being caught flat-footed when treaty obligations translate into even stricter domestic law — likely within the next 24–36 months.

What To Do Now

  • AUDIT EPR REGISTRATION & CERTIFICATE STATUS TODAY: Cross-check all EPR certificates (e-waste, plastic, battery) for validity, target achievement status, and filing deadlines on the CPCB portal. Identify any gaps before CPCB does — proactive disclosure is treated more favourably than reactive compliance.
  • ISSUE AN INTERNAL COMPLIANCE ALERT TO ALL BUSINESS UNITS: Given the multi-stream nature of today's updates (e-waste + plastics + batteries), sustainability and legal teams should immediately brief product, procurement, and operations heads on applicable obligations. Many EPR failures originate in product design and procurement decisions made without compliance input.
  • REVIEW PRO AND RECYCLER CONTRACTS: Validate that your registered PROs and recycling partners have valid CPCB authorisations and are meeting their processing obligations on your behalf. An invalid or suspended recycler certificate directly exposes the producer — conduct due diligence now, not at year-end.
  • MAP YOUR BATTERY AND PLASTIC FOOTPRINT TO THE NEW RULES: For companies in EV, consumer electronics, or FMCG sectors, conduct a rapid materiality assessment against the updated Battery Waste Rules (2025) and revised plastic EPR guidelines. Quantify your FY2026-27 EPR targets and identify whether current collection infrastructure is sufficient.
  • PREPARE FOR DISCLOSURE — ALIGN EPR DATA WITH ISSB/BRSR REPORTING: Begin structuring EPR performance data (collection volumes, recycling rates, EPR certificate spend) in a format compatible with Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) Core disclosures and ISSB S2 value chain metrics. Investors and lenders are increasingly requesting this data in ESG due diligence.

Our Take

Today's cluster of regulatory updates tells a single, coherent story: India is done with the 'awareness and registration' phase of EPR and has entered the 'enforce and penalise' phase with intent. The CPCB urgent notices — issued simultaneously against both recyclers and producers — are a deliberate signal that the regulator is tightening the full value chain at once, preventing companies from offloading liability onto downstream processors. The back-to-back amendments to e-waste and battery rules, layered over a revised plastic EPR framework, reflect a regulatory architecture that is becoming more sophisticated and more difficult to navigate through partial compliance. What's coming next? Expect CPCB to operationalise the deposit-refund scheme for select EEE categories by late FY2027, mandatory third-party EPR audits for Tier 1 producers, and the integration of EPR performance data into the BRSR Core framework for listed companies. For sustainability officers, the message is clear: EPR is no longer a peripheral compliance function — it is a material ESG risk that requires the same rigour, resourcing, and board visibility as carbon reporting.
