CAT's Power Sector Decarbonisation Scorecard: What India's NDC Update and Renewable Energy Trajectory Mean for Capital Markets and BRSR Disclosure

Executive Summary

The Climate Action Tracker's May 2025 virtual event — 'Plugging into Progress: Evaluating Power Sector Decarbonisation for 1.5°C' — has released a structured evaluation of whether countries' renewable energy rollouts are on track to meet the COP28 commitment to triple global renewables capacity by 2030. For India, this is not an abstract global scorecard: India's updated NDC — due in 2025 as part of the Paris Agreement cycle — will be benchmarked against exactly this framework, and its credibility gap or progress story will directly influence sovereign climate risk ratings, green bond demand, and the ESG narrative Indian listed companies can credibly tell institutional investors. The CAT analysis, sourced from climateactiontracker.org, makes clear that NDC ambition without sectoral power decarbonisation evidence is no longer sufficient for 1.5°C alignment. For Indian CFOs and CSOs at BSE 500 companies, this event signals an accelerating convergence: global investors and supply chain counterparties will increasingly triangulate India's national power grid trajectory against company-level Scope 2 disclosures under BRSR Core and ISSB IFRS S2, making grid decarbonisation both a country-level diplomatic issue and an enterprise-level financial materiality question.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/virtual-event-pulling-into-progress-power-decarbonisation/) documents a formal structured evaluation of national power sector decarbonisation trajectories against the 1.5°C pathway, with particular focus on whether the COP28 tripling-renewables pledge — signed by over 130 countries including India — is being translated into credible, measurable NDC commitments in 2025. CAT's framework goes beyond headline renewable capacity targets and evaluates the pace of rollout, policy sufficiency, and alignment with Paris Agreement carbon budgets. The key analytical shift: CAT is now treating power sector decarbonisation as a leading indicator of NDC credibility, not just a sub-component. For India, this matters acutely. India submitted its updated NDC in 2022, committing to 500 GW non-fossil capacity by 2030 and a 45% reduction in emissions intensity of GDP. The 2025 NDC revision cycle — which all Paris signatories are expected to complete ahead of COP30 in Belém, Brazil, in November 2025 — will require India to either deepen these commitments or explain why not. CAT's evaluation framework, now publicly available, will be used by investors, ratings agencies (including those informing MSCI ESG Ratings, S&P Global ESG Scores, and Sustainalytics), and EU counterparties assessing India under CBAM and CSRD value chain due diligence to judge whether India's power sector transition is real or rhetorical. Critically, India's current renewable addition rate — while impressive in absolute terms, with over 200 GW of installed non-fossil capacity as of early 2025 — still faces structural challenges: grid integration bottlenecks, land acquisition delays, state DISCOM financial distress, and a coal capacity pipeline that remains active. CAT's methodology distinguishes between installed capacity and actual clean energy share in generation mix, a distinction that will increasingly matter to Scope 2 emissions calculations that Indian companies must disclose under BRSR Core (mandatory for top 150 listed entities by market cap from FY2023-24) and that institutional investors will scrutinise under ISSB IFRS S2 climate-related disclosure frameworks.

Who Is Affected

  • Power generation and utilities sector: NTPC, Adani Green, Tata Power, JSW Energy, ReNew Power — directly rated on generation mix trajectory and capex alignment with 1.5°C pathways
  • Energy-intensive industries with high Scope 2 exposure: Steel (Tata Steel, JSW Steel, SAIL), Aluminium (Hindalco, Vedanta), Cement (UltraTech, ACC, Ambuja), and Data Centres — whose decarbonisation credibility is structurally dependent on grid emission factor trajectory
  • Top 150 BSE-listed companies by market cap: mandatory BRSR Core disclosures require Scope 1 and 2 GHG reporting; grid emission factor assumptions used in Scope 2 calculations will face investor scrutiny against CAT's power sector ratings
  • Export-oriented manufacturers: companies supplying to EU buyers face CSRD value chain disclosure requirements and CBAM carbon pricing on steel, aluminium, cement, and fertilisers — all sectors where Scope 2 grid intensity is a direct cost and compliance variable
  • CFOs and Treasury teams at companies with green bonds or sustainability-linked loans: covenants tied to renewable energy procurement or Scope 2 reduction targets are exposed if India's grid decarbonisation pace slips
  • ESG analysts and portfolio managers at FIIs, domestic mutual funds with ESG mandates, and development finance institutions (IFC, ADB, AIIB) financing Indian energy transition assets
  • India's Ministry of New and Renewable Energy (MNRE) and MoEFCC officials preparing India's 2025 NDC revision — CAT's evaluation will be a reference document at COP30 negotiations

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • India's updated NDC submission: Due ahead of COP30 (November 2025, Belém) — India must present revised 2035 targets; CAT's power sector evaluation will benchmark the credibility of any renewable energy commitment made
  • BRSR Core — Scope 2 GHG disclosure: Mandatory for top 150 listed companies by market cap from FY2023-24 reporting (published in FY2024-25 annual reports); companies must use a defensible grid emission factor — currently CEA's CO2 baseline database — and disclose methodology
  • BRSR Core — Energy intensity and renewable energy consumption: Leadership indicators require disclosure of percentage of renewable energy consumed; this directly links to grid mix trajectory and on-site/off-site renewable procurement strategies
  • COP28 Tripling Renewables Pledge: India is a signatory; 2025 NDC must demonstrate a credible pathway to contribution — failure to do so will be flagged by CAT and picked up by sovereign ESG rating agencies
  • EU CBAM Phase-In: Full CBAM obligations begin January 2026 for steel, cement, aluminium, fertilisers, hydrogen, and electricity; Indian exporters in these sectors must report embedded carbon including Scope 2 from grid electricity — grid decarbonisation pace directly affects CBAM liability
  • SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard: Companies with validated science-based targets must align Scope 2 reduction trajectories with a 1.5°C pathway; CAT's assessment of India's grid trajectory will inform SBTi's country-level grid decarbonisation assumptions used in target validation
  • ISSB IFRS S2 adoption: While not yet mandated in India, SEBI's stated intent to align BRSR with ISSB means companies should begin preparing climate-related financial disclosures including transition risk scenarios tied to power sector decarbonisation pace

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT power sector evaluation is, at its core, a transition risk stress test applied at the country level — and for Indian businesses, it directly shapes the financial materiality landscape. India's NDC credibility gap, if identified by CAT, will flow into sovereign climate risk assessments used by Moody's, S&P, and Fitch, raising the cost of green sovereign bonds and infrastructure financing. At the corporate level, the most immediate transition risk is Scope 2 emissions intensity: if India's grid emission factor — currently approximately 0.71 tCO2/MWh on the CEA baseline — does not decline at a pace consistent with 1.5°C, then energy-intensive Indian manufacturers face a structural competitive disadvantage in EU export markets under CBAM, and a credibility deficit in meeting SBTi-validated Scope 2 reduction targets. The BRSR Core framework requires listed Indian companies to disclose energy consumption from grid versus renewable sources, and to report total Scope 2 emissions. As institutional investors — particularly those aligned with TCFD, ISSB IFRS S2, and the Net Zero Asset Managers Initiative — increase their scrutiny of Scope 2 trajectories, a stagnant or slow-decarbonising Indian grid becomes a portfolio-level risk flag. Indian companies that have not invested in captive renewable energy, Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) for RE100-quality certificates, or RECs will find their Scope 2 disclosures increasingly difficult to defend against 1.5°C alignment benchmarks that CAT's methodology now makes publicly accessible and comparable. The physical risk dimension is equally present: India's power sector is already experiencing grid stress from extreme heat events — a trend that the IPCC AR6 WG2 report identifies as accelerating under 1.5°C and beyond, creating both reliability risk for industrial consumers and stranded asset risk for coal-heavy generation portfolios.

What To Do Now

  • Sustainability and Strategy teams (immediate, Q2-Q3 FY2025-26): Download CAT's power sector evaluation slides and methodology; map India's rating against your company's Scope 2 emission factor assumptions in BRSR Core disclosures — identify whether your current grid emission factor is defensible or needs updating to CEA's latest CO2 baseline
  • CFOs and Treasury (within 90 days): Review green bond frameworks, sustainability-linked loan covenants, and ESG-linked financing terms for any Scope 2 or renewable energy KPIs; assess exposure if India's grid decarbonisation pace causes covenant breach or target miss — engage lenders proactively with a grid trajectory narrative
  • Procurement and Energy teams (FY2025-26 planning cycle): Accelerate evaluation of long-term PPAs, open access renewable energy, and captive solar/wind projects to structurally decouple Scope 2 intensity from grid trajectory — this is now both a compliance necessity (BRSR Core, SBTi) and a CBAM cost-management strategy for exporters
  • Board Risk Committee (next board meeting): Add 'India NDC revision and power sector decarbonisation trajectory' as a standing agenda item under climate-related financial risk; commission a scenario analysis under ISSB IFRS S2 Appendix B climate scenarios showing the impact of slow vs. fast grid decarbonisation on Scope 2 costs and export competitiveness
  • Investor Relations (ahead of AGM season FY2025-26): Prepare a briefing note for institutional investors on your company's Scope 2 strategy relative to India's grid trajectory, referencing CAT's framework; proactively address how you are managing the gap between current grid emission factor and your net-zero or SBTi pathway — investors aligned with NZAM and PRI will ask this question
  • Legal and Compliance (Q3 FY2025-26): Map CBAM embedded carbon reporting requirements for any steel, aluminium, cement, or fertiliser exports to the EU against current Scope 2 grid emission factor; engage with EU CBAM authorised declarant process and ensure carbon data chain of custody is audit-ready by January 2026

Our Take

CAT's structured power sector evaluation is a signal of a much larger shift: the era of NDC-level ambiguity is ending. By 2026-2027, sovereign climate performance on power decarbonisation will be a hard input into capital market pricing — not a soft ESG narrative. India is at a pivotal juncture. Its renewable energy capacity addition story is genuinely impressive, but CAT's methodology will expose the gap between installed capacity headlines and actual grid carbon intensity, and that gap has direct financial consequences for Indian listed companies across three vectors: Scope 2 disclosure credibility under BRSR Core and ISSB IFRS S2, CBAM cost exposure for industrial exporters, and SBTi target validation integrity. What boards and investors should watch over the next 12-36 months: First, India's 2025 NDC revision will be a defining moment — if it fails to include a credible 2035 power sector decarbonisation trajectory with quantified coal phase-down milestones, expect CAT and allied research bodies to publish critical assessments that will be cited in sovereign ESG ratings downgrades. Second, SEBI is moving toward mandatory ISSB IFRS S2 alignment — the BRSR evolution roadmap telegraphed in SEBI's 2023 consultation suggests that Scope 2 market-based disclosures and transition plan requirements are coming within the next 2-3 years, which will make RE100-quality procurement and grid factor methodology a board-level governance question, not just a sustainability team exercise. Third, the EU's CSRD value chain rules will force large Indian suppliers to multinational companies to provide power sector carbon data that is externally assured — companies that have not invested in renewable energy procurement infrastructure by 2026 will face supply chain disqualification risk, not just reputational exposure. India's power sector transition is no longer a background condition for corporate ESG strategy — it is the single most important structural variable determining whether Indian companies can credibly claim 1.5°C alignment, and the capital market consequences of getting this wrong are now measurable, proximate, and accelerating.
