Draft Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2022: What India's Businesses Must Know About the Next Phase of EPR

Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) released draft amendments to the Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2016 in January 2022, significantly tightening the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework for producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs). The amendments introduce sharper definitions for plastic packaging waste categories, expand the scope of plastic waste processors, and reinforce that EPR obligations will be governed by periodically updated regulations — making compliance a moving target rather than a one-time exercise. Companies that use, produce, or import plastic packaging must treat this not as a routine regulatory update, but as a structural shift in how plastic accountability is governed in India.

What Changed

The 2022 draft amends the foundational Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2016 and builds on the 2021 Amendment Rules (notified August 12, 2021) that first introduced the EPR framework for plastic packaging. Here is what is new in plain language: First, the draft introduces a set of new and revised definitions that significantly broaden the regulatory perimeter. 'Brand owner' is now clearly defined as any individual or company selling goods under a registered brand label or trademark — closing a loophole that previously allowed some brand operators to sidestep liability. 'Plastic packaging' is redefined comprehensively to cover all plastic used for protection, preservation, storage, and transportation of products. Second, the rules introduce two new and important waste categories: 'pre-consumer plastic packaging waste' (waste generated during the manufacturing and packaging process, before the product reaches the end consumer) and 'post-consumer plastic packaging waste' (waste generated after the consumer has used and discarded the packaging). This distinction is critical because it means EPR obligations now extend upstream into manufacturing processes, not just downstream to post-sale collection. Third, the definition of 'plastic waste processors' is expanded to include recyclers, waste-to-energy facilities (including co-processing in cement kilns), waste-to-oil converters, and industrial composters — broadening the ecosystem of compliant end-of-life pathways that PIBOs can use to meet their EPR targets. Fourth, 'end-of-life disposal' is formally defined to include energy recovery (co-processing in cement kilns), waste-to-oil production, and use in road construction per Indian Roads Congress guidelines — legitimising these pathways under the rules. Fifth, and perhaps most consequentially for compliance planning, Rule 9(1) is replaced with language stating that producers, importers, and brand owners shall fulfil their EPR obligations 'in accordance with regulations issued from time to time under these rules.' This means EPR targets, timelines, and mechanisms are no longer static — they will be updated by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) through regulations, requiring companies to continuously monitor for changes. Finally, registration requirements are clarified: entities operating in more than two states or union territories must register with the CPCB (not state boards), creating a clearer compliance pathway for national and multinational brands.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers and manufacturers of plastic packaging materials — from flexible films and rigid containers to multilayer packaging and carry bags
  • Brand owners — any company selling products in plastic packaging under a registered trademark, regardless of whether they manufacture the packaging themselves
  • Importers of plastic packaging products or products packaged in plastic, including importers of multilayer and single-use packaging
  • Plastic waste recyclers, waste-to-energy operators, and industrial composters who are newly classified as 'plastic waste processors' with defined roles in the EPR chain
  • Fast-Moving Consumer Goods (FMCG) companies, e-commerce firms, food and beverage brands, pharmaceutical companies, and consumer durables manufacturers — all heavy users of plastic packaging
  • Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) operating as brand owners or manufacturers, who now have no exemption from EPR obligations
  • Exporters of plastic packaging or plastic-packaged goods, who face expanded definitional scope under the revised 'importer/exporter' clause

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • EPR Compliance (Ongoing): Producers, importers, and brand owners must fulfil EPR obligations for plastic packaging as per CPCB regulations issued under these rules — companies must register on the CPCB's centralised EPR portal and report collection and recycling targets on time
  • Registration Jurisdiction Clarity (Immediate): Entities operating across more than two states or union territories must register with the CPCB centrally; those operating within a single state register with the respective State Pollution Control Board (SPCB) or Pollution Control Committee (PCC) under Rule 9(5)
  • Pre-Consumer Waste Accounting (Upon Finalization): Once the rules are finalized, companies must track and report pre-consumer plastic packaging waste (waste generated during in-house manufacturing and packaging operations) as a distinct waste stream — update internal waste accounting systems accordingly
  • Post-Consumer Waste Collection Targets (Per CPCB Regulations): PIBOs must meet annually escalating collection and recycling targets for post-consumer plastic packaging waste as notified by CPCB — FY2024-25 onward targets have been set; verify current targets on the CPCB EPR portal
  • End-of-Life Pathway Validation (Ongoing): Only CPCB-approved plastic waste processors (recyclers, co-processors, waste-to-oil units) qualify for EPR credit — ensure all third-party processors in your waste management chain are registered and certified
  • Thickness and Specification Compliance (Ongoing): Rule 4(1)(d) amendments specify that thickness exemptions are only as notified by the government — review all plastic product specifications against current MoEFCC notifications
  • Draft Comment Window: The draft was open for public comments to the Secretary, MoEFCC via post or email (satyendra.kumar07@nic.in / amit.love@nic.in) within 60 days of gazette publication — industry associations should verify if final rules have been notified and check for any modifications from the draft stage

Climate Transition Angle

India's plastic waste reforms are not happening in isolation — they are part of a sweeping global realignment of how plastic is governed as both a pollution crisis and a climate risk. The two are deeply connected. Plastic production is almost entirely fossil-fuel dependent; globally, plastic manufacturing accounts for approximately 3.4% of greenhouse gas emissions, and that figure is projected to grow significantly if no structural intervention occurs. The expansion of EPR to cover pre-consumer waste is directly aligned with the logic of Scope 3 emissions accounting. Just as companies are now expected under frameworks like the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) IFRS S2 and the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) to account for emissions across their entire value chain — including raw material extraction and processing — the expanded plastic waste rules now require companies to account for packaging waste generated upstream in their supply chain, not just what the consumer discards. The recognition of waste-to-energy and co-processing as compliant end-of-life pathways also has a climate dimension that requires careful scrutiny. While these pathways recover energy value from plastic, they are not zero-carbon solutions — co-processing in cement kilns, for example, releases CO2. Companies that rely heavily on waste-to-energy to meet EPR targets should be aware that international sustainability standards (particularly the EU Taxonomy and CSRD) treat such pathways with caution and may not recognise them as genuinely circular or low-carbon solutions for disclosure purposes. Supply chain risk is the other critical lens. Companies that source plastic packaging from suppliers who are not EPR-compliant face increasing regulatory and reputational exposure. As the CPCB tightens enforcement and EPR certificate trading becomes more formalised, non-compliant supply chains will become a material liability — both in terms of regulatory penalties and in terms of ESG ratings and investor scrutiny. The physical risk dimension is equally real: plastic pollution degrades watersheds, agricultural soil, and coastal ecosystems on which India's food and textile supply chains depend. Finally, India's plastic EPR framework is converging — albeit imperfectly — with the trajectory of the UN Global Plastics Treaty negotiations, which aim to create binding international obligations on plastic production, design, and waste management. Indian companies with export exposure, particularly to the EU (where the Single-Use Plastics Directive and Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation are already in force), must treat domestic EPR compliance as a floor, not a ceiling.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a Plastic Packaging Audit: Map all plastic packaging used across your product portfolio — distinguish between pre-consumer waste (generated in your facilities) and post-consumer waste (generated by end consumers). Quantify volumes by category (rigid, flexible, multilayer, single-use) to establish your EPR baseline and ensure your data is ready for CPCB reporting cycles.
  • Verify and Update CPCB EPR Portal Registration: Confirm your registration status on the CPCB centralised EPR portal. If your operations span more than two states, ensure registration is with CPCB directly, not just state boards. Update your registration to reflect any new product lines or packaging types covered under the revised definitions.
  • Audit Your Plastic Waste Processor Network: Review all recyclers, co-processors, and waste processors in your EPR fulfilment chain. Confirm they are registered with CPCB or the relevant SPCB as 'plastic waste processors' under the updated definition. Processors without valid registration will not generate recognised EPR credits, leaving you non-compliant regardless of volumes collected.
  • Align Internal ESG Reporting with the New Waste Categories: Update your sustainability reporting templates — including BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report) disclosures for listed companies — to separately account for pre-consumer and post-consumer plastic packaging waste. This granularity is increasingly expected by ESG rating agencies and institutional investors, and will be required for alignment with ISSB IFRS S2 value chain disclosures.
  • Engage Legal and Government Affairs Teams to Track Final Notification: The January 2022 gazette notification was a draft open for public comment. Assign responsibility to track whether and when the final rules have been notified, and identify any changes from the draft. Given that Rule 9(1) now ties EPR obligations to periodically updated CPCB regulations, establish a standing process to monitor CPCB circulars and gazette notifications on plastic waste — this is no longer a set-and-forget compliance exercise.

Our Take

The Draft Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2022 represent a meaningful maturation of India's plastic governance architecture — but the most important signal for business leaders is not in any single definition or deadline. It is in the structural shift embedded in the revised Rule 9(1): EPR obligations are now explicitly dynamic, governed by regulations that will be updated 'from time to time.' This is a deliberate design choice by MoEFCC and CPCB, giving regulators the flexibility to ratchet up targets without going through the full rule-amendment process each time. For companies, this means that plastic compliance is now a live risk — one that requires continuous monitoring, not just an annual checkbox. The convergence of India's EPR framework with global standards like CSRD and the emerging UN Plastics Treaty also means that companies building robust domestic compliance systems today are, in effect, future-proofing themselves for international market access requirements. Those that treat EPR as a cost to be minimised rather than a capability to be built will find themselves structurally disadvantaged as enforcement tightens and as investors increasingly price in plastic-related liability. Our strong advice: invest now in the data infrastructure, supplier engagement, and internal governance needed to make plastic accountability a core competency — the regulatory trajectory makes this a question of when, not if.
