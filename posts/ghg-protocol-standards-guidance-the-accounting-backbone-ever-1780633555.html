Executive Summary

The GHG Protocol provides the world's most widely used greenhouse gas accounting and reporting standards, forming the methodological foundation for virtually every major climate disclosure framework — including SEBI's BRSR Core, the ISSB's IFRS S2, and the EU's CSRD. Indian companies that fail to ground their emissions reporting in GHG Protocol methodology risk producing disclosures that are inconsistent, non-comparable, and increasingly unacceptable to regulators, investors, and global supply chain partners. Understanding and correctly applying these standards is no longer optional — it is the baseline for credible climate action.

What Changed

The GHG Protocol, jointly developed by the World Resources Institute (WRI) and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD), is not a single regulation but a suite of standards and guidance documents that define how organisations measure, manage, and report their greenhouse gas emissions. The core suite includes the Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard (the foundational standard for Scope 1 and 2 emissions), the Scope 3 Standard (covering value chain emissions across 15 categories), the Market-Based Accounting Guidance (for Scope 2 electricity reporting), and the Land Sector and Removals Guidance, among others. While the GHG Protocol itself is not a regulatory mandate in India, it is the methodological engine that powers frameworks that are mandated — most notably SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) framework, which explicitly references GHG Protocol definitions for Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 emissions. As India's regulatory expectations on climate disclosure intensify, and as global standard-setters converge on GHG Protocol methodology, fluency in these standards has become a core corporate competency. Critically, the GHG Protocol is currently undergoing its most significant update in over a decade. WRI and WBCSD have launched a formal review process to update the Corporate Standard and the Scope 3 Standard, with stakeholder consultations ongoing. The updated standards are expected to reflect advances in climate science, greater alignment with ISSB and TCFD frameworks, and stronger guidance on avoided emissions, removals, and net-zero target-setting. Indian companies should monitor these developments closely, as updated standards will cascade into BRSR and other domestic requirements.

Who Is Affected

  • Top 1000 listed companies required to submit BRSR reports under SEBI's mandate — GHG Protocol methodology underpins all emissions disclosures
  • Companies in the BRSR Core cohort (initially the top 150 by market capitalisation) subject to reasonable assurance on ESG disclosures, where methodological rigour is directly scrutinised
  • Export-oriented manufacturers and suppliers in sectors such as textiles, chemicals, steel, cement, and automotive whose global buyers require GHG Protocol-aligned Scope 3 data
  • Companies subject to the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) — iron, steel, cement, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, and hydrogen exporters to the EU
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational corporations whose parent companies report under CSRD, CDP, or ISSB frameworks
  • Companies pursuing Science Based Targets (SBTi) validation, which mandates GHG Protocol-compliant emissions inventories
  • Financial institutions and asset managers conducting portfolio emissions assessments under PCAF (Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials), which is built on GHG Protocol principles

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core Compliance (FY 2023-24 onwards): Top 150 listed companies must disclose Scope 1, Scope 2, and material Scope 3 emissions using GHG Protocol-aligned methodology, subject to reasonable assurance by a third-party auditor
  • BRSR Extended Rollout: SEBI is progressively expanding BRSR Core requirements to the top 1000 listed companies — companies outside the initial cohort should treat FY 2024-25 as a preparation year to build GHG accounting capability
  • EU CBAM Transition Period (now live): Indian exporters of in-scope goods to the EU must report embedded emissions using methodologies consistent with GHG Protocol principles; full financial obligations under CBAM commence in 2026
  • CDP Disclosure Cycle (annual, typically April–July submission window): CDP questionnaires for Climate, Water, and Forests require GHG Protocol-aligned data; scores directly influence investor and customer assessments
  • GHG Protocol Corporate Standard Update (expected 2025-2026): Monitor WRI/WBCSD stakeholder consultation outputs and prepare to transition to updated standards once finalised — establish a methodology review process now
  • Science Based Targets (SBTi) Validation: Companies committing to net-zero targets must submit GHG Protocol-compliant base-year inventories; SBTi has tightened validation timelines and criteria in its Corporate Net-Zero Standard
  • Ongoing: Establish or review organisational and operational boundaries, consolidation approaches (equity share vs. operational control vs. financial control), and Scope 3 materiality assessments in line with GHG Protocol guidance

Climate Transition Angle

The GHG Protocol is not merely an accounting exercise — it is the infrastructure through which physical climate risks and transition risks become quantifiable, comparable, and actionable. Without a robust GHG inventory, companies cannot credibly assess their exposure to carbon pricing, stranded asset risk, or the carbon intensity demands of their customers and capital providers. From a supply chain perspective, Scope 3 accounting — the most complex and consequential part of the GHG Protocol suite — is rapidly becoming a competitive differentiator and a commercial requirement. Global buyers, particularly in Europe and North America, are embedding Scope 3 data requests into procurement contracts. Indian suppliers that cannot provide verified, GHG Protocol-aligned emissions data risk losing market access, regardless of the quality of their products. The convergence of global disclosure frameworks around GHG Protocol methodology is accelerating. IFRS S2 (the ISSB's climate disclosure standard) explicitly references GHG Protocol for emissions measurement. The EU's CSRD and its underlying European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) do the same. The SEC's climate disclosure rule in the United States, though facing legal challenges, also draws on GHG Protocol concepts. For Indian companies with cross-border operations, listings, or investor bases, GHG Protocol fluency is the common language of global climate finance and trade. Looking ahead, the ongoing update to the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard is expected to introduce stronger guidance on the treatment of carbon removals, the distinction between gross and net emissions, and the integration of physical risk data into transition planning. Companies that build deep internal capability in GHG accounting now will be far better positioned to adapt to these evolving requirements — and to use their emissions data strategically for target-setting, capital allocation, and stakeholder communication.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a GHG Protocol Methodology Audit: Review your existing Scope 1, 2, and 3 inventory against the current GHG Protocol Corporate Standard. Identify gaps in boundary-setting, emission factor selection, data quality, and documentation — particularly if your current disclosures were prepared without specialist input
  • Map Scope 3 Materiality: Use the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard's screening methodology to identify which of the 15 categories are material to your business. Prioritise upstream categories (purchased goods and services, capital goods, fuel and energy) and downstream categories (use of sold products, end-of-life treatment) based on sector relevance and data availability
  • Establish an Emissions Data Management System: Move beyond spreadsheet-based tracking. Invest in a dedicated GHG accounting platform or ERP-integrated solution that supports audit trails, emission factor libraries, uncertainty assessments, and scenario modelling — all requirements for third-party assurance under BRSR Core
  • Monitor the GHG Protocol Update Process: Assign a member of your sustainability team to track WRI/WBCSD consultation documents and draft standard releases. Participate in stakeholder consultations where possible. Begin assessing how proposed changes — particularly on Scope 3 recalculation triggers and removals accounting — may affect your reported figures
  • Align Internal Capacity with External Assurance Requirements: Train finance, procurement, operations, and sustainability teams on GHG Protocol fundamentals. Engage your statutory auditor or a specialist ESG assurance provider early to understand what evidence and controls they will require for BRSR Core reasonable assurance — and build those systems before the reporting deadline

Our Take

The GHG Protocol is the closest thing the climate disclosure world has to a universal accounting standard, and its influence on Indian corporate reporting is only going to deepen. As SEBI tightens BRSR requirements, as CBAM reshapes the economics of exports to Europe, and as institutional investors apply increasingly sophisticated climate lenses to portfolio analysis, the quality of a company's GHG accounting will be read as a proxy for the quality of its overall climate governance. Companies that treat GHG Protocol compliance as a checkbox exercise — producing numbers without understanding the methodology, boundaries, or uncertainty behind them — will find themselves exposed when assurance providers, regulators, or activist investors probe the detail. The upcoming update to the Corporate Standard is a pivotal moment: it will likely raise the bar on Scope 3 rigour and bring GHG accounting closer to financial accounting in terms of auditability and precision. Indian business leaders who invest now in building genuine internal capability — not just in reporting, but in using emissions data to drive strategic decisions — will be the ones setting the terms of the climate transition, rather than scrambling to keep up with it.
