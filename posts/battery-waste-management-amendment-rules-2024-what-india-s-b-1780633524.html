Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change has amended the Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 with effect from March 14, 2024, tightening the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework for batteries. The amendments introduce price floors and ceilings on EPR certificates, mandate structured Environmental Compensation guidelines, and significantly liberalise carry-forward provisions for unmet collection targets — replacing volume-averaged calculations with a simpler 'remaining quantity' basis, uniformly capped at 60%. Producers, importers, refurbishers, and recyclers of all battery types must immediately reassess their EPR compliance strategies and portal registrations to align with the revised rules.

What Changed

The Battery Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2024 make four substantive changes to the parent Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022. First, Rule 10 is amended to establish a price band for EPR certificates traded on the CPCB portal: the highest permissible price is set at 100% of the Environmental Compensation (EC) payable for non-fulfilment of EPR obligations, and the lowest permissible price is set at 30% of that EC value. This creates a regulated market corridor — EPR certificates can no longer be traded below 30% of the penalty equivalent, preventing a race to the bottom in certificate pricing. Second, a new sub-rule (18) is inserted to clarify that all certificate exchange prices between registered entities must fall within this regulated band, ensuring market integrity on the CPCB portal. Third, Rule 13(3) is rewritten to require the CPCB to formally prepare, consult on, and recommend to MoEF&CC a comprehensive guideline for the imposition and collection of Environmental Compensation from producers, refurbishers, and recyclers who fail to comply. This replaces a more open-ended provision and introduces a consultative process via the Rule 15 Implementation Committee before guidelines are finalised. Fourth, and perhaps most practically impactful, Schedule II carry-forward provisions are uniformly revised across multiple battery categories (including automotive, industrial, electric vehicle, and portable batteries under clauses vi through xii). The old language calculated carry-forward as a percentage of the average annual quantity placed in the market over multi-year cycles (which varied — 60% for some, 20% for others). The new, uniform language allows carry-forward of up to 60% of the remaining unmet quantity from the applicable compliance cycle. This shift from an average-based to a residual-based calculation is more equitable for companies whose market volumes fluctuate year to year, and it standardises the carry-forward cap at 60% across all major battery categories.

Who Is Affected

  • Battery Producers and Manufacturers: All entities that manufacture batteries in India — including EV batteries (lithium-ion), automotive lead-acid batteries, industrial batteries, and portable consumer batteries — and are registered on the CPCB EPR portal.
  • Battery Importers: Companies importing batteries or battery-containing products (EVs, electronics, UPS systems, power tools) that have EPR obligations under the 2022 Rules.
  • Electric Vehicle (EV) OEMs: Automobile manufacturers selling EVs in India, who bear producer responsibility for the batteries placed in the market through their vehicles.
  • Battery Recyclers and Refurbishers: Entities engaged in collection, refurbishment, or recycling of waste batteries that participate in the EPR certificate ecosystem or are subject to Environmental Compensation for non-compliance.
  • EPR Certificate Traders and Intermediaries: Registered entities buying and selling EPR certificates on the CPCB portal, who must now operate within the newly defined price band.
  • Consumer Electronics and IT Hardware Companies: Brands placing portable battery-containing devices in the Indian market with battery EPR obligations.
  • Telecom Companies and Data Centre Operators: Large users of industrial and stationary batteries with take-back and EPR responsibilities.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Immediate (effective March 14, 2024): All EPR certificate trades on the CPCB portal must fall within the newly defined price band — minimum 30% and maximum 100% of the Environmental Compensation value applicable to the obligated entity. Any trades structured outside this band are non-compliant from the date of notification.
  • Ongoing — EPR Target Fulfilment: Producers must continue meeting annual collection and recycling targets as specified in Schedule II. However, for the current and future compliance cycles, the carry-forward entitlement is now calculated as 60% of the remaining (unmet) battery quantity from the applicable cycle — not as a percentage of the annual average placed in the market. Companies must recalculate their carry-forward positions using the new methodology.
  • Ongoing — Environmental Compensation Readiness: Await CPCB's new guidelines on Environmental Compensation imposition and collection, which the Board is now mandated to prepare in consultation with the Rule 15 Implementation Committee and submit to MoEF&CC. Once approved, these guidelines will govern penalties for non-compliance by producers, refurbishers, and recyclers.
  • Portal Compliance: Ensure all EPR certificate transactions are executed exclusively through registered entities on the CPCB portal at prices within the regulated band. Review existing contractual arrangements with recyclers and aggregators to ensure price terms remain compliant.
  • Registration Verification: Confirm that all entities in your battery waste value chain — recyclers, collection agents, refurbishers — are registered on the CPCB portal, as the price band provisions apply specifically to portal-registered entity transactions.
  • Documentation and Audit Trail: Update internal EPR compliance records to reflect the new carry-forward calculation methodology. Ensure your compliance management system is recalibrated before the next annual EPR reporting cycle.

Climate Transition Angle

Batteries are the material backbone of India's energy transition. The country's stated target of 500 GW of renewable energy by 2030, its rapidly scaling EV ecosystem, and the electrification of industrial and telecom infrastructure all depend on a massive and growing battery supply chain. The waste that this transition generates — lithium, cobalt, nickel, lead, and other critical minerals — represents both an environmental liability and a significant circular economy opportunity. The Battery Waste Management Rules are India's primary regulatory instrument for ensuring that the climate solution does not become a waste crisis. The amendments signal regulatory maturation. By pricing EPR certificates within a defined band, the government is building a functional secondary market for battery recycling compliance — analogous to Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) in the power sector. A credible price floor (30% of the penalty equivalent) ensures recyclers and collectors receive economically viable compensation; a ceiling (100% of penalty) prevents producers from simply buying their way out of physical collection obligations at any cost. From a global standards perspective, this framework is increasingly relevant to companies reporting under the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) IFRS S2 framework or preparing for alignment with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). Both frameworks require disclosure of material resource and waste risks. For companies with significant battery supply chains or battery-intensive products, the cost of EPR compliance, exposure to Environmental Compensation, and the quality of take-back infrastructure are all financially material disclosures. Additionally, the EU Battery Regulation (2023), which mandates recycled content thresholds and carbon footprint declarations for batteries sold in Europe, means that Indian battery manufacturers and EV exporters face convergent regulatory pressure from both domestic and international directions. A robust domestic EPR compliance posture directly supports the due diligence documentation these exporters will need. On physical climate risk: battery recycling facilities and collection infrastructure in India face growing exposure to extreme heat, flooding, and water stress — all of which can disrupt waste battery logistics and processing. Companies building EPR compliance programmes should embed climate resilience into their collection network design, particularly in climate-vulnerable geographies.

What To Do Now

  • Recalculate Your Carry-Forward Position Today: If you are in an active compliance cycle with unmet EPR targets, urgently recalculate your permissible carry-forward under the new 'remaining quantity' methodology (up to 60% of unmet quantity, not 60% of annual average). This will change your liability exposure and your EPR certificate procurement strategy for the remainder of the cycle. Engage your EPR compliance consultant or legal team to model both old and new calculations.
  • Audit All EPR Certificate Contracts and Portal Transactions: Review all existing and pipeline agreements for purchase or sale of EPR certificates. Any price terms that fall below 30% or above 100% of the applicable Environmental Compensation value are now non-compliant. Renegotiate contracts as necessary and brief your procurement and finance teams on the new price band before the next transaction.
  • Engage Your Recycler and Refurbisher Network: The new Rule 13(3) will bring recyclers and refurbishers under the Environmental Compensation regime, not just producers. Conduct a compliance health check with your downstream partners — are they portal-registered? Are they meeting processing standards? Their non-compliance creates reputational and supply chain risk for your EPR programme.
  • Monitor CPCB for Environmental Compensation Guidelines: The CPCB is now mandated to draft and recommend EC guidelines to MoEF&CC. Track CPCB notifications closely, as these guidelines will define the financial penalty structure for non-compliance. Once released, model the financial exposure under various EPR shortfall scenarios and factor this into your sustainability budget and risk register.
  • Integrate EPR Compliance into ESG Reporting and Transition Planning: If your company reports under BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting), GRI, or is preparing for ISSB/CSRD alignment, formally incorporate battery EPR compliance status, EC risk exposure, and recycled material recovery rates into your material topics assessment. This positions your organisation ahead of mandatory ESG disclosure requirements and supports investor-grade climate transition disclosures.

Our Take

The Battery Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2024 represent a quiet but consequential tightening of India's circular economy architecture for batteries. The introduction of a price band for EPR certificates is particularly significant — it transforms what was a compliance mechanism into an embryonic environmental commodity market with real price discovery, which is exactly what India needs to fund a scalable battery recycling industry. The uniform carry-forward cap of 60% on residual quantities is a pragmatic concession to industry volatility, but make no mistake: the direction of travel is toward stricter targets and higher accountability as India's EV and renewable energy markets scale exponentially. Companies that treat EPR as a box-ticking exercise — buying the cheapest certificates available and minimising engagement with actual waste streams — will find themselves exposed as the CPCB's Environmental Compensation guidelines sharpen and as international customers and investors demand verifiable circular economy credentials. The smart move is to use this regulatory moment to build genuine take-back infrastructure, lock in credible recycling partnerships, and embed battery lifecycle management into your broader climate transition strategy. Those who do will find compliance costs manageable and competitive advantage real; those who do not will face escalating penalties and reputational risk in an increasingly scrutinised sector.
