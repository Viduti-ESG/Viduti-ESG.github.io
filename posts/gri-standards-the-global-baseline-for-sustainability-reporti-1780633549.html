Executive Summary

The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) Standards remain the world's most widely adopted framework for sustainability disclosure, providing a universal baseline against which stakeholders — investors, regulators, and supply chain partners — assess corporate ESG performance. For Indian companies, voluntary adoption of GRI is rapidly becoming a de facto requirement as SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework converges with international norms and global customers demand transparent, comparable sustainability data. Understanding and aligning to the GRI Standards today is no longer optional — it is a strategic imperative for market access, investor confidence, and climate credibility.

What Changed

The GRI Standards are a modular, interoperable set of disclosure requirements developed by the Global Reporting Initiative, an independent international standards body. The Standards are structured in three tiers: Universal Standards (GRI 1, 2, and 3), which apply to all reporting organizations regardless of size or sector; Sector Standards, which provide tailored guidance for high-impact industries such as oil and gas, mining, agriculture, and financial services; and Topic Standards, which cover specific economic, environmental, and social issues such as climate change (GRI 305 — Emissions), water (GRI 303), labor practices, and human rights. In 2021, GRI completed a comprehensive overhaul of its Universal Standards, replacing the previous GRI 101, 102, and 103 with the current GRI 1 (Foundation), GRI 2 (General Disclosures), and GRI 3 (Material Topics) — effective for reports published on or after January 1, 2023. This update strengthened the concept of 'double materiality,' requiring companies to assess both how sustainability issues affect the business (financial materiality) and how the business impacts people and the planet (impact materiality). GRI also introduced a formal alignment with the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, raising the bar on human rights due diligence disclosures. Critically, GRI Standards are now explicitly recognized by SEBI as a compatible framework for BRSR Core disclosures, making familiarity with GRI essential for any listed Indian company navigating the evolving mandatory reporting landscape.

Who Is Affected

  • Listed Indian companies (especially NSE/BSE-listed entities in the top 1,000 by market capitalization) subject to SEBI's mandatory BRSR requirements, where GRI alignment supports Core disclosures
  • Export-oriented manufacturers and suppliers — particularly in textiles, chemicals, auto components, pharmaceuticals, and IT services — whose European and North American customers require GRI-aligned ESG reports
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational corporations that must align with parent-company GRI reporting cycles and CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive) obligations in the EU
  • Financial institutions, banks, and asset managers responding to RBI and SEBI sustainability guidelines and investor ESG due diligence requirements
  • Infrastructure, energy, and mining companies covered by GRI Sector Standards, where sector-specific disclosures are increasingly scrutinized by lenders and multilateral agencies
  • SMEs in global supply chains where Tier-1 suppliers are being required to cascade GRI-aligned data requests to their own vendors

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Adopt GRI Universal Standards (GRI 1, 2, and 3) as the foundation of any sustainability report — mandatory for 'in accordance' reporting claims as of January 1, 2023
  • Conduct a formal double materiality assessment (GRI 3) that identifies and prioritizes material topics based on both impact on stakeholders and financial significance to the business — this must be documented and disclosed
  • Disclose against GRI 2: General Disclosures covering organizational profile, governance, strategy, stakeholder engagement, and reporting practices — these are non-negotiable for any GRI-aligned report
  • Identify applicable GRI Sector Standards (e.g., GRI 11 for Oil and Gas, GRI 12 for Coal, GRI 13 for Agriculture, Aquaculture and Fishing) and incorporate sector-specific disclosures — sector standards progressively becoming mandatory for 'in accordance' claims
  • Report Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions under GRI 305 (Emissions) using a recognized methodology such as the GHG Protocol — increasingly required by lenders, investors, and supply chain partners
  • Ensure human rights due diligence disclosures align with GRI 2-23 through 2-26 and relevant Topic Standards (GRI 406–413), reflecting the UN Guiding Principles alignment built into the 2021 update
  • For BRSR Core compliance (mandatory for top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24, expanding to top 1,000 by FY2026-27): cross-reference GRI disclosures to satisfy SEBI's ESG data requirements and minimize duplicative reporting burden
  • Obtain third-party assurance on GRI disclosures — a growing expectation from institutional investors and required for BRSR Core from FY2023-24 onwards for the top 150 listed entities

Climate Transition Angle

GRI Standards sit at the center of the global convergence of sustainability reporting frameworks, making alignment a critical bridge between India's domestic regulatory requirements and international climate expectations. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) — whose IFRS S1 and S2 standards are now being considered for adoption in India — has explicitly built upon GRI's concept of impact materiality and cross-references GRI Topic Standards for non-financial disclosures. Companies that have a strong GRI reporting foundation will find the transition to ISSB-aligned reporting significantly smoother. From a physical climate risk perspective, GRI 201-2 (Financial Implications of Climate Change) and GRI 305 (Emissions) directly support the kind of climate risk disclosure now expected under TCFD-aligned frameworks that underpin both ISSB S2 and SEBI's BRSR. Indian companies in water-stressed sectors — agriculture, textiles, beverages — will find GRI 303 (Water and Effluents) increasingly scrutinized as physical water risk becomes a material financial issue. Similarly, supply chain disruptions linked to extreme weather events demand disclosure under GRI 204 (Procurement Practices) and GRI 308 (Supplier Environmental Assessment). For companies with European customers or investors, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) are partially interoperable with GRI — meaning GRI disclosures can satisfy a significant portion of ESRS requirements, reducing the compliance burden for Indian exporters navigating the EU's value chain due diligence expectations.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a GRI Gap Analysis immediately: Map your current BRSR or sustainability disclosures against GRI Universal Standards (GRI 1, 2, and 3) to identify gaps in materiality assessment methodology, governance disclosures, and stakeholder engagement documentation
  • Formalize your Double Materiality Assessment: Convene a cross-functional team including Finance, Legal, Operations, and Sustainability to conduct and document a GRI 3-aligned materiality assessment — this is the backbone of a credible GRI report and increasingly a prerequisite for investor engagement
  • Build your GHG emissions inventory: If you have not already, initiate Scope 1 and Scope 2 measurement using the GHG Protocol, and begin mapping Scope 3 categories relevant to your sector — GRI 305 compliance and BRSR Core requirements both depend on this data
  • Identify your applicable GRI Sector Standard: Review GRI's published Sector Standards and determine which apply to your primary business activity; begin incorporating sector-specific disclosures into your next reporting cycle to stay ahead of the 'in accordance' requirements
  • Engage an assurance provider early: Third-party limited or reasonable assurance over GRI-aligned disclosures is becoming the market standard and is required for BRSR Core — identify a qualified assurance provider and integrate them into your reporting process from data collection, not just at the review stage

Our Take

GRI Standards are no longer simply a voluntary best-practice framework for progressive companies — they are rapidly becoming the de facto infrastructure of mandatory sustainability disclosure in India and globally. SEBI's BRSR framework has effectively borrowed GRI's architecture, ISSB has built upon its foundation, and the EU's CSRD has formally recognized GRI as a compatible framework. For Indian companies, this convergence is both an opportunity and a warning signal: those who invest now in building robust GRI-aligned reporting systems will gain a significant competitive advantage in accessing global capital, winning export contracts, and demonstrating climate credibility. Those who delay risk being caught in a reactive compliance sprint — expensive, disruptive, and credibility-damaging. The direction of travel is unambiguous: comprehensive, assured, and comparable sustainability disclosure is the new cost of doing business in global markets, and GRI is the common language in which that disclosure must be spoken.
