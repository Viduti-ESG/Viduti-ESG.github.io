Executive Summary

Carbon Tracker has published its updated analytical methodologies underpinning the Climate Action 100+ (CA100+) Net Zero Company Benchmark — the most consequential investor-led climate engagement framework globally, covering 170+ systemically significant emitters and over $65 trillion in assets under management. The methodologies, published on 4 June 2025 and accessible at https://carbontracker.org/reports/carbon-tracker-methodologies/, assess companies across two dimensions: adequacy of climate disclosure and Paris alignment of actual corporate actions. For Indian boards, this is not an abstract global development — CA100+ signatories include LIC, SBI Mutual Fund's global counterparts, and every major foreign institutional investor (FII) holding positions in ONGC, NTPC, Coal India, Tata Power, Reliance Industries, and Adani Enterprises. The methodologies now in effect define the scoring template these investors will use to vote, engage, and ultimately reallocate capital. The update lands at a critical juncture for India: SEBI's BRSR Core framework is now mandatory for the top 150 listed entities, ONGC and NTPC are enrolled in CA100+ engagement, and India's carbon credit trading scheme under the Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022 is moving toward operationalisation. The Carbon Tracker framework effectively sets a higher bar than BRSR Core on alignment indicators — creating a dual compliance burden and a valuation risk for any Indian large-cap that scores poorly on Paris alignment metrics.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker Initiative (carbontracker.org) confirms that the updated CA100+ methodologies now operationalise two distinct but interlocking assessment lenses. First, Disclosure Framework Indicators evaluate whether companies are disclosing against the vocabulary of ISSB IFRS S2, TCFD, and GHG Protocol — covering Scope 1, 2, and material Scope 3 emissions, climate scenario analysis, and board-level climate governance. Second, and more commercially significant, Alignment Assessments evaluate whether company capex plans, production trajectories, and lobbying positions are actually consistent with a 1.5°C pathway as defined by IEA's Net Zero Emissions by 2050 scenario. The sector-specific methodologies for Oil & Gas and Electric Utilities are the most granular — they score companies on whether upstream capex approvals are consistent with no new fossil fuel development post-2021 (IEA NZE), whether power generation portfolios are decarbonising at IEA-compatible rates, and whether climate accounting in financial statements reflects stranded asset risk. The related finding published by Carbon Tracker in the same reporting cycle — that oil companies systematically under-report carbon intensity through automotive investment structures — expands the methodology's reach into the auto and industrial sectors. This directly implicates Indian companies: Tata Motors, Mahindra & Mahindra, and Maruti Suzuki parent Suzuki Motor Corp all carry legacy ICE investment profiles that Carbon Tracker's new 'Oil Companies in Disguise' lens would flag for under-reporting. The Climate Accounting and Audit methodology pillar is particularly significant for Indian audit committees: it requires that impairment tests, asset useful life assumptions, and capital allocation disclosures in audited financial statements be stress-tested against 1.5°C scenarios — a standard that India's current audit practice under SA 570 (Going Concern) and Ind AS 36 (Impairment) does not yet explicitly require, but which institutional investors are beginning to demand via AGM resolutions.

  • Auto OEMs and component makers: Tata Motors, Mahindra & Mahindra, Bharat Forge — exposed to Carbon Tracker's new under-reporting methodology on ICE investment carbon intensity
  • Top 150 BRSR Core companies: all entities subject to SEBI's mandatory BRSR Core assurance obligations face a methodology gap between BRSR Core's disclosure requirements and CA100+'s Paris alignment assessments
  • CFOs and Audit Committees at BSE 500 companies: climate accounting and audit methodology creates direct exposure to investor challenge on impairment assumptions, asset life, and stranded asset provisions in Ind AS financials
  • Chief Sustainability Officers at CA100+ target companies: responsible for translating CA100+ benchmark scores into board-level engagement responses and IR disclosures
  • Foreign Institutional Investors and ESG-screened funds holding Indian large-caps: CA100+ scores directly influence voting decisions, engagement escalation, and portfolio exclusion triggers
  • Indian domestic institutional investors — LIC, SBI MF, HDFC AMC — increasingly co-filing climate resolutions at AGMs; CA100+ methodology provides the scoring basis for such resolutions

  • FY2025-26 Annual Reports: BRSR Core mandatory assurance (top 150 listed entities per SEBI circular SEBI/HO/CFD/CFD-SEC-2/P/CIR/2023/122) must now be read alongside CA100+ disclosure indicators — any material gap between BRSR Core disclosures and CA100+ disclosure framework indicators is an investor relations exposure
  • Audit Committees — FY2026 audit planning: incorporate Carbon Tracker's Climate Accounting and Audit methodology questions into Ind AS 36 impairment review briefings; auditors should be asked whether oil, gas, and coal asset carrying values have been stress-tested against IEA NZE scenario

  • Ongoing stewardship obligation: CA100+ signatory investors are required to escalate engagement to voting action if companies do not demonstrate credible alignment progress — Indian companies must treat each AGM cycle as a live scoring event against Carbon Tracker's published methodology
  • SBTi target-setting: CA100+ alignment assessments cross-reference SBTi approval status; Indian companies without approved SBTi targets in Oil & Gas and Utilities sectors will score adversely on Paris alignment — SBTi submission window is open but Oil & Gas FLAG sector guidance applies

Climate Transition Angle

The Carbon Tracker CA100+ methodology update crystallises a structural tension at the heart of India's energy transition: the country's largest listed emitters — ONGC, Coal India, NTPC — are simultaneously instruments of energy security policy and targets of global investor decarbonisation pressure. The methodology's Paris alignment indicators are calibrated to the IEA NZE 2050 scenario, which calls for no new fossil fuel development approvals from 2021. India's National Energy Policy, however, explicitly supports continued upstream oil and gas development and coal expansion through the 2030s to meet baseload and energy access imperatives. This creates a structural mis-score for Indian SOEs under CA100+ metrics that is not resolvable through disclosure improvement alone — it requires India to develop a credible Just Transition narrative that explains divergence from IEA NZE in terms of developmental equity, which BRSR's BRSR Core Principle 6 (responsible business on environment) begins to address but does not fully resolve. For private sector Indian companies, the transition angle is more actionable. Tata Power's renewable build-out, Adani Green's solar pipeline, and Reliance's green hydrogen ambitions are exactly the type of alignment evidence that CA100+ Alignment Assessments reward — but only if accompanied by credible coal and fossil fuel exit timelines and verified Scope 3 accounting. The ISSB IFRS S2 standard, which SEBI has signalled it will incorporate into future BRSR evolution, uses the same conceptual architecture as CA100+'s disclosure framework indicators. Indian companies investing now in ISSB S2-quality disclosure infrastructure are simultaneously building CA100+ compliance readiness and future-proofing against the next BRSR Core revision expected before FY2027-28.

  • Legal and Compliance (60-day horizon): assess lobbying and trade association positions against CA100+'s policy alignment indicators — Carbon Tracker explicitly scores whether company lobbying is consistent or inconsistent with Paris goals; membership of coal or oil industry associations that lobby against climate policy creates an adverse alignment score that must be disclosed or mitigated
  • SBTi submission prioritisation (for Oil & Gas and Utilities): companies without approved SBTi near-term and long-term targets should initiate SBTi commitment letters now; CA100+ 2025-cycle scoring will penalise absence of SBTi approval as an alignment gap, directly affecting FII voting decisions at FY26 AGMs

Our Take

The Carbon Tracker CA100+ methodology publication is a quiet but structurally significant event for Indian capital markets. Here is what boards and investors should expect over the next 12 to 36 months. First, the methodology's Climate Accounting and Audit pillar will migrate from investor expectation to audit standard — within 24 months, we expect ICAI's Auditing and Assurance Standards Board to face pressure from SEBI and MCA to incorporate climate scenario testing into Ind AS 36 guidance notes, driven by the same institutional investor lobby that uses CA100+ scoring. Companies that wait for regulatory mandate will face a compressed audit remediation window. Second, the under-reporting methodology Carbon Tracker has applied to automakers — finding that ICE investment carbon intensity is being systematically obscured through holding structures — is a direct warning to Indian conglomerates with diversified fossil and clean energy portfolios: Adani Group, Reliance, and Tata Group all carry structural complexity that could generate adverse CA100+-style scores if Scope 3 boundary-setting and intra-group capital flow disclosures are not tightened. Third, and most consequentially for Indian sovereign and quasi-sovereign borrowers, the CA100+ benchmark is increasingly referenced in green bond and sustainability-linked bond (SLB) covenant frameworks used by offshore investors. ONGC and NTPC are active in international debt markets — a deteriorating CA100+ score directly affects the coupon step-up risk on SLBs and the eligibility of future green bond issuances under ICMA Green Bond Principles. The Indian government's own Sovereign Green Bond programme, now in its third issuance cycle, will face credibility questions if CA100+ anchor companies in the public sector score poorly on Paris alignment. The regulatory and commercial logic is converging: Indian companies that treat CA100+ as a distant global investor concern rather than a proximate capital cost risk are making a material misjudgement.
