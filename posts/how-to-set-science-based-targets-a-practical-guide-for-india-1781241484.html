Executive Summary

Science-based targets (SBTs) are rapidly becoming the global benchmark for credible corporate climate commitments, with the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) providing the most widely recognised framework for setting emissions reduction goals aligned with limiting global warming to 1.5°C. For Indian companies — whether responding to investor pressure, supply chain requirements from multinational customers, or upcoming domestic disclosure mandates — understanding how to set and validate science-based targets is no longer optional. This post walks sustainability and compliance teams through the SBTi process, its obligations, and why acting now positions your organisation ahead of the regulatory and market curve.

What Changed

The Science Based Targets initiative is not a government regulation — it is a global standard-setting body backed by CDP, the United Nations Global Compact, World Resources Institute (WRI), and WWF. However, its Corporate Standard has effectively become the de facto international framework for corporate emissions target-setting, and its requirements are increasingly being referenced in mandatory regulations worldwide, including the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standard. The SBTi requires companies to set greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction targets across Scope 1 (direct emissions), Scope 2 (purchased energy), and, critically, Scope 3 (value chain emissions) that are consistent with the level of decarbonisation required to keep global temperature rise to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. Targets are independently validated by SBTi's technical team and, once approved, must be publicly disclosed and progress reported annually. As of 2024, over 7,000 companies globally have committed to or validated targets through SBTi. In India, the number remains relatively small but is growing rapidly, driven by export-oriented sectors, listed companies with foreign institutional investors, and businesses embedded in the supply chains of global corporations that have made their own SBTi commitments.

Who Is Affected

  • Large Indian listed companies subject to SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) Core disclosure requirements, particularly those in the top 1,000 listed entities
  • Export-oriented manufacturers — especially in textiles, pharmaceuticals, chemicals, auto components, and IT services — whose global customers require supply chain emissions disclosures or SBTi alignment
  • Indian subsidiaries or affiliates of multinational corporations that have made SBTi commitments and must account for Scope 3 supply chain emissions
  • Companies seeking foreign institutional investment or green financing, where SBTi validation is increasingly used as a credibility signal by ESG-focused investors
  • SMEs and mid-cap companies that are tier-1 or tier-2 suppliers to large domestic or global corporations with SBTi commitments
  • Financial institutions and banks that are setting their own financed emissions targets and may require portfolio companies to demonstrate credible climate commitments

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • COMMITMENT LETTER: Submit a commitment letter to SBTi via their online portal, publicly pledging to set science-based targets — companies then have 24 months to develop and submit validated targets
  • GHG INVENTORY (PREREQUISITE): Complete a comprehensive GHG inventory covering Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 emissions using the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard — this is required before targets can be set and must use a base year no earlier than 2015
  • SCOPE 3 MATERIALITY SCREENING: Identify which of the 15 Scope 3 categories are material (typically those representing more than 40% of total emissions) — SBTi requires Scope 3 targets if Scope 3 emissions exceed 40% of total footprint
  • TARGET SETTING: Use SBTi-approved methods — the Absolute Contraction Approach (ACA) requiring at minimum 4.2% absolute reduction per year for Scope 1 and 2, or sector-specific pathways where available (e.g., Steel, Cement, ICT, Financial Institutions)
  • SUBMISSION & VALIDATION: Submit targets to SBTi for independent validation — as of 2024, SBTi charges a validation fee (USD 4,950 for companies with revenue above USD 250 million; lower tiers for SMEs) and the review process typically takes 10–14 weeks
  • ANNUAL PROGRESS DISCLOSURE: Once validated, companies must report progress against targets annually through public channels (e.g., CDP disclosure, annual reports, sustainability reports) — failure to report can result in targets being delisted
  • NET-ZERO STANDARD (OPTIONAL BUT RECOMMENDED): Companies may also set long-term net-zero targets under the SBTi Net-Zero Standard, requiring 90–95% absolute emissions reduction by 2050 across all scopes, with residual emissions addressed through carbon removal (not offsets)

Climate Transition Angle

Science-based targets sit at the intersection of physical climate risk and transition risk management. On the transition side, as carbon pricing mechanisms, border carbon adjustments (such as the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, or CBAM, which came into force in 2023), and mandatory climate disclosure rules proliferate globally, companies without credible decarbonisation roadmaps will face higher cost of capital, loss of market access, and reputational damage. SBTi validation provides a third-party verified signal that a company's climate strategy is grounded in science, not marketing. For Indian companies specifically, the connection to global standards is direct and urgent. The ISSB's IFRS S2 standard — which India's SEBI has signalled it will align with in future iterations of BRSR — requires companies to disclose their transition plans and emissions reduction targets. SBTi provides a ready-made, globally recognised methodology that satisfies the 'target-setting' component of these disclosure frameworks. Similarly, the EU's CSRD requires European companies to assess and disclose the climate ambition of their value chain partners — meaning Indian exporters will face questionnaires and contractual requirements from European customers that effectively mandate SBTi-aligned thinking. On physical risk, the process of setting science-based targets forces organisations to deeply understand their emissions hotspots — which are often also their exposure points to physical climate risk. A company that discovers 60% of its Scope 3 emissions come from agricultural raw material inputs, for example, is simultaneously identifying a major physical climate risk (crop yield disruption) and a decarbonisation priority. SBTi target-setting, done well, is therefore an integrated risk management exercise, not just a compliance checkbox.

What To Do Now

  • CONDUCT A GHG INVENTORY IMMEDIATELY: If your organisation does not have a verified Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG inventory, commission one now using the GHG Protocol standard — this is the non-negotiable foundation for everything that follows and typically takes 3–6 months for a first-time exercise
  • ENGAGE YOUR BOARD AND CFO: Science-based targets require capital allocation commitments — renewable energy procurement, supply chain transformation, process efficiency investments. Secure board-level buy-in early and integrate target-setting into your capital expenditure planning cycle
  • IDENTIFY YOUR SECTOR PATHWAY: Check whether SBTi has a sector-specific standard for your industry (available for Steel, Cement, Apparel, ICT, Financial Institutions, and others) — sector pathways provide more tailored and often more achievable glide paths than generic absolute contraction methods
  • SUBMIT YOUR COMMITMENT LETTER WITHIN THE NEXT QUARTER: Submitting the commitment letter is free, signals climate ambition to investors and customers immediately, and starts the 24-month clock — giving your team structured time to develop robust targets without being rushed
  • MAP YOUR SCOPE 3 VALUE CHAIN NOW: Begin engaging key suppliers, logistics partners, and raw material providers for emissions data — Scope 3 data collection is the most time-consuming part of the process, and early supplier engagement builds the relationships needed for credible supply chain decarbonisation

Our Take

Setting science-based targets is quickly transitioning from a voluntary best practice to a de facto market requirement for any Indian company operating at scale in global markets or seeking international capital. What makes SBTi uniquely powerful — and uniquely demanding — is that it does not allow companies to set whatever target feels comfortable; it anchors ambition to what climate science actually requires. For Indian businesses accustomed to setting internal sustainability KPIs, this can feel like a significant shift in approach, and it is. But that rigour is also its value: an SBTi-validated target carries credibility with investors, regulators, and customers that self-declared targets simply do not. Looking ahead, as SEBI tightens BRSR assurance requirements and India moves toward mandatory climate risk disclosures aligned with ISSB standards, companies that have already embedded SBTi methodology into their strategy will be structurally better positioned — both to comply with less effort and to genuinely lead the transition in their sectors. The window to be an early mover in India is still open, but it is closing faster than most boards appreciate.
