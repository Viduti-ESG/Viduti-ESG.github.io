IMO MEPC83 Carbon Levy Negotiations: What India's Shipping Sector, Exporters, and ESG Investors Must Understand Now

Executive Summary

The IMO's MEPC83 negotiations in London are finalising the architecture of a global carbon levy and fuel standard for international shipping — a regulatory moment with direct and underappreciated consequences for India's export economy, its shipping and port sectors, and the ESG disclosure obligations of listed companies with material ocean freight exposure. Climate Action Tracker rates the IMO's current ambition as 'Highly Insufficient,' consistent with 4°C warming, but even the most ambitious MEPC83 outcome will not align with a 1.5°C pathway — meaning further tightening is structurally inevitable. For Indian CFOs and CSOs, the question is not whether a carbon cost on shipping arrives, but at what price, in what form, and how fast it will cascade into supply chain costs, Scope 3 disclosures, and export competitiveness.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/imo-at-a-crossroads-a-defining-juncture-for-climate-action-and-an-equitable-transition/) details that MEPC83, the 83rd session of IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee, is currently negotiating two competing frameworks: a universal flat-rate carbon levy — championed by Small Island Developing States (SIDS) and Least Developed Countries (LDCs) — and the 'J9' proposal put forward by Singapore, which offers a two-tiered Global Fuel Standard paired with a credit trading mechanism rather than a direct levy. The EU, previously aligned with the SIDS bloc, has reportedly softened its position and is open to compromise, materially weakening the prospects of the more ambitious levy architecture. The significance of this divergence cannot be overstated for Indian business. International shipping emissions — covering approximately 3% of global GHG emissions, comparable to Germany's entire national footprint — are explicitly excluded from countries' Nationally Determined Contributions under the Paris Agreement. India's own NDC, updated in 2022 and targeting a 45% emissions intensity reduction by 2030 against 2005 levels, does not cover these shipping emissions. This means that whatever the IMO decides at MEPC83 represents the only binding multilateral lever on a segment of India's trade-linked emissions that currently sits entirely outside BRSR Scope 3 reporting norms and India's domestic climate architecture. A carbon levy at IMO level would impose direct cost pressure on Indian importers and exporters — sectors ranging from iron ore, steel, and textiles to pharmaceuticals and chemicals — regardless of India's domestic carbon pricing trajectory under the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) notified under the Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2022. The J9 alternative introduces a two-tiered GHG intensity standard that, as CAT notes, could make LNG-fuelled vessels more commercially attractive — a serious lock-in risk given India's significant investments in LNG bunkering infrastructure at Paradip, Ennore, and Kochi. If LNG becomes the dominant transitional fuel under a weak IMO standard, Indian port infrastructure capex decisions made in 2025–2027 risk becoming stranded assets by the time the IMO tightens standards in its post-2030 review cycle.

Who Is Affected

  • Indian exporters with high ocean freight dependency: textiles and apparel (Bangladesh-competitive), pharmaceuticals (US/EU export lanes), steel and iron ore (seaborne bulk), chemicals, and auto components — all face direct cost pass-through risk from a carbon levy on shipping
  • Indian Shipping Corporation (SCI), Shipping Corporation of India Land and Assets Ltd, and private fleet operators: compliance cost exposure, fleet retrofit decisions, and green fuel procurement strategies
  • Major Indian ports — JNPT, Mundra, Paradip, Vizag — with bunkering infrastructure investment pipelines that will be shaped by which fuel standard the IMO adopts
  • BSE 500 listed companies with material international shipping in their Scope 3 Category 4 (upstream transportation) or Category 9 (downstream transportation) under GHG Protocol — increasingly relevant under BRSR Core's value chain disclosure requirements for the top 250 listed entities
  • CFOs and treasury heads at export-oriented manufacturers who currently do not price carbon into freight cost modelling or long-term supply chain contracts
  • CSOs and ESG heads at companies with SBTi-validated or BRSR-disclosed net zero targets who have not yet addressed Scope 3 shipping emissions in their decarbonisation roadmaps
  • Indian institutional investors and ESG analysts covering shipping, logistics, and export-driven industrials within BSE 500 portfolios
  • DFIs and green bond issuers financing port infrastructure or green shipping corridors — including India's proposed Green Hydrogen corridors where shipping is a key offtake pathway

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • MEPC83 negotiations concluding April 2025: outcome will define whether a flat-rate carbon levy or the J9 credit-trading mechanism forms the basis of IMO's mid-term measure — Indian shipping companies and exporters must track the final text immediately
  • IMO 2023 GHG Reduction Strategy: indicative target of 20% emissions reduction (aspirational 30%) by 2030 from 2008 baseline — any measures agreed at MEPC83 are expected to operationalise this; Indian fleet operators must begin fleet efficiency audits against these benchmarks now
  • IMO Net Zero by or around 2050 goal: sets the long-term compliance horizon — Indian shipping companies with vessels currently on order or under long-term charter must stress-test against zero-emission fuel compatibility by 2045 at the latest
  • BRSR Core (applicable to top 1,000 listed entities from FY2024-25): while Scope 3 is not yet mandatorily assured under BRSR Core, SEBI's phased roadmap signals value chain emissions will be in scope — companies with material shipping Scope 3 should begin baselining now to avoid disclosure gaps when SEBI mandates it
  • EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) — steel, aluminium, fertilisers, cement, hydrogen: live from October 2023 for reporting, levy from 2026 — embedded carbon in sea freight is not yet in CBAM scope but EU's shipping ETS (effective January 2024) already adds carbon cost to vessels calling EU ports, directly impacting Indian exporters to Europe
  • EU Shipping ETS (EU ETS Phase 4, effective 1 January 2024): 100% of emissions from intra-EU voyages and 50% of emissions from voyages to/from non-EU ports now require ETS allowances — Indian exporters to Europe face freight rate increases of €50–€100/tonne CO2 equivalent depending on vessel type; this is operational today, not theoretical
  • India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) under Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022: currently covers energy-intensive domestic industries — international shipping is outside scope, but the Ministry of Power's roadmap for CCTS expansion could eventually intersect with IMO compliance mechanisms for Indian-flagged vessels

Climate Transition Angle

India sits in a structurally complex position in IMO negotiations. As both a major maritime nation (ranking among the top 15 in flag state tonnage and a significant ship-owning nation through SCI and private operators) and a large developing economy with freight-intensive export growth aspirations, India's negotiating posture has historically leaned toward the J9-style flexibility architecture rather than the flat levy. This is commercially understandable but strategically shortsighted from a transition finance perspective. The CAT analysis makes clear that the flat levy — with revenues directed to climate-vulnerable nations — is both the most cost-effective long-term solution (per UNCTAD's own Comprehensive Impact Assessment) and the most equitable. India, as a country that repeatedly and rightly calls for climate finance flows from developed to developing nations, should recognise that a robust carbon levy on shipping is one of the only multilateral mechanisms currently on the table capable of generating dedicated, predictable climate finance outside the UNFCCC's perennially underfunded channels. From a transition pathway perspective, the J9's LNG-tolerance creates a direct contradiction with India's own Green Hydrogen Mission and its ambition to build green ammonia and green hydrogen export corridors — both of which require zero-emission shipping as the offtake pathway to be commercially viable by 2030. If IMO locks in an LNG-compatible standard at MEPC83, the commercial case for Indian green hydrogen exporters weakens materially, as their European and Japanese customers will face lower carbon cost penalties for using LNG carriers rather than switching to green hydrogen or ammonia-fuelled vessels. This is a $6–8 billion investment decision sitting inside India's National Green Hydrogen Mission that will be shaped, in part, by what happens in London this week. Indian companies with BRSR-disclosed net zero targets that include Scope 3 shipping emissions — or those pursuing SBTi FLAG or Corporate Net Zero Standard validation — will find their 2030 and 2035 Scope 3 trajectories increasingly difficult to defend to institutional investors if IMO's ambition remains at the 'Highly Insufficient' level that CAT currently rates it.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Treasury (immediate — within 30 days): commission a freight carbon cost scenario analysis modelling three IMO outcomes — no levy, J9 credit mechanism, flat carbon levy at $18–$150/tonne CO2 range — and stress-test against your top 10 export trade lanes; build this into FY2026 cost forecasting and supplier contract renegotiation calendars
  • CSO / ESG team (within 60 days): audit your BRSR Core value chain disclosures and SBTi target boundary to determine whether Scope 3 Category 4 and Category 9 shipping emissions are baselined; if not, initiate data collection through your freight forwarders and 3PL partners using GLEC Framework-aligned methodology — this is the methodology most likely to align with future BRSR mandatory Scope 3 reporting
  • Legal / Compliance (within 60 days): review exposure to EU Shipping ETS pass-through in existing freight contracts for Europe-bound shipments; confirm whether your logistics providers are absorbing or passing through ETS allowance costs, and ensure this is captured in your BRSR Core 'Scope 3 significant emission sources' disclosure
  • Shipping and Port teams / Infrastructure investors (within 90 days): pause or condition any LNG bunkering infrastructure commitments pending MEPC83 outcome; if the J9 is adopted, reassess LNG lock-in risk against IMO's post-2030 tightening cycle — build a 2035 regulatory scenario into your infrastructure IRR models
  • Board Risk Committee (next board meeting): table IMO MEPC83 outcome as a named climate transition risk under BRSR Principle 6 and ISSB IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) — both require disclosure of policy and legal transition risks; the IMO carbon levy is now a board-level material risk for any listed company with >5% of COGS linked to international freight
  • Investor Relations / ESG communications: proactively brief institutional investors and ESG analysts on your shipping emissions exposure and transition strategy — foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) subject to EU SFDR or TCFD-aligned mandates will increasingly ask about Scope 3 shipping risks in engagement calls through 2025–2026

Our Take

MEPC83 is the most consequential IMO session since the 2018 Initial GHG Strategy, and the outcome will define the commercial and regulatory landscape for international shipping through 2035. The EU's reported backsliding from the SIDS-led levy position is a significant warning signal: it mirrors the pattern seen in early CBAM negotiations, where political compromises produced a weaker initial instrument that was subsequently tightened faster than industry expected. Indian boards and CFOs who dismiss the IMO carbon levy as a distant diplomatic problem are repeating the same miscalculation that left Indian steel and cement exporters underprepared for CBAM's October 2023 reporting phase. The structural direction is unambiguous — international shipping will be carbon-priced, the only variable is the mechanism, price point, and timeline. What concerns us most about the J9 outcome — which now looks more likely given EU softening — is not the immediate cost exposure but the signal it sends on transition finance. A credit-trading mechanism without predictable revenue generation will not fund the green corridor infrastructure, the methanol and ammonia bunkering networks, or the vessel retrofit financing that Indian shipping and port sectors need to remain competitive in a decarbonising global trade system. India should be using its G20 legacy and its voice at IMO not just to protect short-term freight competitiveness, but to secure a seat in the revenue redistribution architecture of a flat levy — ensuring that Indian climate-vulnerable coastal communities and Indian green shipping infrastructure both benefit from the transition finance flows a robust levy would generate. In the next 12–24 months, watch for: SEBI expanding BRSR Core Scope 3 assurance requirements to include transportation categories; RBI's climate risk disclosure framework (expected 2025–2026) beginning to ask banks about shipping-linked transition risk in export credit portfolios; and EU CSRD double materiality assessments by European customers of Indian exporters flagging upstream Scope 3 shipping emissions as financially material — triggering supplier-level data requests that Indian companies are currently not equipped to answer.
