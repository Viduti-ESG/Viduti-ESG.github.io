Fossil Fuel Export Emissions: The Accountability Gap That Threatens India's Climate Commitments and Supply Chain Credibility

Executive Summary

Climate Action Tracker's latest analysis exposes a structural accountability gap in global climate governance: the top fossil fuel exporting nations — Russia, Australia, the US, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and Canada — account for 50% of total exported fossil fuel emissions, yet these emissions appear in no national inventory and no corporate Scope 3 accounting obligation. For India, this is not an abstract geopolitical debate. India is one of the world's largest importers of coal (from Indonesia and Australia) and oil (from Russia and Saudi Arabia), meaning the 'exported emissions' of these nations are India's Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions in waiting — embedded in the energy and industrial base of every BSE 500 company. The immediate consequence for Indian CFOs and CSOs is a compounding disclosure risk: as ISSB IFRS S2 adoption accelerates globally and SEBI's BRSR Core Scope 3 disclosures tighten, the combustion-side emissions of imported fossil fuels will become a mandatory reporting obligation — exposing Indian corporates to stranded asset risk, SBTi target misalignment, and investor scrutiny that their export-nation counterparts are currently escaping.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/highlighting-the-hypocrisy-fossil-fuel-export-emissions/), authored by Sofia Gonzales-Zuniga, Danial Riaz, and Mia Moisio, quantifies for the first time the scale of what CAT terms the 'fossil fuel exports iceberg effect': countries like Norway and Australia have exported emissions that exceed their entire domestic greenhouse gas inventory. Australia's exported emissions are more than double its domestic footprint. Canada has had exported emissions exceeding domestic emissions since 2015. The US, meanwhile, has significantly grown its exported emissions over the past decade and — per US EIA 2023 projections — plans to increase oil and gas exports to 2050. What this analysis overturns is the comfortable diplomatic fiction that COP28's 'transition away from fossil fuels' language represents a coherent global decarbonisation pathway. It does not. The GHG Protocol's territorial accounting boundary means that Scope 1 emissions from Australian coal combustion in an Indian steel plant appear in India's national inventory and in Indian steelmakers' Scope 1 disclosures — not in Australia's. This is not merely a moral asymmetry; it is a live regulatory and capital market risk for Indian industry. Under SEBI's BRSR Core framework, top 150 listed entities (by market capitalisation) are already required to disclose Scope 3 emissions on a reasonable assurance basis for FY2024-25 onward. For energy-intensive sectors — steel, cement, power, chemicals — Scope 3 Category 1 (purchased goods and services) and Category 3 (fuel and energy-related activities) will directly capture the embedded emissions of imported coal, LNG, and crude oil. The CAT analysis makes clear that the upstream supply of these fuels is not being decarbonised: Indian companies cannot rely on supplier-side action to reduce their Scope 3 numbers. The India-specific regulatory hook deepens further: India's Perform Achieve and Trade (PAT) scheme under the Bureau of Energy Efficiency (BEE) sets specific energy consumption reduction targets for designated consumers, many of whom are large importers of thermal coal and fuel oil. If the PAT scheme evolves — as expected under India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) notified in June 2023 — into a carbon price-linked offset mechanism, the emissions intensity of imported fuels will directly affect the cost of compliance credits. Companies importing high-emission-intensity coal from Australia or Indonesia will face structurally higher compliance costs compared to peers transitioning to domestic renewable energy or cleaner fuel mixes.

Who Is Affected

  • Indian steel manufacturers (primary steel via blast furnace route) importing coking and thermal coal from Australia and Indonesia — direct Scope 1 and Scope 3 Category 1 exposure
  • Indian power generation companies (thermal IPPs and state DISCOMs) relying on imported coal from Indonesia, South Africa, and Australia for capacity balancing — exposed to fuel cost volatility and future carbon border adjustments
  • Indian oil refiners and petrochemical companies importing Russian crude under current pricing arrangements — Scope 3 Category 3 exposure and potential EU CBAM indirect risk if refined products are exported to European markets
  • Top 150 SEBI-listed companies by market cap required to disclose Scope 3 under BRSR Core from FY2024-25 — audit committees face assurance risk if upstream emission factors for imported fossil fuels are not accurately sourced
  • CFOs and treasury heads at energy-intensive BSE 500 companies negotiating long-term fuel supply contracts — need to price in forward carbon cost of imported fossil fuel emissions
  • CSOs and sustainability heads at Indian conglomerates with SBTi near-term or net-zero commitments — Scope 3 Category 1 and 3 targets will be unachievable without a credible imported fuel decarbonisation pathway
  • ESG analysts and institutional investors at domestic mutual funds, foreign portfolio investors (FPIs), and development finance institutions (DFIs) with exposure to Indian energy and industrials — need to reassess Scope 3 transition risk embedded in portfolio companies
  • Export-oriented Indian manufacturers (textiles, auto components, chemicals) supplying EU buyers subject to CSRD — upstream Scope 3 disclosure requirements from EU customers will cascade to Indian suppliers' own fuel and energy emission disclosures

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • SEBI BRSR Core — Scope 3 disclosure (all 15 GHG Protocol categories) required with reasonable assurance for top 150 listed companies from FY2024-25 reporting (annual reports due by September 2025)
  • SEBI BRSR Core — Scope 3 disclosure extended to top 1,000 listed companies from FY2026-27; companies must begin baseline data collection and supply chain emission factor mapping now
  • India Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS), notified June 2023 under Energy Conservation Act 2001 — BEE to operationalise compliance market by FY2025-26; emission intensity of imported fossil fuels will affect PAT cycle 7 and CCTS compliance obligations
  • ISSB IFRS S2 — material Scope 3 emissions including Category 1 (purchased goods) and Category 3 (fuel and energy) required in climate disclosures; Indian subsidiaries of multinationals or Indian companies with international listings face ISSB-aligned reporting obligations from financial years beginning January 2024 onward in early-adopter jurisdictions
  • EU CBAM — full phase-in from January 2026 applies to cement, steel, aluminium, fertilisers, hydrogen, and electricity; Indian exporters in these sectors must verify and disclose embedded carbon content including from imported fossil fuel inputs to avoid CBAM surcharges at EU border
  • SBTi Corporate Net-Zero Standard — companies with validated near-term targets must reduce absolute Scope 3 emissions 42% by 2030 from base year; Indian companies with SBTi commitments that source significant energy from imported coal face a structural compliance gap if supply-side decarbonisation by exporting nations does not materialise
  • CDP Supply Chain Programme — Indian suppliers to global multinationals responding to CDP Supply Chain questionnaire must disclose Scope 3 Category 1 and 3 data; non-disclosure increasingly results in supplier delisting from procurement shortlists

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT analysis fundamentally reframes the physical and transition risk calculus for Indian industry. India sits at the intersection of two colliding forces: it is simultaneously the world's third-largest coal consumer and one of the most climate-vulnerable major economies. The continued — and in some cases growing — fossil fuel export pipelines from Australia, Indonesia, Russia, and the US mean that the feedstock of India's existing industrial base will remain available and price-competitive for at least another decade. This creates a classic carbon lock-in dynamic: Indian steel and power companies can continue to access imported coal cheaply, reducing the near-term economic incentive to accelerate the transition, while simultaneously accumulating transition risk in their balance sheets as global carbon pricing and border adjustment mechanisms tighten. Under ISSB IFRS S2 — which India's MCA and SEBI are watching closely as the foundation for future mandatory climate disclosure standards — companies are required to disclose climate-related risks across the full value chain, including the transition risks associated with carbon-intensive purchased inputs. For Indian steelmakers using Australian metallurgical coal, this means disclosing the scenario analysis implications of a world where Australian coal exports face carbon pricing, export levies, or shipping emission costs under IMO's 2023 GHG Strategy (targeting net-zero shipping by or around 2050). The physical risk dimension is equally acute: climate impacts on Australian and Indonesian coal export infrastructure — cyclones, flooding of mine access roads, port disruptions — are live supply chain risks that BRSR Core's value chain risk disclosures now require Indian companies to assess. India's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC), updated in 2022, targets 45% reduction in emissions intensity of GDP by 2030 and 500 GW of non-fossil electricity capacity. These targets are achievable in principle, but the CAT analysis illustrates that exporting nations are not pricing the transition cost into their export economics. India's policymakers at MoEFCC and NITI Aayog must now grapple with the reality that the 'transition away' language of COP28 will not be operationalised by exporting nations voluntarily — meaning India's own industrial transition timeline cannot be calibrated on the assumption of supply-side tightening of imported fossil fuels. This has direct implications for BEE's energy efficiency roadmaps and MoEFCC's Long-Term Low Emission Development Strategy (LT-LEDS) submitted to UNFCCC in 2022.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Head of Sustainability — within 60 days: commission a Scope 3 Category 1 and 3 materiality assessment using GHG Protocol Scope 3 Calculation Guidance; map all imported fossil fuel volumes (coal, LNG, crude oil, fuel oil) to country-of-origin emission factors to build BRSR Core-ready disclosure datasets ahead of FY2024-25 annual report filing
  • Procurement and supply chain teams — within 90 days: engage key fossil fuel commodity suppliers (coal traders, LNG off-takers, crude oil procurement desks) to obtain primary emission intensity data per GJ of fuel delivered; this is required for credible Scope 3 Category 1 supplier engagement under SBTi's Supplier Engagement Rating and CDP Supply Chain scoring
  • Head of Strategy / CSO — within 6 months: conduct a scenario analysis stress-test under IEA Net Zero by 2050 and IEA Announced Pledges Scenario for the price and availability of imported coal and LNG through 2035; present findings to board risk committee as a climate transition risk disclosure for BRSR Core and any TCFD-aligned investor reports
  • Company Secretary / Audit Committee — before FY2024-25 assurance engagement: brief statutory auditors and ESG assurance providers on the Scope 3 boundary, specifically the treatment of imported fossil fuel emission factors; confirm that the assurance methodology aligns with ISAE 3000 or equivalent and that country-of-origin emission intensity data sources are defensible under SEBI's BRSR Core reasonable assurance requirement
  • Board Risk Committee — at next quarterly meeting: table the CAT analysis alongside India's CCTS roadmap and EU CBAM phase-in timeline as connected transition risk triggers; request management to disclose what percentage of current Scope 1 emissions are dependent on imported fossil fuels and what the forward carbon cost exposure is under a ₹1,000/tonne CO2 and ₹3,000/tonne CO2 scenario
  • Investor Relations / ESG team — within 3 months: proactively update institutional investor and FPI engagement decks to address Scope 3 Category 1 and 3 exposure from imported fossil fuels; anticipate specific questions from UNPRI signatories and Climate Action 100+ members about alignment with SBTi and ISSB S2 disclosure on value chain emissions

Our Take

The CAT analysis is important not because it reveals something unknown — the accounting blind spot for exported fossil fuel emissions has been a feature of the UNFCCC architecture since Kyoto — but because it arrives at a moment when the capital market and regulatory infrastructure to act on this knowledge is finally maturing. ISSB IFRS S2's Scope 3 requirements, SEBI's BRSR Core reasonable assurance mandate, EU CBAM's embedded carbon methodology, and SBTi's Scope 3 absolute reduction targets are collectively creating a regulatory pincer movement that will, within five years, make the 'exported emissions are not my problem' position untenable for Indian listed companies. The exporting nations — Australia, Indonesia, the US, Russia, Saudi Arabia — are not going to resolve this through voluntary action. That means the accountability will migrate downstream, to the importers, combustors, and disclosers: Indian industry. What boards should watch over the next 24 to 36 months: first, expect IOSCO and IESBA to push for mandatory Scope 3 assurance standards that close the current gap between Scope 1/2 (limited assurance becoming reasonable assurance) and Scope 3 (largely unassured today) — this will change the cost and risk profile of Scope 3 non-disclosure overnight. Second, watch for India's CCTS to evolve from a sectoral intensity-based scheme to an absolute cap mechanism as India approaches its 2070 net-zero commitment year; at that point, the carbon cost of imported fossil fuels will be explicitly priced into Indian compliance markets. Third, anticipate that the EU CBAM scope — currently covering six sectors — will expand to cover chemicals, paper, and glass by 2030; Indian exporters in these sectors who have not built imported fossil fuel emission tracking into their cost accounting will face sudden and severe competitive disadvantage. The companies that treat the CAT analysis as a procurement, disclosure, and strategy problem today will be structurally advantaged when these regulatory shifts materialise. Those that wait for regulatory compulsion will be managing crisis, not transition.
