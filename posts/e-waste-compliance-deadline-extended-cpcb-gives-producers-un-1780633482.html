Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has extended the deadline for filing both quarterly and annual returns for FY 2023-24 under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, to 31 December 2024. This extension provides registered producers, manufacturers, refurbishers, recyclers, and other obligated entities additional time to meet their Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) reporting obligations. Companies must treat this as a firm final deadline and use the window to ensure complete and accurate filings — non-compliance risks EPR portal suspension, penalties, and reputational damage.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, entities registered on the CPCB EPR portal are required to file periodic returns — both quarterly and annual — documenting the volume of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) placed on the market, e-waste collected, channelised for recycling, and EPR targets met or missed. For FY 2023-24, these filings were originally due earlier in the calendar year. The CPCB has now issued an official notice extending the timeline for all outstanding quarterly returns (Q1 through Q4 of FY 2023-24) as well as the annual return for FY 2023-24, granting a uniform revised deadline of 31 December 2024. This is a regulatory relief measure, likely issued in response to industry representations citing challenges in data reconciliation across complex supply chains and delays in onboarding recyclers onto the EPR portal. However, it should not be interpreted as a relaxation of underlying EPR obligations — targets, collection duties, and recycling channelisation requirements remain fully in force.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE): All companies that manufacture, import, or brand EEE covered under Schedule I of the E-Waste Rules — including consumer electronics, IT and telecom equipment, large and small household appliances, lighting equipment, and more.
  • Refurbishers: Entities engaged in refurbishment of EEE who are registered under the Rules and have EPR reporting obligations.
  • E-Waste Recyclers and Dismantlers: CPCB/State-authorised recyclers who must report volumes processed and certificates issued.
  • Bulk Consumers: Large institutional users of EEE (government bodies, corporates, hospitals, educational institutions) who have obligations to channel e-waste to authorised recyclers.
  • EPR Compliance Intermediaries and PROs (Producer Responsibility Organisations): Entities facilitating EPR target fulfilment on behalf of producers, who must ensure underlying data flows are complete before the deadline.
  • Consumer Electronics, IT Hardware, Telecom, White Goods, and Lighting Sectors: These are the most heavily impacted industry verticals given the breadth of Schedule I coverage.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • REVISED DEADLINE — 31 December 2024: File all pending quarterly returns (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4) for FY 2023-24 on the CPCB EPR portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in).
  • REVISED DEADLINE — 31 December 2024: Submit the annual return for FY 2023-24, consolidating total EEE placed on market, e-waste collected, and EPR targets achieved.
  • EPR Target Reconciliation: Ensure that EPR certificates (from authorised recyclers) covering your FY 2023-24 collection and recycling targets have been uploaded and linked to your producer account before filing.
  • Recycler Certificate Verification: Cross-check that all recycler partners have uploaded processing certificates on the portal; unfiled certificates on the recycler's side will create gaps in your annual return.
  • No Further Extensions Should Be Assumed: This appears to be a final extension. Companies should not plan for an additional deferral — penalties under the E-Waste Rules and potential suspension of EPR registration apply to non-filers.
  • State Pollution Control Board (SPCB) Coordination: Where state-level authorisations are involved (e.g., for collection centres), ensure state-level reporting is also aligned with the central portal filings by 31 December 2024.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste governance sits at the intersection of the circular economy and climate transition — two themes increasingly scrutinised by global investors and trading partners. The E-Waste Rules' EPR framework is India's primary mechanism for keeping hazardous materials out of landfills and recovering critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, copper, and rare earth elements — that are essential for the clean energy transition (batteries, EVs, wind turbines, solar panels). Poor EPR compliance directly undermines domestic resource circularity and increases dependence on virgin mineral imports, a supply chain vulnerability that carries both physical and transition risk. From a global standards perspective, companies reporting under the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) framework (IFRS S1/S2) or preparing for alignment with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — particularly relevant for Indian subsidiaries of European multinationals or Indian exporters to the EU — will find that e-waste EPR performance feeds into material disclosures on waste management, circular economy practices, and value chain emissions. Similarly, SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) Core framework, now mandatory for the top 150 listed companies, includes indicators on waste generated and managed that are directly informed by EPR return data. Accurate, timely EPR filings are therefore not just a regulatory checkbox — they are the data foundation for credible ESG disclosure.

What To Do Now

  • Audit Your EPR Portal Status Immediately: Log into the CPCB EPR portal and identify which quarterly and annual returns for FY 2023-24 are pending, partially filed, or flagged with errors. Assign a responsible owner and set an internal deadline of 15 November 2024 — giving six weeks of buffer before the regulatory deadline.
  • Reconcile Data Across Your Value Chain: Collect volume data from your sales, logistics, and import teams (EEE placed on market) and match against EPR certificates received from your authorised recycler or PRO partners. Discrepancies must be resolved before filing, not after.
  • Engage Your Recycler and PRO Partners Urgently: Contact all recycler and PRO partners to confirm they have uploaded processing certificates and transaction records on the portal. Gaps on their side will invalidate your return — do not wait until December to discover this.
  • Link EPR Returns to BRSR and ESG Disclosures: Direct your sustainability reporting team to use the finalised FY 2023-24 EPR data to populate BRSR Core waste indicators and any ISSB/GRI 306 (Waste) disclosures. This creates a single source of truth and avoids inconsistencies across filings.
  • Establish a Standing EPR Compliance Calendar: Use this extension as a trigger to build a permanent internal compliance calendar for FY 2024-25 onwards — with internal quarterly filing deadlines set at least 30 days before CPCB due dates, preventing reliance on future extensions.

Our Take

The CPCB's decision to extend the FY 2023-24 e-waste return deadline to 31 December 2024 is pragmatic, but it signals a systemic challenge: India's EPR infrastructure — the portal, the recycler certification ecosystem, and corporate data pipelines — is still maturing. However, the regulatory direction is unambiguous. The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 represent a significant tightening over the predecessor 2016 rules, with steeper EPR targets, stricter documentation requirements, and a more functional digital compliance architecture. CPCB has shown it will enforce — EPR portal suspensions and show-cause notices to non-compliant producers have already been issued in other EPR streams (plastics, batteries). Companies that treat this extension as extra time to get organised rather than a signal that enforcement is lax are taking a serious risk. Looking ahead, we expect CPCB to integrate EPR compliance performance more tightly with product import clearances and company-level ESG ratings, making e-waste filings a boardroom-level concern, not just a regulatory affairs task.
