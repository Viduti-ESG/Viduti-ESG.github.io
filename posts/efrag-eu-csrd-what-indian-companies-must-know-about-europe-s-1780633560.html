EFRAG & EU CSRD: What Indian Companies Must Know About Europe's Sustainability Reporting Revolution

The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), developed in close collaboration with EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group), is rapidly becoming the world's most demanding sustainability disclosure framework — and its reach extends well beyond European borders

Executive Summary

The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), developed in close collaboration with EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group), is rapidly becoming the world's most demanding sustainability disclosure framework — and its reach extends well beyond European borders. Indian companies that supply to, invest in, or operate within EU markets are increasingly expected to provide granular ESG data to their European counterparts. Boards and sustainability officers who treat CSRD as a 'European problem' do so at significant commercial and reputational risk.

What Changed

The CSRD replaced the older Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) and dramatically expanded the scope, depth, and assurance requirements for sustainability disclosures across the EU. EFRAG, as the technical architect of the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), has developed a comprehensive set of standards covering climate, biodiversity, water, social factors, and governance — all underpinned by the concept of 'double materiality,' meaning companies must report both on how sustainability issues affect their business AND how their business affects the world. What makes this directly relevant to India is the 'value chain' provision. EU-based companies subject to CSRD are required to collect and disclose sustainability data from their entire value chain — including non-EU suppliers, joint venture partners, and investee companies. This means that even if an Indian company is not itself subject to CSRD, it will face mounting requests from its EU customers and investors for ESRS-aligned ESG disclosures. EFRAG continues to publish implementation guidance, Q&A documents, and sector-specific standards that shape exactly what data will be requested.

Who Is Affected

  • Large Indian exporters to the EU across sectors including textiles, pharmaceuticals, automotive components, chemicals, IT services, and agri-commodities
  • Indian subsidiaries or joint ventures of EU-headquartered companies already subject to CSRD reporting obligations
  • Indian companies listed on EU exchanges or with significant EU institutional investor bases
  • SMEs in the supply chain of large EU corporates, who face indirect disclosure requests even if not formally regulated
  • Indian financial institutions and asset managers with EU clients or co-investment structures
  • Companies preparing for BRSR Core alignment, as CSRD/ESRS represents the global benchmark those frameworks are converging toward

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Wave 1 (FY2024, reporting in 2025): ~11,500 large EU public-interest entities already under NFRD — their value chain data requests to Indian suppliers are live NOW
  • Wave 2 (FY2025, reporting in 2026): All large EU companies (500+ employees OR €40M+ turnover OR €20M+ balance sheet, meeting 2 of 3 criteria) — significantly expands the number of EU buyers requesting Indian supplier ESG data
  • Wave 3 (FY2026, reporting in 2027): Listed EU SMEs — broadens the net further into mid-market supply chains
  • Non-EU parent companies with EU-listed securities or €150M+ EU net turnover and at least one large EU subsidiary or branch: subject to CSRD from FY2028 — directly capturing large Indian multinationals with EU presence
  • EFRAG sector-specific ESRS standards (covering oil & gas, mining, retail, food & beverages, etc.) expected to be finalised progressively — Indian exporters in these sectors should monitor EFRAG's publication calendar closely
  • Limited assurance on CSRD disclosures required from Wave 1; reasonable assurance expected by 2028 — internal data quality must improve now, not at deadline

Climate Transition Angle

CSRD and the ESRS are not merely a compliance exercise — they are the EU's primary mechanism for redirecting capital toward companies credibly managing climate transition risks. ESRS E1 (Climate Change) is the most detailed and non-waivable standard, requiring companies to disclose Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions, transition plans aligned with the Paris Agreement's 1.5°C pathway, physical climate risk assessments, and carbon price assumptions embedded in financial planning. For Indian companies, this creates an urgent intersection with physical climate risk. India is among the world's most climate-vulnerable economies — extreme heat, monsoon variability, and water stress directly affect operations across manufacturing, agriculture, and infrastructure. EU buyers and investors now need to understand these physical risks within their own supply chain disclosures. Indian companies that can quantify and communicate their climate resilience will be commercially advantaged over those that cannot. The CSRD's ESRS framework was deliberately designed to interoperate with the ISSB's IFRS S1 and S2 standards, which India's SEBI and ICAI are actively considering for integration into the BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting) framework. Companies investing in CSRD-readiness are simultaneously building the foundation for next-generation domestic compliance. The direction of travel is unambiguous: granular, assured, comparable sustainability data is becoming a non-negotiable condition of market participation globally.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a CSRD Exposure Mapping: Identify all EU-based customers, investors, and partners and assess which are already subject to CSRD Wave 1 or Wave 2. Prioritise those relationships where ESG data requests are imminent or already arriving — this is your most urgent compliance trigger.
  • Perform a Double Materiality Assessment (DMA): Even if not formally required under Indian law, conducting a DMA aligned with ESRS methodology will future-proof your reporting, satisfy EU value chain queries, and align with emerging BRSR Core expectations. Engage a qualified ESG advisor to facilitate the process.
  • Build Scope 3 Emissions Capability: ESRS E1 requires EU companies to disclose Scope 3 emissions — which means your emissions become their emissions. Begin collecting activity data across your own supply chain, establish emission factors for key materials and logistics, and invest in credible carbon accounting software or methodology.
  • Review and Strengthen ESG Data Governance: CSRD requires limited (and eventually reasonable) third-party assurance. Audit your current data collection processes for GHG emissions, energy, water, waste, and social indicators. Identify gaps, assign data ownership, and implement controls as if assurance is already required — because for your EU customers, it effectively is.
  • Engage Legal and Commercial Teams on Contractual Implications: EU companies may begin embedding CSRD data-sharing obligations into supplier contracts. Ensure your legal, procurement, and sustainability teams are aligned on what data you can provide, at what frequency, and under what confidentiality terms — before you are negotiating from a position of weakness.

Our Take

CSRD and EFRAG's ESRS represent a structural shift, not a regulatory cycle. The EU has effectively established sustainability disclosure as a condition of market access, and through value chain provisions, that condition now applies to Indian companies whether or not they ever set foot in Brussels. What we are witnessing is the gradual emergence of a global ESG disclosure floor — shaped by CSRD in Europe, ISSB internationally, and progressively by SEBI's BRSR Core domestically. Indian companies that move early — investing in emissions accounting, double materiality assessments, and data governance — will not only avoid the commercial disruption of being unable to answer EU buyer questionnaires, but will build a genuine competitive advantage as sustainability transparency becomes a procurement and investment criterion. The question is no longer whether Indian businesses will need to report to this standard, but whether they will be ready when their largest customers demand it.
