Carbon Tracker's Enagas Report: Why European Gas Network Stranded Asset Risk Is a Live Governance and Portfolio Question for Indian Investors and Utilities

Executive Summary

Carbon Tracker's February 2024 report on Enagas — the third in a trilogy covering European gas network operators Snam, Italgas, and Enagas — delivers a clear capital markets warning: the energy transition is structurally undermining future cash flows from gas transport infrastructure, and institutional investors holding equity and debt in these companies face material, under-disclosed stranded asset risk. For Indian CFOs, CSOs, and ESG investors, this is not a distant European problem. It is a direct read-across to India's own gas pipeline expansion ambitions, the stranded asset exposure embedded in Indian utility and infrastructure portfolios, and the rapidly tightening disclosure obligations under SEBI's BRSR Core and ISSB IFRS S2 that will require Indian companies to quantify exactly this kind of transition risk. The report's core finding — that Paris alignment, hydrogen realism, asset life assumptions, and regulatory incentives are all misaligned at major European gas networks — is a template that Indian regulators, investors, and boards should apply urgently to domestic gas infrastructure, where capital allocation decisions made today carry 30-40 year asset life implications.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker (https://carbontracker.org/reports/decarbonising-gas-networks-part-2-enagas/), published 8 February 2024, completes a three-part institutional research series examining how Snam (Italy), Italgas (Italy), and Enagas (Spain) are — or are not — aligning their business models with net zero pathways. Senior Corporate Research Analyst Chris Moore assesses each company across four critical dimensions: Paris alignment of operations and capex, the realism of hydrogen as a decarbonisation pathway for gas networks, whether asset economic lives reflect lower future gas demand, and whether national regulatory frameworks are incentivising faster decarbonisation or entrenching fossil fuel infrastructure lock-in. The reports represent a significant escalation in how financial think tanks are engaging with infrastructure transition risk — moving beyond carbon footprinting to challenge the regulatory compact itself, arguing that gas network regulators in Italy and Spain are not sufficiently focused on decarbonisation. This is the ISSB IFRS S2 'transition risk' disclosure thesis made operational: Carbon Tracker is doing the asset-level analysis that companies themselves are expected to disclose under IFRS S2 para 10(b), which requires disclosure of transition risks that could reasonably be expected to affect the entity's cash flows, access to finance, or cost of capital over the short, medium, and long term. The fact that an independent think tank is producing this analysis — rather than the companies themselves — is itself the disclosure gap. For India, the direct regulatory connection is SEBI's BRSR Core framework, mandated for the top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24 and expanding to the top 250 from FY2024-25. BRSR Core's Principle 6 Essential Indicators require Scope 1, Scope 2, and — critically — Scope 3 emissions disclosure, along with transition risk assessment. Indian gas pipeline operators (GAIL, Gujarat Gas, Mahanagar Gas, Indraprastha Gas, Petronet LNG) and power utilities with gas-fired generation assets (NTPC, Torrent Power, Adani Power) are carrying structurally similar long-duration asset risk to Snam and Enagas — and are currently disclosing far less about it.

Who Is Affected

  • Indian gas distribution and transmission companies: GAIL (India) Ltd, Gujarat Gas Ltd, Mahanagar Gas Ltd, Indraprastha Gas Ltd, Petronet LNG — all carrying long-duration gas infrastructure whose economic life assumptions must now be stress-tested against 1.5°C and 2°C scenarios
  • Power utilities with gas-fired generation exposure: NTPC Ltd, Torrent Power, Adani Power, and state DISCOMs with gas PPAs — facing equivalent stranded asset risk as electrification accelerates and renewable generation undercuts gas economics
  • Indian institutional investors and asset managers — including LIC, SBI Mutual Fund, HDFC AMC, Nippon India AMC — with significant holdings in infrastructure and utility stocks/bonds that carry undisclosed transition risk on gas assets
  • Foreign institutional investors (FIIs) and ESG-labelled funds investing in Indian infrastructure: already applying SFDR Article 8/9 standards, they will require Paris-aligned asset life disclosures that Indian investee companies cannot currently produce
  • CFOs and treasury teams at BSE 500 companies with gas-linked capex programmes: capital allocation decisions made without scenario analysis are now an audit committee and board governance risk under ISSB IFRS S2
  • CSOs and sustainability heads at Indian energy companies preparing BRSR Core submissions: the Carbon Tracker framework — Paris alignment, hydrogen realism, asset lives, regulatory adequacy — is precisely the transition risk disclosure structure they must populate
  • PNGRB (Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board) and MoPNG policy teams: the Carbon Tracker critique of Spain's and Italy's regulators for failing to incentivise decarbonisation is a direct governance warning for India's own gas network regulatory compact

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • SEBI BRSR Core — Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions disclosure: mandatory for top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24 (i.e., already in force); top 250 from FY2024-25 — gas and power companies must quantify emissions from all operated assets including pipelines and generation fleet
  • SEBI BRSR Core — transition risk disclosure: companies must disclose risks and opportunities arising from energy transition; gas infrastructure operators must now assess asset stranding probability under IEA NZE 2050 and IPCC 1.5°C scenarios — no explicit deadline but FY2024-25 BRSR filings will be the first major test
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) — while not yet mandated by SEBI, the Ministry of Corporate Affairs' (MCA) National Roadmap on Sustainability Reporting (2023) signals convergence; companies proactively applying IFRS S2 must disclose transition risks including asset impairment risk under scenario analysis per para 10(b) and 13
  • GHG Protocol — Scope 3 Category 11 (use of sold products): gas distribution companies selling fossil gas to end consumers must account for downstream combustion emissions; this is the most material and most under-reported category for Indian gas utilities
  • SBTi Net Zero Standard — companies with SBTi commitments (TATA group entities, Mahindra, Infosys among early Indian adopters) must ensure capital allocation to gas assets does not conflict with validated near-term and long-term targets; SBTi's Oil & Gas sector guidance explicitly restricts new fossil fuel infrastructure capex
  • EU CSRD/ESRS E1 — Indian exporters supplying into European markets or Indian subsidiaries of European groups: ESRS E1 requires disclosure of GHG emissions across the value chain and Paris-aligned transition plans; gas-dependent supply chains face scrutiny from European parent entity sustainability reporting obligations effective from FY2024 for large EU companies

Climate Transition Angle

The Carbon Tracker Enagas report crystallises the central transition risk calculus that Indian gas infrastructure boards have not yet confronted at the required level of granularity. India's government has committed to 500 GW of non-fossil fuel capacity by 2030 and net zero by 2070 under its NDC and LT-LEDS. The IEA's India Energy Outlook 2021 projects natural gas demand peaking before 2040 under its Sustainable Development Scenario. Yet India continues to expand its City Gas Distribution (CGD) network — PNGRB has authorised CGD operations across 307 Geographical Areas covering approximately 98% of India's population — with capex decisions being made on asset lives of 25-40 years that extend well beyond any credible Paris-aligned gas demand curve. The three specific decarbonisation failures Carbon Tracker identifies at Enagas — insufficient gas leak reduction (fugitive methane emissions represent a Scope 1 disclosure obligation under BRSR Core and are subject to GHG Protocol's Scope 1 accounting rules), unrealistic hydrogen transition assumptions (India's National Hydrogen Mission's blending timeline of 2025 remains technically and commercially unproven at scale), and asset lives that fail to reflect declining gas demand — are not European peculiarities. They are structural features of how gas networks everywhere are avoiding the financial write-down that Paris alignment mathematically implies. For Indian gas utilities, this creates two specific capital market risks: first, impairment charges that have not been provisioned will eventually surface in audit processes, particularly as ICAI begins to align Indian accounting standards with IASB's evolving guidance on climate-related uncertainties in financial statements; second, international ESG-labelled bond issuances by Indian gas utilities (green bonds, sustainability-linked bonds) are increasingly vulnerable to greenwashing challenges if asset life assumptions embedded in bond prospectuses are not Paris-stress-tested. The RBI's 2023 Discussion Paper on Climate Risk and Sustainable Finance specifically flags transition risk in fossil fuel infrastructure as a systemic banking sector concern, meaning lenders to Indian gas networks face parallel regulatory pressure to require scenario-tested disclosures.

What To Do Now

  • CFO and Treasury (immediate, within 60 days): commission an independent asset impairment stress test on all gas infrastructure assets using IEA NZE 2050, IEA SDS, and IPCC 1.5°C scenarios; document assumptions on asset economic lives and compare against Paris-aligned gas demand projections — this is both an IFRS accounting risk and an ISSB IFRS S2 scenario analysis obligation
  • CSO and Sustainability team (FY2024-25 BRSR Core filing): structure transition risk disclosure using the Carbon Tracker four-pillar framework as a quality benchmark — Paris alignment of operations and capex, hydrogen realism, asset life appropriateness, and regulatory adequacy; this goes substantially beyond minimum BRSR Core requirements and will materially differentiate disclosure quality for ESG-rated investors
  • Board Risk Committee (next scheduled meeting): add 'gas infrastructure stranded asset risk' as a standing agenda item; request management to present scenario analysis outputs and confirm that capex approval processes incorporate 1.5°C transition risk gating — this is a direct board governance obligation under SEBI's Corporate Governance framework (LODR Regulation 17) and ISSB IFRS S2's governance disclosure requirements
  • Investor Relations team (before next earnings call): proactively brief institutional investors — particularly FIIs applying SFDR and domestic ESG funds — on the company's position on gas asset transition risk; failure to do so creates the same credibility gap that Carbon Tracker is now publicly identifying at Enagas and Snam
  • Legal and Compliance (within 90 days): audit all outstanding green bond or SLB documentation for gas-linked assets to identify Paris-alignment representations that may not be supportable under ISSB IFRS S2 scenario analysis; coordinate with auditors on ICAI's emerging guidance on climate uncertainties in financial statement disclosures
  • Government Affairs and Regulatory Engagement: monitor PNGRB's regulatory asset base (RAB) framework and engage proactively on accelerated depreciation or asset life revision mechanisms — Carbon Tracker's finding that European regulators are insufficiently focused on decarbonisation is a warning that India needs to get ahead of this before stranded asset write-downs become a regulatory and capital markets crisis

Our Take

Carbon Tracker's Enagas report is, on the surface, a European utilities story. Its real significance for Indian boards and investors is as a preview of the institutional investor engagement that India's gas sector has not yet experienced but will. The pattern is consistent and accelerating: independent financial analysis identifies the gap between a company's disclosed assumptions (asset lives, hydrogen upside, regulatory support) and what Paris alignment actually requires; institutional investors use that analysis to engage or divest; regulatory scrutiny follows; and eventually, accounting and audit standards catch up to force the write-downs that markets had already priced in. European gas utilities are at stage three of that sequence. Indian gas infrastructure is at stage one — but the distance between stage one and stage three is compressing rapidly, for three reasons. First, SEBI's BRSR Core framework is generating standardised, auditable Scope 1 and transition risk data for the first time, creating the evidence base for exactly the kind of Carbon Tracker-style analysis to be applied to GAIL, Gujarat Gas, or IGL. Second, India's green bond market — now operating under SEBI's Green Bond Framework (2023) and subject to ICMA Green Bond Principles — is creating a financial accountability mechanism: if gas network bonds are marketed as green or sustainability-linked, the Paris-alignment gap becomes a legal and reputational liability, not just an ESG rating issue. Third, global capital allocators are rapidly losing patience with 'future hydrogen' narratives as a justification for continued gas infrastructure investment; the IEA's World Energy Outlook 2023 confirmed that hydrogen blending in gas networks will have negligible impact on emissions trajectories at any credible scale before 2035. Indian boards that continue to rely on hydrogen optionality to justify 30-year gas network capex should expect Carbon Tracker, IEEFA, or CREA to produce the Indian equivalent of this Enagas report within 18-24 months. The question for Indian CFOs is whether they will be disclosing ahead of that analysis — or responding to it under pressure.
