CPCB Issues Interim EPR Credit Transfer Framework for Tyre Waste Recyclers: What You Need to Know

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent interim notice establishing a framework for the generation and transfer of EPR end-product credits (certificates) by tyre waste recyclers in India. This move signals the regulator's intent to operationalise the tyre waste EPR ecosystem

Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent interim notice establishing a framework for the generation and transfer of EPR end-product credits (certificates) by tyre waste recyclers in India. This move signals the regulator's intent to operationalise the tyre waste EPR ecosystem more rigorously, creating both compliance obligations and market opportunities for producers, importers, and recyclers. Companies in the tyre manufacturing, automotive, and waste management sectors must act swiftly to align their EPR compliance systems with the updated credit transfer mechanism.

What Changed

India's EPR framework for waste tyres — governed under the Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules and operationalised through CPCB's EPR portal — requires tyre producers and importers to meet annual recycling and recovery targets. To facilitate this, registered tyre waste recyclers generate EPR certificates (end-product credits) that producers can purchase to offset their obligations. The CPCB's interim notice addresses a critical operational gap: the mechanism by which these credits are generated and transferred between recyclers and producers on the centralised EPR portal. The interim arrangement likely introduces a standardised, time-bound process for credit issuance, validation, and transfer — providing regulatory clarity while a permanent system is finalised. This is consistent with CPCB's broader pattern of issuing interim protocols across plastic, battery, and tyre EPR streams as the portal infrastructure matures. In plain terms: if you are a tyre recycler, you now have updated instructions on how to log your recycling output and issue tradeable credits. If you are a tyre producer or importer, you need to ensure your compliance team is purchasing credits through the updated mechanism to avoid shortfalls against your EPR targets.

Who Is Affected

  • Tyre manufacturers (domestic producers) registered under the EPR framework for waste tyres
  • Importers of new tyres and vehicles with tyres, who carry EPR obligations under Indian rules
  • Registered tyre waste recyclers and retreaders who generate EPR end-product credits on the CPCB portal
  • Authorised collection agents and aggregators operating in the waste tyre supply chain
  • Automotive OEMs with take-back or EPR obligations linked to end-of-life tyres
  • EPR compliance consultants and intermediaries facilitating credit transactions on behalf of producers
  • State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) responsible for oversight of recycler registration and compliance

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Tyre waste recyclers must follow the interim procedure outlined in the CPCB notice to generate EPR end-product credits on the centralised portal — non-compliance may result in credits being deemed invalid
  • Recyclers should verify their registration status on the CPCB EPR portal is active and up to date before initiating any credit generation under the new interim arrangement
  • Producers and importers must confirm that EPR credits being purchased or transferred to their accounts are generated under the updated interim mechanism to ensure these credits are recognised for annual target compliance
  • All parties should monitor the CPCB website (cpcb.nic.in) and EPR portal for the transition from interim to permanent arrangements, as timelines for finalisation have not been publicly confirmed
  • Companies should reconcile their EPR obligation targets for the current financial year against available verified credits under the new framework before the annual compliance reporting deadline
  • Recyclers should maintain documentary evidence of all recycling quantities, process outputs, and portal submissions in line with the interim arrangement to withstand SPCB or CPCB audits
  • Deadline for compliance with interim arrangement: Not explicitly stated in available notice information — companies should treat this as immediately effective and seek clarification from CPCB or their SPCB without delay

Climate Transition Angle

At first glance, tyre waste EPR may appear to be a narrow regulatory compliance issue. In the broader context of India's climate transition and circular economy ambitions, however, it is anything but. End-of-life tyres are a significant source of carbon emissions and toxic pollutants when incinerated or landfilled illegally. A well-functioning EPR credit market incentivises formal, low-emission recycling — including pyrolysis-derived fuel oil, reclaimed carbon black, and steel wire recovery — directly displacing virgin, carbon-intensive raw materials. From a supply chain perspective, global automotive and tyre manufacturers face mounting pressure under frameworks such as the ISSB's IFRS S2 (climate-related disclosures) and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) to disclose and reduce Scope 3 emissions, including those associated with product end-of-life. A credible, auditable EPR credit system in India — exactly what CPCB is working to build — provides the data infrastructure needed to substantiate these disclosures. Furthermore, as India advances its National Action Plan on Climate Change and circular economy policy, robust EPR enforcement for waste streams like tyres is a prerequisite for attracting green finance, meeting Extended Producer Responsibility commitments under international trade agreements, and demonstrating regulatory equivalence with EU and US sustainability standards that multinational supply chain partners increasingly require.

What To Do Now

  • Download and review the full CPCB interim notice immediately: Access the official PDF at cpcb.nic.in and circulate it to your EPR compliance lead, legal team, and any registered recycling partners — do not rely on secondary summaries for a regulatory notice of this nature
  • Audit your EPR portal account: Both producers and recyclers should log into the CPCB EPR portal, verify account details, and identify any pending credit generation or transfer transactions that may need to be reprocessed under the interim arrangement
  • Engage your recycler network proactively: Producers who depend on third-party recyclers to generate credits on their behalf should communicate the new interim requirements immediately and confirm that recyclers are compliant — a recycler's non-compliance directly creates a gap in your own EPR obligations
  • Map your annual EPR target against verified credit availability: With the financial year progressing, conduct a gap analysis now — how many tonnes of tyre waste must you account for, how many valid credits do you currently hold, and what additional procurement is needed under the updated mechanism?
  • Build a regulatory monitoring cadence: CPCB's EPR frameworks are evolving rapidly across all waste streams. Assign a team member or engage an external ESG/compliance advisor to track CPCB portal updates, circulars, and notices on a weekly basis to avoid being caught off guard by future interim or permanent changes

Our Take

This interim notice is a microcosm of a larger story unfolding across India's EPR landscape: the regulator is serious about making EPR credit markets functional, transparent, and enforceable, but the infrastructure is still catching up to the policy ambition. The fact that CPCB is issuing interim arrangements — rather than waiting for a perfect, permanent solution — reflects pragmatic urgency and should be read as a signal that enforcement pressure on producers and recyclers will only intensify. Companies that treat EPR compliance as a box-ticking exercise, purchasing credits at the last minute without robust documentation, are accumulating regulatory risk. The smarter play is to embed EPR credit management into your supply chain governance now, build direct relationships with verified, high-quality recyclers, and position your organisation to benefit from what will eventually become a liquid, price-transparent tyre waste credit market — one that sophisticated investors will scrutinise as part of broader ESG due diligence.
