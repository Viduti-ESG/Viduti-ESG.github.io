Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) notified the Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 on August 24, 2022, superseding the older Battery (Management and Handling) Rules, 2001. The new framework introduces a comprehensive Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) regime covering all battery types — from portable and automotive to industrial and electric vehicle (EV) batteries — making producers, importers, and recyclers directly accountable for end-of-life battery collection, refurbishment, and recycling. For businesses across India's rapidly growing EV, consumer electronics, energy storage, and manufacturing sectors, compliance is no longer optional: it is a legal obligation with measurable targets and a centralized EPR registration system managed by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB).

What Changed

The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 replace the two-decade-old Battery (Management and Handling) Rules, 2001, which were widely regarded as inadequate for the scale and complexity of India's modern battery economy. The old rules had limited scope and weak enforcement mechanisms. The 2022 Rules mark a fundamental shift in philosophy: instead of placing the burden of battery waste management on consumers and local bodies, the new framework holds producers responsible through Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR). EPR, in plain terms, means that the company which manufactures, sells, or imports a battery must also take responsibility for what happens to that battery at the end of its useful life. Producers are required to register on the CPCB's centralized EPR portal, set collection and recycling targets, and demonstrate compliance annually. The rules cover all battery chemistries and sizes — including lithium-ion, nickel, cobalt, and lead-acid variants — and apply irrespective of whether the battery is sold standalone or embedded within a device or vehicle. The rules define four key battery categories: (1) Portable Batteries — sealed, under 5 kg, not for industrial or EV use; (2) Automotive Batteries — used for starting, lighting, or ignition in motor vehicles; (3) Industrial Batteries — designed for industrial use, excluding portable, EV, and automotive types; and (4) Electric Vehicle (EV) Batteries — specifically designed to power hybrid and electric road vehicles. Each category carries distinct EPR collection and recycling obligations as specified in Schedule II of the Rules. Additionally, the framework recognizes refurbishers and recyclers as formal entities that must register and operate within an environmentally sound management framework.

Who Is Affected

  • Battery Producers: Any entity manufacturing batteries (or devices containing batteries) under its own brand, or selling batteries manufactured by third parties under its brand name
  • Battery Importers: Companies importing batteries — standalone or embedded in equipment such as laptops, smartphones, power tools, EVs, and energy storage systems
  • Electric Vehicle (EV) Manufacturers and OEMs: Companies manufacturing or selling EVs, which carry specific EV battery EPR obligations given the high material content (lithium, cobalt, nickel) and scale of deployment
  • Consumer Electronics Companies: Manufacturers and importers of smartphones, laptops, wearables, and other portable electronics with embedded rechargeable batteries
  • Industrial Equipment Manufacturers: Companies making UPS systems, telecom infrastructure, solar storage, and heavy industrial machinery that use sealed or unsealed industrial batteries
  • Battery Recyclers and Refurbishers: Entities engaged in dismantling, processing, reconditioning, or recycling waste batteries — all of whom must register with CPCB
  • Retailers and Bulk Consumers: Dealers and large-volume consumers (such as telecom towers, data centers, and utilities) who have obligations to facilitate collection and return of waste batteries
  • Public Waste Management Authorities: Gram Panchayats, Municipal Corporations, and their service agencies who interact with battery waste streams at the local level

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • EPR Registration (Immediate / Ongoing): All producers (manufacturers and importers) must register on the CPCB's centralized EPR portal before placing batteries on the market. Registration is mandatory — operating without it constitutes a violation of the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986
  • Collection Targets: Producers must meet phased collection targets for waste batteries as specified in Schedule II of the Rules. Targets are defined as a percentage of batteries placed on the market in prior years, and ramp up progressively over time to encourage rapid scale-up of collection infrastructure
  • Recycling and Refurbishment Targets: Collected waste batteries must meet minimum recycling and/or refurbishment targets as specified in Schedule II. Recyclers and refurbishers must also be CPCB-registered, and producers are responsible for ensuring only registered entities handle their waste batteries
  • EPR Credit Mechanism: The Rules introduce a tradeable EPR credit system — producers who exceed their targets can generate credits, which can be purchased by those falling short. This market-based mechanism is managed through the CPCB portal and mirrors global EPR best practices
  • Annual Reporting: Producers, recyclers, and refurbishers must submit annual returns to CPCB through the EPR portal, detailing quantities of batteries sold, collected, recycled, and refurbished. Non-submission or misreporting attracts penalties under the Environment (Protection) Act
  • Environmentally Sound Management (ESM): All waste batteries must be handled, stored, transported, and processed in accordance with ESM standards. Hazardous battery materials — including lead, cadmium, lithium, and cobalt compounds — must not be disposed of in landfills or through open burning
  • Take-Back and Deposit-Refund Systems: Producers of portable batteries are encouraged (and in some cases required) to implement take-back schemes, potentially including deposit-refund mechanisms, to incentivize consumer return of spent batteries
  • Labelling Requirements: Batteries placed on the market must carry prescribed labelling, including EPR registration numbers and symbols indicating that the battery should not be disposed of as general municipal waste
  • Effective Date: The Rules came into force on the date of publication in the Official Gazette — August 24, 2022. Producers who have not yet registered or put EPR systems in place are already in a state of non-compliance

Climate Transition Angle

The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 are far more than a housekeeping regulation — they sit at the intersection of India's climate transition ambitions, global supply chain scrutiny, and the emerging international ESG disclosure landscape. Understanding these connections is essential for companies that see themselves as part of the clean energy economy. India's National Electric Mobility Mission and its target of 30% EV penetration by 2030 will place millions of lithium-ion battery packs into circulation over the next decade. Without a robust end-of-life management system, this transition risks creating a toxic waste crisis that undermines the very environmental credentials of clean transportation. The 2022 Rules are, in effect, the regulatory infrastructure that makes the EV transition environmentally credible — not just economically viable. From a physical risk perspective, improper battery disposal — particularly of lead-acid and lithium-ion chemistries — poses significant soil and groundwater contamination risks in industrial corridors and urban areas. Companies with supply chains running through regions with poor waste management infrastructure face reputational and liability exposure if their end-of-life batteries are traced back to irresponsible handling. This is a material risk that institutional investors and ESG rating agencies are increasingly factoring into company assessments. On the global standards front, the European Union's new Battery Regulation (2023/1542) — one of the most ambitious battery regulations in the world — introduces mandatory carbon footprint declarations, minimum recycled content requirements, and digital battery passports for batteries sold in the EU. Indian exporters supplying batteries or battery-embedded products to European markets will need to align their EPR compliance data and recycled content tracking with EU requirements. The ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standard and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) both require companies to disclose material risks and opportunities related to the circular economy and resource transition — of which battery waste is a growing component. Indian companies with international investors or listed subsidiaries in global markets should treat CPCB EPR compliance as the foundation of a broader ESG data architecture, not a standalone regulatory tick-box.

What To Do Now

  • Register on the CPCB EPR Portal Immediately: If your company manufactures, imports, or sells batteries or battery-embedded products in India and has not yet obtained EPR registration, this is your most urgent action. Assign a dedicated compliance officer to complete registration, upload the required documentation, and maintain the account on an ongoing basis
  • Conduct a Full Battery Inventory and Market Placement Audit: Map every SKU or product line that contains a battery — including embedded batteries in devices — and quantify the volumes placed on the Indian market in each of the past three financial years. This data forms the baseline for calculating your EPR collection and recycling targets under Schedule II
  • Establish Verified Collection and Recycling Partnerships: Identify and contract with CPCB-registered recyclers and refurbishers. Do not rely on informal or unregistered collection channels — doing so will not count toward your EPR targets and exposes your company to liability. Consider joining or forming a Producer Responsibility Organisation (PRO) to pool collection infrastructure costs with other producers
  • Build an EPR Credit Tracking and Reporting System: Implement internal systems (or integrate with CPCB's portal) to track battery collection, handover to recyclers, and EPR credit generation in real time. Annual reporting errors or gaps are a common cause of regulatory notices — invest in data accuracy from the outset
  • Align Internal ESG Disclosures with EPR Data: If your company publishes a Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR), sustainability report, or responds to ESG questionnaires from investors or customers, incorporate your EPR targets, actual collection rates, and recycled material content data. This positions your company ahead of the curve as global buyers and investors increasingly demand circular economy metrics aligned with ISSB and EU standards

Our Take

The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 represent one of the most significant upgrades to India's extended producer responsibility framework in recent years, and their importance will only grow as the country's EV and energy storage sectors scale rapidly. What makes this regulation particularly consequential is its timing: it arrives just as global supply chains for critical battery minerals — lithium, cobalt, nickel, and manganese — are coming under intense regulatory and investor scrutiny, and as India positions itself as both a major EV market and a potential battery manufacturing hub under the PLI (Production Linked Incentive) scheme. Companies that treat EPR compliance as a mere administrative formality will find themselves poorly positioned when global customers and investors start demanding auditable end-of-life battery data as a condition of doing business. Those who invest now in building robust collection infrastructure, verified recycling partnerships, and integrated ESG reporting systems will not only stay ahead of domestic enforcement but will also be better placed to access export markets under the EU Battery Regulation and to satisfy the disclosure expectations of ISSB-aligned investors. The next frontier — which CPCB is likely to address in amendments — will be mandatory minimum recycled content requirements and battery passport-style traceability, mirroring the EU trajectory. The time to build the data and operational foundations for that future is now.
