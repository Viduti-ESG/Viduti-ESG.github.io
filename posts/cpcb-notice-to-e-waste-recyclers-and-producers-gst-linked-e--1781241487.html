Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) issued a critical notice on June 15, 2023, mandating that e-waste recyclers must now use GST-linked e-invoices for all sales and purchases of recovered materials and e-waste in order to generate valid EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) Certificates on the EPR Portal. Producers are simultaneously required to accept only GST-linked invoices from recyclers as legally recognised proof of recycling. This effectively closes a documentation loophole and tightens the audit trail across India's formal e-waste recycling chain.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, notified by the Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change on November 2, 2022, established a digital EPR Portal through which recyclers generate EPR Certificates — the instruments that allow producers to demonstrate they have met their mandatory e-waste collection and recycling targets. Until this notice, the documentation standards for transactions on the EPR Portal were not explicitly harmonised with India's GST e-invoicing framework. The CPCB's June 15, 2023 directive now makes GST-linked e-invoices the sole acceptable documentation for: (1) recyclers recording the purchase of e-waste and the sale of recovered/end-product materials on the EPR Portal, and (2) producers accepting proof of recycling from those recyclers. In plain terms: if a recycler cannot show a GST e-invoice for their e-waste transactions, they cannot generate a legitimate EPR Certificate. And if a producer accepts non-GST documentation from a recycler, that recycling activity will not count toward the producer's EPR obligation.

Who Is Affected

  • Registered E-Waste Recyclers: All recyclers authorised under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 who process and recover materials from electrical and electronic equipment (EEE).
  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE): Any company — domestic or multinational — that manufactures, imports, or sells EEE products listed in Schedule I of the 2022 Rules (covering consumer electronics, IT equipment, lighting, medical devices, etc.).
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs): Entities that facilitate EPR compliance on behalf of multiple producers and coordinate with recyclers — their documentation flows are directly impacted.
  • Bulk Consumers and Collection Agents: While the notice is addressed to recyclers and producers, bulk consumers who channel e-waste to recyclers should ensure their own transaction records are GST-compliant to maintain a clean chain of custody.
  • Large and Mid-Sized Manufacturers: Companies with significant annual production volumes and correspondingly large EPR targets face the highest financial exposure if recycling claims are invalidated due to non-compliant invoicing.
  • SME Recyclers: Smaller recycling units that may not yet be fully integrated into the GST e-invoicing system face the greatest operational adjustment and risk of EPR Certificate invalidation.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE — Recyclers must transition all sales and purchase transactions related to e-waste and recovered/end-product materials to GST e-invoices generated through the GST Portal. Non-GST or manual invoices will no longer be valid for EPR Portal submissions.
  • IMMEDIATE — Recyclers must link these GST e-invoices to their records on the CPCB EPR Portal as the basis for generating EPR Certificates. Ensure your EPR Portal account is configured to upload or reference GST invoice data.
  • IMMEDIATE — Producers must update their internal procurement and compliance verification processes to accept only GST-linked sales invoices from e-waste recyclers as valid proof of recycling toward EPR targets.
  • ONGOING — Producers and PROs must audit existing recycler agreements and contracts to include a contractual requirement for GST e-invoice compliance, creating a legal basis for rejecting non-compliant documentation.
  • COMPLIANCE REVIEW — Companies should conduct an internal audit of all EPR Certificates already generated and recycling claims already submitted in FY 2022-23 to assess whether documentation meets the new GST-linked standard, and proactively engage CPCB if gaps are identified.
  • REGISTRATION CHECK — Recyclers not yet registered for GST e-invoicing (applicable to businesses with annual turnover above the notified threshold) must complete registration immediately. Those below the threshold should seek legal clarity on applicability.
  • DEADLINE CONTEXT — While no grace period is specified in the notice (effective June 15, 2023), EPR targets for FY 2023-24 will be assessed against this standard; non-compliant recycling transactions risk being excluded from target calculations.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally, and the recovery of critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, copper, rare earth elements — from end-of-life electronics is increasingly central to the climate transition. India's ability to build a circular economy for these materials directly supports domestic manufacturing of EVs, solar panels, and energy storage systems, reducing dependence on virgin mineral extraction and the associated carbon footprint. This CPCB notice, while procedural in nature, is part of a broader global tightening of traceability standards in recycling and resource recovery. The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standards both require companies to account for material risks and circularity in their supply chains. For multinational producers selling into India, failure to maintain rigorous, auditable EPR compliance records creates a supply chain transparency gap that will increasingly be scrutinised by global investors and regulators. From a physical risk perspective, India's dependence on imported critical minerals makes domestic recycling infrastructure a strategic climate resilience asset. Strengthening the formal recycling sector through enforceable documentation standards — as this notice does — is a foundational step toward reducing that import dependency and building supply chain resilience against resource scarcity scenarios projected under 2°C and higher warming pathways. Companies with Science-Based Targets (SBTs) or net-zero commitments should also note that scope 3 emissions accounting increasingly includes the end-of-life treatment of sold products. A credible, GST-verified EPR compliance record provides the documentation backbone for such scope 3 disclosures.

What To Do Now

  • Recyclers: Register or verify your GST e-invoicing capability immediately — contact your GST Suvidha Provider (GSP) or the GST Portal helpdesk. Ensure your billing software or ERP system is configured to generate e-invoices for all e-waste purchase and recovered material sale transactions, and that invoice reference numbers can be uploaded to the CPCB EPR Portal.
  • Producers & PROs: Issue a formal compliance advisory to all your registered recycling partners requiring GST e-invoice submission with immediate effect. Update vendor onboarding checklists and recycler empanelment criteria to make GST e-invoice compliance a mandatory qualification.
  • Legal & Compliance Teams: Amend recycler contracts and PRO agreements to include a clause explicitly requiring GST e-invoice documentation for all EPR-related transactions, with a right to reject non-compliant claims. Specify that the producer/PRO bears no liability for EPR shortfalls arising from a recycler's failure to comply.
  • Sustainability & Finance Teams: Cross-check your FY 2022-23 EPR Certificate portfolio against the new documentation standard. If any certificates were generated without GST-linked invoices, consult with your legal team and consider voluntary disclosure to CPCB to avoid penalties during the next compliance assessment cycle.
  • IT & Data Teams: Build a data linkage between your GST invoice repository and your EPR Portal submissions to enable real-time verification and streamline annual EPR reporting. This integration will also support future ESG and sustainability disclosures that require auditable recycling data.

Our Take

This notice may read like a technical clarification, but its significance should not be underestimated. By anchoring EPR Certificate generation to the GST e-invoicing infrastructure, CPCB is effectively bringing the weight of India's tax enforcement architecture to bear on e-waste compliance — a smart and overdue move. The informal recycling sector has long been a weak link in India's e-waste chain, with a substantial portion of e-waste being processed outside the formal system. Requiring GST e-invoices creates a hard filter: recyclers operating informally or without proper GST registration simply cannot generate valid EPR Certificates, which in turn means producers cannot use their output to meet EPR targets. For producers, this raises the stakes considerably — your EPR compliance is now only as strong as your recyclers' GST compliance. We expect CPCB to follow this notice with stricter scrutiny of EPR Portal submissions and potentially enhanced penalties for producers found to have claimed recycling credits based on non-GST documentation. Companies that proactively audit their recycler networks and upgrade their documentation systems now will be well-positioned — those that wait risk EPR shortfalls, penalties, and reputational exposure as India's ESG regulatory environment continues to harden.
