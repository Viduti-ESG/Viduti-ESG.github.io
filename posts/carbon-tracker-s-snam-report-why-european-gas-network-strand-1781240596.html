Carbon Tracker's Snam Report: Why European Gas Network Stranded Asset Risk Should Alarm Indian Infrastructure Investors, Gas Utilities, and BRSR Disclosers Now

Executive Summary

Carbon Tracker's February 2024 report on Snam — Europe's largest gas transmission infrastructure operator — delivers a systemic warning that energy transition is rendering future cash flows from gas transport and distribution structurally uncertain, with asset life assumptions, hydrogen decarbonisation narratives, and regulatory frameworks failing to adequately reflect Paris-aligned scenarios. For Indian stakeholders, this is not a European story: it is a direct preview of the stranded asset and disclosure risk trajectory facing India's gas infrastructure sector — GAIL, Gujarat Gas, Indraprastha Gas, Mahanagar Gas, Petronet LNG — and the institutional investors who hold their equity and bonds. With SEBI's BRSR Core mandating climate-related financial disclosures aligned to ISSB IFRS S2 for India's top 150 listed companies from FY2024-25, the transition risk valuation methodology Carbon Tracker applies to Snam is the same analytical lens that global asset managers and proxy advisors will shortly apply to Indian gas utilities.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker (https://carbontracker.org/reports/decarbonising-gas-networks-part-1-snam/), published 08 February 2024, forms Part 1 of a three-report series covering Snam, Italgas, and Enagas — the three major European listed gas network companies whose stocks and bonds are embedded in European infrastructure and utility fund portfolios. The analytical framework Carbon Tracker deploys is instructive: it assesses Paris alignment of each operator's decarbonisation pathway, the credibility of hydrogen retrofit narratives as a transition strategy, the appropriateness of existing asset economic lives given declining gas demand projections, and whether national regulators are building sufficient decarbonisation incentives into the tariff and concession frameworks that underpin these companies' regulated asset base (RAB) valuations. The core finding — that gas network decarbonisation is activity-dependent (transmission versus distribution) and geography-dependent, and that hydrogen as a like-for-like gas substitute is increasingly challenged as a mass network decarbonisation pathway — has direct structural parallels in India. India's City Gas Distribution (CGD) sector, regulated under PNGRB, is mid-build across 295 Geographical Areas (GAs) under the 9th and 10th CGD bidding rounds. The network economic life assumptions embedded in PNGRB's tariff authorisations and the capital work-in-progress (CWIP) on the balance sheets of IGL, MGL, Gujarat Gas and their peers reflect a demand trajectory that has not been stress-tested against India's own net zero by 2070 commitment or the IEA's Net Zero Emissions by 2050 scenario. Carbon Tracker's methodology — shortening asset economic lives, stress-testing RAB against demand scenarios, and requiring quantified methane leak reduction commitments — is precisely the analytical lens that ISSB IFRS S2 scenario analysis (paragraph 18, disclosure of climate-related risks over short, medium, and long-term horizons) will require Indian gas utilities to apply and disclose under BRSR Core. The report also highlights that investor engagement with gas network operators must extend beyond company-level action to regulatory advocacy — companies must actively push national regulators to reform frameworks to incentivise decarbonisation. In India's context, this means GAIL, Gujarat Gas, and Petronet LNG boards should be preparing to engage PNGRB and MoPNG on whether the CGD regulatory framework — currently designed to incentivise network rollout, not network managed decline or hydrogen transition — is fit for a 2070 net zero pathway. This is a board governance question, not merely a technical one.

Who Is Affected

  • Indian City Gas Distribution companies: Indraprastha Gas (IGL), Mahanagar Gas (MGL), Gujarat Gas, Adani Total Gas — mid-rollout on 9th and 10th CGD round networks, with long asset life assumptions embedded in balance sheets and tariff orders
  • Gas transmission and processing majors: GAIL India, Petronet LNG, GSPL — with pipeline RAB, LNG terminal capex, and long-term offtake commitments exposed to demand scenario risk
  • Institutional investors and debt holders: Infrastructure debt funds, insurance companies (LIC, GIC Re), and pension funds with India CGD equity or NCD exposure; ESG-screened funds benchmarking against MSCI ESG or FTSE4Good indices where stranded asset methodology is being tightened
  • CFOs and treasury teams at listed gas utilities preparing BRSR Core disclosures for FY2024-25 under SEBI's mandatory ISSB-aligned climate scenario analysis requirements
  • Chief Sustainability Officers at BSE 500 energy companies where Scope 1 methane emissions from gas infrastructure will come under increasing scrutiny from CDP, SBTi Oil & Gas pathway, and proxy advisors (ISS, Glass Lewis)
  • Board risk committees at Indian gas sector companies where the intersection of regulated asset base valuation, Paris-aligned scenario analysis, and audit committee sign-off on climate-related financial disclosures creates a new governance exposure
  • Export-facing Indian energy companies and project developers where EU Taxonomy alignment and CBAM-adjacent green finance criteria require demonstration that gas assets are transition-credible or time-limited

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • SEBI BRSR Core — FY2024-25 (mandatory for top 150 listed companies by market cap): Climate-related financial disclosures including Scope 1, 2, 3 GHG emissions, transition risk assessment, and scenario analysis aligned to ISSB IFRS S2 — gas utilities must now disclose the financial materiality of demand scenario risk on asset valuations
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (effective for reporting periods beginning on or after 01 January 2024): Paragraph 18 requires disclosure of climate-related risks and opportunities over short, medium, and long-term horizons — Indian companies voluntarily adopting or compelled through BRSR Core alignment must stress-test gas network asset lives against 1.5°C and 2°C scenarios
  • GHG Protocol / CDP disclosure: Methane fugitive emissions from gas transmission and distribution must be quantified and disclosed under Scope 1 — CPCB's draft methane monitoring guidelines and India's updated UNFCCC NDC both reference improved methane accounting; CDP Oil & Gas questionnaire 2024 explicitly asks for methane intensity targets
  • SBTi Oil & Gas pathway (applicable to GAIL, ONGC, and gas-sector companies with SBTi commitments or under investor pressure to commit): Requires absolute Scope 1+2 reduction targets and methane intensity targets aligned to IEA NZE scenario — asset life and demand assumptions are implicit in any credible SBTi submission
  • PNGRB CGD network regulatory review: While no formal deadline exists, the Carbon Tracker methodology signals that RAB-based valuations and concession period assumptions in CGD GAs must be reviewed for Paris alignment — boards should proactively engage PNGRB before investor pressure forces reactive disclosure
  • EU Green Bond Standard and SFDR Article 9 fund eligibility: Indian gas infrastructure bonds or green bonds sold into European markets will face increased scrutiny on Paris alignment, asset life assumptions, and hydrogen transition credibility — CFOs planning ECB or Masala Bond issuance in 2024-25 must pre-empt this

Climate Transition Angle

Carbon Tracker's Snam analysis crystallises the central tension in gas infrastructure globally: network operators are stranded between the physical reality of continued gas demand in the near term and the financial reality that long-duration gas network assets — pipelines with 40-60 year economic lives, LNG regasification terminals with 25-30 year amortisation schedules, CGD network steel embedded in urban geographies — are being valued on demand assumptions that are inconsistent with Paris-aligned scenarios. In India, this tension is acutely visible: the government's Gas Vision 2030 targets raising natural gas's share of the primary energy mix from approximately 6% to 15%, while simultaneously India's NDC commits to achieving net zero by 2070 and 50% non-fossil electricity capacity by 2030. These two policy vectors are not currently reconciled in any publicly disclosed scenario analysis by any major Indian gas utility. The hydrogen dimension is particularly important for India. Snam has heavily promoted hydrogen blending and hydrogen-ready infrastructure as a decarbonisation narrative — Carbon Tracker's analysis interrogates whether this is Paris-credible or a form of transition risk obfuscation. Indian gas majors and the government's National Green Hydrogen Mission (NGHM), which targets 5 MMTPA of green hydrogen by 2030, face the same credibility test. If green hydrogen at scale for gas network blending remains technically and commercially constrained (as Carbon Tracker argues for Europe), then the transition narrative underpinning Indian CGD network expansion capex requires much harder scrutiny — both from audit committees signing off on impairment assessments and from investors evaluating BRSR Core climate disclosures. For India's capital markets, the stranded asset risk embedded in gas network balance sheets connects directly to the RBI's climate risk framework (RBI Discussion Paper on Climate Risk, July 2022) and the expected incorporation of climate scenario analysis into bank credit risk assessments. Lenders to GAIL, IGL, Gujarat Gas, and Petronet LNG — SBI, HDFC Bank, Axis Bank — will increasingly be required to assess whether loan books to gas infrastructure are consistent with 1.5°C or 2°C transition pathways. The Carbon Tracker methodology is the international template for that assessment.

What To Do Now

  • CFOs at IGL, MGL, Gujarat Gas, GAIL, Petronet LNG: Commission an independent asset life stress-test of your gas network RAB and CWIP against IEA NZE 2050 and IEA STEPS scenarios — this is both an ISSB IFRS S2 compliance requirement under BRSR Core FY2024-25 and a proactive defence against activist investor or proxy advisor challenge; timeline: Q1 FY2025-26 at latest
  • Chief Sustainability Officers at BSE 500 gas and energy utilities: Map your Scope 1 methane fugitive emissions against GHG Protocol Oil and Gas Sector guidance and CDP 2024 Oil & Gas questionnaire requirements — quantify methane intensity ratios and set time-bound reduction targets before your next AGM season; connect this to SBTi Oil & Gas pathway if your company is under investor pressure to commit
  • Board Risk Committees at gas infrastructure companies: Add 'Paris alignment of gas network asset life and demand assumptions' as a standing agenda item under climate-related financial risk — require management to present scenario analysis results and compare against Carbon Tracker's Snam methodology; this is a governance best practice that proxy advisors (ISS, Glass Lewis) will increasingly score
  • Investor Relations teams at listed gas utilities: Proactively engage your top 10 institutional shareholders — particularly any ESG-screened funds or European asset managers with SFDR Article 8/9 mandates — to brief them on your climate scenario analysis work and your regulatory engagement strategy with PNGRB and MoPNG; do not wait for a Carbon Tracker-style report on your company to force reactive disclosure
  • Treasury and finance teams planning green bond or ECB issuance: Engage your international legal counsel and ESG rating advisors now to assess whether your gas infrastructure assets meet EU Green Bond Standard or Climate Bonds Initiative (CBI) certification thresholds — the Carbon Tracker methodology is being used by EU fund managers to challenge gas infrastructure green bond eligibility
  • Legal and compliance teams: Review impairment testing assumptions for gas network assets in your next annual accounts — auditors (Big Four, in particular) are beginning to challenge whether climate scenario analysis is adequately reflected in IND AS 36 impairment assessments; the Carbon Tracker report creates a public analytical benchmark that auditors can cite

Our Take

Carbon Tracker's Snam report is not primarily about Snam. It is the opening salvo in what will become a systematic, methodology-driven reassessment of gas infrastructure equity and debt valuations globally — and India's listed gas sector is squarely in the trajectory. The analytical tools Carbon Tracker deploys — Paris alignment scoring, asset life stress-testing, hydrogen transition credibility assessment, regulatory framework adequacy review — are the same tools that ISSB IFRS S2, BRSR Core, and increasingly RBI's climate risk guidelines are building into mandatory disclosure and credit assessment architecture. What is voluntary institutional investor engagement in Europe today becomes mandatory disclosure in India within 12-24 months. The boards of India's gas utilities face a compound governance challenge that is not yet fully visible in their boardrooms: they are mid-cycle on massive capex rollout (CGD rounds 9 and 10, GAIL's pipeline expansion, Petronet's LNG terminal capacity additions) based on demand assumptions and asset life conventions that have not been publicly stress-tested against India's own NDC commitments, let alone a 1.5°C scenario. When Indian institutional investors — LIC, EPFO, NPS trust funds — are required to incorporate climate risk into their asset allocation and stewardship frameworks (which RBI and SEBI are progressively mandating), the gas sector will face a repricing event unless companies have pre-emptively built credible, Paris-aligned transition narratives backed by quantified commitments. Watch for three developments in the next 24-36 months: first, a Carbon Tracker or IEEFA equivalent analysis targeting Indian CGD and gas transmission companies by name — GAIL's pipeline RAB and IGL's network asset life assumptions are analytically vulnerable on the same dimensions as Snam's; second, SEBI tightening BRSR Core to explicitly require asset-level climate scenario analysis for energy infrastructure companies, mirroring ISSB IFRS S2's sector-specific requirements; and third, EU CBAM-adjacent green finance criteria and supply chain due diligence requirements (CSDDD) creating a de facto Paris alignment test for any Indian gas sector company seeking European capital or operating in European supply chains. Companies that treat this as a future compliance problem rather than a present capital strategy problem will find themselves on the wrong side of a valuation gap that, once visible to markets, is very difficult to close.
