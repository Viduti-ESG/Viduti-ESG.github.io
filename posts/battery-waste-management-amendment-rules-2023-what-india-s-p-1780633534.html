Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change has significantly tightened the Battery Waste Management Rules by issuing the Battery Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2023, effective October 25, 2023. The amendments strengthen Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations, overhaul the EPR Certificate trading framework, mandate stricter registration and annual reporting requirements, and introduce a price-band mechanism for EPR Certificates — bringing India's battery waste governance closer to global best practice. All battery producers, importers, recyclers, and refurbishers operating in India must urgently review and update their compliance programmes to align with the revised rules.

What Changed

The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 provided the foundational framework for EPR in India's battery sector. The October 2023 amendment notification (S.O. 4669(E)) makes targeted but consequential changes across multiple rules. Here is what has materially changed: First, the definition of 'battery' has been broadened to explicitly include both disposable (primary) and rechargeable (secondary) batteries along with battery packs — closing definitional gaps that previously created compliance ambiguity for complex battery systems used in electric vehicles (EVs) and energy storage. Second, the scope of 'producer' has been expanded to include entities that import batteries or devices incorporating batteries, and those that manufacture or assemble batteries for sale under another producer's brand — ensuring that the EPR net captures contract manufacturers and importers who previously operated in a grey zone. Third, EPR obligations now explicitly extend to batteries kept for a producer's own use (captive consumption), not just batteries placed on the market. This has significant implications for industrial and telecom players operating large captive battery fleets. Fourth, the EPR Certificate (EPRC) framework has been substantially reformed: certificates will now be issued based on the weight of waste batteries processed or refurbished and the weight of battery material recovered, rather than a simpler unit-based count. Category-wise cross-use of EPRCs is prohibited — a certificate earned in one battery category cannot offset obligations in another. Critically, no EPRCs will be generated for recycling or refurbishing imported waste batteries under the Hazardous Waste Rules, 2016, and such activity must be separately accounted for and reported. Fifth, a regulated EPRC trading platform mechanism has been introduced. One or more electronic trading platforms may be established — through accredited agencies and with Central Government approval — governed by guidelines issued by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB). The CPCB will set minimum and maximum prices for EPRCs every six months, or more frequently as needed, factoring in collection costs and environmental compensation. Sixth, producers must now file an annual return in Form 1(C) with CPCB by June 30 each year for batteries manufactured, assembled, or imported in the preceding financial year. Recyclers and refurbishers must upload quarterly data on processed waste battery weights to the CPCB portal. Producers must also file Form 3 annual returns for pre-consumer waste batteries. Seventh, a sustainability production mandate has been introduced: producers must take measures for sustainable production of batteries or battery packs in accordance with guidelines to be issued by CPCB. Finally, producers that cease operations are not absolved of EPR obligations — they must continue to fulfil their EPR duties for batteries already placed on the market until operations fully wind down.

Who Is Affected

  • Battery manufacturers and assemblers — including those producing under third-party or OEM arrangements for other brands
  • Battery importers — including companies importing finished batteries, battery packs, or devices with embedded batteries (mobile phones, laptops, EVs, industrial equipment)
  • Electric vehicle (EV) manufacturers and EV fleet operators with captive battery use
  • Consumer electronics companies (smartphones, laptops, wearables, power tools)
  • Telecom tower operators and data centre operators with large captive UPS/backup battery fleets
  • Industrial equipment manufacturers using lead-acid or lithium-ion batteries
  • Energy storage system (ESS) developers and grid-scale battery storage project owners
  • Battery recyclers and refurbishers registered under the BWM Rules
  • Importers of waste batteries or scrap batteries under the Hazardous Waste Management Rules, 2016
  • EPR compliance intermediaries, Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs), and EPRC traders

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE (from October 25, 2023): The amended rules are effective from the date of Gazette publication. All producers, importers, recyclers, and refurbishers must treat the revised definitions, EPR scope, and EPRC rules as binding from this date.
  • REGISTRATION: Every producer (including importers and contract manufacturers newly brought within scope) must obtain EPR registration from CPCB through the centralised online portal using Form 1(A). CPCB will issue a registration certificate in Form 1(B) that remains valid until cancelled or withdrawn. Producers already registered must update their registration details to reflect any changes in business operations.
  • ANNUAL RETURN — BATTERIES PLACED ON MARKET: File Form 1(C) with CPCB by June 30 each year, covering batteries manufactured, assembled, or imported in the preceding financial year. The first filing under the amended rules covers FY 2023–24, due by June 30, 2024.
  • ANNUAL RETURN — PRE-CONSUMER WASTE: File Form 3 annually for pre-consumer waste batteries generated during manufacturing, assembly, or import of batteries or battery packs in the preceding financial year.
  • COLLECTION & RECYCLING/REFURBISHING TARGETS: Meet the collection, recycling, and refurbishing targets as specified in Schedule II of the BWM Rules, 2022, now extended to batteries kept for own use in addition to batteries placed on the market.
  • QUARTERLY DATA UPLOAD — RECYCLERS & REFURBISHERS: Upload total weight of waste batteries processed on a quarterly basis to the CPCB portal for EPRC generation. This is a continuous obligation from the date of notification.
  • EPRC USAGE RESTRICTION: Ensure EPR Certificates are used only for off-setting, carrying forward, or trading within the same battery category in which they were generated. Update internal compliance tracking systems to enforce category-wise EPRC accounting.
  • IMPORTED WASTE BATTERY ACCOUNTING: Maintain separate accounts and reporting for recycling or refurbishing of imported waste batteries (under Hazardous Waste Rules). No EPRCs will be generated for this activity.
  • SUSTAINABILITY PRODUCTION MEASURES: Monitor CPCB for forthcoming guidelines on sustainable battery production and prepare to implement measures upon notification.
  • OPERATIONAL CLOSURE OBLIGATION: If planning to wind down operations, ensure EPR obligations for batteries already on the market are fulfilled through the closure date — factor this into any M&A, restructuring, or exit planning.

Climate Transition Angle

The Battery Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2023 are not merely a compliance housekeeping exercise — they sit at the intersection of India's clean energy transition, circular economy ambitions, and growing global ESG disclosure expectations. India's EV and energy storage transition is accelerating rapidly. The FAME-II scheme, Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes for advanced chemistry cells, and state-level EV policies are collectively driving exponential growth in battery deployment. Without a robust end-of-life management system, this transition risks creating a new waste crisis — leaching toxic heavy metals (lead, cadmium, cobalt, lithium) into soil and groundwater, and releasing greenhouse gases from uncontrolled battery disposal. The 2023 amendment directly addresses this risk by tightening the EPR net and improving the traceability of waste battery flows. From a physical climate risk perspective, battery waste mismanagement has documented links to water contamination and biodiversity loss — both physical risks that are increasingly material to supply chain resilience and corporate liability. Companies with operations in flood-prone or water-stressed regions (increasingly common as climate impacts intensify) face amplified risks from improper battery storage and disposal. On global standards alignment, the International Sustainability Standards Board's IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — both of which are shaping expectations for Indian companies listed or operating globally — require disclosure of circular economy practices and waste management as part of value chain sustainability reporting. The EU's own Battery Regulation (Regulation 2023/1542), which mandates recycled content requirements, carbon footprints, and digital battery passports, is already affecting Indian battery exporters and suppliers to European OEMs. India's strengthened BWM Rules create a domestic regulatory floor that helps Indian companies build credibility in these global value chains. For investors applying ESG screens, the amended rules raise the bar on what constitutes adequate battery waste governance. Companies that cannot demonstrate EPRC compliance, recycling target achievement, and sustainable production practices will face increasing scrutiny from domestic and foreign institutional investors, particularly as SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) framework continues to evolve.

What To Do Now

  • CONDUCT AN EPR SCOPE AUDIT: Map all battery-related activities across your business — manufacturing, assembly, importation of batteries or battery-embedded devices, captive battery use, and pre-consumer waste generation. Determine whether your current EPR registration and obligations reflect the expanded definitions and scope under the amended rules. Pay particular attention to contract manufacturing arrangements and captive battery fleets that may now be newly in scope.
  • UPDATE REGISTRATION AND COMPLIANCE SYSTEMS: Log in to the CPCB centralised EPR portal to verify your registration is current, update any changed business information, and ensure your annual return filing system is configured to meet the June 30, 2024 deadline for Form 1(C). If you are a newly covered entity under the amended producer definition, initiate registration immediately.
  • RESTRUCTURE YOUR EPRC TRACKING AND TRADING STRATEGY: Review your existing EPRC inventory and trading arrangements in light of the new category-wise restriction — EPRCs can no longer be used across battery categories. Engage your compliance team and any PROs or intermediaries to re-engineer your EPRC procurement and offsetting strategy. Monitor CPCB for the announcement of authorised EPRC trading platforms and the first EPRC price band notification.
  • ENGAGE YOUR SUPPLY CHAIN: If you are a downstream user of batteries (EV manufacturer, electronics brand, industrial OEM), engage your battery suppliers to understand their EPR registration status and recycling target performance. Supplier EPR non-compliance creates reputational and legal exposure for your brand, and is increasingly a disclosure risk under BRSR and ISSB-aligned reporting frameworks.
  • PREPARE FOR SUSTAINABILITY PRODUCTION GUIDELINES: Proactively assess your battery or battery pack production processes against emerging sustainability criteria — energy efficiency, recycled material content, hazardous substance reduction. Engage with CPCB's consultation processes when guidelines are drafted, and begin internal capability-building so you are not caught flat-footed when the guidelines become mandatory.

Our Take

The Battery Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2023 represent a meaningful step-change in India's EPR governance maturity. The introduction of a regulated, price-banded EPRC trading mechanism — modelled loosely on the renewable energy certificate market — is particularly significant: it transforms EPR compliance from a pure administrative exercise into a market-based instrument that will, over time, incentivise investment in collection infrastructure and recycling capacity. The prohibition on cross-category EPRC use closes a loophole that had the potential to undermine the environmental integrity of the system. What to watch next: CPCB's implementation of the trading platform guidelines and the first EPRC price band will be critical stress tests of whether the market mechanism delivers real-world waste recovery improvements or remains a paper compliance system. We also expect CPCB to issue the sustainable production guidelines in 2024, which could introduce binding design-for-recyclability requirements that reshape product development timelines for battery manufacturers. Companies that treat these amendments as a minimum compliance threshold — rather than a ceiling — and invest early in closed-loop battery supply chains will be best positioned as India's battery economy scales toward the terawatt-hour era.
