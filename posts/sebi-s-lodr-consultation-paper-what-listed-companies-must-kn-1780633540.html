Executive Summary

In February 2023, SEBI released a landmark consultation paper proposing sweeping reforms to the Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements (LODR) Regulations, 2015 — the foundational rulebook governing what India's listed companies must disclose to markets and regulators. The proposals aim to reduce redundancy in filings, sharpen the quality of material disclosures, and significantly strengthen compliance mechanisms, including those tied to ESG and Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR). For sustainability and compliance officers, this signals a decisive regulatory shift: disclosure is no longer a checkbox exercise, but a core governance and investor-trust function.

What Changed

SEBI's consultation paper does not introduce a new regulation outright — rather, it proposes targeted amendments and clarifications to the existing LODR framework, inviting public comment before finalising rules. The paper addresses three broad problem areas that SEBI identified through market surveillance and stakeholder feedback. First, information overload: listed entities were filing large volumes of disclosures, many of which were duplicative, immaterial, or poorly structured — making it harder, not easier, for investors to extract meaningful signals. SEBI proposed rationalising these requirements by consolidating overlapping obligations and introducing clearer materiality thresholds for event-based disclosures. Second, compliance gaps: despite existing mandates, SEBI observed persistent delays, incomplete filings, and inconsistent application of LODR provisions — particularly among mid- and small-cap companies. The paper proposed stronger enforcement levers, including enhanced penalties, board-level accountability, and tighter timelines. Third, ESG and sustainability alignment: with BRSR already mandated for the top 1,000 listed companies by market capitalisation and BRSR Core (with assurance requirements) being phased in, the consultation paper sought to embed sustainability disclosures more firmly within the LODR architecture — giving them the same regulatory weight as financial disclosures rather than treating them as supplementary reports.

Who Is Affected

  • All companies listed on Indian stock exchanges (BSE, NSE) — including equity, debt, and SME-listed entities — are subject to LODR and will be affected by any amendments that follow this consultation.
  • Top 1,000 listed companies by market capitalisation face the most immediate impact, as they are already subject to mandatory BRSR and are likely to face enhanced scrutiny on sustainability disclosures under any revised framework.
  • Mid- and small-cap listed entities should take note: the paper signals SEBI's intent to close compliance gaps that have been more prevalent in this segment, suggesting stricter enforcement is coming.
  • Compliance officers, company secretaries, CFOs, and boards — particularly audit committees and ESG/sustainability committees — bear direct accountability under the proposed strengthened compliance architecture.
  • Institutional investors, mutual funds, and ESG-focused asset managers will benefit from improved disclosure quality and are watching this reform closely as it affects their stewardship and voting decisions.
  • Legal, audit, and advisory firms supporting listed entities on LODR compliance and BRSR reporting will need to update their service frameworks once amendments are finalised.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Monitor SEBI's official gazette and circulars for final amendments to LODR Regulations following the close of the consultation period — the paper was published February 20, 2023, and final rules are expected to be notified in subsequent months.
  • Review materiality policies: SEBI proposed clearer and more stringent thresholds for what constitutes a 'material' event requiring immediate disclosure. Companies should audit their existing materiality frameworks against proposed benchmarks and update board-approved policies accordingly.
  • Strengthen timelines for event-based disclosures: proposals include tightening the window for reporting material events from 24 hours to as little as 12 hours in certain categories. Compliance teams must ensure internal escalation protocols can meet accelerated deadlines.
  • Embed BRSR within the LODR compliance calendar: sustainability disclosures — including Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) under BRSR Core — are proposed to be treated as regulated disclosures with enforcement consequences for non-filing or misrepresentation.
  • Board and audit committee accountability: proposed amendments would require explicit board sign-off on the adequacy of disclosure controls, not just the content of individual filings. Boards should review and formalise their oversight of the disclosure function.
  • Prepare for third-party assurance: the phased rollout of BRSR Core assurance (mandatory for top 150 companies from FY2023-24, expanding thereafter) is closely linked to the LODR strengthening agenda. Companies not yet engaged with assurance providers should begin scoping now.
  • Engage with the consultation process: stakeholders were invited to submit comments on the paper's proposals. While the formal comment window has closed, companies can engage through industry associations (CII, FICCI, NASSCOM) to shape final rulemaking.

Climate Transition Angle

This consultation paper carries significant implications for how climate and sustainability risks are disclosed in India's capital markets — and how those disclosures connect to the global transition finance architecture. The proposed integration of BRSR more firmly into the LODR framework is a critical step toward making climate-related financial disclosures mandatory and enforceable rather than aspirational. BRSR's essential indicators already require companies to report on energy consumption, greenhouse gas emissions (Scope 1 and 2), water usage, and waste management. The strengthened compliance architecture proposed in this paper means these disclosures will face the same scrutiny as financial statements — and misrepresentations could carry regulatory consequences. From a global standards perspective, this reform positions India's disclosure regime more closely with the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) framework — specifically IFRS S1 (General Sustainability Disclosures) and IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) — and with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). As Indian companies with global supply chain relationships, foreign listings, or international investors face growing pressure to align with these standards, a stronger domestic LODR framework provides a regulatory foundation rather than leaving companies to self-navigate global expectations. Physical climate risks — including water stress, extreme heat, flooding, and supply chain disruption — are increasingly material events under any credible materiality framework. Tightened LODR materiality thresholds will compel companies in climate-exposed sectors (agriculture, real estate, infrastructure, utilities, textiles) to develop more robust processes for assessing and disclosing climate-related risks in real time, not just in annual reports. For transition risks, the proposed reforms arrive at a moment when India's energy transition — driven by the national target of 500 GW of non-fossil capacity by 2030 and net-zero by 2070 — is generating material shifts in the competitive landscape. Companies in carbon-intensive sectors that fail to disclose credible transition plans may find themselves flagged by both regulators and institutional investors applying ESG screening.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a LODR Disclosure Audit: Map all current disclosure obligations under LODR against SEBI's proposed reforms. Identify gaps in materiality determination, filing timeliness, and board-level sign-off processes — and prioritise remediation before final amendments are notified.
  • Integrate BRSR into Your Governance Structure: Elevate BRSR from an annual reporting exercise to a continuous disclosure function. Assign board-level ownership (ideally through an ESG or sustainability committee), establish quarterly data collection rhythms, and link KPI tracking to the same systems used for financial reporting.
  • Develop a Climate Materiality Assessment: Using TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) methodology as a guide, assess which physical and transition climate risks cross the threshold of material events under LODR. Document this assessment and ensure it is reviewed at least annually by the board.
  • Engage an Assurance Provider Early: If your company falls within the top 1,000 listed entities, begin scoping BRSR Core limited assurance now. Assurance-ready data requires clean, auditable ESG data infrastructure — and building that takes time. Don't wait for the mandate to force a rushed engagement.
  • Align Internal Reporting with ISSB/CSRD Standards: Even if full ISSB alignment is not yet required in India, begin mapping your BRSR disclosures to IFRS S1 and S2 indicators. This future-proofs your reporting for international investors and positions your company ahead of likely convergence between SEBI's framework and global standards.

Our Take

SEBI's LODR consultation paper is not a routine housekeeping exercise — it represents a structural shift in how India's capital markets regulator views the relationship between disclosure quality, corporate governance, and investor protection in the ESG era. By proposing to embed sustainability disclosures within the enforceable LODR architecture, SEBI is sending a clear message: climate and ESG information is material financial information, and it will be regulated accordingly. For companies that have treated BRSR as a compliance burden to be minimised, this is a wake-up call. The firms that will fare best are those that invest now in the data systems, governance structures, and assurance processes needed to make sustainability disclosure as rigorous as their financial reporting. With ISSB standards gaining global traction and India's own climate commitments accelerating, the direction of travel is unmistakable — and the window to get ahead of it, rather than react to it, is narrowing.
