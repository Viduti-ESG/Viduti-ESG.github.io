Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued a formal directive to the Central Board of Indirect Taxes & Customs (CBIC) clarifying the conditions under which imported consignments of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) can be released at Indian ports. Effective June 30, 2023, producers importing any of the 85 specified EEE items will need a valid EPR Registration Certificate from the CPCB's EPR Portal — or face held consignments. This notice closes a critical compliance window and signals zero tolerance for unregistered imports beyond the grace period.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, all producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) are required to register on CPCB's centralised EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) Portal and obtain an EPR Registration Certificate before importing their products into India. EPR is a policy principle that holds manufacturers and importers financially and operationally responsible for the end-of-life management of their products — in this case, ensuring that e-waste generated from their products is properly collected and recycled. In April 2023, the Ministry of Finance's Department of Revenue flagged a practical bottleneck: many producers had not yet completed their EPR registration, causing imported consignments to pile up at customs. CPCB had earlier, on April 13, 2023, offered a 30-day interim relief — allowing customs to release consignments if producers submitted a written undertaking committing to obtain EPR registration. The May 9, 2023 letter now supersedes and extends that interim arrangement. The key change is this: the 30-day undertaking window has been replaced with a single hard deadline of June 30, 2023. Producers who submitted undertakings to customs can use the period until June 30 — not just 30 days from the date of undertaking — to obtain and submit their EPR Registration Certificate. After June 30, 2023, no consignment will be released without a valid EPR Registration Certificate. Additionally, a separate relief is provided for producers of 21 older EEE items whose EPR Authorisation under the previous 2016 Rules has lapsed — they can get their goods released by showing proof of a fresh EPR Portal application, but again only until June 30, 2023.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers and importers of 85 EEE items classified under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — spanning Information Technology & Telecommunication Equipment (ITEW17–ITEW27), Consumer Electrical & Electronic Equipment (CEEW6–CEEW19), Large & Small Household Appliances (LSEEW1–LSEEW34), Electrical & Electronic Tools (EETW1–EETW8), Toys, Leisure & Sports Equipment (TLSEW1–TLSEW6), Medical Devices (MDW1–MDW10), and Lighting Equipment (LIW1–LIW2).
  • Producers and importers of 21 older EEE items (ITEW1–ITEW16 and CEEW1–CEEW5) whose Extended Producer Responsibility Authorisation (EPRA) under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2016 has already expired.
  • Electronics brands — both Indian and foreign — that source finished goods or components from overseas and clear them through Indian customs.
  • Contract manufacturers and authorised importers acting on behalf of EEE brand owners.
  • Compliance, supply chain, and legal teams at electronics, appliance, telecom equipment, medical device, and lighting companies operating in India.
  • Customs brokers and freight forwarders managing import clearances for EEE producers.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • DEADLINE — June 30, 2023: Hard cutoff for all interim relief arrangements. After this date, customs will only release EEE consignments upon presentation of a valid EPR Registration Certificate from the CPCB EPR Portal. No exceptions or extensions have been signalled.
  • IMMEDIATE ACTION: Producers who have already submitted an undertaking to customs must now prioritise completing their EPR registration on CPCB's EPR Portal (https://eprewaste.cpcb.gov.in) before June 30, 2023 — the clock runs from the date of undertaking submission, not from any fixed calendar date.
  • FOR 85 EEE ITEMS (Rules 2022): Submit the Bill of Entry along with a written undertaking to the relevant customs authority if EPR registration is still pending. Track your application status on the CPCB EPR Portal and ensure the Registration Certificate is submitted to customs before June 30, 2023.
  • FOR 21 EEE ITEMS (Rules 2016 — expired EPRA): Apply immediately on the CPCB EPR Portal under the 2022 Rules. Submit an acknowledgement of application along with a copy of the expired EPRA to customs to secure release of consignments — valid only until June 30, 2023.
  • COMPLIANCE TRACKING: CPCB is actively maintaining a register of all undertakings submitted to customs. Producers should be aware that non-compliance will be visible to regulators. Designate a single point of contact within your organisation for coordinating with customs and CPCB on compliance status.
  • POST JUNE 30: Ensure your EPR Registration Certificate is renewed annually and kept current. Any future import without a valid certificate will result in consignment holds with no interim relief available.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste is one of the fastest-growing solid waste streams globally, and India is the third-largest generator of electronic waste in the world, producing over 1.6 million metric tonnes annually (Global E-Waste Monitor, 2020). The enforcement of EPR registration for EEE imports is not merely a bureaucratic compliance exercise — it is a foundational building block of India's circular economy strategy and its broader climate transition agenda. From a physical risk perspective, improper e-waste disposal leads to toxic leaching into soil and groundwater, compounding the environmental vulnerabilities that climate change is already intensifying — particularly in water-stressed regions of India. By holding producers accountable for the end-of-life of their products, EPR regulations create financial incentives to design for durability, repairability, and recyclability, which directly reduces the carbon footprint embedded in manufacturing new electronics. For companies aligned with or preparing for global sustainability disclosure frameworks — including the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) standards (IFRS S1 and S2) or the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — EPR compliance in key markets like India is increasingly a supply chain due diligence requirement. Global institutional investors and European customers are scrutinising whether Indian suppliers have robust waste management obligations in place. Failure to maintain EPR registration is therefore not just a regulatory risk; it is a reputational and ESG rating risk. Companies that treat EPR compliance as a strategic priority — rather than a last-minute paperwork exercise — are better positioned to demonstrate responsible value chain stewardship to global stakeholders.

What To Do Now

  • REGISTER IMMEDIATELY: If your company has not yet registered on the CPCB EPR Portal for e-waste (https://eprewaste.cpcb.gov.in), initiate the process today. Gather required documents — company registration, import records, product category details — and submit your application well before June 30, 2023 to allow processing time.
  • AUDIT YOUR IMPORT PIPELINE: Work with your customs broker and supply chain team to identify all pending or upcoming EEE consignments that may be held at ports. Cross-check which product categories fall under the 85 or 21 EEE item lists and confirm the EPR status for each.
  • SUBMIT UNDERTAKINGS WHERE NEEDED: For any consignment currently held at customs, submit the Bill of Entry and written undertaking immediately to secure interim release. Retain proof of submission, as CPCB is tracking these undertakings and will follow up on compliance.
  • ASSIGN OWNERSHIP AND SET INTERNAL DEADLINES: Designate a specific compliance officer or team responsible for EPR registration and customs coordination. Set an internal deadline of June 15, 2023 — two weeks before the regulatory deadline — to confirm EPR Certificate receipt and submission to customs, creating a buffer for any procedural delays.
  • BUILD EPR INTO YOUR ANNUAL COMPLIANCE CALENDAR: Beyond the immediate crisis, integrate EPR registration renewal, annual e-waste collection target reporting, and producer liability audits into your standing compliance framework. Consider engaging a certified EPR facilitator or recycling channel partner to meet your annual collection targets under the 2022 Rules.

Our Take

This CPCB notice is deceptively short in length but enormous in implication. The fact that India's apex pollution control board had to write to the Ministry of Finance to intervene in customs clearances reveals just how operationally consequential EPR non-compliance has become — this is no longer a back-office environmental checkbox. The June 30, 2023 deadline is firm, and given CPCB's stated intent to track undertakings against actual EPR registrations, companies that miss this window should expect scrutiny that goes beyond held shipments. More broadly, this notice is a clear signal of the direction of travel: India is systematically tightening the linkage between import privileges and producer responsibility obligations. With the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 expanding the scope of covered products and introducing steeper collection targets compared to the 2016 Rules, EPR in India is maturing from a voluntary gesture to a hard market-access condition. Business leaders should view investment in EPR systems — collection infrastructure, recycler partnerships, digital compliance tracking — not as a cost, but as a strategic moat that protects supply chain continuity and strengthens ESG credentials in an increasingly scrutinised global marketplace.
