SASB Oil & Gas Standards Revision: What Carbon Tracker's ISSB Submission Means for India's Upstream, Midstream, and Integrated Energy Majors

Carbon Tracker, the London-based climate finance think tank, has formally submitted its response to the ISSB's exposure draft on SASB Standards for Oil and Gas — a development that signals the imminent tightening of sector-specific disclosure requirements for upstream, integrated, and midstream oil

Executive Summary

Carbon Tracker, the London-based climate finance think tank, has formally submitted its response to the ISSB's exposure draft on SASB Standards for Oil and Gas — a development that signals the imminent tightening of sector-specific disclosure requirements for upstream, integrated, and midstream oil and gas companies under the global IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards architecture. For Indian companies — ONGC, Oil India, Reliance Industries, BPCL, HPCL, and their listed subsidiaries — this is not a distant global standard: SEBI's BRSR Core framework already references ISSB alignment as its stated direction of travel, and any hardening of SASB oil and gas metrics will cascade directly into what Indian ESG auditors, institutional investors, and eventually SEBI will demand. The core implication is this: carbon intensity metrics, methane emissions disclosures, stranded asset risk quantification, and climate scenario analysis for oil and gas assets are moving from voluntary best practice to verifiable, investor-grade disclosure obligations — and Indian energy majors are structurally underprepared. Carbon Tracker's submission, published 26 November 2025 and available at https://carbontracker.org/reports/sasb-standards-on-oil-and-gas/, sets out the think tank's priority recommendations to the ISSB on strengthening the SASB oil and gas standards, with particular emphasis on closing reporting gaps that currently allow companies to obscure true carbon intensity of their portfolios.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker (carbontracker.org) confirms that the ISSB has issued an exposure draft proposing revisions to the SASB Standards specifically for the oil and gas sector — covering Exploration & Production, Midstream, Refining & Marketing, and Oil & Gas Services sub-sectors. Carbon Tracker's formal submission to this consultation sets out detailed recommendations on what the revised standards must capture to give capital markets a true picture of transition risk embedded in oil and gas assets. The SASB standards, now under ISSB stewardship since the consolidation of SASB into the IFRS Foundation in 2022, function as the industry-specific 'topical' layer beneath the cross-cutting IFRS S1 (general sustainability disclosures) and IFRS S2 (climate disclosures) standards. For oil and gas specifically, SASB metrics include Scope 1 and 2 GHG emissions intensity, methane emissions as a percentage of natural gas throughput, volume of produced water, hydrocarbon spills, and proven and probable reserves by resource type — metrics that directly feed into investors' assessment of stranded asset exposure. What Carbon Tracker is pushing for — based on their broader body of research — is a material strengthening of: (1) carbon intensity reporting across the full asset lifecycle, not just operational emissions; (2) disclosure of capex allocation toward high-cost, high-carbon reserves that are inconsistent with 1.5°C pathways; (3) explicit scenario analysis linking reserve portfolios to IEA Net Zero Emissions by 2050 and Announced Pledges Scenario outcomes; and (4) methane intensity disclosures aligned with the Global Methane Pledge thresholds. This matters enormously for India because SEBI's BRSR Core — mandatory for the top 150 listed companies by market capitalisation from FY2023-24, expanding to the top 250 from FY2024-25 — has been explicitly designed by SEBI to converge with ISSB standards over time. SEBI's Consultation Paper of July 2023 and the subsequent BRSR Core notification are unambiguous on this convergence intent. When ISSB tightens SASB oil and gas metrics, BRSR Core's 'Key Performance Indicators' for energy-intensive sectors will follow — and ONGC, Reliance, and BPCL will be first in the audit committee's line of fire. Beyond BRSR, Indian oil and gas companies with international bond issuances, GDR listings, or foreign institutional investor (FII) ownership above threshold levels are already subject to investor stewardship expectations aligned with ISSB S1/S2. Institutional investors including Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, BlackRock, and the major UK and European asset managers that hold significant positions in Indian energy sector equities are beginning to use SASB oil and gas metrics as a baseline for engagement letters and proxy voting decisions. A revision that raises the floor on what must be disclosed — particularly on methane, reserve carbon intensity, and capex alignment — will translate into direct investor pressure on Indian energy boards within 12-18 months of final standard publication.

Who Is Affected

  • Upstream oil and gas producers: ONGC and Oil India — both BRSR Core-mandated disclosers — whose reserve portfolios and methane emission intensities from ageing fields in Assam, Rajasthan, and offshore basins will face new scrutiny under tightened SASB E&P metrics
  • Integrated energy majors: Reliance Industries (refining, petrochemicals, upstream JVs) and GAIL (midstream gas transmission) — subject to SASB Midstream and Refining & Marketing sub-sector standards simultaneously
  • Public sector oil marketing companies: BPCL and HPCL, whose refining Scope 1 intensity and transition capex allocation between fossil and clean energy assets will be a focal point of revised SASB Refining standards
  • Listed oil field services companies: companies such as Deep Industries, Hindustan Oil Exploration (HOEC), and Selan Exploration, which fall under SASB Oil & Gas Services and are increasingly in the scope of supply chain due diligence by larger operators
  • CFOs and treasury teams at Indian oil and gas companies with outstanding green bonds, sustainability-linked bonds, or syndicated loans with ESG-linked covenants — where SASB metric deterioration triggers ratchet clauses or covenant breaches
  • Chief Sustainability Officers and BRSR reporting teams at BSE 500 energy sector companies who must anticipate ISSB-aligned metric additions in the next SEBI BRSR revision cycle
  • ESG analysts and portfolio managers at domestic mutual funds (SBI MF, HDFC MF, Nippon India MF) and FII desks with energy sector exposure, who use SASB metrics as inputs to proprietary ESG scoring models
  • Audit committees and statutory auditors (Big Four and Grant Thornton India) engaged in BRSR Core 'reasonable assurance' engagements, who will need to update their audit procedures to reflect any expanded SASB oil and gas metric set

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • ISSB Exposure Draft on SASB Oil & Gas Standards — consultation period open as of November 2025; final revised standards expected to be published within 12-18 months of consultation close, likely mid-to-late 2026; companies should track IFRS Foundation workplan at ifrs.org for precise timeline
  • BRSR Core reasonable assurance — mandatory for top 150 NSE/BSE-listed companies by market cap from FY2023-24 reporting; expanding to top 250 companies from FY2024-25; energy sector companies in both cohorts must ensure their ESG KPIs are auditable and consistent with ISSB-aligned metrics as SEBI updates the BRSR framework
  • SEBI BRSR framework update — SEBI has signalled intent to revise BRSR KPIs in line with ISSB S1/S2 adoption trajectory; Indian oil and gas companies should anticipate a SEBI circular or consultation paper on sector-specific BRSR enhancements within 18-24 months, likely referencing updated SASB oil and gas metrics directly
  • Methane disclosure alignment — companies participating in India's upstream gas sector should note that the Global Methane Pledge (to which India is not a signatory but which influences investor expectations) sets a 30% methane reduction target by 2030 from 2020 levels; SASB revised standards are expected to sharpen methane intensity metric definitions to align with this benchmark
  • Stranded asset scenario analysis — IFRS S2 paragraph 22 already requires climate-related scenario analysis; revised SASB oil and gas standards will likely mandate specific IEA scenario references (NZE 2050, APS) for reserve-level analysis; Indian companies with IND AS-compliant financial statements must assess whether impairment disclosures under IND AS 36 are consistent with disclosed climate scenarios — a gap that statutory auditors are beginning to probe
  • EU CBAM and CSRD supply chain obligations — Indian oil and gas product exporters (particularly petroleum coke, LPG, naphtha) supplying EU-domiciled buyers face indirect exposure to CSRD value chain disclosure requirements and CBAM carbon pricing from 2026 full implementation; SASB metric alignment strengthens Indian exporters' ability to respond to EU customer data requests

Climate Transition Angle

The revision of SASB oil and gas standards sits at the precise intersection of physical climate risk, energy transition capital allocation, and financial materiality — and for India, the stakes are asymmetric and underappreciated. India's oil and gas sector faces a dual transition pressure: domestically, the government's push for energy security through domestic upstream development (OALP bid rounds, deep-water exploration) sits in tension with the capital market's growing demand for credible transition plans; externally, India's refining exports — particularly from Reliance's Jamnagar complex, the world's largest refining hub — face increasing carbon intensity scrutiny from European and North American buyers operating under CSRD value chain disclosure obligations. Carbon Tracker's research trajectory — as evidenced by their concurrent work on 'Oil Companies in Disguise' (published June 2026, examining how automaker investments mask fossil fuel carbon intensity) — signals that the think tank is building an evidence base for systemic under-reporting of carbon intensity across fossil fuel value chains. Applied to the SASB oil and gas context, this means pressure for metrics that capture embedded carbon in products sold (Scope 3 Category 11 — use of sold products), not just operational emissions. For ONGC and Reliance, Scope 3 Category 11 dwarfs Scope 1 and 2 combined, and its inclusion in mandatory SASB disclosures would fundamentally alter how institutional investors price transition risk in these stocks. India's own carbon market — the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) notified by MoEFCC in June 2023, and the PAT Scheme under BEE for energy-intensive sectors — creates a domestic price signal for carbon that, while nascent, is beginning to interact with global disclosure frameworks. As SASB oil and gas metrics tighten under ISSB oversight, the carbon intensity numbers that Indian companies report under BRSR Core will increasingly be used as inputs to both domestic CCTS baseline-setting and international investor ESG scoring — making accurate, assured, and ISSB-consistent reporting a financial asset, not merely a compliance cost. Companies that get ahead of the curve on methane intensity measurement, carbon intensity per barrel of oil equivalent, and capex alignment with transition scenarios will find access to green finance, sustainability-linked loans, and international capital markets materially easier than peers who treat SASB as a box-ticking exercise.

What To Do Now

  • BRSR and sustainability reporting teams (immediate — within 30 days): Download and review Carbon Tracker's full submission to the ISSB SASB Oil & Gas exposure draft at https://carbontracker.org/reports/sasb-standards-on-oil-and-gas/ and map Carbon Tracker's priority recommendations against your current BRSR Core KPI disclosures to identify gaps — particularly on methane intensity, reserve carbon intensity, and transition capex ratios
  • CFO and treasury (within 60 days): Commission a gap analysis comparing current BRSR Core energy and emissions disclosures against the full SASB Exploration & Production, Midstream, or Refining & Marketing standards (as applicable) — this analysis should also assess consistency between climate scenario disclosures and IND AS 36 impairment assumptions, a growing area of statutory audit scrutiny
  • Chief Sustainability Officer (within 90 days): Initiate a Scope 3 Category 11 (use of sold products) quantification exercise if not already underway — given Carbon Tracker's clear direction of advocacy, ISSB will face pressure to include Scope 3 in revised SASB oil and gas standards; being prepared with a credible Scope 3 inventory positions the company ahead of the next BRSR revision cycle and strengthens responses to FII stewardship questionnaires
  • Board Risk Committee (next scheduled meeting): Add 'SASB Oil & Gas Standards Revision and BRSR Convergence Risk' as a standing agenda item; request management to provide a 12-month regulatory watch brief covering ISSB exposure draft finalisation, anticipated SEBI BRSR update, and implications for the company's sustainability-linked financing covenants
  • Investor Relations (within 45 days): Brief key institutional shareholders — particularly FIIs and ESG-mandated domestic funds — on the company's current SASB metric coverage and the roadmap for alignment with revised standards; proactive IR on SASB convergence reduces the risk of being caught off-guard by escalated engagement letters or negative ESG score revisions when the final standards are published
  • Legal and compliance (within 60 days): Review all sustainability-linked bond documentation, ESG-linked loan covenants, and green finance frameworks for references to SASB metrics or ISSB standards as KPI anchors — identify any covenant language that could be triggered by metric redefinition under revised SASB standards and engage legal counsel on renegotiation options if necessary

Our Take

Carbon Tracker's engagement with the ISSB on SASB oil and gas standards is not an isolated advocacy exercise — it is part of a coordinated, multi-year effort by the climate finance community to close the credibility gap between what oil and gas companies report and what their actual transition risk exposure looks like to a sophisticated capital markets investor. The pattern is now well established: Carbon Tracker publishes the analytical case for tighter disclosure (as they have done with their 'Oil Companies in Disguise' series on automotive-fossil fuel linkages), submits formal technical input to standard-setters, and then watches as those recommendations migrate — with a 2-4 year lag — into mandatory disclosure frameworks. Indian boards that treat this as a European or North American problem are making a strategic error of the first order. The trajectory for India is clear and accelerating. SEBI has already signalled ISSB convergence as BRSR's long-term destination. The MoEFCC-led CCTS will require carbon intensity baselines that overlap substantially with SASB oil and gas metrics. India's largest institutional investors — LIC, SBI Life, the National Pension System — are under growing pressure from IRDAI and PFRDA to integrate climate risk into portfolio management, which means they will begin demanding SASB-aligned disclosures from portfolio companies in the energy sector within the next 24-36 months. And as EU CSRD value chain obligations bite, Reliance's refining customers in Europe will begin passing SASB-derived data requests down the supply chain as early as 2026-27. The companies that will navigate this environment successfully are those that treat the SASB oil and gas revision not as a compliance burden but as a strategic disclosure opportunity: an invitation to tell a credible, investor-grade story about how they are managing the energy transition. For ONGC, that story must include a credible methane abatement roadmap and a candid assessment of which fields remain economic under a 2°C scenario. For Reliance, it requires a clear capex narrative distinguishing between high-carbon refining expansion and the new energy transition investments the market already knows are underway. The standard is tightening. The question is whether Indian energy majors lead the disclosure curve or are dragged across it.
