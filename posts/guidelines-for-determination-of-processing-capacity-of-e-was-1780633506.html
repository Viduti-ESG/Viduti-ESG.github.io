Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued new guidelines directing State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) and Pollution Control Committees (PCCs) on how to standardise the determination of processing capacity of e-waste recycling facilities. This move is designed to bring uniformity and credibility to capacity assessments across India's fragmented e-waste recycling ecosystem — a critical input for the functioning of the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework. Companies registered under EPR obligations for electronics and electrical equipment must pay close attention, as recycler capacity directly governs the availability and validity of EPR certificates they rely upon for compliance.

What Changed

Under India's E-Waste Management Rules, producers of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) are required to meet annual EPR targets by channelling end-of-life products to registered recyclers and obtaining EPR certificates as proof of fulfilment. However, a persistent challenge has been the inconsistency with which different SPCBs and PCCs assess and certify the 'processing capacity' of recycling facilities — leading to over-reporting, certificate fraud, and mismatches between actual recycling throughput and claimed capacity. The CPCB's new guidelines establish a standardised methodology for SPCBs and PCCs to determine, verify, and formally approve the processing capacity of e-waste recyclers. This means capacity figures will now be based on defined parameters — likely including installed machinery throughput, operational hours, input material handling capability, and environmental compliance status — rather than self-reported or inconsistently audited numbers. The net effect is a tightening of the supply side of EPR certificates, raising the bar for what qualifies as a legitimate recycling facility.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers and importers of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) with EPR obligations under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022
  • E-waste recyclers and dismantlers registered with SPCBs/PCCs seeking capacity authorisation or renewal
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) facilitating EPR certificate procurement on behalf of producers
  • Brand owners in consumer electronics, IT hardware, telecom equipment, large and small home appliances, and lighting sectors
  • E-commerce platforms and retailers with EPR take-back obligations
  • State Pollution Control Boards and Pollution Control Committees responsible for authorising and auditing recycling facilities
  • Investors and lenders with exposure to e-waste recycling infrastructure or EPR-linked businesses

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • SPCBs/PCCs must adopt CPCB's standardised methodology when evaluating and approving processing capacity claims of e-waste recycling facilities — new applications and renewals must comply with the updated guidelines
  • Recycling facilities must ensure their capacity assessments are aligned with the new parameters — any mismatch between authorised capacity and actual throughput may result in suspension or cancellation of authorisation
  • Producers and PROs should audit their existing EPR certificate portfolios to confirm they are sourced from recyclers whose capacity has been validated under the updated guidelines
  • EPR-registered producers must verify that recyclers in their supply chain proactively seek re-assessment from the relevant SPCB/PCC if their previous capacity authorisation was based on older, non-standardised methodology
  • Compliance teams should monitor their SPCB/PCC portals and CPCB's centralised EPR portal for updated recycler capacity listings and any notifications requiring re-submission of capacity documentation
  • No specific deadline has been published with this notice — however, given the 'Urgent Notice' classification, immediate review and engagement with registered recyclers is strongly advised ahead of the next annual EPR target cycle

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally, and its responsible management sits at the intersection of circular economy goals, toxic material containment, and critical mineral recovery — all of which are material to India's climate transition narrative. Devices contain recoverable quantities of gold, copper, cobalt, and lithium, materials essential for the clean energy transition (batteries, EVs, solar infrastructure). Informal or under-capacity recycling not only creates environmental and health hazards but also forfeits the opportunity to recover these transition-critical materials domestically. From a global standards perspective, the ISSB's IFRS S2 (climate-related disclosures) and the EU's CSRD both require companies to assess and disclose risks and opportunities across their value chains — including end-of-life management of products. Indian companies exporting to the EU or listed on global exchanges will increasingly face questions about the integrity of their EPR compliance and the environmental standards of their recycling partners. Standardised capacity determination by SPCBs/PCCs directly strengthens the evidential basis for such disclosures. Furthermore, as India develops its own sustainability reporting standards (aligned with ISSB), credible EPR data will become a key input for Scope 3 emissions and waste-related metrics under emerging disclosure frameworks.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your recycler network immediately: Contact all registered e-waste recyclers in your EPR fulfilment chain and confirm whether their capacity authorisations have been — or will be — reviewed under the new CPCB guidelines. Do not wait for your annual EPR reconciliation to discover a capacity shortfall.
  • Review your EPR certificate inventory: Cross-check the volume of EPR certificates held against the verified processing capacities of issuing recyclers. Certificates linked to recyclers with inflated or unverified capacity claims may face scrutiny from CPCB during target verification.
  • Engage your PRO proactively: If you use a Producer Responsibility Organisation, request a written confirmation that their recycler panel is being updated to reflect the revised capacity standards, and ask for documentation of re-assessments as they occur.
  • Build capacity risk into your EPR procurement strategy: Given that the new guidelines may reduce the effective certified capacity available in the market in the short term, begin planning for EPR certificate procurement earlier in the compliance year — do not rely on last-quarter sourcing.
  • Update internal ESG disclosures and due diligence processes: Incorporate recycler capacity verification as a formal step in your EPR compliance SOP, and flag this regulatory development in your next sustainability report or ESG data submission as evidence of strengthened supply chain oversight.

Our Take

This CPCB guideline is a quiet but consequential intervention in India's EPR ecosystem. For years, the credibility of e-waste EPR compliance has been undermined by the opacity around recycler capacity figures — creating a market where certificate availability did not always reflect genuine recycling activity. By standardising how SPCBs and PCCs determine processing capacity, CPCB is effectively cleaning up the supply side of the EPR certificate market, which should — over time — reduce fraud, increase actual recycling rates, and make EPR data more defensible to international scrutiny. The short-term disruption is real: producers and PROs may find that previously reliable recycling partners now hold lower certified capacities, tightening certificate supply. But this is the necessary medicine for a system that needs credibility more than it needs convenience. We expect this to be a precursor to broader digitalisation and real-time monitoring of recycler throughput on the CPCB EPR portal — and companies that build robust, verified recycler relationships now will be far better positioned when that next layer of oversight arrives.
