Executive Summary

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) provides the globally recognized framework for companies to set credible, Paris Agreement-aligned emissions reduction targets — and demand from Indian corporates, investors, and supply chain partners to adopt SBTi commitments is accelerating rapidly. As ESG disclosure requirements tighten under SEBI's BRSR Core framework and global customers apply Scope 3 pressure across supply chains, obtaining SBTi validation is shifting from a voluntary differentiator to a commercial and regulatory necessity. Indian companies that act now will be better positioned to access green finance, retain global customers, and meet emerging mandatory climate disclosure standards.

What Changed

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) is a partnership between CDP, the United Nations Global Compact (UNGC), World Resources Institute (WRI), and the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF). It defines and promotes best practices in science-based target setting, independently assesses and approves companies' targets, and is widely regarded as the gold standard for corporate climate commitments globally. SBTi offers a suite of services and standards that allow companies to commit to reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in line with climate science — specifically, pathways consistent with limiting global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels as required by the Paris Agreement. The SBTi Corporate Standard covers Scope 1 (direct emissions), Scope 2 (purchased energy), and increasingly Scope 3 (value chain) emissions, requiring companies to set near-term targets (5–10 years) and long-term net-zero targets (by 2050 at the latest). Recent updates to the SBTi framework include the release of the Net-Zero Standard, the FLAG (Forest, Land and Agriculture) Guidance for land-intensive sectors, and ongoing development of sector-specific pathways for steel, cement, chemicals, apparel, and financial institutions — all of which are highly relevant to India's industrial base. The SBTi also updated its Corporate Manual in 2023–2024, tightening criteria around Scope 3 coverage thresholds and the use of carbon credits, signaling a continued move toward greater rigor and transparency.

Who Is Affected

  • Large Indian exporters supplying to European, US, and UK markets where customers face CSRD or SEC climate disclosure obligations and are pushing Scope 3 targets down their supply chains
  • Listed companies subject to SEBI's BRSR Core mandatory ESG disclosures, particularly the top 150 listed entities by market capitalization from FY2024–25
  • Manufacturing, steel, cement, textiles, chemicals, and agribusiness companies covered by SBTi's sector-specific standards including FLAG guidance
  • Financial institutions — banks, NBFCs, and asset managers — as the SBTi Financial Institutions (SBTI-FI) standard requires financed emissions targets and portfolio alignment
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational corporations whose parent companies have existing SBTi commitments and require value chain alignment
  • SMEs increasingly being asked by large buyers, investors, or global reporting frameworks to demonstrate emissions reduction commitments consistent with science-based pathways
  • Companies seeking green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, or ESG-indexed investment, where SBTi validation provides credibility to climate claims

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Commitment Letter: Companies must submit a commitment letter to SBTi pledging to set science-based targets; once submitted, targets must be developed and submitted for validation within 24 months
  • Near-Term Targets: Companies must set targets covering at least 67% of Scope 1 and 2 emissions, and if Scope 3 exceeds 40% of total emissions, must set a Scope 3 target — targets must show emissions reductions consistent with a 1.5°C pathway
  • Net-Zero Target (SBTi Net-Zero Standard): Companies committing under the Net-Zero Standard must set both a near-term and a long-term net-zero target, with net-zero no later than 2050; residual emissions must be neutralized through permanent carbon removal
  • Annual Disclosure: Validated companies must disclose progress against their SBTi targets annually through CDP or equivalent public reporting platforms — non-disclosure can lead to removal from the SBTi's public Companies Taking Action list
  • Scope 3 Coverage Threshold: From 2024 onward, SBTi requires companies in most sectors to address Scope 3 emissions if they constitute more than 40% of total GHG footprint — most Indian manufacturers and retailers will exceed this threshold
  • FLAG Sector Deadline: Companies in forest, land, and agriculture sectors (including food & beverage, paper, palm oil) must align with FLAG guidance — SBTi has indicated these companies must include land-sector targets as part of their validated commitments
  • Financial Institutions: Banks and asset managers must set portfolio-level financed emissions targets under the SBTi-FI framework, covering lending and investment portfolios aligned with sectoral decarbonization approaches (SDA)
  • Carbon Credit Restriction: As of updated SBTi guidance, carbon offsets cannot be used to achieve near-term science-based targets — only genuine in-house or value chain emissions reductions count toward SBTi compliance

Climate Transition Angle

SBTi sits at the intersection of physical climate risk management and transition risk strategy. Companies that set science-based targets are, by definition, building a roadmap to decarbonize their operations and value chains — which directly reduces their exposure to transition risks such as carbon pricing, stranded asset risk, and loss of market access as low-carbon product standards tighten globally. From a supply chain perspective, the ripple effect of SBTi adoption is profound. European companies subject to the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) are legally required to disclose and manage their Scope 3 emissions — which means they will increasingly demand that Indian suppliers either have SBTi-validated targets or provide verified emissions data that allows the buyer to calculate their own Scope 3 footprint. Indian exporters who cannot demonstrate credible decarbonization plans risk being delisted from global supply chains. The alignment with ISSB standards (IFRS S2 — Climate-related Disclosures) is equally significant. ISSB S2 requires companies to disclose their transition plans, climate targets, and progress — and an SBTi-validated target is widely accepted as evidence of a credible, science-aligned transition plan. SEBI's BRSR Core framework is itself progressively converging with ISSB standards, meaning SBTi adoption today will reduce compliance burden tomorrow. India's domestic climate policy trajectory — including the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) under the Energy Conservation (Amendment) Act 2022 — further reinforces the need for companies to establish a GHG baseline and reduction pathway now.

What To Do Now

  • Establish Your GHG Baseline: Before committing to SBTi, companies must complete a robust GHG inventory across Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions using the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard — engage a qualified third party to conduct or verify this inventory, as SBTi validation requires credible underlying data
  • Submit Your SBTi Commitment Letter: Register on the SBTi portal and submit a commitment letter to formally signal your intent — this starts the 24-month clock and publicly demonstrates climate ambition to investors, customers, and regulators
  • Identify Applicable SBTi Pathway and Sector Standard: Determine which SBTi sector-specific standard applies to your business (Corporate Standard, FLAG, Financial Institutions, or a sector pathway for steel/cement/apparel) and understand the specific decarbonization trajectory and coverage requirements for your industry
  • Map Scope 3 Hotspots and Engage Your Supply Chain: Conduct a Scope 3 screening to identify the most material upstream and downstream emission categories — prioritize supplier engagement, procurement policy changes, and product design reviews that will be required to meet Scope 3 target thresholds
  • Integrate SBTi into Board-Level Climate Governance: Ensure your Board or a dedicated ESG/Risk Committee formally owns the SBTi commitment — link executive remuneration to emissions reduction milestones, and embed SBTi progress into your annual BRSR and investor ESG reporting to demonstrate accountability

Our Take

SBTi is no longer a nice-to-have badge for sustainability reports — it is rapidly becoming the baseline expectation for any Indian company with serious global ambitions, access to institutional capital, or exposure to European and US regulatory supply chain requirements. What makes the current moment particularly significant is the convergence of pressures: SEBI's BRSR Core is demanding verified climate data, CSRD is pushing Scope 3 accountability into Indian supply chains, the ISSB framework is being adopted by major trading partners, and India's own Carbon Credit Trading Scheme is creating domestic incentives for emissions accounting. Companies that invest in SBTi validation now are not just buying regulatory compliance — they are building the organizational capability in GHG accounting, transition planning, and stakeholder disclosure that will be table stakes for doing business by 2030. Our strong advice: treat SBTi not as a communications exercise but as a strategic transformation program, and start with the GHG inventory today.
