Executive Summary

Climate Action Tracker's latest analysis — published under the Systems Change Lab project and available at https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/by-the-numbers-the-climate-action-we-need-this-decade/ — delivers an unambiguous verdict: current global policies, including India's existing NDC commitments, will achieve less than 1% GHG emissions reduction by 2030 against 2023 baselines, against a scientifically required 42% cut to hold 1.5°C. With the next round of nationally determined contributions due by early 2025, India faces a structural fork in the road: submit a credible, sector-specific NDC that unlocks green capital and preserves export market access, or risk accelerating sovereign climate risk downgrades, carbon border exposure, and SBTi non-alignment penalties that are already beginning to cascade through institutional investor mandates. For Indian CFOs and CSOs, the immediate operational consequence is this: whatever sectoral targets India embeds in its 2025 NDC will become the regulatory scaffolding against which BRSR Core disclosures, BEE PAT cycle targets, CPCB compliance trajectories, and investor net zero alignment assessments are benchmarked over the next decade. This is not a distant policy event — it is the reference frame being set right now.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/by-the-numbers-the-climate-action-we-need-this-decade/), authored by a 14-member team including Sophie Boehm, Kelly Levin, and Joel Jaeger under the Systems Change Lab project, provides the most granular public breakdown yet of what 1.5°C-aligned NDCs must contain — sector by sector, with quantified 2030 and 2035 benchmarks. The key numbers: a 42% global GHG reduction by 2030 and 56% by 2035, both relative to 2023 levels. Today's full policy implementation trajectory delivers less than 1% by either milestone year. Even full implementation of current NDCs puts the world on a 2.6–2.8°C warming pathway. What this overturns is the comfortable narrative that incremental NDC upgrades and long-term net zero pledges are sufficient. The analysis makes explicit that without a 'quantum leap in ambition' in the 2025 NDC round — backed by timebound, quantitative, sector-specific targets for power, transport, land use, industry, and buildings — the 1.5°C window closes irreversibly before 2035. For India, whose NDC submitted in 2022 committed to 45% emissions intensity reduction by 2030 (against 2005 levels) and 50% cumulative electric power installed capacity from non-fossil sources by 2030, the 2025 round demands a material step-up: absolute economy-wide targets for 2035, sector-specific EV sales benchmarks, renewable energy deployment floors, and methane reduction timelines aligned with the Global Methane Pledge India has not yet formally endorsed. The India-specific regulatory consequence is direct: SEBI's BRSR Core framework, mandatory for the top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24 and expanding to the top 1,000 by FY2026-27, already requires Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG intensity disclosure with third-party assurance. ISSB IFRS S2 — which MCA's sustainability reporting roadmap is expected to reference — requires companies to disclose alignment with jurisdictional climate targets. If India's 2025 NDC raises the ambition bar with sector-specific 2035 targets, it automatically tightens the compliance and disclosure baseline for every listed Indian company operating in power, cement, steel, chemicals, automotive, and logistics sectors.

Who Is Affected

  • Power sector: IPPs, coal-dependent utilities, and RE developers — NTPC, Adani Green, Tata Power, JSW Energy — whose capex plans will be benchmarked against any 2035 power sector decarbonisation target in the new NDC
  • Hard-to-abate industries: Steel (Tata Steel, JSW Steel, SAIL), cement (UltraTech, ACC, Ambuja), and petrochemicals (Reliance, ONGC) facing SBTi Science-Based Target setting timelines and potential EU CBAM exposure if India's NDC fails to credibly reflect sectoral transition
  • Automotive OEMs and component suppliers: Maruti Suzuki, Mahindra, Tata Motors, Bosch India — EV sales benchmarks in the NDC will directly influence CAFE norms, PLI scheme continuity, and EU EV export market access
  • Export-oriented manufacturers in textiles, chemicals, leather, and agri-processing supplying EU buyers under CSRD/ESRS supply chain due diligence obligations, where India's NDC ambition signals sovereign transition credibility
  • CFOs and Heads of Treasury at BSE 500 companies accessing green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, or foreign institutional investment — NDC credibility is a direct input to ESG ratings (MSCI, Sustainalytics, S&P CSA) which drive FII allocation decisions
  • Chief Sustainability Officers and BRSR compliance leads responsible for FY2025-26 disclosures requiring Scope 3 ambition statements and transition plan outlines under evolving SEBI guidance
  • Board Risk Committees with fiduciary exposure to stranded asset risk in fossil fuel-linked balance sheets and climate litigation risk if transition planning is demonstrably misaligned with science
  • Institutional investors — domestic MFs, insurance funds under IRDAI's ESG disclosure framework, pension funds, and FIIs with Paris-aligned mandates — reassessing India sovereign and corporate climate risk premiums

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • India NDC 2025 submission: Due by early 2025 (UNFCCC deadline approximately February 2025) — MoEFCC and MoF coordination required; expected to include enhanced 2035 targets and sector-specific benchmarks following COP29 Baku signals
  • BRSR Core third-party assured GHG disclosures: Mandatory for top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24 (already in effect); expanding to top 1,000 companies by FY2026-27 — Scope 1 and Scope 2 intensity metrics must be reported with limited assurance
  • BEE PAT Cycle VIII targets (2023-2026): Designated Consumers in power, cement, steel, aluminium, fertiliser, pulp & paper, chlor-alkali, and textiles must meet specific energy consumption norms — NDC sectoral targets will inform Cycle IX design post-2026
  • EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM): Transition phase reporting live from October 2023; definitive pricing phase from January 2026 — Indian exporters of steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, and hydrogen must track embedded carbon against EU benchmarks now tied to NDC credibility signals
  • SBTi Corporate Net Zero Standard: Companies with SBTi commitments must submit near-term targets (42-46% Scope 1+2 reduction by 2030 from a base year) within 24 months of commitment — India's NDC 2035 target provides the jurisdictional reference baseline SBTi uses for FLAG and ISIC-aligned target validation
  • ISSB IFRS S2 adoption: MCA sustainability standards roadmap (expected consultation 2025) will align Indian reporting with ISSB S2 climate scenario analysis, transition plan, and physical risk disclosure requirements — NDC sector targets become the policy scenario anchor
  • SEBI ESG rating provider framework (SEBI circular CIR/2023): ESG Rating Providers must incorporate sovereign climate commitment credibility into methodology — a weak India NDC 2025 directly depresses Indian corporate ESG ratings through the sovereign anchor effect

Climate Transition Angle

The Climate Action Tracker analysis makes visible a structural problem Indian boards must internalise: the gap between current policy trajectories and 1.5°C is not a linear shortfall that time and incremental improvement will close. It is a compounding credibility deficit that, if unaddressed in the 2025 NDC round, will trigger a cascade of capital market consequences over the 2025-2030 window. Specifically, ISSB IFRS S2 requires companies to assess climate scenarios consistent with the goals of the Paris Agreement — if India's NDC signals a 2.6–2.8°C national pathway, multinational institutional investors using physical risk models (NGFS scenarios, IPCC AR6) will reprice Indian infrastructure, agriculture, and coastal real estate assets upward on physical risk and downward on transition readiness simultaneously. For Indian companies on SBTi pathways, the 2025 NDC round matters because SBTi's Country Pathway Tool and FLAG (Forest, Land, and Agriculture Guidance) use NDC sector benchmarks as policy context for target validation. A stronger NDC with explicit 2035 power sector and land-use targets would make it easier — not harder — for Indian steel, cement, and agri-business companies to set credible near-term SBTi targets without being penalised for sector-level ambiguity. Conversely, a weak NDC increases the probability that SBTi and CDP frameworks classify Indian sectoral pathways as misaligned, triggering disclosure flags that FIIs and ESG-screened funds use to underweight Indian equities. India's transition to a green industrial economy — via the PLI scheme for solar, battery storage, green hydrogen, and EV manufacturing — requires a credible NDC as its sovereign-level anchor to attract the USD 2.5 trillion in climate finance the country needs through 2030, per the International Energy Agency's India Energy Outlook.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Treasury (immediate, Q1 2025): Map your company's Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions intensity trajectory against both India's current 2022 NDC benchmarks AND the 1.5°C-aligned sector pathways quantified by Climate Action Tracker — identify the delta and stress-test your green bond covenants and SLL pricing step-downs against the more ambitious scenario
  • Chief Sustainability Officer (within 60 days): Conduct a sector-specific NDC scenario analysis: if India submits a 2025 NDC with explicit 2035 power, transport, or industry targets, what does that mean for your BEE PAT obligations in Cycle IX, your BRSR Core intensity targets, and your SBTi validation timeline? Present the gap analysis to the board risk committee before FY2024-25 annual report sign-off
  • Board Risk Committee (Q1 2025 board meeting): Commission a formal climate scenario analysis under ISSB IFRS S2 framework — use the NGFS 'Delayed Transition' and 'Net Zero 2050' scenarios with India-specific policy assumptions bracketed around the NDC 2025 outcome — this is both fiduciary prudence and pre-emptive preparation for MCA's forthcoming ISSB-aligned standards
  • Export-oriented businesses (steel, aluminium, chemicals, textiles) — Head of Regulatory Affairs: Engage immediately with CBAM reporting infrastructure — the current CBAM transition phase requires quarterly embedded carbon reports; model the cost impact under both a weak and strong India NDC scenario, since EU benchmark evolution is tied to member state NDC credibility assessments
  • IR and ESG Communications teams (before AGM season, April-May 2025): Proactively address the NDC ambition gap in investor engagement materials — institutional investors with Paris-aligned mandates will ask direct questions about your company's resilience to a 2.6°C versus 1.5°C policy pathway; have a quantified answer ready, not boilerplate language
  • Procurement and supply chain heads (within 90 days): Initiate Scope 3 Category 1 and Category 11 (use of sold products) baseline assessments — as India's NDC raises sectoral ambition, downstream and upstream supply chain emissions will become material BRSR Core disclosures and CSRD-driven buyer requirements simultaneously; the window to establish baselines before these become hard obligations is closing

Our Take

The 2025 NDC round is the single most consequential climate policy event of this decade for Indian business, and it is receiving a fraction of the boardroom attention it deserves. Here is what I expect to unfold over the next 12-36 months. First, India will submit an enhanced NDC in early 2025 that raises the absolute emissions reduction ambition modestly and includes sector-specific signals — particularly on renewable energy capacity, EV sales penetration, and potentially green hydrogen production targets — but will likely stop short of an absolute economy-wide 2035 GHG cap. This will be received by international climate finance bodies as 'improved but insufficient,' which will sustain India's 'Highly Insufficient' CAT rating and create a persistent sovereign climate credibility discount that institutional ESG frameworks will price into Indian corporate ratings. Second, the EU's CBAM definitive phase beginning January 2026 will accelerate a reckoning for Indian heavy industry: companies in steel, cement, and fertilisers that have not invested in transition credibility — measured partly through NDC alignment and SBTi commitments — will face compounding disadvantage in EU export markets just as domestic BEE PAT Cycle IX tightens energy norms post-2026. The companies that are already running 1.5°C scenario analysis, have third-party assured BRSR Core data, and have initiated SBTi target-setting will be able to demonstrate transition readiness to both regulators and capital providers. Those that have not will face the twin pressure of higher cost of capital and shrinking export market access simultaneously. Third, and most importantly for boards: MCA's sustainability reporting standards, expected for consultation in 2025 and aligned with ISSB IFRS S1/S2, will require Indian listed companies to disclose transition plans — which are, by definition, anchored to government climate policy. A company whose transition plan references India's NDC as its policy context will need that NDC to be credible, quantified, and sector-specific to withstand audit scrutiny and investor challenge. The boards and CFOs who are building their transition plans now, using the 1.5°C-aligned sector benchmarks that Climate Action Tracker has quantified, are not just doing good ESG — they are building audit-defensible, investor-grade climate disclosures ahead of a regulatory wave that will arrive whether India's NDC is bold or timid. The gap between action and ambition is not just a climate problem. For Indian business in 2025, it is a capital markets problem.
