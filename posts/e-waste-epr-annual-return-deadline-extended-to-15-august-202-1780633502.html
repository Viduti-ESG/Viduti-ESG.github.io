Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has granted a 46-day extension for the filing of Annual Returns on the E-Waste Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) Portal, moving the deadline from 30 June 2025 to 15 August 2025. This relief applies to all entities registered under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — including producers, importers, refurbishers, and recyclers. Companies should treat this extension not as a pause, but as a final window to ensure their EPR compliance data is accurate, complete, and audit-ready.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, all registered entities on the CPCB's E-Waste EPR Portal are required to submit Annual Returns documenting their e-waste generation, collection, channelisation, and recycling or refurbishment activities for the preceding financial year (FY 2024–25 in this cycle). The original filing deadline was 30 June 2025. On 30 June 2025 — the very day of the deadline — CPCB issued a notice to all registered entities extending this deadline by approximately six weeks to 15 August 2025. While CPCB has not publicly detailed the specific reasons for the extension in the available notice, such extensions are typically granted in response to portal technical issues, industry requests for additional time to reconcile data, or delays in EPR credit issuance and verification that affect accurate reporting. It is critical to understand that this is an extension, not an exemption. All reporting obligations remain fully intact. Entities that fail to file by 15 August 2025 remain subject to penalties, suspension of EPR certificates, and regulatory action under the Environment Protection Act, 1986.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE): Manufacturers and brand owners who place EEE products on the Indian market and hold EPR registration with CPCB.
  • Importers of EEE: Companies importing finished electronic goods, components, or sub-assemblies that fall under the scheduled categories of e-waste rules.
  • Refurbishers: Entities registered to refurbish used EEE and obligated to report volumes channelised through their facilities.
  • E-Waste Recyclers and Dismantlers: CPCB/State Pollution Control Board (SPCB)-authorised recyclers who must report quantities of e-waste received, processed, and EPR credits generated.
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs): PROs operating on behalf of producers must ensure the data they have consolidated and reported on behalf of their clients is submitted accurately.
  • Large Enterprises and SMEs alike: The obligation applies to all registered entities regardless of size — there is no SME carve-out under the E-Waste Rules.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • REVISED DEADLINE — 15 August 2025: File Annual Returns for FY 2024–25 on the CPCB E-Waste EPR Portal (https://ewasteeprbharat.nic.in) before 11:59 PM on this date. No further extensions should be assumed.
  • Producers: Report total EEE placed on the market (category-wise, in metric tonnes), EPR targets for the year, e-waste collected, and EPR certificates/credits redeemed against targets.
  • Recyclers & Dismantlers: Report total e-waste received (source-wise), quantities processed, EPR credits generated and transferred, and any residual waste disposal details.
  • Refurbishers: Report quantities of EEE received for refurbishment, refurbished units sold, and e-waste channelised to authorised recyclers.
  • PROs: Confirm reconciliation of EPR credits between producer clients and recycler partners; ensure all credits are logged and matched in the portal before filing.
  • Data Reconciliation Deadline (Internal): Companies should target completing internal data reconciliation by 1 August 2025 to allow two weeks for portal submission and troubleshooting.
  • Penalty Exposure: Non-compliance with Annual Return filing can attract penalties under the Environment Protection Act, 1986, and may result in suspension or cancellation of EPR registration, blocking future sales of EEE products in India.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste EPR compliance sits at the intersection of the circular economy and climate transition — two pillars that global investors and regulators are increasingly treating as inseparable. Electronics manufacturing is a resource-intensive sector with significant embedded carbon; responsible end-of-life management through authorised recycling directly reduces the need for virgin material extraction, cutting lifecycle emissions. From a physical risk perspective, the informal e-waste recycling sector — which EPR frameworks aim to displace — is concentrated in climate-vulnerable urban and peri-urban areas. Formalising the recycling chain through EPR not only reduces toxic exposure but builds supply chain resilience by securing a domestic source of recovered critical minerals (cobalt, lithium, copper, rare earths) essential for India's clean energy transition — particularly for EV batteries and solar components. On the disclosure front, international frameworks are rapidly converging on supply chain material traceability. The ISSB's IFRS S2 standard requires companies to disclose climate-related risks across their value chains, and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — which affects Indian subsidiaries and exporters to Europe — demands disclosure of circular economy metrics including waste and material flows. Robust EPR Annual Return data is the foundational layer for building these disclosures. Companies that invest now in clean, auditable EPR data will have a significant advantage when SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) Core disclosures on resource use and waste are further tightened, as is widely expected.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your EPR portal data immediately: Log in to the CPCB E-Waste EPR Portal today and verify that all FY 2024–25 transactions — EEE sales volumes, e-waste collection records, EPR credit purchases, and recycler certificates — are accurately entered and match your internal ERP or inventory records. Discrepancies now are far easier to resolve than post-filing.
  • Engage your PRO or recycler partners without delay: If you rely on a PRO or directly contracted recyclers to generate and transfer EPR credits, confirm with them in writing by 10 July 2025 that all credits for FY 2024–25 have been generated, verified, and transferred to your account on the portal. Credit shortfalls must be addressed before filing.
  • Assign a dedicated compliance owner and set an internal deadline of 1 August 2025: Do not wait until 14 August. Assign a named individual (typically the Head of EHS or Corporate Compliance) as accountable for the filing. Set an internal deadline of 1 August 2025 for a complete draft return to allow buffer time for corrections and approvals.
  • Document everything for future BRSR and ESG disclosures: As you compile Annual Return data, simultaneously extract the metrics you will need for BRSR Core reporting — total e-waste generated, percentage channelised to authorised recyclers, EPR target fulfilment rate. Build this into a standing ESG data management process, not a one-off compliance exercise.
  • Monitor CPCB portal advisories actively: Given that this extension was announced on the deadline date itself, ensure your compliance team has subscribed to CPCB notifications and checks the E-Waste EPR Portal notice board regularly. Any further guidance on filing formats, technical issues, or clarifications will appear there first.

Our Take

The last-minute extension of the E-Waste EPR Annual Return deadline is a practical relief, but it should not obscure the larger regulatory direction of travel. India's EPR framework for e-waste is maturing rapidly — CPCB has been steadily tightening enforcement, increasing scrutiny of EPR credit authenticity, and expanding the list of obligated product categories. The days of treating EPR compliance as a checkbox exercise are numbered. For companies with operations or exports touching the EU, the stakes are even higher: the European Battery Regulation and CSRD supply chain provisions are creating hard commercial incentives for traceable, certified end-of-life management. Indian producers and recyclers that build robust compliance infrastructure now — clean data, verified credits, auditable recycling chains — will be positioned not just to satisfy CPCB, but to meet the growing due diligence demands of global customers and institutional investors. Use the six weeks wisely.
