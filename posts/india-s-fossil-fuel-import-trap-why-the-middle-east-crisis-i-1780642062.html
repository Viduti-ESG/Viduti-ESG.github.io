India's Fossil Fuel Import Trap: Why the Middle East Crisis Is a BRSR Materiality Event, Not Just an Energy Policy Story

Climate Action Tracker's analysis by Dr. Nandini Das — India country lead — lays out a structural indictment of India's energy architecture: 85–90% crude oil import dependence, 45–50% fossil gas import exposure, and over 240 million tonnes of annual coal imports sit at the heart of every macro shock

Executive Summary

Climate Action Tracker's analysis by Dr. Nandini Das — India country lead — lays out a structural indictment of India's energy architecture: 85–90% crude oil import dependence, 45–50% fossil gas import exposure, and over 240 million tonnes of annual coal imports sit at the heart of every macro shock India has absorbed since 2003. For Indian CFOs and CSOs, this is not geopolitical noise — it is a BRSR Core materiality disclosure obligation, an ISSB IFRS S2 physical and transition risk event, and a direct threat to operating cost predictability across energy-intensive sectors. The renewable acceleration underway is real, but the simultaneous expansion of gas infrastructure risks locking India into a second fossil dependency trap — one that is arguably harder to exit than coal. The full analysis is available at: https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/breaking-the-cycle-of-energy-shocks-indias-renewable-opportunity/

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (CAT), published at https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/breaking-the-cycle-of-energy-shocks-indias-renewable-opportunity/, frames the current Middle East crisis — which is disrupting approximately 20% of global energy supply — not as a temporary geopolitical disruption but as the latest iteration of a structurally recurring vulnerability in India's energy system. CAT's Dr. Nandini Das documents that fossil fuels constitute ~75% of India's primary energy, with import dependence spanning all three major fuels: crude oil (85–90% imported), fossil gas (45–50% imported), and high-grade coal (significant import share on top of one billion tonnes of domestic production). The analytical shift here is important: CAT is characterising coal's expanded utilisation not as a transition-era compromise but as a 'shock absorber' that provides short-term resilience while entrenching long-term vulnerability — a framing that has direct implications for how Indian companies classify and disclose energy-related financial risks. For Indian listed companies, this reframing has immediate regulatory consequence. SEBI's BRSR Core framework — mandatory for the top 150 listed companies by market capitalisation from FY2023-24 and extending to the top 1,000 from FY2024-25 — requires third-party assured disclosure on energy intensity, Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions, and transition risks. If imported fossil fuel price volatility constitutes a reasonably foreseeable material financial risk — which CAT's data strongly supports — then CFOs and audit committees of energy-intensive companies cannot treat it as a footnote disclosure. Under ISSB IFRS S2, which SEBI is progressively aligning BRSR toward, this class of risk requires scenario analysis, quantified financial impact ranges, and governance-level acknowledgment. The coexistence CAT identifies — accelerating renewable deployment alongside new gas infrastructure expansion — creates a specific stranded asset disclosure risk that Indian power sector companies, fertiliser manufacturers, and gas distribution companies must now assess explicitly. The policy contradiction CAT highlights — using emergency clauses to run imported-coal plants at full capacity while simultaneously fast-tracking wind and battery storage clearances — signals that India's energy transition is bifurcating into a public-sector infrastructure dilemma and a private-sector investment opportunity. For institutional investors with exposure to Indian power utilities, city gas distribution (CGD) networks, and LNG terminal developers, the CAT analysis constitutes new material information about the long-term asset life and revenue certainty of gas infrastructure investments.

Who Is Affected

  • Power generation companies — thermal (coal and gas), IPPs, and state DISCOMs — facing stranded asset risk from long-life fossil fuel infrastructure as renewables achieve cost parity
  • Energy-intensive industrials: steel (coking coal imports), cement, aluminium, chemicals, and fertilisers whose input cost structures are directly exposed to imported fossil fuel price volatility
  • City Gas Distribution (CGD) companies and LNG terminal developers whose capital expenditure pipelines assume gas as a durable transition fuel — a thesis CAT explicitly challenges
  • Top 1,000 BSE/NSE-listed companies subject to BRSR Core assured disclosures, particularly those with Scope 1 emissions from fossil fuel combustion or Scope 3 exposure through energy procurement
  • CFOs and Audit Committees responsible for BRSR Core third-party assurance sign-off and materiality determination under SEBI's LODR framework
  • Chief Sustainability Officers at companies with SBTi commitments or net zero targets who must now reconcile India's energy system trajectory with their own Scope 2 and Scope 3 decarbonisation pathways
  • ESG-focused FIIs, domestic mutual funds with ESG mandates, and green bond investors holding exposure to Indian energy infrastructure — particularly those applying ISSB IFRS S2 or EU SFDR Article 8/9 screening criteria
  • Export-oriented manufacturers supplying EU customers subject to CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) — where grid emission intensity driven by coal dependence directly affects CBAM liability calculations from 2026

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core — Top 150 listed companies (by market cap): Third-party assured ESG disclosures including energy intensity and GHG emissions mandatory from FY2023-24 annual reports (filing deadline typically September 2024 for FY24); top 1,000 companies phased in by FY2026-27
  • SEBI LODR Regulation 34(2)(f): Annual report must include BRSR with 'Leadership Indicators' covering transition risk assessment — fossil fuel import exposure now meets the threshold of a reasonably foreseeable material risk requiring named disclosure
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (Climate-Related Disclosures): For companies voluntarily adopting or preparing for ISSB alignment (which SEBI's IOSCO membership signals as the convergence direction), physical risk (energy supply disruption) and transition risk (stranded fossil assets) must be quantified under at least two climate scenarios
  • EU CBAM — Transitional phase data reporting: Indian exporters of steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, and electricity must report embedded carbon emissions quarterly from October 2023; financial CBAM liability commences January 2026 — grid emission intensity from coal-heavy dispatch directly increases CBAM cost exposure
  • PAT Scheme Cycle VII (BEE): Designated consumers in power, steel, cement, aluminium, and other sectors must meet specific energy consumption targets — coal-heavy operations with low efficiency face PAT penalty risk as BEE tightens norms in line with India's NDC commitments
  • SBTi Corporate Net Zero Standard: Companies with validated SBTi targets must demonstrate Scope 2 market-based emission reductions — in India's current grid, this requires active renewable energy procurement (PPAs, RECs) rather than reliance on grid average factors that embed coal-heavy dispatch
  • RBI's Sustainable Finance Framework and Climate Risk Circular (2023): Banks and NBFCs must assess transition and physical climate risk in credit portfolios — fossil fuel-dependent borrowers in energy-intensive sectors face rising probability-weighted credit risk reclassification

Climate Transition Angle

India's 2022 updated NDC commits to 50% non-fossil installed electricity capacity by 2030 and a 45% reduction in GDP emissions intensity versus 2005 levels. The National Electricity Plan 2023 projects 500 GW of non-fossil capacity by 2030. These are Paris Agreement-aligned anchors. But CAT's analysis exposes the gap between installed capacity targets and actual energy system transformation: adding renewable capacity while simultaneously running coal plants at higher capacity factors and expanding gas infrastructure does not reduce fossil fuel financial exposure — it layers new clean capacity on top of an unreformed fossil base, with the economic and carbon consequences determined by dispatch merit order, not installed megawatts. For Indian companies with BRSR Core or ISSB IFRS S2 disclosure obligations, this gap is the central transition risk. Under IFRS S2's scenario analysis requirement, companies must assess their financial position under a below-2°C scenario (where coal assets face accelerated retirement and carbon pricing escalates) and a 'current policies' scenario (where continued fossil dependence results in sustained import cost volatility and inflation pass-through). The CAT data — 240 million tonnes of annual coal imports, 85–90% crude oil import dependence — provides the quantitative baseline for that scenario analysis. Companies that do not perform this analysis face not just regulatory exposure under BRSR but credibility risk with ISSB-aligned institutional investors who are increasingly applying IFRS S2 screens to emerging market portfolios. The stranded asset dimension is particularly acute for gas infrastructure. CAT explicitly identifies the lock-in risk of capital-intensive gas pipelines, LNG terminals, and CGD networks. Under TNFD's nature-related financial disclosure framework — which India's large infrastructure developers and lenders are beginning to engage with — long-lived fossil infrastructure in climate-vulnerable geographies also carries physical risk from demand destruction as the energy transition accelerates. For Indian companies investing in gas infrastructure today with a 25–30-year asset life assumption, the ISSB S2 scenario analysis will need to model a credible early retirement scenario — and that has direct implications for impairment testing under Ind AS 36.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Finance teams (immediate — next 90 days): Commission a quantified energy import cost sensitivity analysis mapping your company's P&L exposure to a 20–30% fossil fuel price shock scenario; this analysis feeds directly into BRSR Core 'Leadership Indicators' on transition risk and satisfies the materiality threshold for SEBI LODR disclosure
  • CSO / Sustainability teams (FY2024-25 reporting cycle): Map your Scope 2 emissions using both location-based (grid average) and market-based (contracted renewable energy) methodologies per GHG Protocol; if your company lacks active renewable PPAs or REC procurement, your BRSR and ISSB-aligned disclosure will show structurally high Scope 2 intensity — begin renewable PPA tendering or REC procurement to address this before the next assured BRSR filing
  • Board Risk Committee (next board cycle): Add 'fossil fuel import price volatility' as a named principal risk in the risk register with quantified financial impact range; this is now a BRSR Core material topic and an ISSB IFRS S2 transition risk — failure to name it creates audit committee liability on BRSR assurance sign-off
  • Capital expenditure and strategy teams (12-month horizon): Apply ISSB S2-aligned scenario analysis to any proposed investment in gas infrastructure (pipelines, LNG, CGD networks) — model asset life under a below-2°C trajectory and stress-test against a 2035 early retirement scenario; present findings to board before capital allocation approval
  • Export-oriented manufacturers (immediate): Calculate your EU CBAM embedded carbon liability using India's current grid emission factor (approximately 0.71 kgCO2/kWh per CEA 2023 data) and stress-test against a higher factor scenario if coal dispatch intensity increases; begin tracking embedded emissions at product level to comply with CBAM reporting obligations that commenced October 2023
  • Investor relations teams (next quarterly investor update): Proactively brief FII and ESG-mandated investors on your company's energy supply risk management strategy, renewable procurement roadmap, and BRSR transition risk disclosures — do not wait for a proxy season challenge or an ESG rating downgrade to trigger this conversation

Our Take

CAT's analysis should be read by Indian boards not as a climate advocacy document but as a financial risk map. The three-decade pattern of recurring fossil fuel price shocks hitting India's economy is now intersecting with a regulatory disclosure architecture — BRSR Core, ISSB IFRS S2, EU CBAM — that requires companies to quantify and govern exactly this class of risk. The window in which 'fossil fuel price volatility' could be treated as a generic macroeconomic disclaimer in an annual report is closing. Within 24 months, we expect SEBI to mandate scenario-based transition risk quantification in BRSR Core filings, likely drawing on ISSB IFRS S2 methodology, as part of India's commitments under its IOSCO membership and the G20 Sustainable Finance Working Group agenda. Companies that have not built this analytical capability internally will face a compressed, expensive scramble to achieve assurance-ready disclosures. The gas infrastructure question is the sleeper risk that Indian boards and infrastructure investors are underpricing. The political logic of gas-as-transition-fuel is seductive, but CAT's data on import dependence (45–50% of fossil gas imported) combined with Middle East supply disruption risk creates a scenario where India's gas infrastructure buildout — estimated at over USD 60 billion in pipeline and terminal investments planned through 2030 — faces dual obsolescence risk: geopolitical supply disruption in the near term and demand destruction from renewable-plus-storage cost curves in the medium term. Any Indian company or PE fund with capital in CGD networks, LNG terminals, or gas-to-power assets needs an honest ISSB S2-style scenario analysis on those assets before the next capital raise or refinancing. The risk is no longer hypothetical — it is the central question any sophisticated infrastructure debt or equity investor will ask. India's renewable acceleration is real, and the policy signals — fast-tracked wind and battery storage clearances even during the current energy crisis — show that the government understands the structural argument. But the transition will only de-risk Indian companies if it is accompanied by a parallel shift in how boards, CFOs, and investors account for the stranded asset exposure in the existing fossil infrastructure base. The companies that will win the next decade are those that treat energy transition not as a CSR narrative but as a balance sheet restructuring exercise — retiring fossil exposure, locking in long-term renewable PPAs, and building the disclosure infrastructure to prove it to international capital markets. BRSR Core and ISSB S2 are the instruments that will separate the leaders from the laggards, and enforcement is closer than most Indian boards currently assume.
