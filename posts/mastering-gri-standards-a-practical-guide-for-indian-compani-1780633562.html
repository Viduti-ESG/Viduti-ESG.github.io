Mastering GRI Standards: A Practical Guide for Indian Companies Navigating Sustainability Reporting

Executive Summary

The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) Standards remain the world's most widely adopted sustainability reporting framework, and Indian companies — whether driven by SEBI's BRSR mandate, investor due diligence, or global supply chain requirements — increasingly need to understand how to use them correctly. Misapplication of GRI Standards is one of the most common and costly compliance mistakes organisations make, leading to greenwashing allegations, audit failures, and lost credibility with stakeholders. This post breaks down how to use the GRI Standards effectively, who needs to act, and what your sustainability team should do right now.

What Changed

The GRI Standards are a modular, interrelated set of disclosure requirements published by the Global Reporting Initiative, a globally recognised independent standards body headquartered in Amsterdam. The Standards were substantially updated in 2021, with the revised Universal Standards (GRI 1, 2, and 3) taking effect from 1 January 2023, replacing the previous GRI 101, 102, and 103 framework. This was one of the most significant overhauls in GRI's history. The updated framework introduces a clearer three-tier architecture: GRI 1 (Foundation) sets out the purpose, key concepts, and requirements for using the Standards; GRI 2 (General Disclosures) covers organisational profile, governance, strategy, and stakeholder engagement; and GRI 3 (Material Topics) provides a structured methodology for determining and reporting on material topics. These are then supplemented by Sector Standards and Topic Standards covering specific environmental, social, and governance issues such as emissions (GRI 305), water (GRI 303), occupational health and safety (GRI 403), and anti-corruption (GRI 205), among many others. A critical distinction that many organisations miss is the difference between reporting 'in accordance with' GRI Standards — which requires meeting all applicable requirements — versus 'with reference to' GRI Standards, which is a more flexible approach allowing selective use of disclosures. Both pathways are legitimate, but they carry different credibility signals to investors and regulators, and companies must clearly state which pathway they have followed.

Who Is Affected

  • Top 1,000 listed companies in India required to file BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report) with SEBI — GRI alignment strengthens BRSR disclosures significantly
  • Indian subsidiaries and supply chain partners of European multinationals subject to CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive), which references GRI as an accepted framework
  • Export-oriented manufacturers in textiles, chemicals, automotive, and pharmaceuticals facing GRI-based supplier assessments from global buyers
  • Private equity and venture-backed companies preparing for IPO or seeking ESG-focused institutional investment
  • Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs) and CPSEs increasingly expected to align with GRI under DPE (Department of Public Enterprises) sustainability guidelines
  • Mid-sized companies in the MSME segment that are part of listed company supply chains, as Scope 3 disclosure requirements push GRI expectations downstream
  • Financial institutions, banks, and NBFCs responding to RBI's disclosure expectations and investor ESG scoring frameworks that use GRI as a benchmark

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE: Confirm whether your organisation is reporting 'in accordance with' GRI or 'with reference to' GRI — this must be explicitly stated in your sustainability report and GRI Content Index
  • IMMEDIATE: Ensure your GRI Content Index is complete, accurate, and publicly accessible — this is the primary audit trail for any GRI-based disclosure and is scrutinised by ESG rating agencies such as MSCI, Sustainalytics, and CDP
  • BY NEXT REPORTING CYCLE: Complete a formal materiality assessment aligned with GRI 3 requirements, including identification of actual and potential impacts across your value chain — the 2021 update requires 'impact materiality' not just financial materiality
  • BY NEXT REPORTING CYCLE: Map your existing BRSR disclosures against the GRI Content Index to identify gaps — SEBI's BRSR Core indicators align closely with several GRI Topic Standards, enabling dual-use reporting efficiency
  • ONGOING: Sector Standards (e.g., GRI 11 for Oil & Gas, GRI 13 for Agriculture) are being rolled out progressively — companies in covered sectors must integrate applicable Sector Standards into their reporting as they become effective
  • ONGOING: Maintain documented evidence for all GRI disclosures — third-party assurance over GRI reports is increasingly expected by institutional investors and is mandatory for BRSR Core disclosures for the top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24
  • 2024-2025: Watch for GRI's forthcoming Sector Standards covering financial services, mining, and infrastructure — begin gap assessments now if your sector is in the pipeline

Climate Transition Angle

GRI Standards are not purely a reporting compliance exercise — they are a foundational tool for managing and communicating your company's exposure to climate transition risks and physical climate risks. GRI 201 (Economic Performance) now encourages disclosure of financial implications of climate risks, directly paralleling the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) and the newly effective ISSB Standards (IFRS S2). For Indian companies, the stakes are particularly high. India's geographic exposure to physical climate risks — extreme heat, monsoon variability, flooding, and water stress — means that GRI disclosures on water (GRI 303), energy (GRI 302), and emissions (GRI 305) are no longer just reputational exercises. They are signals of operational resilience that sophisticated investors and lenders are actively pricing. Companies that cannot produce credible, GRI-aligned data on these metrics are increasingly disadvantaged in capital markets. The convergence of global frameworks is accelerating. The ISSB's IFRS S1 and S2 Standards, now adopted or under consideration by regulators in over 20 jurisdictions, explicitly acknowledge GRI as a complementary framework for impact-focused disclosures. The European Union's CSRD mandates the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), which were developed in collaboration with GRI to ensure interoperability. For Indian exporters and companies with European investors or customers, this means GRI compliance is increasingly a market access requirement, not just a voluntary best practice. Building GRI competency now positions your organisation at the intersection of every major global reporting standard that matters.

What To Do Now

  • CONDUCT A GRI FRAMEWORK AUDIT: Map your current sustainability report against the 2021 GRI Universal Standards (GRI 1, 2, and 3) to identify structural gaps — many companies are still inadvertently using the old 2016 framework structure, which is no longer current
  • INVEST IN MATERIALITY ASSESSMENT: Commission or refresh your materiality assessment using GRI 3's double materiality lens, covering both your impacts on people and planet AND the financial risks and opportunities that sustainability issues pose to your business — this is the backbone of credible GRI reporting and increasingly required by BRSR and CSRD-linked stakeholders
  • BUILD YOUR GRI CONTENT INDEX: Create or update a structured GRI Content Index that maps each required disclosure to the relevant section of your report, indicates omissions with clear justifications, and is published as a standalone document on your website — this is the single most important credibility signal for ESG raters and investors
  • ENGAGE YOUR DATA OWNERS: GRI reporting requires granular, verifiable data from operations, HR, procurement, legal, and finance — establish internal data governance protocols so that sustainability disclosures are treated with the same rigour as financial reporting, including clear ownership, collection timelines, and audit trails
  • PLAN FOR ASSURANCE: Engage a third-party assurance provider familiar with GRI's assurance protocols to conduct limited or reasonable assurance over your key GRI disclosures — this is already mandatory for BRSR Core disclosures and will become a baseline expectation across your investor and customer base within 2-3 years

Our Take

GRI Standards have quietly become the de facto common language of global sustainability disclosure — and Indian companies that treat them as a box-ticking exercise are making a strategic error they may not be able to correct quickly. The 2021 framework update was not cosmetic; it fundamentally shifted reporting from a communications function to an accountability function, embedding impact assessment, stakeholder engagement, and value chain thinking at the core of what disclosure means. As SEBI continues to strengthen BRSR requirements, as European customers demand CSRD-compatible supplier data, and as the ISSB framework gains regulatory traction across Asia, the companies that have invested in robust GRI infrastructure will find themselves with a significant competitive and reputational advantage. Those that have not will face a scramble to catch up under regulatory pressure and investor scrutiny simultaneously. The time to build this capability is now — not in response to the next enforcement notice.
