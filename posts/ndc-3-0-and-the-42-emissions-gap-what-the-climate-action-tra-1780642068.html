Executive Summary

The Climate Action Tracker's latest sectoral analysis — published at https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/by-the-numbers-the-climate-action-we-need-this-decade/ — confirms that current global policies will deliver less than 1% emissions reduction by 2030, against the 42% cut needed to keep 1.5°C alive, creating a structural climate credibility crisis that now has direct consequences for Indian corporates accessing international capital, export markets, and institutional ESG mandates. India's own NDC 3.0, due under the Paris Agreement by early 2025, will set the national emissions trajectory through 2035 — and the ambition level India signals in that submission will immediately filter into how global investors assess the stranded asset risk, transition readiness, and BRSR Climate-Related disclosures of every BSE 500 company operating in carbon-intensive sectors. For CFOs and CSOs, this is not a geopolitical abstraction: the gap between current policy trajectories and 1.5°C pathways is precisely the basis on which SBTi validates corporate targets, ISSB IFRS S2 requires scenario analysis, and EU CBAM prices carbon at the border — three mechanisms with direct, near-term financial exposure for Indian listed companies.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (original article: https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/by-the-numbers-the-climate-action-we-need-this-decade/), co-authored by Sophie Boehm, Clea Schumer, Kelly Levin and eleven other researchers under the Systems Change Lab project, provides the most granular sectoral breakdown yet of the emissions reductions needed by 2030 and 2035 to limit warming to 1.5°C. The core finding: current policies put the world on track for 2.6–2.8°C of warming, and the gap between current commitments and a 1.5°C pathway is now so wide that it cannot be closed without unprecedented deployment pace across every sector — power, transport, agriculture, industry, land use — simultaneously. What this builds on and what it overturns: previous NDC cycles allowed countries to set directional pledges without hard sectoral sub-targets. The new CAT analysis explicitly argues that NDC 3.0 submissions — due by early 2025 under UNFCCC rules — must embed quantitative, time-bound sectoral targets (EV sales percentages, renewable capacity additions, methane reduction rates) alongside economy-wide GHG targets for 2030 and 2035. This is a substantive shift from the architecture of India's current 2022 NDC, which commits to 45% emissions intensity reduction from 2005 levels and 50% non-fossil power capacity by 2030 — but lacks the granular sectoral sub-targets that international investors and standard-setters are now demanding as the credibility threshold. The India-specific consequence is immediate and multi-layered. First, India's NDC 3.0 ambition level will be scrutinised against the CAT's 1.5°C sectoral benchmarks by global rating agencies (MSCI, Sustainalytics, CDP), and any gap will be mapped back onto Indian corporates' transition risk scores. Second, SEBI's BRSR Core framework — mandatory for the top 150 listed companies from FY2024-25 and expanding to the top 1,000 by FY2026-27 — requires Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions disclosure with third-party assurance; if India's NDC trajectory is rated 'Insufficient' or 'Critically Insufficient' by CAT, Indian companies face heightened scrutiny on the credibility of their own climate targets under BRSR. Third, ISSB IFRS S2 — now being assessed for adoption by ICAI and MCA's sustainability reporting roadmap — requires companies to disclose against 1.5°C and 2°C scenarios; the CAT data directly informs the physical and transition risk parameters used in those scenario models.

Who Is Affected

  • Power sector: NTPC, Adani Green, Tata Power, JSW Energy — companies whose capital expenditure plans, renewable capacity additions, and coal retirement timelines are directly benchmarked against NDC sectoral targets and CAT's power sector decarbonisation pathway
  • Steel, cement, aluminium, and chemicals: Tata Steel, JSW Steel, UltraTech Cement, Hindalco — high-emissions manufacturers facing EU CBAM pricing from October 2023 (transitional) and full implementation from 2026, where India's NDC ambition level affects the carbon price differential applied at the EU border
  • Automotive OEMs and auto-ancillaries: Maruti Suzuki, Tata Motors, Mahindra — companies whose EV transition timelines and Scope 3 downstream emissions are directly exposed to NDC sectoral sub-targets for transport, which CAT now recommends should include quantitative EV penetration benchmarks
  • Agri-food and FMCG: ITC, HUL, Dabur — companies with significant Scope 3 land-use and agriculture emissions, where CAT's land sector targets (including mangrove restoration, deforestation rates) will increasingly appear in CDP questionnaires and TNFD disclosures
  • BSE 500 listed companies with international institutional shareholders (FPIs holding >5% in aggregate): subject to investor stewardship escalation if corporate climate targets are misaligned with updated NDC trajectories
  • CFOs: responsible for BRSR Core assurance readiness, climate-related financial disclosure under ISSB S2 as it approaches India adoption, and EU CBAM compliance cost modelling
  • Chief Sustainability Officers: must translate NDC 3.0 ambition levels into updated SBTi target submissions, BRSR Principal 6 disclosures, and CDP Climate response upgrades
  • Board Risk Committees: required under SEBI's LODR amendments to oversee climate-related risks; the CAT's finding that current policies deliver <1% emissions reduction by 2030 constitutes a material transition risk that must be disclosed
  • ESG-focused FPIs, domestic mutual funds with ESG mandates (SEBI's ESG fund circular, November 2023), and DFIs including SBI, NaBFID, and IREDA whose green bond frameworks reference NDC alignment

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • India NDC 3.0 submission to UNFCCC: due by early 2025 (February 10, 2025 is the working deadline referenced in UNFCCC COP29 decisions); Indian companies must engage with MoEFCC's NDC consultation process now, as sectoral targets will define regulatory expectations for 2030-2035
  • BRSR Core — Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions disclosure with reasonable assurance: mandatory for top 150 listed entities from FY2024-25 reporting cycle (annual report due by September 2025); Scope 3 disclosure mandatory from FY2025-26
  • BRSR Core expansion to top 1,000 listed companies: FY2026-27, with limited assurance on Scope 1/2; companies outside top 150 must begin baseline measurement now
  • EU CBAM full implementation: January 1, 2026; Indian steel, cement, aluminium, fertiliser, and hydrogen exporters must have verified Scope 1 embedded emissions data for all EU-bound shipments, benchmarked against EU ETS default values
  • SBTi near-term and net-zero target validation: SBTi's Corporate Net-Zero Standard requires targets aligned with 1.5°C pathways; the CAT sectoral benchmarks are the underlying reference data; companies with pending SBTi submissions must review alignment against updated 2030/2035 sectoral benchmarks
  • ISSB IFRS S2 climate scenario disclosure: while not yet mandated in India, MCA's Companies (Accounts) Amendment Rules trajectory and ICAI's sustainability standards adoption roadmap suggest India-aligned standards by 2026-27; preparatory scenario analysis against 1.5°C and 2.6-2.8°C (current policy) pathways should begin in FY2025
  • CDP Climate questionnaire 2025 (submission window: April–July 2025): updated scoring methodology will reference NDC 3.0 ambition levels and CAT sectoral benchmarks; companies rated B or below face investor escalation under SEBI's ESG fund stewardship requirements

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT's finding that current policies deliver less than 1% emissions reduction by 2030 — against a required 42% — is not merely a scientific alarm. It is the quantified basis for transition risk pricing across every asset class exposed to carbon-intensive sectors in India. For Indian corporates, this creates three compounding transition risk vectors that boards must now explicitly model. First, physical risk acceleration: a 2.6–2.8°C trajectory — the outcome of current policies — implies materially higher frequency of extreme weather events, monsoon disruption, water stress in key industrial belts (Vidarbha cotton, Odisha steel, Rajasthan cement), and coastal infrastructure risk that directly affects asset valuations, insurance costs, and working capital cycles. ISSB IFRS S2 Paragraph 10 requires disclosure of physical risks under both a low (1.5°C) and high (current policy) warming scenario — and the CAT's numbers are precisely what populates the high-warming scenario. Second, transition risk from policy discontinuity: if India's NDC 3.0 adopts more ambitious 2035 sectoral sub-targets — as CAT recommends — this will accelerate regulatory tightening under BEE's PAT Scheme cycles, CPCB's industrial emission standards revisions, and MoEFCC's environment clearance conditions for coal-based power and greenfield heavy industry. Companies that have not built transition capex into their 5-year plans on the assumption of policy continuity are carrying unpriced transition risk on their balance sheets. Third, capital access risk: India's green bond market (SEBI Green Bond Framework, 2023) and the RBI's evolving climate risk disclosure framework for banks will increasingly require borrowers to demonstrate NDC-aligned transition plans. The International Platform on Sustainable Finance (IPSF), of which India is a member, is moving toward taxonomy interoperability standards; Indian companies accessing green finance in EU, UK, or Singapore capital markets will face credibility challenges if their transition plans reference an NDC rated 'Insufficient' by CAT. This is not a future risk — it is already appearing in green bond eligibility assessments and bilateral DFI loan covenants in 2024-25.

What To Do Now

  • Sustainability / Government Affairs teams — within 60 days: engage formally with MoEFCC's NDC 3.0 consultation process; submit sector-specific inputs on feasible 2030 and 2035 decarbonisation pathways, particularly on renewable energy deployment rates, EV transition timelines, and industrial energy efficiency targets under BEE PAT Scheme; the NDC sectoral targets India adopts will become the regulatory benchmarks against which BRSR disclosures are assessed
  • CFO / Finance teams — within 90 days: commission a CAT-aligned transition risk scenario analysis for FY2025 financial planning; model the balance sheet impact of both a 1.5°C rapid-transition scenario and a 2.8°C current-policy scenario against key asset categories, energy costs, carbon cost exposure (EU CBAM, domestic carbon market), and insurance premiums; this is a board-level disclosure obligation under SEBI LODR Schedule V and an ISSB S2 preparedness imperative
  • Chief Sustainability Officer — immediately: audit existing SBTi near-term targets against CAT's updated 2030 and 2035 sectoral benchmarks; if targets were validated before CAT's 2024 sectoral update, a voluntary re-assessment may be needed to maintain credibility with institutional investors applying Net Zero Investment Framework (NZIF) or CA100+ stewardship standards
  • Company Secretary / Board Risk Committee — before next board meeting: table the CAT findings as a material transition risk update; ensure the board risk register explicitly captures the <1% vs. 42% emissions reduction gap as a macro risk driver affecting the company's regulatory environment, export eligibility, and cost of capital; document the board's oversight of this risk in the Annual Report's BRSR Management and Process Disclosures section
  • Investor Relations — ahead of FY2025 annual report: proactively communicate to institutional investors how the company's climate targets align with (or plan to converge toward) NDC 3.0 sectoral benchmarks; ESG-focused FPIs operating under SEBI's ESG fund circular and UNPRI signatories will be filing stewardship escalation requests with companies whose climate plans are misaligned with updated CAT benchmarks through the 2025 AGM season
  • Procurement / Supply Chain — within 6 months: initiate Scope 3 Category 1 (purchased goods and services) emissions mapping aligned with GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain Standard; CAT's sectoral targets for agriculture, land use, and industry will cascade into supply chain procurement standards required by EU CSRD-reporting European customers from FY2025 onwards

Our Take

The CAT's 2030 sectoral benchmarks represent a recalibration of the ambition floor — not just for governments, but for every corporate climate commitment that references national policy as its baseline. The gap between less than 1% actual reduction and the 42% required is so structurally large that it is now functioning as a credibility discount applied to all actors — governments and corporates alike — who claim alignment with Paris without demonstrating sector-specific, time-bound interim pathways. For India, this matters acutely because India's NDC 3.0 submission will arrive at a moment when three regulatory architectures are simultaneously tightening their scrutiny of national-to-corporate climate alignment: BRSR Core with mandatory assurance, ISSB S2 approaching domestic adoption, and EU CBAM in full implementation by 2026. The trajectory I expect over the next 12–36 months is as follows. India will submit an NDC 3.0 that modestly upgrades the 2030 intensity target and introduces a 2035 headline target, but will likely stop short of the granular sectoral sub-targets CAT recommends — this is both a political economy constraint and a data readiness issue. However, the gap between India's NDC ambition and the CAT 1.5°C benchmark will be used by rating agencies to justify continued 'Insufficient' or 'Almost Sufficient' national ratings for India, and this rating will feed directly into MSCI ESG Country Scores and Sustainalytics' sovereign risk overlays — which institutional investors apply when screening Indian equities and bonds. The companies most exposed are those in sectors where the CAT sectoral gap is widest: coal power, industrial steel and cement, and high-emission agriculture. The companies best positioned are those that have already decoupled their own climate targets from the national NDC trajectory — setting SBTi-validated absolute reduction targets referenced to global sectoral benchmarks rather than India's intensity-based NDC metric. Boards that are still using 'we are aligned with India's NDC' as their climate governance defence will find that argument increasingly untenable with international institutional shareholders by the 2026 AGM season. The smarter move, starting now, is to set targets that exceed NDC ambition and reference CAT sectoral benchmarks directly — because that is the language that preserves capital access in a world where the emissions gap is no longer deniable.
