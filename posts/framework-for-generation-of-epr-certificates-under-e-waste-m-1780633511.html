Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued a framework governing the generation of Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) certificates under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, formalising how producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) must account for end-of-life electronics they place on the Indian market. This framework establishes a certificate-based compliance mechanism — where EPR certificates, generated through registered recyclers and dismantlers, serve as proof that a company has met its e-waste collection and channelisation targets. Companies that fail to acquire sufficient EPR certificates risk non-compliance penalties, restrictions on market operations, and increasing reputational exposure as ESG scrutiny of supply chains intensifies globally.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 replaced the earlier 2016 rules and introduced a digitalised, market-based EPR system in India — a significant structural shift. Rather than simply filing collection reports, producers and importers are now required to meet annual EPR targets expressed as a percentage of the quantity of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) they sell or import, and to demonstrate fulfilment of those targets through EPR certificates traded or acquired on the CPCB's centralised online portal. The CPCB's framework notice operationalises the certificate generation mechanism in detail. EPR certificates are generated when registered e-waste recyclers or dismantlers process e-waste and report it on the CPCB portal. These certificates are then available to PIBOs — either through direct agreements with recyclers or via the EPR exchange marketplace. The framework clarifies the chain of custody: from e-waste collection, through authorised dismantling or recycling, to certificate issuance and transfer. This brings India's e-waste compliance architecture closer in spirit to internationally recognised producer responsibility schemes and creates a verifiable, auditable paper trail for corporate sustainability reporting.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE): Indian manufacturers of laptops, mobile phones, consumer electronics, IT equipment, white goods, and other Schedule I items listed under the 2022 Rules.
  • Importers of EEE: Companies importing any scheduled electronic product for sale in India, regardless of their size or sector.
  • Brand Owners (OBOs): Entities whose brand name appears on EEE sold in India, even if manufacturing is outsourced — including large FMCG-adjacent electronics brands.
  • E-Waste Recyclers and Dismantlers: CPCB/State Pollution Control Board-authorised recyclers who generate EPR certificates and whose operational standards directly affect certificate validity.
  • EPR Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs): Intermediaries that aggregate EPR obligations for multiple PIBOs and coordinate certificate procurement — subject to their own registration and reporting obligations.
  • Retail and E-Commerce Platforms: Increasingly in scope as facilitators of EEE sales, with potential obligation to ensure seller compliance on their platforms.
  • Large Corporates with IT Asset Disposal (ITAD) Programmes: Enterprises disposing of bulk IT equipment who may act as bulk consumers under the Rules and have parallel channelisation duties.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • REGISTRATION (Immediate): All producers, importers, and brand owners not yet registered on the CPCB EPR portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in) must register without delay — registration is a prerequisite to any certificate acquisition or target fulfilment.
  • ANNUAL EPR TARGET SETTING: PIBOs must declare the quantity of EEE placed on the market in the previous financial year; CPCB assigns annual collection/recycling targets as a percentage of this quantity, scaling up year-on-year through 2023–2024 and beyond as per the Rules.
  • EPR CERTIFICATE PROCUREMENT: PIBOs must procure EPR certificates equivalent to their assigned annual target by entering into agreements with CPCB-authorised recyclers/dismantlers or by purchasing certificates through the CPCB EPR marketplace.
  • CERTIFICATE RECONCILIATION — FY END DEADLINE: EPR certificate targets must be reconciled against actual certificates held by the end of each financial year (March 31). PIBOs should not wait until Q4 — certificate procurement should be planned and executed across the year.
  • ANNUAL RETURN FILING: Submit annual returns on the CPCB portal detailing EEE placed on market, EPR targets, certificates acquired, and any shortfall — non-filing attracts enforcement action.
  • RECYCLER DUE DILIGENCE (Ongoing): PIBOs must verify that recyclers/dismantlers they contract with hold valid CPCB/SPCB authorisation; certificates from unauthorised processors are invalid for compliance purposes.
  • SHORTFALL PENALTY: Failure to meet EPR targets or acquire sufficient certificates may attract Environmental Compensation (EC) as notified by CPCB, which is proportional to the uncovered tonnage — companies should model worst-case EC exposure as a financial risk item.
  • RECORD KEEPING: Maintain documented records of all EPR certificate transactions, recycler agreements, and portal filings for a minimum of 5 years for audit and litigation purposes.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste EPR compliance is not merely a domestic regulatory box-tick — it sits at the intersection of circular economy principles, supply chain decarbonisation, and global sustainability disclosure standards, all of which are rapidly converging on Indian businesses. From a physical risk perspective, unmanaged e-waste contains hazardous materials — lead, mercury, cadmium — that contaminate soil and groundwater, compounding climate-driven water stress already acute in Indian manufacturing hubs. Formalising e-waste recycling chains through the EPR certificate framework directly mitigates these localised environmental liabilities. On the supply chain side, multinational buyers and investors are increasingly requiring Scope 3 emissions data and circularity metrics from Indian suppliers. The recovery of critical minerals — cobalt, lithium, copper, rare earths — from formalised e-waste recycling reduces dependence on virgin extraction, lowering embedded carbon in electronics supply chains. Indian producers who can demonstrate certified, traceable e-waste management will have a tangible advantage in ESG-screened procurement processes. From a disclosure standpoint, the ISSB's IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) and the EU's CSRD both require companies to assess and disclose material environmental impacts across their value chain. E-waste generation and end-of-life management are increasingly recognised as material topics under these frameworks, particularly for technology, consumer electronics, and industrial sectors. India's own BRSR Core (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting), mandatory for the top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24, includes supply chain sustainability and waste management as reportable indicators — EPR certificate data provides exactly the kind of quantified, third-party-verifiable metric that strengthens BRSR disclosures.

What To Do Now

  • AUDIT YOUR EPR STATUS TODAY: Confirm registration on the CPCB EPR portal, validate your declared EEE tonnage placed on market for the last completed financial year, and reconcile any pending certificate obligations. Engage your compliance or legal team to assess exposure for prior years.
  • MAP YOUR RECYCLER NETWORK: Identify and shortlist CPCB/SPCB-authorised e-waste recyclers and dismantlers in your operational geography. Conduct due diligence on their authorisation validity, processing capacity, and ESG standards — a recycler losing its authorisation mid-year invalidates certificates already paid for.
  • BUILD EPR CERTIFICATE PROCUREMENT INTO ANNUAL PLANNING: Treat EPR certificate acquisition as a line item in your annual compliance budget. Model your target tonnage, estimate certificate costs (which fluctuate based on marketplace supply-demand), and execute procurement agreements in Q1-Q2 rather than scrambling in Q3-Q4.
  • INTEGRATE EPR DATA INTO SUSTAINABILITY REPORTING: Align your EPR certificate data — quantities, recycler details, material streams recovered — with your BRSR, GRI 306 (Waste), or ISSB disclosure workflows. This turns a compliance obligation into a reportable circular economy metric visible to ESG-rating agencies and investors.
  • ENGAGE A PRODUCER RESPONSIBILITY ORGANISATION (PRO) IF NEEDED: Smaller producers or importers without dedicated compliance teams should evaluate empanelled PROs who can aggregate targets, manage recycler relationships, and handle portal filings — but retain contractual oversight and do not outsource accountability entirely.

Our Take

India's shift to a certificate-based, digitally traceable EPR system for e-waste is a structural maturation of environmental regulation — and the CPCB's framework notice for certificate generation is a signal that enforcement rigour is increasing, not decreasing. For too long, EPR compliance in India was treated as a paperwork exercise; the marketplace-based certificate mechanism changes that calculus by creating a real financial cost for non-fulfilment and a verifiable audit trail that regulators, investors, and global buyers can scrutinise. Companies that move early — building robust recycler networks, integrating EPR data into ESG disclosures, and treating circularity as a strategic asset rather than a compliance liability — will be better positioned as India's regulatory architecture continues to tighten in line with global best practice. Expect CPCB to increase Environmental Compensation rates, expand the list of scheduled EEE categories, and introduce stricter recycler auditing in the next 12–24 months. The time to build compliance maturity is now, not at the next enforcement notice.
