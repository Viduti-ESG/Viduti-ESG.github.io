Executive Summary

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) notified the E-Waste (Management) Amendment Rules, 2023 on 30 January 2023, effective 1 April 2023. Two critical changes stand out: solar photovoltaic (PV) panels and medical devices have been formally brought under the hazardous substance restrictions of the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, and the trigger for hazardous substance disclosure has been shifted from a fixed product-documentation requirement to an on-demand basis as required by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB). Companies operating in the solar energy and healthcare equipment sectors must immediately review their supply chains, product compositions, and Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) frameworks to ensure compliance.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — India's primary regulatory framework governing the lifecycle management of electrical and electronic equipment waste — have been amended in two specific but consequential ways through this notification. First, Rule 16(5), which governs how manufacturers must disclose information about restricted hazardous substances present in their products, has been modified. Previously, this disclosure was required to appear 'in the product user documentation' — meaning it had to be proactively included in manuals, spec sheets, or packaging materials. The amendment replaces this with a requirement to disclose 'as and when required by the Central Pollution Control Board.' In practical terms, this shifts the obligation from automatic, always-on disclosure to a responsive, regulator-triggered disclosure model. Companies must now be ready to produce detailed hazardous substance data on CPCB's request, rather than embedding it in every product document. Second, and more significantly from an industry scope perspective, Schedule II of the Rules — which lists restricted hazardous substances and their applications — has been expanded. Two new entries have been added: Serial No. 36 covers cadmium and lead in solar panels, solar cells, and solar photovoltaic (PV) panels, cells, and modules; and Serial No. 37 covers lead in medical devices, with an explicit exclusion for implanted and infected products. This means that for the first time, solar PV manufacturers and medical device companies are formally subject to the substance restriction and EPR obligations under the E-Waste Rules for these specific hazardous materials.

Who Is Affected

  • Solar PV manufacturers, assemblers, and importers — including those producing or sourcing solar panels, solar cells, and photovoltaic modules for rooftop, utility-scale, or off-grid applications
  • Medical device manufacturers and importers — particularly those whose products contain lead-based components such as solders, radiation shielding elements, or electronic sub-assemblies (excluding implanted and infected products)
  • Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) and contract manufacturers supplying components to solar and medtech companies, who may need to provide substance declarations
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) compliance teams and Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) operating across these two newly covered sectors
  • Importers and brand owners of foreign-manufactured solar PV equipment and medical devices who are the deemed 'producers' under India's E-Waste Rules
  • Environmental compliance officers and legal teams at conglomerates with diversified portfolios spanning energy, electronics, and healthcare

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Effective Date — 1 April 2023: All obligations under the Amendment Rules came into force. Companies in the solar PV and medical device sectors should treat this date as the baseline for compliance assessment.
  • Substance Restriction Compliance: Manufacturers and importers of solar panels, PV modules, and medical devices (non-implanted) must ensure that cadmium and lead content in covered products complies with the threshold limits specified in Schedule II of the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022. Review existing product specifications against permitted concentration values.
  • EPR Registration and Target-Setting: Producers newly brought under Schedule II coverage (solar PV and medical devices) must register on the CPCB EPR portal if not already registered, and set EPR targets for collection and recycling of end-of-life products containing the restricted substances.
  • CPCB Disclosure Readiness: Under the amended Rule 16(5), companies must maintain up-to-date, accessible records of hazardous substance content across their product lines — ready to be submitted to CPCB upon request. This replaces the earlier static documentation requirement but demands a more rigorous internal data management system.
  • Supply Chain Due Diligence: Engage tier-1 and tier-2 suppliers to obtain material declarations (preferably aligned with IEC 62474 or equivalent standards) confirming the presence or absence of cadmium and lead in components used in covered product categories.
  • Existing Inventory Review: Audit stock and products manufactured before 1 April 2023 to determine whether they contain restricted substances above threshold limits and assess how these will be managed, disclosed, or phased out.
  • Legal and Documentation Update: Update product compliance declarations, technical files, and import documentation to reflect the new Schedule II entries. Ensure that EPR annual returns filed with CPCB account for the newly regulated product categories.

Climate Transition Angle

At first glance, this amendment may appear to be a narrow regulatory housekeeping exercise. In reality, it sits at the intersection of two of the most important climate transition trends affecting Indian industry: the explosive growth of renewable energy infrastructure and the rapid expansion of the domestic medical technology sector. India's solar capacity ambitions are staggering — the government targets 500 GW of renewable energy by 2030, with solar PV forming the backbone of that expansion. However, the lifecycle environmental cost of solar panels, particularly the cadmium in thin-film technologies and the lead in solders and junction boxes, has historically been under-regulated in India. By formally bringing these substances under the E-Waste Rules, the government is signalling that the clean energy transition must itself be clean — that the environmental liability of end-of-life solar panels cannot be externalised onto communities or landfills. For companies, this means that EPR obligations for solar PV will become increasingly material as the installed base ages and the first wave of large-scale decommissioning begins within the next decade. From a global standards perspective, this move aligns India more closely with the European Union's RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) Directive, which has long restricted cadmium and lead in electrical and electronic equipment, and with the EU's emerging Solar PV End-of-Life framework under the WEEE Directive. For Indian solar manufacturers exporting to Europe or seeking European investment, this domestic regulatory alignment reduces the compliance gap and strengthens the case for ESG-grade supply chain credentials. The ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standard and the EU's CSRD both require companies to assess and disclose material climate-related risks, including physical risks (such as extreme weather damage to solar assets) and transition risks (such as regulatory changes affecting product design and end-of-life costs). The inclusion of solar PV in India's E-Waste Rules is precisely the kind of transition risk that sustainability officers must now factor into product lifecycle cost models, capital expenditure planning, and investor disclosures. Companies that have not begun integrating e-waste EPR costs into their solar project financial models are materially underestimating their long-term liability exposure.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct an immediate product and substance audit: Map all solar PV and medical device product lines against Schedule II entries to determine which products contain cadmium or lead above permissible thresholds. Engage your materials engineering or EHS team to produce a substance inventory aligned with IEC 62474 or RoHS declaration formats.
  • Register or update your CPCB EPR profile: If your company manufactures, imports, or brands solar PV panels or relevant medical devices and is not yet registered under the EPR framework, initiate registration on the CPCB centralised portal immediately. Existing registrants should update their product category listings to include the newly covered items.
  • Build a CPCB-ready disclosure system: Since Rule 16(5) now requires hazardous substance information 'as and when required by CPCB,' establish an internal data management process — ideally a Product Compliance Management System — that can generate substance disclosure reports on demand, with version control and audit trails.
  • Engage your supply chain proactively: Issue supplier questionnaires or formal Material Declaration Requests to all tier-1 component suppliers for solar and medtech product lines. Specify that declarations must cover cadmium and lead content and be updated whenever materials or processes change. Build contractual obligations for substance compliance into new supplier agreements.
  • Integrate EPR costs into financial and ESG planning: Work with your CFO and sustainability team to model the long-term cost of EPR targets for solar panels — including collection, dismantling, and recycling — and incorporate these into project IRR calculations, product pricing strategies, and BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting) disclosures to investors.

Our Take

The E-Waste First Amendment Rules, 2023 are deceptively significant. On the surface, two new Schedule II entries and a tweak to a disclosure clause may seem like minor administrative updates. But read in the context of India's climate ambitions and the global regulatory direction of travel, they represent a clear and irreversible policy signal: the solar energy sector will be held accountable for the full environmental lifecycle of its products, not just the clean kilowatt-hours they generate. The modification to Rule 16(5) also deserves attention — by making disclosure CPCB-triggered rather than document-embedded, the regulator has given itself a powerful supervisory tool while simultaneously raising the stakes for companies that lack robust internal substance data systems. We expect the CPCB to use this authority actively as enforcement capacity improves. Looking ahead, companies should anticipate further expansions of Schedule II to cover additional solar PV materials (such as per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances used in panel coatings) and a potential tightening of EPR collection targets for solar as India's decommissioning cycle accelerates post-2030. Those who build compliance infrastructure now — rather than scrambling when enforcement begins — will be best positioned to compete for institutional capital and long-term project contracts in a market where ESG credentials are increasingly non-negotiable.
