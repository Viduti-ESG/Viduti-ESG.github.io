Behind the Rules: How ISSB Sets IFRS Sustainability Standards — And Why Indian Companies Must Pay Attention

Executive Summary

The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), operating under the IFRS Foundation, follows a rigorous, multi-stage due process to develop globally consistent sustainability disclosure standards — most notably IFRS S1 (General Requirements) and IFRS S2 (Climate-Related Disclosures). Understanding how these standards are created is not merely academic: it gives Indian companies early warning of what is coming, allows them to engage in shaping the rules, and enables proactive alignment with frameworks that Indian regulators — including SEBI — are increasingly mirroring. Companies that track the ISSB's pipeline today will avoid costly compliance scrambles tomorrow.

What Changed

The IFRS Foundation, the independent not-for-profit body that oversees global accounting and sustainability reporting standards, has published a transparent account of how IFRS Standards — including those issued by the ISSB — are developed and maintained. The standard-setting process is structured, consultative, and iterative, designed to ensure that standards reflect the needs of investors, preparers, regulators, and the public across jurisdictions. At a high level, the process moves through several defined stages: agenda consultation (identifying priority topics), research and discussion papers (exploring potential approaches), exposure drafts (formal proposals open for public comment), standard finalisation (incorporating feedback and issuing the standard), and post-implementation review (assessing real-world effectiveness). Each stage includes mandatory public consultation periods, typically 90–120 days, during which any stakeholder — including Indian companies and regulators — can submit formal comments. For sustainability specifically, the ISSB has signalled that its near-term agenda beyond IFRS S1 and S2 will focus on biodiversity, human capital, and human rights disclosures. These are not distant abstractions — they are the next wave of mandatory reporting obligations that will cascade into Indian regulatory frameworks, supply chain contracts, and investor expectations within the next three to five years.

Who Is Affected

  • Listed companies on Indian stock exchanges (BSE/NSE), particularly the top 1,000 by market capitalisation already subject to SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework
  • Indian subsidiaries and joint ventures of multinational corporations headquartered in jurisdictions adopting ISSB standards (EU, UK, Japan, Singapore, Australia, and others)
  • Export-oriented manufacturers and suppliers integrated into global value chains where buyers are subject to CSRD or ISSB-aligned reporting obligations
  • Financial institutions — banks, asset managers, and insurers — increasingly required to disclose portfolio-level climate and sustainability risks
  • CFOs, Chief Sustainability Officers, and Investor Relations teams responsible for sustainability reporting strategy and investor communication
  • Audit and assurance firms advising on sustainability disclosure readiness and limited/reasonable assurance engagements

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IFRS S1 & S2 (already issued, June 2023): Companies in jurisdictions that have adopted or endorsed these standards must disclose material sustainability-related risks and opportunities (S1) and climate-specific information including Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions (S2) — early adopters began reporting in FY2024
  • SEBI BRSR Core (effective FY2023–24 onwards): India's BRSR Core framework is explicitly designed to be interoperable with ISSB standards; top 150 listed companies must provide assured BRSR Core disclosures — expand to top 250 by FY2024–25 and top 500 by FY2025–26
  • ISSB Agenda Consultation (ongoing): The ISSB periodically releases agenda consultations — monitor the IFRS Foundation website and submit responses to shape standards on biodiversity (TNFD-aligned), human capital, and just transition topics before they are finalised
  • Exposure Draft Comment Periods: When the ISSB releases exposure drafts (expected on biodiversity and human capital disclosures in 2025–2026), Indian companies have a 90–120 day window to submit formal comments — missing this window means losing the opportunity to influence rules that will affect you
  • ISSB Jurisdictional Adoption Tracker: Continuously monitor which of your key trading partners and investor-domicile countries have adopted ISSB standards, as contractual and investor-driven disclosure requirements will flow down to Indian entities regardless of domestic mandates

Climate Transition Angle

The ISSB's standard-setting process is the architectural backbone of the global climate disclosure infrastructure. IFRS S2 is explicitly designed to be consistent with the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) recommendations and to interoperate with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS). This means that a standard shaped in Basel or London today becomes a supply chain requirement, investor screening criterion, or trade barrier for Indian companies within two to three years. From a physical risk perspective, IFRS S2 requires companies to disclose their exposure to acute events (cyclones, floods, heatwaves) and chronic shifts (sea-level rise, changes in precipitation) — risks that are acutely relevant to Indian industries from agriculture and textiles to infrastructure and financial services. The Ganges basin, coastal manufacturing clusters, and rain-fed agricultural supply chains all carry material physical climate risk that investors will soon demand to be quantified and disclosed under ISSB-aligned frameworks. The transition risk dimension is equally significant. As the ISSB refines requirements around carbon pricing exposure, stranded asset risk, and climate scenario analysis (using 1.5°C and 2°C pathways), Indian companies in carbon-intensive sectors — steel, cement, chemicals, thermal power — must build the internal data and modelling capabilities now to meet these disclosures credibly. Companies that treat ISSB alignment as a future problem will find themselves at a competitive and capital-access disadvantage against peers who have invested early.

What To Do Now

  • Map your regulatory exposure: Identify which jurisdictions your investors, lenders, customers, and partners are based in, and determine whether those jurisdictions have adopted or are adopting ISSB standards — this maps your de facto compliance timeline even before Indian mandates expand
  • Subscribe to the ISSB's due process pipeline: Register on the IFRS Foundation website to receive notifications on new discussion papers and exposure drafts; assign a team member to review and respond to consultations on upcoming topics (biodiversity, human capital) — your response shapes standards that will govern your disclosures
  • Conduct a gap assessment against IFRS S1 and S2: Even if not yet mandated domestically, benchmark your current BRSR and ESG disclosures against IFRS S1 and S2 requirements to identify data gaps, particularly around Scope 3 emissions, climate scenario analysis, and governance disclosures
  • Invest in Scope 3 data infrastructure now: IFRS S2's Scope 3 requirements are the most technically demanding element — begin supplier engagement, primary data collection, and spend-based estimation methodologies immediately, as building this capability takes 18–24 months minimum
  • Brief your board and audit committee: Present the ISSB standard-setting timeline and its interoperability with SEBI BRSR Core to your board; ensure that sustainability disclosure is treated as a governance and financial reporting matter — not just a CSR or communications exercise

Our Take

The ISSB's transparent, consultative standard-setting process is both a technical mechanism and a strategic opportunity — and most Indian companies are not yet using it as one. The window to influence rules on biodiversity disclosure, human capital metrics, and just transition reporting is open right now, through formal comment submissions and participation in ISSB roundtables and outreach events. Indian industry associations, the Institute of Chartered Accountants of India (ICAI), and SEBI itself have begun engaging with ISSB processes, but corporate-level participation remains thin. This is a missed opportunity. Beyond participation, the deeper significance of understanding how ISSB standards are made is this: the pipeline is predictable. Companies that track the research agenda today can build systems, train teams, and engage supply chains in advance — converting a compliance burden into a competitive advantage. The question for Indian business leaders is no longer whether ISSB-aligned disclosure will be required; it is whether you will be ready when it is.
