IMO's Net Zero Framework Postponement to April 2026: What India's Shipping-Dependent Exporters, Port Operators, and ESG Investors Must Prepare For Now

Executive Summary

In October 2025, the International Maritime Organization failed to adopt its Net Zero Framework — a global fuel emissions standard paired with a market-based pricing mechanism — following blocking pressure from the United States and Saudi Arabia, deferring the decision to MEPC 84 in April 2026. The Climate Action Tracker (CAT) warns this delay risks locking shipping onto a 4°C emissions trajectory rather than the 2°C pathway the NZF would have enabled (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/rescuing-shippings-net-zero-framework/). For India, the consequences are immediate and commercially material: Indian exporters moving roughly 95% of trade by volume through international shipping, major port operators at JNPA, Mundra, and Vizag, and Indian shipbuilders competing for next-generation vessel contracts all face accelerating compliance cost uncertainty — and their European buyers are already pricing Scope 3 shipping emissions into CSRD-aligned supply chain disclosures. The April 2026 IMO vote is not a distant geopolitical event; it is a board-level capital allocation decision for every Indian company with material ocean freight exposure.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (climateactiontracker.org), authored by Michael Petroni and Finn Hossfeld, confirms that the IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee failed at its October 2025 session to adopt the Net Zero Framework measures that had been agreed in principle earlier in the year. The postponement to MEPC 84 (April 2026) was driven by opposition from the United States — which remains categorically opposed to the framework — and Saudi Arabia, whose economy is structurally exposed to declining bunker fuel demand embedded in any credible shipping decarbonisation regime. The NZF, in its current form, would introduce two interlocking mechanisms: a Global Fuel Standard (GFS) that mandates a progressive reduction in the greenhouse gas intensity of marine fuels, and a market-based mechanism (MBM) — effectively a carbon levy or credit trading system — that prices excess emissions and channels revenues into an IMO Fund to support zero-emission fuel infrastructure and a just transition for developing maritime nations. CAT's modelling projects the NZF would reduce shipping emissions 14–22% below 2008 levels by 2030, improving the sector's current 'Highly Insufficient' rating to merely 'Insufficient' — still consistent with 3–4°C of warming — but critically establishing the regulatory architecture for steeper reductions post-2035 that could make the framework 1.5°C-compatible by 2050. What this overturns is the assumption, held widely in Indian shipping and port circles, that IMO decarbonisation rules would arrive on a predictable schedule tied to the 2023 GHG Strategy milestones. That strategy committed to 'at least 20%' emissions reduction by 2030 and net zero 'by or around 2050.' The NZF was the operational instrument to enforce those targets through MARPOL Annex VI amendments. Its postponement creates a 6-month regulatory vacuum — but more critically, it signals that the US under its current administration will use its IMO membership to actively obstruct binding global shipping emissions rules, a posture that may persist beyond April 2026. For Indian trade policy and port investment planning, this is a structural uncertainty, not a procedural delay. The India-specific implication is layered. First, India is among the world's top 10 flag states and a significant ship recycling nation under the Hong Kong Convention; any IMO framework that reshapes vessel economics directly affects Indian yards at Alang. Second, India's major exporters — textiles from Tirupur, pharmaceuticals from Hyderabad and Ahmedabad, auto components from Pune and Chennai, chemicals from Surat — ship predominantly on foreign-flagged vessels whose owners will pass through compliance costs via freight rates the moment the NZF is adopted. Third, the EU's own unilateral shipping inclusion under the EU ETS, which has been operational since January 2024 and covers 50% of emissions on voyages to/from EU ports, is already creating a two-tier freight market. Indian exporters sending containerised cargo to Rotterdam, Hamburg, or Antwerp are already absorbing EU ETS carbon costs embedded in freight invoices — whether or not their CFOs have mapped this as a Scope 3 cost or a procurement risk.

Who Is Affected

  • Indian merchandise exporters with >15% revenue from EU, UK, or US ocean freight routes — particularly textiles, pharma, chemicals, auto components, and agri-commodities
  • Indian port operators and port trusts: JNPA (Nhava Sheva), Adani Ports (Mundra, Hazira, Kattupalli), DP World India, Vizag Port — all facing infrastructure investment decisions tied to green fuel bunkering (methanol, ammonia, green hydrogen) whose economic case shifts materially with NZF adoption
  • Indian shipbuilders and ship recyclers: Cochin Shipyard, Hindustan Shipyard, Alang yards — competing for new vessel orders where green propulsion specifications are being set by European and Japanese owners anticipating NZF-aligned fuel standards
  • CFOs and Chief Procurement Officers at listed Indian companies with material ocean freight in their Scope 3 Category 4 (upstream transportation) inventory — relevant to BRSR Core disclosures and ISSB IFRS S2 alignment
  • ESG and fixed income investors in Indian port infrastructure, logistics REITs, and shipping-adjacent companies — particularly those benchmarked against ESG indices that are beginning to price IMO transition risk
  • Indian banks and DFIs (SBI, EXIM Bank, IIFCL) with project finance exposure to port terminals and vessel financing, where stranded asset risk rises if NZF adoption forces accelerated retrofitting or early vessel retirement

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • MEPC 84, April 2026: The next IMO vote on NZF adoption — Indian maritime and trade ministries (MoPSW, DGSHIP, MEA) must submit India's formal position before this session; India's vote matters given its status as a major flag state and developing economy beneficiary of the IMO Fund
  • EU ETS for shipping — already in force since January 2024: Vessels of 5,000 GT+ on EU port calls must surrender EU Allowances (EUAs) for 50% of emissions in 2024, rising to 100% by 2026; Indian exporters on EU routes are absorbing this cost now through freight surcharges — CFOs must quantify and disclose this in FY25 annual reports
  • EU FuelEU Maritime Regulation — effective January 2025: Sets GHG intensity limits for energy used by ships on EU routes; escalates 2% below 2020 baseline by 2025, 6% by 2030, 80% by 2050 — directly sets the fuel specification standard for vessels carrying Indian goods to Europe regardless of IMO NZF outcome
  • BRSR Core (mandatory for top 150 listed entities, FY2023-24; top 250 from FY2024-25): Scope 3 emissions disclosure under Principle 6 — CFOs must now decide whether ocean freight emissions (Category 4) are material enough to require quantification; NZF uncertainty makes this a forward-looking risk, not just a historical accounting question
  • ISSB IFRS S2 — India's roadmap to adoption: ICAI and SEBI are monitoring ISSB adoption timelines; IFRS S2 requires disclosure of climate-related risks including transition risks from sector decarbonisation regulations — IMO NZF trajectory is a named transition risk for shipping-dependent Indian companies
  • Ship Recycling: India's Recycling of Ships Act 2019 (Hong Kong Convention aligned) — as older, non-NZF-compliant vessels face accelerated retirement under any post-2026 framework, Alang yards must be positioned to absorb the resulting scrapping volumes; DGSHIP must assess capacity and compliance readiness

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT's core finding — that NZF adoption determines whether shipping settles on a 2°C or 4°C emissions pathway — carries direct implications for India's own Paris Agreement commitments and its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) update cycle. India's updated NDC targets 45% emissions intensity reduction of GDP by 2030 and 50% of cumulative electric power from non-fossil sources. Shipping is not currently disaggregated within India's NDC, but international shipping emissions — by IMO convention — sit outside national inventories. This accounting gap means Indian companies can simultaneously claim NDC alignment domestically while their ocean freight supply chains generate uncounted, unpriced Scope 3 emissions that are material to global temperature outcomes. The transition angle sharpens when you consider India's emerging green hydrogen and green ammonia ambitions. The National Green Hydrogen Mission, with its ₹19,744 crore outlay, explicitly targets maritime fuel exports as a demand driver. India's NTPC, Adani Green, and Greenko are all developing green hydrogen production capacity partly premised on shipping sector offtake as marine fuel. If the NZF is adopted — even in its current 'Insufficient' form — it creates binding demand pull for zero-emission fuels on global shipping routes from 2030, directly validating the investment thesis for Indian green hydrogen producers targeting maritime export markets. A delayed or weakened NZF is, paradoxically, a demand risk for India's green hydrogen ambitions, not just a global climate policy failure. On physical climate risk: shipping route disruption from climate impacts — particularly cyclone intensification in the Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea affecting Indian coastal port operations — is already documented in the physical risk literature. The TNFD's LEAP framework and ISSB IFRS S2 both require assessment of physical climate risk to operations. Indian port operators have not yet systematically integrated IMO transition risk with physical climate risk in their board-level risk registers — this is a governance gap that institutional investors should flag in their stewardship engagement with port companies listed on BSE/NSE.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Treasury — within 60 days: Quantify EU ETS freight surcharge exposure for FY25 and FY26 across all EU-bound ocean freight contracts; treat this as a known, escalating cost line (rising to 100% EUA coverage in 2026), not a contingent liability — budget accordingly and disclose in BRSR Core Scope 3 commentary
  • Chief Sustainability Officer / ESG team — within 90 days: Map Scope 3 Category 4 (upstream transportation) and Category 9 (downstream transportation) for ocean freight lanes; use GHG Protocol Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Standard methodology; flag IMO NZF as a named regulatory transition risk in the company's climate risk register under ISSB IFRS S2 / BRSR Core alignment
  • Procurement / Supply Chain Head — immediately: Engage top 3–5 shipping line counterparties (Maersk, MSC, CMA CGM, Evergreen) to obtain carbon intensity ratings of allocated vessel capacity; require green freight data feeds compatible with CDP supply chain reporting — this protects against Scope 3 audit exposure as BRSR assurance requirements tighten post-FY2025
  • Board Risk Committee — before Q4 FY26 board meeting: Commission a scenario analysis under two pathways — (a) NZF adopted at MEPC 84 April 2026, and (b) NZF further delayed or weakened — assessing freight cost impact, export competitiveness risk, and green hydrogen demand implications; disclose material findings in FY26 Annual Report under BRSR Principle 6
  • Government affairs / policy team — before February 2026: Engage Ministry of Ports, Shipping and Waterways (MoPSW) and the Ministry of External Affairs on India's MEPC 84 submission; India should align with SIDS and LDCs in supporting NZF adoption to protect the IMO Fund mechanism that benefits developing nations — and to preserve the demand signal for India's green hydrogen export strategy
  • Port operators and infrastructure investors — within 6 months: Commission a green bunkering infrastructure feasibility study for methanol and ammonia at JNPA, Mundra, and Paradip; NZF adoption or EU FuelEU Maritime alone will create near-term commercial demand — first-mover advantage in South Asian green bunkering is a real competitive position

Our Take

The IMO NZF postponement is a preview of the fracturing multilateral climate governance architecture that Indian boards and investors must now price into their strategic planning. The US opposition to binding shipping emissions rules is not an aberration — it is consistent with a broader posture of withdrawing from multilateral climate frameworks, from Paris Agreement re-exit to IRA rollbacks. But here is what sophisticated Indian companies should understand: unilateral regulatory action by the EU does not wait for IMO consensus. The EU ETS for shipping is live. FuelEU Maritime is live. The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), while currently covering steel, cement, aluminium, and fertilisers, has an explicit review clause for potential expansion to shipping. India's exporters are already operating under a de facto European shipping emissions regime — the IMO NZF, if and when adopted, would merely globalise what the EU has already unilaterally imposed. The deeper strategic signal is this: the 'by or around 2050' ambiguity in the NZF's net zero formulation — flagged explicitly by CAT — is a red flag for long-dated infrastructure and vessel financing. Indian banks and DFIs financing port terminals with 20–30 year asset lives, or vessel acquisition loans with 15-year tenors, are implicitly underwriting a transition risk they have not yet quantified. The stranded asset risk from premature vessel retirement or bunkering infrastructure obsolescence (LNG being the canonical example, given CAT's concern about NZF locking in LNG) should be stress-tested against MEPC 84 outcomes in every credit committee presentation from Q1 2026 onwards. Looking 12–36 months out: if MEPC 84 adopts the NZF in April 2026 — even in its current insufficient form — it will trigger an immediate renegotiation of long-term shipping contracts, accelerate retrofitting decisions for Indian-flagged vessels, create a commercial bunkering investment race in major South Asian ports, and force every BRSR Core-reporting company with material ocean freight to upgrade its Scope 3 Category 4 methodology from estimate-based to activity-data-based calculation. India's SEBI will face pressure from global institutional investors — already aligned with ISSB S2 — to require shipping transition risk as a named disclosure item in BRSR. The companies that have done the work by April 2026 will be differentiated in capital market conversations. Those that have not will face the same audit scrutiny that drove the first wave of BRSR Core restatements. The window to act is now, not after the IMO vote.
