EU CSRD and EFRAG's Evolving Standards: What Indian Companies Must Know About Europe's Sustainability Reporting Revolution

Executive Summary

The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), underpinned by standards developed by the European Financial Reporting Advisory Group (EFRAG), is reshaping the global landscape of sustainability and financial reporting — and its reach extends well beyond European borders. Indian companies that supply to, invest in, or operate within the EU market are increasingly subject to its disclosure requirements, either directly or through value chain obligations imposed on their European counterparts. Proactive alignment with CSRD and ESRS standards is no longer optional for globally integrated Indian businesses — it is a market access and investor confidence imperative.

What Changed

The EU's CSRD, which came into force in January 2023, replaces the older Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) and dramatically expands the scope, depth, and enforceability of sustainability disclosures for companies operating in or trading with the European Union. EFRAG, the technical body tasked with developing the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), has published a comprehensive set of standards covering climate, environment, social, governance, and sector-specific topics that companies must report against. Unlike previous voluntary or high-level frameworks, ESRS mandates 'double materiality' — meaning companies must disclose not only how sustainability issues affect their financial performance (financial materiality), but also how their operations impact people and the planet (impact materiality). This dual lens fundamentally changes the nature of corporate sustainability disclosure from a reputational exercise to a rigorous, auditable financial reporting obligation. EFRAG continues to publish guidance, Q&As, and implementation support materials to help companies navigate the complexity of ESRS. Sector-specific standards and standards for non-EU companies with significant EU exposure are also in development, signalling that the regulatory perimeter will continue to expand.

Who Is Affected

  • Large EU-listed companies and large EU undertakings (balance sheet > €20M, turnover > €40M, or >250 employees) — already in or entering scope from FY2024 onwards
  • Listed SMEs on EU regulated markets — phased in from FY2026 (with opt-out until 2028)
  • Non-EU parent companies with net turnover > €150M in the EU and at least one large or listed subsidiary/branch in the EU — in scope from FY2028
  • Indian exporters and suppliers to EU-based companies — indirectly affected through value chain disclosure requirements imposed on their European customers
  • Indian companies with European institutional investors or dual-listed entities — subject to investor-driven disclosure expectations aligned with ESRS
  • Indian IT, pharma, textiles, automotive components, and chemicals sectors — among the highest-exposure industries given deep EU trade and investment ties
  • Indian conglomerates and multinationals with European subsidiaries — directly subject to CSRD obligations for those entities

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • FY2024 (reports due 2025): Large EU public-interest entities (>500 employees) already subject to NFRD must report under full ESRS for the first time — their value chain data requests to Indian suppliers begin NOW
  • FY2025 (reports due 2026): Other large EU companies (not previously under NFRD) enter scope — expanding the universe of EU buyers requesting ESRS-aligned sustainability data from Indian value chain partners
  • FY2026 (reports due 2027): Listed SMEs on EU regulated markets enter scope — further broadening supply chain disclosure pressure on Indian MSMEs
  • FY2028 (reports due 2029): Non-EU companies with €150M+ EU turnover and an EU subsidiary/branch must file a CSRD-compliant sustainability report — Indian multinationals should begin gap assessments immediately
  • Ongoing — EFRAG Sector-Specific Standards: EFRAG is developing sector ESRS (e.g., oil & gas, mining, textiles, food & agriculture); Indian companies in these sectors should monitor EFRAG's work programme closely
  • Ongoing — EFRAG Voluntary Standard for SMEs (VSME): Indian SMEs supplying EU customers can use EFRAG's VSME standard to respond to value chain data requests in a structured, proportionate way
  • Double Materiality Assessment: All in-scope companies must conduct and document a formal double materiality assessment — this is the foundational step before any ESRS disclosure can be prepared
  • Third-Party Assurance: CSRD requires limited assurance on sustainability statements from FY2024 reporters, with reasonable assurance expected to follow — Indian entities preparing consolidated reports must factor in audit readiness

Climate Transition Angle

ESRS E1 — the climate change standard — is one of the most detailed and consequential components of the ESRS framework. It requires companies to disclose their transition plans, Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions, climate-related physical and transition risks, and alignment (or misalignment) with a 1.5°C pathway. This directly mirrors the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) and is closely interoperable with the ISSB's IFRS S2 Climate standard — creating a converging global architecture for climate financial disclosure. For Indian companies, the Scope 3 emissions requirement is particularly significant. EU companies must report upstream and downstream value chain emissions, meaning they will request primary emissions data from their Indian suppliers. Companies that cannot provide credible, verified Scope 3 data risk being excluded from EU supply chains or replaced by lower-carbon alternatives — a direct commercial consequence of climate disclosure gaps. India's own BRSR Core framework, mandated by SEBI for the top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24, shares conceptual DNA with ESRS — both require value chain disclosures, assurance, and climate risk reporting. Indian companies that invest in BRSR Core compliance are simultaneously building the capabilities needed to respond to CSRD-driven value chain requests. The convergence of BRSR, ISSB, and ESRS creates a strong strategic rationale for harmonised, integrated sustainability reporting infrastructure.

What To Do Now

  • Map Your EU Exposure: Audit all EU revenue streams, customer relationships, investor bases, and subsidiary structures to determine whether your company faces direct CSRD obligations (as a non-EU parent) or indirect obligations (as a value chain partner) — and by when
  • Conduct a Double Materiality Assessment: Engage your sustainability and finance teams to identify which ESRS topics are material to your business under both the financial and impact lenses — this is the non-negotiable starting point for CSRD alignment and should be documented and defensible
  • Establish Scope 3 Data Collection Protocols: Begin collecting primary activity data across your supply chain to calculate and verify Scope 3 emissions — prioritise categories most relevant to your sector and most likely to be requested by EU customers under ESRS E1
  • Align BRSR Core and ESRS Reporting Infrastructure: Leverage your existing SEBI BRSR Core compliance programme as the foundation for CSRD readiness — identify gaps between BRSR disclosures and ESRS requirements, particularly on climate transition plans, biodiversity (ESRS E4), and workforce due diligence (ESRS S1)
  • Engage Legal and Assurance Partners Early: CSRD sustainability statements require third-party limited assurance — brief your statutory auditors or sustainability assurance providers now on ESRS requirements to avoid bottlenecks when reporting obligations crystallise

Our Take

CSRD and the ESRS standards represent the most consequential shift in corporate reporting since the introduction of mandatory financial audits — and EFRAG's ongoing work to refine, expand, and enforce these standards means the regulatory surface area will only grow. For Indian business leaders, the temptation may be to treat CSRD as a 'European problem' that only affects subsidiaries or compliance teams in Brussels. That view is dangerously shortsighted. The value chain provisions, the non-EU parent company rules, and the sheer weight of EU institutional investor expectations mean that CSRD's gravitational pull will be felt in boardrooms across Mumbai, Bengaluru, and Chennai well before 2028. The companies that begin building double materiality frameworks, Scope 3 data pipelines, and assurance-ready disclosure processes today will not only comply — they will differentiate themselves as trusted, transparent partners in a global economy that is rapidly pricing climate and sustainability risk into every transaction.
