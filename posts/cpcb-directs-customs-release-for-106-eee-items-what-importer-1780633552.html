Executive Summary

On 31 August 2023, the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) issued a formal letter to Customs Authorities directing the release of imported consignments belonging to producers of 106 specified Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) items. This intervention signals that CPCB is actively coordinating with border agencies to enforce EPR compliance as a pre-condition for market access. Companies importing or manufacturing EEE products must treat valid EPR registration — and timely target fulfilment — as a non-negotiable gateway to trade, not merely a back-office reporting obligation.

What Changed

Under India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, producers, importers, and brand owners of Electrical and Electronic Equipment are required to register on the CPCB EPR portal and meet annual e-waste collection and channelisation targets. When a producer fails to demonstrate valid EPR registration or compliance status, Customs Authorities can — and increasingly do — hold consignments at the port of entry. The CPCB letter dated 31 August 2023 represents a specific directive issued on behalf of 106 EEE producers, instructing Customs to release their consignments. This type of letter is typically issued when a producer can demonstrate active EPR registration and/or partial or full target fulfilment, resolving a hold that had been placed on their imports. The significance lies in the precedent: CPCB is functioning as an active gatekeeper between the EPR registry and physical trade flows, meaning lapses in EPR compliance can now directly disrupt supply chains at the border.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers and importers of any of the 106 EEE categories listed under Schedule I of the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — including IT and telecom equipment, consumer electronics, large and small household appliances, lighting equipment, and solar panels.
  • Indian subsidiaries and branches of multinational electronics brands that import finished goods or semi-finished assemblies into India.
  • Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) sourcing components internationally, where the importing entity qualifies as a 'producer' under the Rules.
  • E-commerce platforms and marketplace sellers who qualify as producers under the Rules by virtue of selling EEE products under their own brand or through private labels.
  • Logistics and customs clearance agents acting on behalf of EEE producers — they must be aware of EPR compliance status before filing Bills of Entry.
  • Compliance and legal teams at mid-to-large electronics companies who may not have connected their customs clearance workflows to EPR registration status.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • EPR Registration (Immediate / Ongoing): All producers and importers of EEE items must hold a valid, active EPR registration on the CPCB centralised portal (https://eprewaste.cpcb.gov.in) before importing any consignment. Registration must be renewed and kept current.
  • Annual EPR Targets (Financial Year Basis): Producers must meet e-waste collection targets as specified in their EPR plan. Targets are calculated as a percentage of the quantity of EEE placed on the market in prior years — non-fulfilment triggers hold notices at Customs.
  • EPR Certificate Submission to Customs: At the time of import, producers or their agents should be prepared to furnish EPR registration certificates and compliance status documents to Customs Authorities as proof of adherence.
  • Extended Producer Responsibility Plan Filing: Producers must file an annual EPR plan with CPCB at the start of each financial year, detailing projected sales volumes and corresponding collection commitments.
  • Consignment Release Letters (As Needed): If a consignment is held, producers must proactively engage CPCB with evidence of registration and target compliance to obtain a release letter — a process that can take days to weeks, causing costly demurrage and supply chain disruption.
  • Quarterly / Annual Reporting: EPR-registered producers must submit periodic reports on e-waste collected, channelised to authorised dismantlers/recyclers, and certificates obtained — failures here can trigger future holds.
  • Deadline Reference — 31 August 2023: The CPCB letter was issued on this date for 106 specific producers. Companies not covered by this letter should treat this as a warning signal and self-audit their EPR status immediately.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally, and its proper management sits at the intersection of the circular economy, toxic material control, and climate-related physical risk. Electronic devices contain critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements — whose extraction carries significant carbon and biodiversity costs. By enforcing EPR at the import stage, India is embedding circular economy principles directly into trade infrastructure, ensuring that the end-of-life cost of electronics is internalised by those who profit from their sale. From a global standards perspective, the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) under IFRS S2 requires companies to disclose material climate-related risks across their value chains, including those arising from regulatory non-compliance. A supply chain disruption caused by a Customs hold due to EPR non-compliance is precisely the kind of transition risk — regulatory and operational — that investors and lenders are beginning to scrutinise. Similarly, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its underlying European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) require companies with EU market exposure to report on circular economy metrics, including e-waste management practices in global operations. For Indian EEE producers with export ambitions or foreign investment, demonstrating robust domestic EPR compliance is increasingly a credential, not just a legal obligation. It signals governance maturity, supply chain resilience, and readiness for the global regulatory environment that is rapidly converging on extended producer responsibility as a baseline requirement.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct an immediate EPR compliance audit: Verify that your company's EPR registration on the CPCB portal is active, reflects current product categories and sales volumes, and that your EPR plan for FY 2023-24 has been filed. If you have not yet registered, initiate the process without delay — Customs holds are operational, not theoretical.
  • Integrate EPR status into customs clearance workflows: Brief your customs agents, freight forwarders, and import-export teams that EPR registration certificates must be validated before filing Bills of Entry for EEE goods. Create a pre-shipment checklist that includes EPR compliance as a mandatory clearance criterion.
  • Verify and document e-waste target fulfilment: Confirm with your authorised e-waste dismantlers and recyclers that collection certificates (EPR certificates) have been issued and uploaded to the CPCB portal for all quantities claimed. Gaps here are the most common trigger for Customs holds.
  • Establish a CPCB liaison protocol: Designate a senior compliance officer or engage a regulatory consultant with direct CPCB contact experience. In the event of a Customs hold, the ability to respond within 24-48 hours with the correct documentation can mean the difference between a minor delay and weeks of costly demurrage.
  • Map your product portfolio against the 106 EEE categories: Ensure that every SKU you import or manufacture is correctly categorised under the E-Waste Rules schedules and that corresponding EPR targets are reflected in your plan. Miscategorisation is a common compliance gap that surfaces only at the point of enforcement.

Our Take

The CPCB's August 2023 letter to Customs Authorities is a small but telling document. It confirms what many compliance teams have been slow to accept: EPR in India has moved beyond a registration exercise into an enforcement regime with real teeth at the border. The fact that 106 producers needed a release letter on a single date suggests both that Customs holds are being applied at scale and that CPCB is managing a high volume of compliance-linked trade interventions. Looking ahead, we expect this coordination between CPCB and Customs to deepen — potentially including automated data-sharing between the EPR portal and the ICEGATE customs system, which would make real-time compliance status checks at the point of import a reality. Companies that treat EPR as a strategic compliance priority today — rather than a paperwork afterthought — will have a material operational advantage as enforcement intensifies. For sustainability and ESG officers, this is also a disclosure moment: supply chain disruptions caused by regulatory non-compliance in environmental obligations are precisely the kind of governance failures that institutional investors are flagging in ESG due diligence. Get ahead of it now.
