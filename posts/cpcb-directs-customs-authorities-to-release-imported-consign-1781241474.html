Executive Summary

On June 30, 2023, the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) issued a letter to Customs Authorities directing the release of imported consignments belonging to producers of 106 categories of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) covered under India's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework. This intervention signals that CPCB is actively coordinating with border agencies to ensure that EPR-compliant producers are not unduly penalised at the import stage. Companies importing EEE products or components must ensure their EPR registrations and obligations are current — or risk consignment holds that can disrupt supply chains and operations.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment listed across 106 item categories are required to register on the CPCB EPR portal and meet annual e-waste collection and channelisation targets. Customs Authorities, as part of India's broader regulatory enforcement apparatus, can flag or hold imported consignments of EEE goods when producers are not demonstrably compliant with EPR obligations. The June 30, 2023 letter from CPCB to Customs represents a significant procedural clarification: CPCB has communicated directly to port and customs officials, likely providing a list of registered and compliant producers whose consignments should be released without undue delay. This is a dual-edged action — it rewards compliance by facilitating trade, while implicitly putting non-compliant or unregistered producers on notice that their imports may face scrutiny or detention. In plain terms, if your company imports EEE goods into India and is registered on the CPCB EPR portal with obligations in good standing, this letter works in your favour. If you are not registered or have pending EPR targets, this coordination between CPCB and Customs increases your exposure to import delays and regulatory action.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers, importers, and brand owners of any of the 106 categories of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) listed under Schedule I of the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022
  • Consumer electronics companies importing finished goods such as laptops, mobile phones, televisions, refrigerators, washing machines, and lighting equipment
  • Industrial electronics importers dealing with medical devices, monitoring and control instruments, and IT and telecommunications equipment
  • Component importers whose products are classified as EEE sub-assemblies under the applicable HSN codes
  • Foreign Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) selling into the Indian market through distribution arrangements, who hold producer liability under EPR rules
  • Logistics and customs clearing agents acting on behalf of EEE producers, who need to ensure their clients' EPR documentation is in order before filing bills of entry

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • EPR Registration (Immediate): All producers of the 106 EEE categories must be registered on the CPCB EPR Portal (https://eprewaste.cpcb.gov.in). Non-registered entities importing EEE goods risk consignment detention at customs.
  • Annual EPR Target Filing: Producers must submit their annual EPR targets for e-waste collection and channelisation for FY 2023-24 on the CPCB portal. Targets are based on sales data from previous years.
  • EPR Certificate Procurement: Producers who cannot collect e-waste directly must procure EPR certificates from CPCB-registered recyclers or dismantlers to offset their annual obligations — ensure certificates are current and cover applicable volumes.
  • Customs Documentation Readiness (Immediate): Ensure that your EPR registration certificate and compliance status documents are available to your customs clearing agent for submission at the port of entry, aligned with the CPCB's communication to Customs Authorities.
  • Quarterly Reporting: Producers are required to file quarterly reports on e-waste collected, channelised, and recycled on the CPCB portal — lapses in reporting can flag a company as non-compliant, jeopardising future import clearances.
  • Importer of Record Review: Companies where the importer of record differs from the EPR-registered entity must resolve this discrepancy immediately to avoid mismatch issues at Customs.

Climate Transition Angle

India's EPR framework for e-waste is far more than a domestic compliance checkbox — it sits at the intersection of circular economy policy, climate risk management, and global supply chain sustainability standards that international investors and trading partners increasingly scrutinise. E-waste contains significant quantities of hazardous materials (lead, mercury, cadmium) as well as critical minerals (cobalt, lithium, rare earth elements) that are essential to the clean energy transition — solar panels, EV batteries, and wind turbines all depend on supply chains that intersect with EEE manufacturing. Improper e-waste disposal not only creates toxic environmental liabilities but also represents a systemic loss of materials needed for the low-carbon economy. India's EPR framework, by mandating formal recycling, directly supports the recovery of these critical resources. From a global standards perspective, companies operating in India that also report under the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) framework — specifically IFRS S1 and S2 — or are subject to the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) through their European parent entities or supply chain relationships, will find that e-waste EPR compliance feeds directly into their Scope 3 value chain disclosures and product stewardship narratives. The EU's Ecodesign for Sustainable Products Regulation (ESPR) is also placing circular economy obligations on electronics sold in Europe, creating regulatory convergence that Indian EEE exporters cannot ignore. Getting EPR compliance right in India is not just about avoiding a customs hold — it is about building the operational and data infrastructure to meet multi-jurisdictional sustainability disclosure requirements.

What To Do Now

  • Audit Your EPR Registration Status Immediately: Log into the CPCB EPR portal and verify that your producer registration is active, that your product categories are correctly listed, and that there are no outstanding compliance flags that could trigger a customs hold on incoming shipments.
  • Brief Your Customs Clearing Agents: Share a copy of your CPCB EPR registration certificate and current compliance status with all customs clearing agents and freight forwarders handling your EEE import consignments. Instruct them to reference EPR compliance proactively when filing bills of entry for covered goods.
  • Reconcile Your EPR Targets vs. Actuals: For FY 2022-23, confirm that your e-waste collection and channelisation actuals match or exceed your declared EPR targets. If there is a shortfall, immediately engage a CPCB-registered recycler to procure the necessary EPR certificates before the compliance window closes.
  • Map All 106 EEE Categories to Your Product Portfolio: Conduct an internal product classification exercise to ensure every imported SKU is correctly mapped to the relevant EEE schedule category. Misclassification is a common source of compliance gaps and regulatory exposure.
  • Establish a Cross-Functional EPR Compliance Team: Bring together your sustainability, legal, supply chain, and finance teams to build a quarterly EPR reporting calendar, assign ownership, and integrate EPR data into your broader ESG disclosure and BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report) processes.

Our Take

The CPCB's decision to issue a direct communication to Customs Authorities on behalf of compliant EEE producers is a landmark moment in India's EPR enforcement architecture. For the first time, we are seeing explicit coordination between the country's apex pollution control regulator and its trade border agencies — a sign that EPR is graduating from a self-declaration exercise to a hard-edged trade compliance requirement. Companies that have treated EPR registration as a paperwork formality should treat this notice as a wake-up call: the cost of non-compliance is no longer just a penalty on paper but a very real, very disruptive halt to your import operations. Looking ahead, we expect CPCB to expand this coordination model to other EPR streams — plastic packaging, batteries, and tyres — as those frameworks mature. The companies that invest now in robust EPR compliance systems, real-time portal monitoring, and supply chain traceability will be the ones that avoid costly disruptions and build the credibility that ESG-conscious investors and global customers increasingly demand.
