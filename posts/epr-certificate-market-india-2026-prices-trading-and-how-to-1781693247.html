The EPR Certificate Market in India (2026): Prices, Trading and How to Hit Your Targets

India's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) market has matured from a paperwork obligation into a real, priced market for recycling certificates — worth roughly $1.5 billion in 2025 and projected to reach $5 billion by 2030. This guide explains how the certificate market works across plastic, e-waste, battery and tyre waste, what credits cost today, what changed under the 2026 rules, and exactly how to calculate and meet your obligation using Green Curve's free EPR Market tools.

What "the EPR market" actually means

Extended Producer Responsibility makes producers, importers and brand owners (PIBOs) responsible for recycling the packaging and products they put on the market. You discharge that responsibility by obtaining EPR certificates — digital proof, issued by CPCB-registered recyclers, that a given quantity of waste was collected and recycled. Where you cannot recycle enough yourself, you buy certificates from recyclers who have a surplus. That buying and selling is the EPR market.

Three features define it. First, certificates are category-specific — a rigid-plastic certificate cannot satisfy a multilayer obligation, and a lead-acid battery credit cannot cover lithium-ion. Second, prices sit inside a CPCB price band set at 30%–100% of the environmental-compensation rate, to stop a race to the bottom. Third, the market is now genuinely liquid: roughly 3.7 million tonnes of plastic certificates changed hands in 2023, traded on the CPCB EPR portals and private exchanges.

How EPR credits are generated and traded

The flow is consistent across all four waste streams:

  • Collection — waste is gathered from the market by producers, aggregators or recyclers.
  • Processing — a CPCB-registered recycler processes it and the system issues category-specific EPR certificates.
  • Verification — CPCB/SPCB cross-checks invoices, GST records and third-party reports. GST-compliant e-invoicing has cut verification from about 30 days to 7–10 days.
  • Trading — certificates are listed and bought on CPCB portals and private platforms; credits remain valid into the following year within their category.
  • Reconciliation — PIBOs file annual returns matching certificates held against the obligation.

A word of caution: CPCB flagged over six lakh fake certificates in 2023. Traceability and a clean audit trail — invoices, GST records, recycler registration numbers — are now as important as the price you pay.

What changed in 2026

The Plastic Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2026, notified on 19 January 2026, tightened the regime in three important ways:

  • End-of-Life (EOL) certificates withdrawn — companies can no longer use EOL disposal certificates to meet recycling targets. Real recycling, not disposal, now counts.
  • Mandatory recycled content — rigid plastic (Category I) must contain at least 30% recycled material in FY2025-26, rising to 60% by FY2028-29. This is a supply-side obligation that needs early engagement with recyclers.
  • Limited carry-forward — FY2025-26 shortfalls can be carried forward up to three years, provided at least one-third of the deficit is cleared each year.

How to calculate and meet your obligation

Your obligation is simple in principle: the recycling target percentage applied to the quantity you place on the market. For plastic that target is around 80% of packaging placed on market; for lead-acid batteries it is as high as 90%. The cost is the shortfall (in tonnes) multiplied by the certificate price.

The reason to act early is economics, not just compliance. Environmental Compensation (EC) — what CPCB levies on the unmet portion — is deliberately set higher than the cost of genuine compliance. Buying certificates is almost always cheaper than defaulting, and far cheaper than a blocked import consignment at the port.

EPR feeds your BRSR — treat it as one pipeline

The numbers you generate for EPR are the same numbers SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) asks for. Plastic placed on market and product responsibility map to Principle 2; recycled quantities, certificates and waste circularity map to Principle 6; and any Environmental Compensation paid is material to your penalties disclosure. Companies that run EPR and BRSR as one data pipeline avoid duplicate effort and the embarrassment of inconsistent figures across filings.

Our take

EPR in India has crossed the line from compliance chore to managed cost — a line item with a price curve, a seasonality pattern, and real penalties for getting it wrong. The 2026 amendments push it further still, replacing easy end-of-life shortcuts with genuine recycled-content obligations that ramp every year. The PIBOs who come out ahead will be the ones who quantify their obligation early, procure certificates before the Q4 price spike, lock in recycler relationships for recycled content, and wire the resulting data straight into their BRSR.
