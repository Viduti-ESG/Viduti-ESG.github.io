Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice targeting recyclers and refurbishers operating under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, signalling heightened regulatory scrutiny of downstream EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) value chain actors. This notice reinforces that compliance obligations under India's e-waste framework extend well beyond producers and importers — recyclers and refurbishers are now firmly in the regulatory spotlight. Companies in the e-waste processing ecosystem must act immediately to audit their registrations, EPR certificates, and reporting obligations to avoid penalties, suspension, or cancellation of authorisations.

What Changed

India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — which replaced the earlier 2016 Rules — overhauled the country's approach to managing end-of-life electronics by introducing a formalised EPR framework administered through a centralised online portal managed by CPCB. Under this framework, producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) are required to meet annual e-waste collection and recycling targets, but they can only fulfil these targets by channelling waste through CPCB-registered recyclers and refurbishers. The latest CPCB urgent notice appears to address non-compliance, irregular reporting, or operational irregularities among registered recyclers and refurbishers — entities that serve as the critical last mile in the EPR chain. This could encompass issues such as failure to update capacity declarations, discrepancies in EPR certificate issuance, non-submission of quarterly or annual returns, or operation beyond the scope of granted authorisations. The notice is a clear signal that CPCB is moving from framework-building to active enforcement, and that recyclers and refurbishers can no longer treat compliance as a back-office formality.

Who Is Affected

  • CPCB-registered e-waste recyclers processing IT equipment, consumer electronics, large and small household appliances, lighting equipment, and other notified categories under the 2022 Rules
  • Refurbishers of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) who are registered or required to register under the 2022 Rules
  • Collection centres, dismantlers, and aggregators that feed into the formal recycling chain and may hold ancillary authorisations
  • Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) who depend on registered recycler and refurbisher networks to fulfil their annual EPR targets — their compliance is directly tied to the legitimacy of their recycling partners
  • EPR compliance service providers and intermediaries managing EPR certificate transactions on behalf of PIBOs
  • State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) and State Pollution Control Committees (SPCCs) that co-regulate authorisations at the state level

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Registration & Authorisation Validity: Recyclers and refurbishers must ensure their CPCB registration and state-level authorisation (from SPCB/SPCC) are current, accurate, and reflect actual operational capacity — any lapse or mismatch must be rectified immediately
  • EPR Certificate Issuance: Recyclers are obligated to generate and transfer EPR certificates to PIBOs through the CPCB EPR portal only for quantities actually processed and verified — fraudulent or inflated certificate issuance is a specific enforcement target
  • Quarterly & Annual Returns: Submission of processing data, tonnage handled, and waste disposal records via the CPCB online portal must be completed within prescribed timelines — entities in default should file outstanding returns without delay
  • Capacity Declaration Accuracy: Declared processing capacity must match on-ground infrastructure; CPCB inspections are increasingly being used to verify physical capacity against portal declarations
  • Compliance with Notified E-Waste Categories: Recyclers must process only the categories of EEE for which they hold valid authorisation — processing unauthorised categories is a violation that can trigger cancellation of registration
  • Response to CPCB Notices: Any entity that has received a show-cause notice or direction under this urgent notice must respond within the stipulated timeframe (typically 15–30 days) with documentary evidence of compliance or a corrective action plan
  • Downstream Disposal Records: Recyclers must maintain auditable records of how residual waste, hazardous fractions, and recovered materials are disposed of or sold, in compliance with Hazardous Waste Management Rules

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste regulation is not merely a domestic compliance matter — it sits at the intersection of India's climate commitments, circular economy transition, and global supply chain sustainability standards. The formal recycling of e-waste is essential for recovering critical minerals such as cobalt, lithium, copper, and rare earth elements that are foundational to clean energy technologies, including electric vehicles, solar panels, and energy storage systems. A well-functioning, compliant recycler ecosystem directly supports India's energy transition by reducing dependence on virgin mineral extraction, which carries significant carbon and ecological costs. From a physical climate risk perspective, informal or non-compliant e-waste recycling — which this notice implicitly targets by strengthening formal sector oversight — contributes to soil and groundwater contamination, particularly in climate-vulnerable geographies. As India faces increasing climate stress, protecting land and water resources through proper e-waste processing is a material environmental risk management imperative. On the global standards front, multinational companies sourcing from or operating in India are increasingly required under frameworks such as the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosures, and the EU Battery Regulation to demonstrate responsible end-of-life management of products placed on global markets. Indian recyclers and refurbishers that achieve and maintain strong CPCB compliance will be better positioned to serve as verified partners in these global supply chains. Conversely, non-compliant recyclers risk being excluded from the formal EPR ecosystem entirely, losing access to the growing volume of e-waste being channelled through regulated routes.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct an immediate compliance audit: Review your CPCB portal account, registration status, authorisation expiry dates, and all pending return filings — identify and close any gaps before a regulatory inspection or notice reaches you
  • Reconcile EPR certificate data: Cross-check all EPR certificates issued against actual processing records and tonnage data; flag and investigate any discrepancies, and do not issue certificates for unprocessed or unverified quantities
  • Engage your legal and environmental compliance team: If you have received a CPCB notice or show-cause order, engage qualified environmental law counsel immediately and prepare a factual, documented response within the prescribed timeline
  • Upgrade your data management systems: Invest in digital record-keeping for intake, processing, residue disposal, and certificate issuance — CPCB's enforcement is increasingly data-driven, and paper-based systems will not withstand scrutiny
  • If you are a PIBO, verify your recycling partners now: Audit the registration and compliance status of every recycler and refurbisher in your EPR network — your own EPR target fulfilment is only as strong as the legitimacy of the certificates your partners generate

Our Take

This CPCB notice is a watershed moment for India's formal e-waste sector. Since the 2022 Rules were notified, the EPR certificate market has grown rapidly — but so have concerns about certificate fraud, capacity inflation, and shell recyclers issuing credits without genuine processing activity. CPCB's decision to issue an urgent notice specifically targeting recyclers and refurbishers signals that the regulator is no longer willing to allow the formal framework to be undermined by bad actors. We expect this to be the beginning of a sustained enforcement wave: inspections will increase, authorisations of non-compliant entities will be suspended or cancelled, and the knock-on effect will be felt by PIBOs whose EPR targets rest on certificates from now-invalidated recyclers. The medium-term outcome — a leaner, more credible formal recycling sector — is the right direction for India's circular economy ambitions. But the short-term disruption will be real, and only those recyclers and refurbishers who have invested in genuine compliance will emerge stronger. For producers and brands, this is also a wake-up call to treat recycler due diligence as a core procurement and ESG risk management function, not an afterthought.
