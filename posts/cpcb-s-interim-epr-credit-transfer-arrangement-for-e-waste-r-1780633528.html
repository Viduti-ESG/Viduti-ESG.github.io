CPCB's Interim EPR Credit Transfer Arrangement for E-Waste Recyclers: What Producers Must Know

Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice establishing an interim arrangement for the generation and transfer of EPR end-product credits (certificates) by e-waste recyclers, signalling a critical transitional phase in India's e-waste compliance ecosystem. Producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) registered under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 must pay close attention, as the validity and traceability of their EPR certificates — the backbone of their compliance proof — are directly implicated. Companies that rely on recycler-issued credits to meet their annual EPR targets face immediate operational and documentation risks if they do not adapt to the interim protocol.

What Changed

Under India's E-Waste Management Rules, 2022, producers of electrical and electronic equipment are legally obligated to meet Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) targets — essentially, ensuring that a prescribed quantity of end-of-life e-waste is collected and channelled to authorised recyclers. The mechanism for proving compliance is the EPR certificate, or 'end-product credit,' which authorised recyclers generate on the CPCB's centralised EPR portal and transfer to the respective producer or importer. The CPCB's notice indicates that there is a transitional or technical gap in the existing portal-based system for credit generation and transfer — likely related to system upgrades, reconciliation of recycler registrations, or data integrity concerns on the EPR portal. To prevent a compliance vacuum during this period, CPCB has introduced an interim arrangement that prescribes a specific, time-bound process for recyclers to generate and transfer these credits outside of or alongside the standard digital workflow. In plain terms: the rules of how e-waste recyclers issue proof-of-recycling certificates to producers are temporarily modified. Both recyclers and producers must follow the interim process precisely — failing to do so could result in credits that are not recognised by CPCB for annual EPR target fulfilment, exposing producers to non-compliance penalties.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) of electrical and electronic equipment listed under Schedule I of the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 — including consumer electronics, IT and telecom equipment, and large/small household appliances
  • Authorised E-Waste Recyclers registered on the CPCB EPR portal who generate and transfer end-product credits to PIBOs
  • E-Waste Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) that aggregate EPR obligations and credits on behalf of multiple producers
  • Refurbishers and dismantlers in the e-waste value chain whose outputs feed into the recycler credit system
  • Compliance and sustainability teams at large manufacturing conglomerates, electronics multinationals with Indian operations, and fast-moving consumer electronics brands
  • Environmental auditors and consultants who verify EPR compliance documentation on behalf of producers

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • E-waste recyclers must follow the CPCB-prescribed interim process for generating end-product credits — do not rely solely on the standard portal workflow until CPCB confirms the permanent arrangement is restored or updated
  • All credit transfers conducted during the interim period must be documented with supporting records (weight slips, invoices, recycling certificates) that can be presented to CPCB during audits or target verification
  • Producers and PROs must confirm with their recycling partners that credits issued during this interim phase are being generated in accordance with CPCB's notice — verbal assurances are insufficient; obtain written confirmation and copies of interim certificates
  • PIBOs should cross-verify that interim credits received are reflected or will be reconciled in the official CPCB EPR portal against their annual targets — proactively seek CPCB clarification if reconciliation timelines are unclear
  • Do not defer e-waste recycling activity or credit generation pending resolution of the interim arrangement — EPR annual targets and their associated deadlines remain unchanged under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022
  • Monitor the CPCB website (cpcb.nic.in) and EPR portal notifications actively for the withdrawal of the interim arrangement and restoration of standard credit generation procedures
  • Deadline for annual EPR target compliance: as per the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022, producers must fulfil targets for each financial year — ensure interim credits are secured well before the close of the relevant compliance year

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste EPR is not merely a domestic regulatory checkbox — it sits at the intersection of circular economy policy, climate risk, and global sustainability reporting standards. Electronic equipment contains critical minerals such as cobalt, lithium, copper, and rare earth elements whose extraction is both carbon-intensive and geopolitically sensitive. A robust, credible EPR credit system ensures that these materials are recovered and re-enter the supply chain, directly reducing Scope 3 emissions associated with virgin material extraction for producers. From a climate transition perspective, any disruption or opacity in the EPR credit system undermines the integrity of circular economy claims that Indian companies are increasingly making in their sustainability reports. Under the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) framework — specifically IFRS S2 — and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), companies with global investors or European customers will face scrutiny over the traceability and verifiability of their waste management and circularity data. An interim credit arrangement that lacks clear audit trails could compromise the quality of data feeding into these disclosures. Additionally, physical climate risks — such as flooding of recycling facilities or heat-stress-driven operational disruptions — can further strain the recycler network. Companies should view this interim notice as a prompt to assess the resilience of their entire EPR supply chain, not just its current regulatory adequacy. The transition to a low-carbon, circular economy in India's electronics sector depends on a credit infrastructure that is both technically robust and climate-resilient.

What To Do Now

  • Immediately contact your authorised e-waste recycling partners and PROs to confirm their awareness of the CPCB interim notice and verify that they are issuing credits in full compliance with the interim arrangement — request copies of all interim certificates issued on your account
  • Assign your compliance or legal team to download and review the original CPCB notice from the official source (cpcb.nic.in) and prepare an internal FAQ or briefing note for your operations, finance, and sustainability teams on how EPR credit documentation will be affected during this period
  • Conduct an immediate audit of your EPR credit position for the current financial year — calculate the gap between credits already secured and your annual target, and accelerate recycling activity if necessary to avoid a year-end shortfall exacerbated by interim processing delays
  • Engage your sustainability reporting team to flag this interim arrangement as a data quality risk in your ESG disclosures — if you report against GRI, BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting), or are preparing for ISSB-aligned disclosure, ensure that EPR credit traceability documentation is audit-ready
  • Establish a direct communication channel with CPCB's EPR cell for timely updates on when the standard credit generation and transfer process will resume, and document all correspondence for your compliance records

Our Take

CPCB's interim arrangement notice is a symptom of a larger growing pain: India's EPR infrastructure for e-waste is scaling rapidly, but the underlying digital systems and verification mechanisms are still catching up with the ambition of the 2022 Rules. This is not a reason for alarm, but it is a reason for vigilance. The companies that will be caught off-guard are those treating EPR compliance as a once-a-year reconciliation exercise rather than an active, year-round supply chain management discipline. We expect CPCB to continue tightening EPR enforcement — including stricter audits of recycler capacity claims and credit validity — as India aligns its circular economy framework with global standards ahead of international trade and investment scrutiny. The interim notice is, in effect, a stress test of your EPR supply chain's robustness. Use it as such.
