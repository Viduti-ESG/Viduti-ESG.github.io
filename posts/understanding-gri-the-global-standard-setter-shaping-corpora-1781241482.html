Executive Summary

The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) is the world's most widely adopted sustainability reporting framework, providing companies with a structured, stakeholder-focused methodology to disclose environmental, social, and governance (ESG) impacts. As India's regulatory landscape evolves — with SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) increasingly aligning with global norms — understanding GRI's foundations is no longer optional for Indian businesses seeking credibility with international investors and supply chain partners. Companies that proactively adopt GRI Standards position themselves ahead of tightening disclosure mandates and build lasting trust with stakeholders.

What Changed

The Global Reporting Initiative was founded in 1997 in the aftermath of the Exxon Valdez oil spill, with a mandate to create a common language for organisations to report on their sustainability impacts. Over nearly three decades, GRI has evolved from a niche environmental reporting guideline into the de facto global baseline for sustainability disclosure, used by over 10,000 organisations across 100+ countries. In 2021, GRI released its landmark updated Universal Standards — GRI 1 (Foundation), GRI 2 (General Disclosures), and GRI 3 (Material Topics) — which came into mandatory effect for GRI reporters from January 1, 2023. These updated standards shifted the framework's philosophy firmly toward 'impact materiality': requiring companies to report on how their operations affect the economy, environment, and people — not just how ESG factors affect the company financially. This is a critically important distinction that sets GRI apart from financially-oriented frameworks like ISSB/IFRS S1 and S2. GRI operates as an independent international organisation headquartered in Amsterdam, with a global network of offices and partners. It collaborates formally with the ISSB, EFRAG (which governs CSRD in Europe), and the UN, making it a central pillar of the global sustainability disclosure architecture that Indian companies must navigate.

Who Is Affected

  • Large listed Indian companies (NSE/BSE) subject to SEBI's BRSR Core requirements, where GRI alignment can streamline compliance
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational corporations headquartered in the EU, UK, or US — where parent companies face mandatory GRI-aligned or GRI-compatible reporting obligations under CSRD, UK SDR, or SEC climate rules
  • Export-oriented manufacturers and suppliers whose global customers (especially in Europe and North America) require GRI-based sustainability disclosures as part of supply chain due diligence
  • Indian companies seeking international ESG-linked financing, green bonds, or sustainability-linked loans — where lenders and rating agencies use GRI disclosures as a benchmark
  • Mid-sized companies preparing voluntarily for future regulatory expansion, particularly as SEBI has indicated BRSR will be extended beyond the top 1,000 listed companies
  • ESG consultants, CFOs, sustainability officers, and board-level risk committees across sectors including manufacturing, IT, pharmaceuticals, infrastructure, and financial services

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • GRI Universal Standards 2021 (GRI 1, 2, and 3) are now mandatory for all organisations claiming to report 'in accordance with GRI Standards' — this has been in effect since January 1, 2023
  • Companies must conduct a formal materiality assessment aligned with GRI 3 to identify and prioritise material topics before selecting applicable topic-specific standards (e.g., GRI 302 for Energy, GRI 305 for Emissions, GRI 403 for Occupational Health & Safety)
  • GRI 2 requires comprehensive general disclosures including governance structure, strategy, policies, and stakeholder engagement — all of which must be updated to reflect the 2021 standards in any report published after January 1, 2023
  • Organisations reporting under GRI must publish a GRI content index — a structured table mapping each disclosure to the relevant section of the report — to ensure verifiability and comparability
  • While GRI reporting itself is voluntary globally (except where mandated by specific jurisdictions), SEBI's BRSR framework explicitly references GRI Standards as an acceptable international framework for supplementary disclosures, creating a de facto compliance pathway for Indian listed companies
  • Companies seeking to align with CSRD (applicable to Indian subsidiaries of EU-headquartered firms or large Indian companies with EU operations) must note that ESRS standards have significant overlap with GRI — early GRI adoption reduces future compliance burden
  • No single global statutory deadline for GRI adoption exists, but companies with EU nexus face CSRD reporting timelines beginning FY2025 for the largest companies, cascading to smaller entities by FY2026-2028

Climate Transition Angle

GRI's relevance to India's climate transition cannot be overstated. The framework's topic-specific standards — particularly GRI 305 (Emissions), GRI 302 (Energy), GRI 303 (Water), and GRI 201 (Economic Performance) — provide the granular disclosure architecture that investors, regulators, and civil society need to assess whether Indian companies are genuinely transitioning to low-carbon business models or merely engaging in greenwashing. Critically, GRI's impact materiality approach is complementary — not competitive — to the ISSB's financial materiality approach (IFRS S1 and S2). The ISSB and GRI signed a formal collaboration agreement in 2022, establishing that the two frameworks together form a 'building blocks' approach to comprehensive sustainability reporting. For Indian companies, this means GRI disclosures on how operations impact climate systems (e.g., Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions) can be paired with ISSB-aligned disclosures on how climate risks impact financial performance — giving stakeholders a complete picture. From a physical risk perspective, GRI disclosures on water stress, biodiversity, and supply chain impacts are increasingly material for Indian companies in sectors like agriculture, textiles, and cement, which are acutely exposed to monsoon variability, heat stress, and resource scarcity. Investors using TCFD-aligned analysis increasingly cross-reference GRI disclosures to validate physical risk assessments. As India advances its National Action Plan on Climate Change and deepens its NDC commitments, GRI-aligned reporting will be a key mechanism for demonstrating private sector accountability in the transition.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a GRI-aligned double materiality assessment: Map your company's significant impacts on the environment and society (impact materiality) alongside the ESG risks and opportunities that affect your financial performance (financial materiality). This single exercise anchors both GRI and ISSB/BRSR Core compliance simultaneously.
  • Audit your existing BRSR disclosures against GRI 2 (General Disclosures) and GRI 3 (Material Topics): Many Indian companies will find significant overlap — identify gaps systematically and build a disclosure roadmap that satisfies both SEBI and GRI requirements without duplicating effort.
  • Identify applicable GRI topic-specific standards for your sector: Use GRI's Sector Standards (currently available for Oil & Gas, Coal, Agriculture, Mining, and Financial Services, with more in development) to ensure your disclosures reflect industry-specific material topics that generic templates miss.
  • Engage your supply chain and value chain partners on GRI-aligned data collection: Scope 3 emissions, supplier labour practices, and water usage across the value chain are increasingly required under GRI 308, GRI 414, and GRI 305-3. Begin building data systems and supplier questionnaires now, before regulatory pressure makes this urgent.
  • Seek third-party assurance on key GRI disclosures: As SEBI has mandated reasonable assurance for BRSR Core metrics, aligning your GRI content index with assured data points builds investor credibility and pre-empts future mandatory assurance requirements under evolving Indian and global regulations.

Our Take

GRI is not just a reporting framework — it is the foundational language of global sustainability accountability, and Indian companies that treat it as a peripheral, voluntary exercise are underestimating a significant strategic risk. We are entering a period where sustainability disclosures are rapidly transitioning from voluntary best practice to regulatory requirement, and the companies that have invested in robust GRI-aligned systems will adapt to each new mandate — BRSR Core, CSRD, ISSB — with speed and confidence, while their competitors scramble. What makes GRI particularly powerful for Indian businesses is its dual utility: it satisfies domestic regulators who reference it in BRSR guidance, and it satisfies international investors and supply chain customers who have been using it for decades. The next five years will see India's disclosure ecosystem converge significantly with global standards. Our view is that GRI adoption today is not compliance overhead — it is competitive infrastructure.
