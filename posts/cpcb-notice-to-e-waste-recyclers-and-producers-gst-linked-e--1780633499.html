Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued a binding notice requiring all e-waste recyclers to generate EPR Certificates exclusively through GST-linked e-invoices, and mandating that producers accept only such GST-authenticated invoices as valid proof of recycling. This tightens the traceability and accountability framework under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, closing a significant loophole where unverified paper records could be used to fulfil Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations. Companies that fail to align their invoicing and record-keeping systems with this requirement risk invalidation of their EPR credits and potential non-compliance penalties.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, notified by the Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change on November 2, 2022 (G.S.R 801(E)), established a digital EPR Portal through which recyclers must generate EPR Certificates — the documents that prove to producers that a certain quantity of e-waste has been responsibly recycled. Until now, the specific format and authentication standard for the underlying invoices was not explicitly enforced. The CPCB's notice dated June 15, 2023, issued by the WM-III Division, closes this gap with two clear mandates: First, recyclers must now maintain records of all sales and purchases — covering both recovered end-products (such as metals, plastics) and raw e-waste inputs — using e-invoices generated through the GST Portal. Second, producers must only accept GST-linked invoices from recyclers as proof of recycling when claiming EPR compliance. In essence, the EPR ecosystem is now anchored to India's GST infrastructure, making every transaction digitally traceable and cross-verifiable by tax and environmental authorities simultaneously.

Who Is Affected

  • Registered E-Waste Recyclers: All recyclers registered on the CPCB EPR Portal who process e-waste and sell recovered materials — regardless of scale or geography.
  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE): All companies that manufacture or import EEE covered under the E-Waste Rules and are obligated to meet annual EPR targets for e-waste collection and recycling.
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) Compliance Teams: In-house sustainability, legal, and finance teams at electronics OEMs, IT hardware companies, consumer electronics brands, and white goods manufacturers.
  • EPR Compliance Service Providers & PROs: Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) that aggregate EPR obligations and work with recyclers on behalf of producers.
  • CFOs and Finance Teams at Recycling Units: Since GST e-invoicing requirements may mandate a minimum turnover threshold compliance under GST law, finance teams must assess their e-invoicing eligibility and readiness.
  • Importers and Brand Licence Holders: Companies importing EEE into India and holding EPR obligations under the Rules.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE — Recyclers must generate GST-linked e-invoices for ALL sales and purchases of recovered materials (end-products) and raw e-waste through the GST Portal, with effect from the notice date of June 15, 2023.
  • IMMEDIATE — Recyclers must link these GST e-invoices to their EPR Certificate generation process on the CPCB EPR Portal. EPR Certificates generated without this documentation linkage may be considered invalid.
  • IMMEDIATE — Producers must update their internal procurement and EPR compliance verification protocols to only accept GST-authenticated invoices from recyclers as proof of recycling. Paper invoices or non-GST records will no longer suffice.
  • ONGOING — Recyclers must maintain auditable records of all GST e-invoices related to e-waste transactions and make these available for CPCB inspection or EPR audit purposes.
  • ONGOING — Producers must reconcile EPR credits claimed on the portal against GST-verified recycler invoices, ensuring no discrepancy between reported recycling quantities and authenticated transaction records.
  • REVIEW — Finance and compliance teams at recycler units must verify whether they meet the GST e-invoicing turnover threshold (currently ₹5 crore and above as per GST law) and take steps to comply with GST e-invoicing regulations if not already doing so.

Climate Transition Angle

This regulatory tightening is far more than an administrative housekeeping measure — it sits at the intersection of India's circular economy ambitions and its commitments under global climate frameworks. E-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally, and the responsible recovery of critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, copper, rare earth elements — from e-waste is increasingly recognised as essential to building a resilient, low-carbon economy. Without credible traceability in recycling chains, the circular economy narrative risks becoming 'greenwashing by default.' From a supply chain risk perspective, global buyers and investors are increasingly demanding proof of responsible material sourcing. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standards both require companies to report on supply chain Scope 3 emissions and resource circularity. Indian exporters and suppliers to multinational corporations will face growing pressure to demonstrate that their EPR compliance is backed by verifiable data — exactly what GST-linked e-invoicing enables. Physical climate risks are also relevant here: extreme heat, flooding, and erratic monsoons disrupt informal e-waste collection and recycling operations disproportionately. As India's formal recycling sector grows under regulatory pressure, companies investing in formalised, GST-compliant recycling partners are simultaneously building supply chain resilience against these climate-driven disruptions. The CPCB's move to anchor EPR to GST infrastructure is, in this sense, a foundational step toward climate-aligned material traceability.

What To Do Now

  • Audit Your Recycler Network Immediately: Producers should contact all empanelled recyclers and PROs to confirm they are GST-registered, e-invoice compliant, and have updated their processes on the CPCB EPR Portal. Any recycler unable to provide GST e-invoices should be flagged as a compliance risk.
  • Update Internal EPR Verification Protocols: Revise your EPR proof-of-recycling acceptance policy in writing — legal and compliance teams should formally document that only GST-linked e-invoices will be accepted, and communicate this to procurement and sustainability teams.
  • Integrate GST and EPR Portal Workflows (Recyclers): If you are a recycler, work with your IT or ERP team to ensure that e-invoices generated on the GST Portal can be referenced directly when uploading EPR Certificate data on the CPCB portal. Avoid manual data re-entry, which increases error and audit risk.
  • Conduct a Gap Assessment on Historical EPR Credits: Review EPR credits already claimed or pending for FY 2022-23 and assess whether the underlying recycler invoices meet the new GST e-invoice standard. Where gaps exist, consult legal counsel on remediation steps before the next compliance cycle.
  • Engage Your CFO and Tax Team: Since this requirement bridges environmental and tax compliance, ensure your CFO and GST/tax team are aware of the obligation. For recyclers near or below the e-invoicing turnover threshold, proactively assess whether voluntary GST e-invoicing adoption is needed to remain a credible EPR partner.

Our Take

This notice from CPCB is deceptively significant. On the surface, it looks like a procedural update about invoicing formats. In reality, it represents a structural shift in how India's EPR ecosystem will function: by tethering environmental compliance to the GST backbone, regulators are creating a dual-verification system where tax authorities and environmental enforcement agencies can cross-reference data simultaneously. This makes fraudulent EPR certificate generation — a known problem in the e-waste sector — exponentially harder. For responsible producers and recyclers, this is welcome news; it levels the playing field and protects the integrity of the EPR market. For laggards still relying on informal or paper-based recycling arrangements, the window to get compliant is closing rapidly. We expect CPCB to follow this notice with enforcement actions targeting EPR certificates that cannot be backed by GST-verified transactions, and we anticipate further integration of EPR compliance data with MCA and SEBI disclosure requirements as India's ESG regulatory architecture matures. Companies should treat this not as a one-time compliance task, but as a signal to invest in robust, digitally traceable EPR supply chains now.
