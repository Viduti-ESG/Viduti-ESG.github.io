Executive Summary

The Climate Action Tracker's latest analysis (climateactiontracker.org/blog/highlighting-the-hypocrisy-fossil-fuel-export-emissions/) exposes a foundational flaw in global climate accounting: the emissions embedded in fossil fuel exports — coal, oil, and gas — are counted neither in the exporting country's NDC nor in the importing country's production-side ledger, creating a $multi-trillion accountability vacuum that regulators are now beginning to close. For India, the world's second-largest coal importer and a major consumer of Australian thermal coal and LNG from the US and Australia, this is not an abstract geopolitical debate — it is a direct supply-chain carbon risk, a Scope 3 disclosure obligation under BRSR Core, and an emerging country-of-origin trade policy risk that CFOs and CSOs at power utilities, steel producers, aluminium smelters, and cement manufacturers must quantify now. The CAT report identifies Australia, the US, Canada, Norway, and the UK as countries whose exported emissions dwarf or substantially exceed their domestic emission reductions — the 'iceberg effect'. India is the primary destination market for several of these export flows, meaning Indian importers are sitting atop the submerged portion of this iceberg. As ISSB IFRS S2 adoption accelerates and EU CBAM expands its product scope, the carbon embedded in India's imported fossil fuel feedstock will become a hard financial number on balance sheets — not a sustainability footnote.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (CAT), authored by Sofia Gonzales-Zuniga, Danial Riaz, and Mia Moisio, and published at https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/highlighting-the-hypocrisy-fossil-fuel-export-emissions/, presents a granular 2022 dataset showing that the top 10 fossil fuel exporting nations account for approximately 60% of total exported fossil fuel combustion emissions globally. Russia, Australia, the US, Indonesia, Saudi Arabia, and Canada collectively represent 50% of these exported emissions. The CAT's central argument — and its regulatory significance — is that current national emissions accounting under the UNFCCC framework assigns combustion emissions to the consuming country, meaning exporting nations bear zero NDC liability for the downstream carbon consequence of their exported fossil fuels. This accounting asymmetry directly undermines the COP28 'transition away' commitment and the reaffirmation at the UN General Assembly in September 2024. For India, this has three concrete and immediate implications. First, India's power sector, which remains heavily dependent on imported thermal coal from Australia and Indonesia (two of the top exporters identified by CAT), is exposed to an intensifying global policy push to price, restrict, or disclose exported fossil fuel emissions. Australia's coal exports are projected to remain at near-record highs through at least 2030 (per DCCEEW 2024 data cited in the CAT report), and the US is expected to significantly increase LNG exports to 2050 per US EIA 2023 projections — both of which are major supply lines into India's energy system. Second, as international pressure mounts on fossil fuel exporters to internalise export emission costs — through carbon border mechanisms, export levies, or bilateral trade conditionalities — the landed cost of imported coal and LNG into India will rise, creating fuel cost volatility and stranded asset risk for Indian utilities and industrial consumers. Third, and most immediately for listed Indian companies, SEBI's BRSR Core framework — mandatory for the top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24, extending to the top 1,000 from FY2024-25 — already requires Scope 3 value chain emission disclosure on a 'reasonable assurance' basis for the largest filers. The GHG Protocol Category 1 (purchased goods and services) and Category 3 (fuel and energy-related activities) are directly triggered by imported fossil fuel consumption, meaning the carbon volume that exporting countries refuse to account for domestically will now appear — mandatorily — on Indian companies' BRSR disclosures.

Who Is Affected

  • Power generation utilities (thermal): NTPC, Adani Power, Tata Power, JSW Energy — all with imported coal exposure from Australia and Indonesia; direct Scope 3 Category 1 and 3 obligations under BRSR Core
  • Steel producers: JSW Steel, Tata Steel, SAIL, JSPL — dependent on coking coal imports primarily from Australia; CAT's analysis of Australia's elevated coal export trajectory directly affects feedstock carbon intensity and EU CBAM cost exposure from FY2026 onwards
  • Aluminium and cement sectors: Hindalco, Vedanta, UltraTech Cement — energy-intensive sectors with significant imported fossil fuel or imported electricity exposure; BRSR Core Scope 3 Category 8 (upstream leased assets and processing of sold products) is relevant
  • LNG importers and city gas distribution companies: Petronet LNG, GAIL, Adani Total Gas — US LNG import volumes set to rise materially; CAT flags US exported gas emissions growing at one of the fastest rates globally, raising long-term supply security and carbon cost questions
  • CFOs at BSE 500 listed companies in energy-intensive sectors: responsible for quantifying fuel import carbon costs under BRSR Core and preparing for ISSB IFRS S2-aligned disclosures as India's ICAI sustainability standards (INBR framework under development) approach mandatory status
  • Chief Sustainability Officers and ESG Heads: must now incorporate 'fossil fuel export policy risk' as a named scenario in TCFD-aligned climate risk registers and BRSR Core principle-by-principle disclosures
  • Institutional investors: domestic MFs, insurance companies, and FPIs with holdings in power, metals, and energy — the CAT analysis is a leading indicator of policy-driven fuel cost volatility and stranded asset crystallisation risk
  • Export-oriented Indian manufacturers supplying EU markets: the EU CBAM's current scope (steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, hydrogen, electricity) will expand; as European buyers tighten their own Scope 3 Category 1 supply chain audits under CSRD/ESRS E1, Indian suppliers using high-carbon imported coal inputs will face qualification risk

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core — Scope 3 GHG disclosure (including upstream fossil fuel categories): mandatory for top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24 (already in effect); extending to top 1,000 listed entities from FY2024-25 (i.e., disclosures due in Annual Reports filed by September 2025)
  • BRSR Core — reasonable assurance on Scope 3 by third-party assurance providers: SEBI has signalled phased mandatory assurance; boards must ensure assurance-readiness of Scope 3 Category 1 and 3 data by FY2025-26 at latest
  • EU CBAM — transitional reporting phase ended December 2025; definitive carbon pricing on imports of covered products (steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers) begins January 2026: Indian exporters to EU must now have certified embedded carbon intensity data for all covered products, including carbon in imported fossil fuel inputs
  • ISSB IFRS S2 — climate-related financial disclosures including Scope 3 material emission sources: jurisdictions adopting ISSB standards are accelerating (Australia from 2025, UK from 2025, Singapore from 2025); Indian companies with cross-listed securities or institutional FPI investors face effective ISSB S2 alignment pressure now even ahead of formal ICAI adoption
  • SBTi corporate net zero targets — for Indian companies with validated SBTi targets (currently 80+ Indian companies): SBTi's Corporate Net Zero Standard requires Scope 3 Category 1 (purchased fossil fuels) to be addressed in value chain decarbonisation plans; CAT's report strengthens the case for SBTi to tighten its fossil fuel procurement guidance in the next standard review cycle
  • India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) — MoEFCC notified framework in June 2023; PAT Cycle VII ongoing: as India's domestic carbon market matures, the carbon cost of imported fossil fuels will increasingly interface with domestic carbon credit pricing, requiring CFOs to model both simultaneously
  • CDP disclosure cycle 2025 (responses due July 2025): CDP's Climate questionnaire C6 (Scope 3 emissions) and C4 (targets) will increasingly scrutinise whether companies have quantified and disclosed fossil fuel import-related upstream emissions; non-disclosure is now a red flag for CDP scoring

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT analysis crystallises a transition risk that Indian boards have been able to treat as theoretical: the carbon embedded in imported fossil fuels is moving from an unaccounted externality to a priced, disclosed, and potentially trade-restricted liability. India's energy transition pathway under its updated NDC — targeting 500 GW of non-fossil capacity by 2030 and net zero by 2070 — is built on the premise of a managed coal phase-down over multiple decades. But the CAT data reveals that the major coal-exporting nations supplying India (Australia, Indonesia) have no credible policy trajectory to reduce export volumes before 2030, and Australia explicitly sees LNG exports as a 'transition fuel' role extending beyond 2050. This means the physical coal and gas supply will remain available and competitively priced — but the financial and regulatory cost of using it will escalate non-linearly. For Indian steel and power companies with EU export exposure, the CBAM carbon price on embedded emissions in exported steel — calculated on actual embedded carbon, not sector benchmarks, from 2026 — will for the first time put a hard euro price on the decision to use Australian coking coal versus green hydrogen-based direct reduced iron. At current EU ETS carbon prices (~€60-70/tonne), the CBAM surcharge on a tonne of Indian blast-furnace steel with Australian coal inputs could reach €80-120 per tonne by 2028, potentially exceeding the steel's own margin. This is the transition risk made financially concrete. BRSR Core's requirement to disclose Scope 3 Category 11 (use of sold products) and Category 1 (purchased goods) in the same document as financial statements means Indian CFOs can no longer treat this as a sustainability team problem — it is a P&L forecast variable. The CAT's spotlight on the 'iceberg effect' of exported emissions will accelerate the push within ISSB, GHG Protocol, and future UNFCCC stocktake negotiations to assign producer-country carbon liability for exported fossil fuels, which would directly alter the geopolitical economics of India's coal import agreements.

What To Do Now

  • CFO and Finance team (within 60 days): Commission a formal Scope 3 Category 1 and Category 3 carbon inventory for all imported fossil fuel volumes — coal, LNG, crude oil — disaggregated by country of origin. Map this against the CAT's export trajectory data for Australia, Indonesia, and the US to model a 3-year landed carbon cost scenario under CBAM and potential domestic CCTS pricing
  • CSO and Sustainability team (within 90 days): Update the company's BRSR Core Scope 3 disclosure methodology to explicitly address GHG Protocol Category 1 (purchased goods — fossil fuels) and Category 3 (fuel and energy-related activities upstream emissions). Ensure the methodology is assurance-ready and aligned with ISSB IFRS S2 paragraph 29 (Scope 3 screening and materiality assessment)
  • Procurement and Supply Chain Head (within 6 months): Initiate supplier-level carbon intensity data collection from Australian and Indonesian coal suppliers and US/Australian LNG counterparties. This is both a BRSR Core data requirement and a CBAM compliance prerequisite for any steel, aluminium, or cement exported to EU markets
  • Board Risk Committee (next board meeting): Incorporate 'fossil fuel export policy risk' as a named and quantified item in the board-level climate risk register. Reference the CAT analysis and COP28 'transition away' commitment as the policy trigger. Request management to present a scenario analysis under a 'carbon border adjustment on fossil fuel exports by exporting nations' stress case — this is the tail risk that is now moving into the foreseeable horizon
  • Investor Relations team (ahead of next AGM / investor day): Prepare a dedicated briefing note for institutional investors and ESG analysts on how the company's imported fossil fuel exposure is being managed, quantified, and disclosed. FPIs and domestic institutional investors subject to SEBI's stewardship code are increasingly required to engage on Scope 3 exposure; pre-empt the question with a credible, data-backed answer
  • Legal and Compliance team (within 120 days): Review all long-term coal and LNG import contracts for carbon price pass-through clauses, force majeure definitions that may cover carbon border measures, and renegotiation rights triggered by material regulatory change. The EU CBAM's definitive phase from January 2026 may constitute a material contract risk event for existing supply arrangements

Our Take

The CAT's fossil fuel export emissions analysis is not simply an advocacy document — it is a pre-regulatory signal of where the next phase of international climate finance and trade policy is heading, and Indian boards that read it only as geopolitical commentary will be caught unprepared. Here is the trajectory I expect over the next 24-36 months: First, the UNFCCC's Global Stocktake process and the upcoming COP30 in Belém (2025) will face intense pressure from climate-vulnerable nations — many of them in South and Southeast Asia, India's neighbourhood — to close the exported emissions accounting gap. If even a soft disclosure obligation is agreed for exporting nations' upstream fossil fuel production, it will immediately trigger a repricing of long-term supply contracts and alter the risk premium on coal and LNG assets globally. Second, the EU CSRD's ESRS E1 standard — which requires companies to report on their entire value chain's GHG emissions, including upstream fossil fuel extraction — will force European buyers of Indian manufactured goods to push carbon intensity disclosures back up their supply chains to Indian producers, who will in turn need to disclose the origin-specific carbon intensity of their imported coal and gas inputs. This creates a cascade of Scope 3 data demands that Indian companies are not yet systematically collecting. Third, within India, I expect SEBI to tighten BRSR Core assurance requirements and potentially introduce mandatory Scope 3 Category 1 quantification thresholds for the top 500 listed companies by FY2026-27 — aligned with ICAI's forthcoming India Sustainability Reporting Standards (INBR), which are expected to closely track ISSB S1/S2. The companies that have built robust Scope 3 import carbon accounting infrastructure by then will have a material compliance and capital cost advantage. The deeper strategic question for Indian industrial companies is this: the fossil fuel export supply they currently depend on is not going to become cleaner or cheaper — the political economy of the exporting nations makes that clear from the CAT data. But the regulatory and financial cost of consuming that supply is going to rise sharply and non-linearly. The companies that treat this as a transition finance challenge — investing now in fuel switching, green hydrogen feasibility, and supply chain decarbonisation — will access cheaper green capital from DFIs, multilateral climate funds, and ESG-benchmarked FPIs. Those that wait for regulatory compulsion will face a simultaneous hit of rising fuel carbon costs, CBAM border adjustments, and a cost of capital premium. Boards should decide now which side of that equation they want to be on.
