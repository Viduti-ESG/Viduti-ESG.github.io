India's 2nd Amendment to Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2022: What Producers, Importers & Brand Owners Must Know

Executive Summary

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) notified the Plastic Waste Management (Second Amendment) Rules, 2022 on July 7, 2022, introducing critical definitional clarity and strengthening the Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework for plastic packaging. The amendment formally defines key concepts — including biodegradable plastics, plastic packaging, post-consumer and pre-consumer plastic packaging waste, waste-to-energy, and recycled plastic use — bringing India's regulatory vocabulary closer to global standards. Producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) must take immediate note: EPR obligations under Schedule II are now explicitly mandatory, and registration requirements with the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) have been tightened for entities operating across multiple states.

What Changed

The Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2016 have been amended for the second time in 2022 (the first amendment came in January 2022). This amendment, effective from the date of its publication in the Gazette — July 7, 2022 — makes targeted but consequential changes across Rules 3, 4, and 9 of the parent rules. At its core, the amendment does three things. First, it dramatically expands the definitional framework in Rule 3 by adding or refining over a dozen terms. These include 'biodegradable plastic' (distinct from compostable plastic, certified by CPCB under BIS standards, and required to degrade without leaving microplastics or toxic residues), 'plastic packaging,' 'post-consumer plastic packaging waste,' 'pre-consumer plastic packaging waste,' 'plastic waste processor,' 'recycler,' 'reuse,' 'use of recycled plastic,' 'waste-to-energy,' and 'end-of-life disposal.' This definitional expansion is not merely academic — it creates clear legal categories that determine which obligations apply to which actors. Second, the amendment updates Rule 4 on prohibitions and restrictions: references to biodegradable plastics are now included alongside compostable plastics in provisions relating to carry bags and packaging, and the applicable Indian Standard is updated from IS 17088:2008 to IS/ISO 17088:2021, aligning with the latest international compostability standard. Third, and most critically for business compliance, Rule 9 on EPR is substantially revised. Sub-rule (1) is replaced to make explicit that producers, importers, and brand owners shall fulfil EPR obligations for plastic packaging as specified in Schedule II. References to collection plans and a two-year implementation window have been removed, signalling that the grace period is over — EPR is now a present, enforceable obligation. The amendment also clarifies that entities operating in more than two states or union territories must register with the CPCB (not just state pollution control boards), centralising oversight for larger national players.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers: Manufacturers of plastic packaging materials, plastic carry bags, multilayer packaging, and plastic sheets — regardless of sector
  • Importers: Any entity importing plastic packaging, products packaged in plastic, carry bags, multilayer packaging, or plastic sheets into India
  • Brand Owners (including those with registered trademarks): Companies that sell products under their own brand in plastic packaging — this now explicitly includes trademark holders
  • Plastic Waste Processors: Units engaged in recycling, co-processing (e.g., in cement or steel plants), waste-to-oil conversion, or waste-to-energy operations
  • Recyclers: Units involved in the recycling of plastic waste, including industrial composting
  • Fast-Moving Consumer Goods (FMCG) sector: High exposure due to large volumes of plastic packaging across product categories
  • E-commerce companies: Significant users of plastic packaging for logistics and delivery — now more clearly captured under the brand owner definition
  • Retail and organised trade: Entities using carry bags or packaging under their own brand or trademark
  • Multi-state businesses: Companies operating across more than two states or union territories must now register with CPCB centrally, not just with state boards

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Effective immediately (July 7, 2022): All provisions of the Second Amendment Rules came into force on the date of Gazette notification — there is no phased implementation grace period
  • EPR compliance — mandatory now: Producers, importers, and brand owners must fulfil EPR targets for plastic packaging as per Schedule II of the PWM Rules; the earlier reference to a collection plan and two-year window has been deleted, confirming EPR is a live obligation
  • CPCB registration — multi-state operators: Entities operating in more than two states or union territories must register with the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB); single-state operators continue to register with the relevant State Pollution Control Board (SPCB) or Pollution Control Committee (PCC) under Rule 13(2)
  • Biodegradable plastic compliance: Producers or sellers of biodegradable plastics (as newly defined) must ensure CPCB certification and conformity with IS/ISO 17088:2021 (updated from IS 17088:2008); products must not generate microplastics or toxic residues under ambient environmental conditions
  • Trademark holders: Companies whose products are sold under registered trademarks are now explicitly included in the definition of 'brand owner' — such entities must assess their EPR exposure and register accordingly if not already done
  • Post-consumer and pre-consumer waste tracking: With formal definitions now in place, businesses should establish systems to separately account for and report pre-consumer (manufacturing stage) and post-consumer (end-user stage) plastic packaging waste — this data will be critical for EPR target calculations and CPCB audits
  • End-of-life disposal pathways: Companies channelling plastic waste to co-processors, waste-to-energy plants, or waste-to-oil units must ensure these facilities fall within the newly defined categories and comply with applicable guidelines; feedstock chemical production for plastic remanufacturing is separately classified as recycling

Climate Transition Angle

India's tightening of plastic waste regulation is not happening in isolation — it is part of a global regulatory convergence on plastic pollution that carries direct climate implications. Plastics are derived predominantly from fossil fuels; the production, incineration, and landfilling of plastic waste contributes to greenhouse gas emissions across the entire value chain. The amendment's formal recognition of waste-to-energy, co-processing, and waste-to-oil pathways — while practical transition mechanisms — must be read alongside India's net-zero commitments and the need to eventually phase down fossil-fuel-derived materials. For businesses reporting under frameworks such as the ISSB's IFRS S1/S2 standards or preparing for alignment with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — increasingly relevant for Indian exporters to European markets — plastic waste management is a material topic under both physical risk (pollution, ecosystem degradation) and transition risk (policy, regulatory, and market shifts toward circular economy models). The new definitions around recycled plastic use and the distinction between pre- and post-consumer waste directly mirror the language used in CSRD-aligned value chain disclosures and the EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation (PPWR). The push for higher recycled content in plastic packaging, implicit in these rules, also connects to supply chain resilience: companies that develop domestic recycled plastic supply chains reduce their exposure to virgin polymer price volatility — itself linked to global oil markets and carbon pricing trends. Early movers who build robust EPR compliance systems will be better positioned as India inevitably tightens recycled content mandates and aligns with the global plastics treaty currently being negotiated under UNEA.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your EPR registration status immediately: Confirm whether your organisation is registered under the PWM Rules as a producer, importer, or brand owner — and check whether you qualify as a 'multi-state operator' requiring CPCB-level (rather than SPCB-level) registration. If not registered, initiate the process without delay as non-compliance carries enforcement risk
  • Map your plastic packaging footprint using the new definitions: Conduct an internal classification exercise using the newly defined categories — plastic packaging, post-consumer waste, pre-consumer waste, and recyclate use — to accurately quantify your EPR targets under Schedule II and prepare for CPCB reporting cycles
  • Review product claims on biodegradable and compostable plastics: If your packaging carries biodegradable or compostable claims, verify that these materials are CPCB-certified and conform to IS/ISO 17088:2021 (not the older IS 17088:2008 standard); non-conforming claims now carry heightened legal exposure
  • Engage your plastic waste processing partners: Verify that your recyclers, co-processors, and waste-to-energy partners meet the definitions and regulatory requirements under the amended rules; update contracts and due diligence checklists to reflect the new legal categories, and obtain documentation of their own registrations
  • Build EPR data infrastructure for climate disclosure alignment: Begin tracking plastic packaging volumes, recycled content use, and waste recovery rates in a manner consistent with both CPCB reporting requirements and emerging global disclosure standards (ISSB IFRS S2, CSRD, Global Plastics Treaty obligations); companies that integrate EPR data into their sustainability reporting now will avoid costly retrofitting as disclosure mandates tighten

Our Take

The Second Amendment to India's Plastic Waste Management Rules is, in substance, a regulatory maturation moment. The deletion of the two-year collection plan window and the explicit restatement of EPR as a mandatory obligation under Schedule II sends an unambiguous signal: the CPCB and MoEFCC are moving from framework-building to enforcement. The new definitional architecture — particularly the distinctions between biodegradable and compostable plastics, pre- and post-consumer waste, and the formal recognition of waste-to-energy and recycled content use — gives regulators the precise legal tools they need to hold businesses accountable in ways that were previously difficult to operationalise. For Indian companies with significant plastic packaging footprints, the window for a 'wait and watch' approach has firmly closed. More significantly, businesses should anticipate that these rules are a stepping stone: India is an active participant in the UN Global Plastics Treaty negotiations, and domestic rules will need to evolve to reflect whatever binding international obligations emerge — likely including mandatory recycled content targets, design-for-recyclability standards, and extended producer liability for transboundary plastic pollution. Companies that build adaptive, data-driven EPR compliance systems now will find themselves structurally ahead when the next wave of tightening arrives.
