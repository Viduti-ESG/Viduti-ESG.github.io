Executive Summary

The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), operating under the IFRS Foundation, has established two landmark global standards — IFRS S1 and IFRS S2 — that are rapidly becoming the baseline for sustainability and climate-related financial disclosures worldwide. Indian companies with international operations, foreign investors, or global supply chain relationships are already feeling indirect pressure to align with these standards, even before a domestic mandate arrives. With SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework increasingly converging toward ISSB principles, and major trading partners like the EU and UK adopting compatible regimes, the window for proactive preparation is narrowing fast.

What Changed

The ISSB was established in 2021 at COP26 in Glasgow and released its first two standards — IFRS S1 and IFRS S2 — in June 2023, marking a historic consolidation of fragmented sustainability reporting frameworks (TCFD, SASB, CDSB, and others) into a single, coherent global baseline. IFRS S1 sets out general requirements for companies to disclose material information about sustainability-related risks and opportunities across their entire value chain. It requires companies to report on governance, strategy, risk management, and metrics and targets — the same four-pillar architecture that underpins TCFD. IFRS S2 goes further, specifically addressing climate-related disclosures, including both physical risks (floods, droughts, extreme heat) and transition risks (carbon pricing, stranded assets, shifting market demand). Crucially, IFRS S2 mandates Scope 1, 2, and 3 greenhouse gas emissions disclosure and scenario analysis — tools that many Indian companies are only beginning to adopt. The International Accounting Standards Board (IASB) works in parallel to ensure that sustainability disclosures under ISSB standards are connected to and consistent with financial statements prepared under IFRS Accounting Standards, creating an integrated view of a company's financial health and climate exposure. This 'connectivity' principle is a significant shift from treating sustainability as a separate, non-financial exercise — it places climate risk squarely inside the financial reporting infrastructure.

Who Is Affected

  • NSE/BSE-listed companies in the top 1,000 by market capitalisation already subject to SEBI's BRSR Core disclosures — ISSB alignment is the logical next step
  • Indian subsidiaries and joint ventures of multinational corporations headquartered in jurisdictions adopting ISSB-aligned standards (EU, UK, Australia, Japan, Singapore, Canada)
  • Export-oriented manufacturers supplying to European or North American customers subject to CSRD or SEC climate disclosure rules — Scope 3 supplier data will be demanded
  • Indian companies seeking international capital — foreign institutional investors (FIIs) and ESG-focused funds increasingly screen on ISSB-aligned metrics
  • Financial institutions, banks, and asset managers whose loan books and portfolios carry climate-exposed assets — physical and transition risk assessment is increasingly expected
  • Mid-size companies in high-emitting sectors: steel, cement, chemicals, textiles, automotive, and real estate, regardless of current listing status, as supply chain disclosure obligations cascade downward

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IFRS S1 & S2 effective date: Annual reporting periods beginning on or after 1 January 2024 — jurisdictions adopting these standards are now in their first reporting cycle
  • India (SEBI BRSR Core): Top 150 listed companies must provide reasonable assurance on BRSR Core KPIs from FY 2023-24; top 1,000 companies follow on a comply-or-explain basis — ISSB concepts are embedded in these KPIs
  • Scope 1 & 2 GHG emissions disclosure: Required under IFRS S2 from Year 1 of adoption; Scope 3 disclosures permitted one-year relief in most jurisdictions but expected from Year 2
  • Climate scenario analysis: Companies must disclose how their business strategy performs under at least two climate scenarios, including a 1.5°C or 2°C pathway — many Indian companies have not yet conducted this exercise
  • Industry-specific metrics: IFRS S2 references SASB industry standards for sector-specific disclosures; companies should identify their applicable SASB standard immediately
  • Value chain disclosures: Companies must use 'reasonable and supportable' information available about their full value chain — not just owned operations — placing new data demands on procurement and supplier management teams
  • Third-party assurance: While not universally mandated on Day 1, jurisdictions and investors are moving rapidly toward requiring limited and then reasonable assurance on sustainability disclosures — build internal controls now

Climate Transition Angle

ISSB standards are not a reporting exercise in isolation — they are the financial architecture of the global climate transition. By requiring companies to disclose how climate risks affect their cash flows, asset values, and cost of capital, IFRS S1 and S2 create the conditions under which capital markets can price climate risk accurately and redirect investment toward resilient, low-carbon business models. For Indian companies, the physical risk dimension is particularly urgent. India faces some of the world's most severe climate hazards — extreme heat affecting labour productivity, erratic monsoons disrupting agricultural supply chains, coastal flooding threatening port and logistics infrastructure, and water scarcity constraining manufacturing. IFRS S2's requirement to assess and disclose physical risks means companies can no longer treat these as external, unquantifiable events — they must be integrated into financial planning and asset valuation. On the transition risk side, India's own Net Zero commitments, the carbon border adjustment mechanisms being introduced by the EU (CBAM), and the global shift in investor preferences are creating a new competitive landscape. Companies that can demonstrate credible transition plans — with science-based targets, capital expenditure aligned to decarbonisation, and transparent Scope 3 emissions data — will access cheaper capital and retain international customers. Those that cannot will face higher financing costs, supply chain exclusion, and reputational risk. ISSB standards are the language in which this competitive differentiation will be expressed. The convergence between ISSB, CSRD (EU), SEC climate rules (USA), and SEBI BRSR is not accidental — it reflects a coordinated global effort to make climate risk visible in financial markets. Indian companies that prepare for ISSB alignment are simultaneously positioning themselves for compliance across multiple regulatory regimes.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a gap assessment: Map your current BRSR, GRI, or TCFD disclosures against the four pillars of IFRS S1 and S2 (Governance, Strategy, Risk Management, Metrics & Targets) to identify disclosure gaps and data deficiencies — prioritise Scope 3 and scenario analysis as the highest-gap areas for most Indian companies
  • Establish your GHG emissions baseline: If you have not yet completed a comprehensive GHG inventory covering Scope 1, 2, and material Scope 3 categories, commission one immediately using the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard — this is the non-negotiable foundation for IFRS S2 compliance and investor credibility
  • Run at least one climate scenario: Engage a sustainability advisory firm or use publicly available tools (IEA, NGFS scenarios) to assess how your business strategy holds up under a 1.5°C transition scenario and a high-physical-risk scenario — document assumptions, data sources, and financial implications
  • Engage your CFO and Board: ISSB standards explicitly require Board-level governance of climate risk — sustainability cannot remain siloed in CSR or EHS teams. Brief your Audit Committee on IFRS S2 requirements, assign a Board member with climate oversight responsibility, and integrate climate risk into your Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) framework
  • Map your supply chain exposure: Identify Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers in high-emission or climate-vulnerable sectors, begin collecting activity data, and develop a supplier engagement programme — both because IFRS S2 requires it and because your multinational customers will demand it within 12–24 months

Our Take

The ISSB standards represent the most significant development in corporate reporting since the introduction of mandatory financial auditing — and their arrival in India's disclosure ecosystem is a matter of 'when,' not 'if.' SEBI has made clear through successive iterations of BRSR that India's large listed companies will be held to globally comparable sustainability disclosure standards, and the ISSB framework is the international benchmark against which Indian regulation will continue to converge. More immediately, the pressure is coming not from domestic regulators but from global capital markets: foreign institutional investors managing trillions of dollars are embedding ISSB alignment into their due diligence checklists, and Indian companies that cannot provide credible, assured, ISSB-compatible data risk being screened out of ESG mandates and green bond markets. Our view is that the next 18 to 24 months are the critical preparation window — companies that invest now in GHG accounting infrastructure, scenario analysis capabilities, and Board-level climate governance will not only achieve compliance but will build a genuine competitive advantage as climate disclosure becomes as routine and expected as financial reporting.
