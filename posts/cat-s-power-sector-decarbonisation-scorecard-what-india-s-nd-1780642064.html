Executive Summary

The Climate Action Tracker's May 2025 virtual event — 'Plugging into Progress: Evaluating Power Sector Decarbonisation for 1.5°C' — delivered a pointed verdict: the COP28 pledge to triple global renewables capacity by 2030 is structurally at risk because most countries' new NDCs are not yet translating political commitment into credible, quantified power sector targets. For India, this is not an abstract global concern — India's own NDC 2.0, submitted in 2022, targets 50% cumulative electric power installed capacity from non-fossil sources by 2030, and the pace of grid-scale solar, wind, and storage deployment will determine whether India is ranked among CAT's 'gathering speed' performers or flagged as a laggard. That determination directly affects sovereign climate credibility, green bond pricing, export supply chain eligibility under EU CBAM and CSRD, and the ESG ratings of every Indian listed company with a reported Scope 2 footprint. CFOs and CSOs at BSE 500 companies should read this as an early warning: the credibility of India's national power decarbonisation trajectory is now a material input into company-level ESG assessments, BRSR Core disclosures, and SBTi target validation — and the gap between NDC ambition and actual grid reality is where investor scrutiny is intensifying.

What Changed

Reporting by the Climate Action Tracker (climateactiontracker.org — https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/virtual-event-pulling-into-progress-power-decarbonisation/) on their 21 May 2025 webinar reveals a new, structured evaluation framework for country-level power sector decarbonisation, specifically designed to assess whether national NDCs — which governments are currently finalising and submitting in 2025 ahead of COP30 in Belém, Brazil — are consistent with the 1.5°C pathway and the COP28 Global Renewables and Energy Efficiency Pledge. That pledge, signed by 130+ countries including India, commits to tripling global renewable energy capacity to at least 11,000 GW by 2030 and doubling the rate of energy efficiency improvement. What is new and consequential is the sectoral granularity of CAT's evaluation: rather than assessing only national economy-wide emissions, the framework now disaggregates by sector — beginning with power — and evaluates whether the specific policies, grid investment plans, and capacity auction pipelines within that sector are sufficient to meet the NDC and 1.5°C alignment thresholds. This shift from economy-wide to sector-specific NDC accountability is exactly the direction that ISSB IFRS S2, the GHG Protocol's Scope 2 guidance, and SBTi's sector-specific pathways (including its Power Sector Science-Based Target methodology) are also moving. For India specifically, the CAT framework will evaluate whether India's 500 GW non-fossil capacity target by 2030 — the headline figure in India's updated NDC — is backed by a credible policy and investment stack. As of mid-2025, India has approximately 220 GW of installed renewable capacity, meaning it must add roughly 280 GW in five years, a pace that requires not just auction announcements but grid infrastructure, storage mandates, land clearance reform, and transmission investment that current policy execution has not consistently delivered at scale. The second-order implication for Indian companies is this: CAT's country-level power sector ratings feed directly into frameworks that institutional investors use to assess sovereign climate risk — including Climate Policy Initiative analyses, MSCI sovereign ESG scores, and the inputs used by foreign portfolio investors to set country-level ESG overweights or underweights. A CAT finding that India's power sector NDC is 'insufficient' or 'critically insufficient' relative to 1.5°C does not stay in a policy document — it migrates into FPI risk models, green bond spread calculations, and the Scope 2 emissions factors that companies must use under the GHG Protocol's market-based and location-based accounting methods.

Who Is Affected

  • Power and utilities sector: NTPC, Adani Green, Tata Power, CESC, Torrent Power, NHPC — companies whose entire business model is the subject of this decarbonisation evaluation and whose capital expenditure plans are now scrutinised against 1.5°C sector pathways
  • Energy-intensive industries with large Scope 2 footprints: steel (JSW, Tata Steel), aluminium (Hindalco, Vedanta), cement (UltraTech, Shree Cement), chemicals, and data centres — all of whom depend on the pace of grid decarbonisation to reduce their Scope 2 emissions and meet SBTi or BRSR Core intensity targets
  • Indian exporters to the EU subject to CBAM (carbon border adjustment) and CSRD supply chain disclosure requirements — for whom the carbon intensity of India's grid is a direct cost variable starting with CBAM's full enforcement from January 2026
  • CFOs and treasury teams at listed companies issuing or planning green bonds, sustainability-linked bonds, or accessing climate-labelled credit facilities — where India's grid decarbonisation credibility is a pricing input
  • Chief Sustainability Officers and BRSR preparers at SEBI-regulated top 1,000 listed entities — who must disclose Scope 2 emissions under BRSR Core and will face investor questions about grid emission factor trajectories
  • ESG analysts and fund managers at domestic and foreign institutional investors — including pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and ESG-dedicated AUM — who are incorporating CAT country scores into portfolio climate risk assessments
  • SEBI, MoEFCC, and BEE policy teams — who will need to respond to CAT's evaluation if India's power sector is rated as misaligned, particularly as India prepares its position for COP30 in November 2025

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • India's NDC 2.0 target — 50% cumulative installed electric power capacity from non-fossil sources by 2030 — is a sovereign commitment under the Paris Agreement; Indian listed companies' Scope 2 reduction targets are implicitly dependent on the government delivering this; CSOs should formally track and disclose this dependency in BRSR Core Principle 6 responses
  • SEBI BRSR Core (mandatory for top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24, top 250 from FY2024-25): Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG emissions must be disclosed with assurance; the grid emission factor used for Scope 2 calculation must be disclosed and justified — CAT's evaluation of India's grid trajectory is directly relevant to the credibility of these disclosures
  • EU CBAM full enforcement from January 2026: Indian exporters of steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, and electricity must report and pay for embedded carbon using verified emissions data; the carbon intensity of India's grid directly determines the Scope 2 component of embedded emissions in manufactured exports
  • COP30, Belém, Brazil — November 2025: India's revised or confirmed NDC and power sector commitments will be assessed in real time by CAT and other trackers; this is the credibility checkpoint that institutional investors will watch for sovereign and company-level ESG rating implications
  • SBTi Power Sector pathway: Indian power companies seeking SBTi validation must demonstrate alignment with a sector-specific decarbonisation trajectory consistent with 1.5°C; CAT's new evaluation framework uses compatible methodology and will serve as a cross-reference point for SBTi validators
  • BEE's Perform Achieve and Trade (PAT) Scheme Cycle 7 targets and the proposed Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) under MoEFCC's January 2023 notification — both require power sector benchmarking against efficiency and emissions intensity metrics that will be stress-tested against CAT's sector evaluation

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT power sector evaluation sits at the precise intersection of physical climate risk and transition risk for Indian business. On the transition side: the speed of India's grid decarbonisation determines the trajectory of every Indian company's Scope 2 emissions — which under ISSB IFRS S2 (adopted voluntarily by several Indian companies and referenced in SEBI's BRSR Core design) must be disclosed alongside climate-related financial risks. If India's grid decarbonisation falls behind the 1.5°C pathway, Indian companies face a structural Scope 2 emissions problem that no amount of renewable energy certificates (RECs) or captive solar can fully offset at scale — particularly for heavy industries where grid dependence is unavoidable for baseload operations. This is not a 2035 problem; it is showing up right now in SBTi validation rejections, green finance term sheet conditions, and EU CSRD value chain disclosure obligations. On the physical risk side, India's power sector is acutely exposed to climate-driven disruptions: hydropower output volatility due to monsoon variability (a documented risk to NHPC and other hydro operators), thermal plant efficiency losses during extreme heat events (materially affecting coal plant capacity factors), and transmission infrastructure stress during peak summer demand. CAT's evaluation, by focusing on the 1.5°C-aligned decarbonisation pathway, implicitly highlights the cost of inaction: a grid that remains coal-heavy into the 2030s is simultaneously a high-transition-risk grid and a physically vulnerable one. For boards applying TCFD or ISSB S2 scenario analysis, both the 1.5°C transition scenario (rapid coal phase-down, stranded asset risk for coal-linked companies) and the 3°C physical risk scenario (grid reliability failure, cooling load surge) must be modelled — and CAT's sectoral framework provides the benchmark trajectories for the transition scenario. Indian companies with coal-linked power purchase agreements, coal mine captive plants, or significant coal sector equity exposure should treat a CAT 'insufficient' rating on India's power sector as a trigger to update their stranded asset provisioning and transition plan disclosures under BRSR Core.

What To Do Now

  • Sustainability/ESG team (immediate — Q2 FY2026): Download and review CAT's power sector evaluation slides and methodology; map India's current CAT rating against your company's Scope 2 baseline and SBTi or internal net zero target trajectory; identify the grid emission factor assumption embedded in your BRSR Core Scope 2 disclosure and stress-test it against a scenario where India's grid decarbonises 20% slower than NDC implies
  • CFO/Treasury (within 90 days): For any green bond, SLB, or climate-labelled facility with KPIs linked to Scope 2 intensity reductions, assess whether the KPI design is robust to grid decarbonisation delay; renegotiate or disclose material dependency on national grid trajectory in sustainability-linked financing documentation
  • Procurement/Supply Chain Head (ahead of CBAM full enforcement, January 2026): Quantify the Scope 2 component of embedded carbon in EU-bound exports using India's current grid emission factor (CEA's latest: approximately 0.716 tCO2/MWh for FY2022-23); model the financial impact of CBAM carbon pricing at €50–€80/tCO2 on export margins; initiate captive renewable or open-access RE procurement to reduce grid dependence for export-linked manufacturing
  • Board Risk Committee (next scheduled meeting): Commission a formal scenario analysis presentation covering India's power sector decarbonisation risk under 1.5°C and 2.5°C pathways, using CAT's framework as the reference; ensure this is reflected in the company's TCFD/ISSB S2 climate risk disclosure in the next annual report
  • Investor Relations (before COP30, October 2025): Prepare a proactive ESG investor briefing note that addresses India's NDC power sector commitments, your company's Scope 2 reduction roadmap, and your independence from or exposure to grid carbon intensity — international ESG investors will be asking this question more pointedly as COP30 approaches
  • Government Affairs/Policy Engagement: Engage through industry bodies (CII, FICCI, IEEMA) to advocate for accelerated transmission infrastructure investment, storage mandate implementation, and RPO (Renewable Purchase Obligation) enforcement — these are the policy levers that determine whether India lands on CAT's 'gathering speed' or 'not delivering' list, and that determination has direct capital market consequences for Indian industry

Our Take

CAT's move to sector-specific NDC evaluation — starting with power — is not a research exercise. It is the architecture for the next generation of climate accountability, and it is arriving at exactly the moment when India is finalising its COP30 positioning, when ISSB S2 sector-specific guidance is being embedded into national disclosure frameworks, and when EU CBAM is shifting from transition to enforcement phase. The power sector is the right place to start because it is the foundation of every other sector's decarbonisation: you cannot decarbonise Indian steel, cement, chemicals, or data centres without decarbonising the grid. India's 500 GW non-fossil target by 2030 is credible in headline terms but fragile in execution terms — transmission buildout, storage procurement, land acquisition, and domestic manufacturing of solar modules all face well-documented bottlenecks. If CAT formally rates India's power sector as 'insufficient' against 1.5°C in its published tracker (a real possibility given current execution gaps), the consequences will not be confined to diplomatic embarrassment at COP30. They will flow into MSCI and Sustainalytics country-level ESG adjustments, into green bond spread pricing for Indian sovereign and corporate issuers, and into the SBTi validation pipeline for Indian companies whose Scope 2 trajectories are implicitly underwritten by the NDC grid target. The 12–36 month horizon I am watching: SEBI's anticipated update to BRSR to incorporate ISSB S2-aligned climate scenario disclosure requirements; MoEFCC's rollout of the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme and its intersection with PAT cycle outcomes in the power sector; and the emergence of a grid-level carbon accounting standard for India that reconciles CEA's annual emission factors with the real-time, marginal emission factors that sophisticated Scope 2 accounting and CBAM compliance will eventually require. Boards that treat power sector decarbonisation as someone else's problem — a utility or government issue — are mispricing the transition risk sitting inside their own Scope 2 number.
