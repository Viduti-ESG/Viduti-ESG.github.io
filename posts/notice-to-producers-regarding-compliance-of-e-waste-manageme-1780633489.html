Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice to producers covered under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022, flagging non-compliance with Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations. Producers who manufacture or import electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) are legally required to register on the EPR portal, set annual collection targets, and ensure end-of-life management of their products through authorised recyclers. Failure to comply exposes companies to regulatory action, penalties, and reputational risk — making immediate remediation a business priority.

What Changed

The E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 replaced the earlier 2016 framework and significantly strengthened India's approach to managing electronic waste. The 2022 Rules introduced a mandatory, market-based EPR mechanism under which producers must obtain EPR authorisation from the CPCB, register on the centralised EPR portal, set product-category-wise collection targets, and purchase EPR certificates from registered dismantlers and recyclers to demonstrate fulfilment of those targets. CPCB's latest urgent notice signals that a material number of producers have either failed to register on the EPR portal, have not filed required returns, or have not met their prescribed collection targets for the applicable financial year. This notice is a formal enforcement signal — not merely a reminder — and indicates that CPCB is moving from awareness-building to active compliance monitoring and potential prosecution. Companies that remain non-compliant risk having their EPR authorisation cancelled, facing show-cause notices, and being named publicly by the regulator.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE): Any company that manufactures, imports, or sells EEE in India — including consumer electronics, IT hardware, telecom equipment, lighting, and large/small household appliances — falls under this notice.
  • Brand Owners and OEMs: Companies that brand or market EEE products, even if manufacturing is outsourced, are classified as 'producers' under the Rules and bear EPR obligations.
  • E-commerce platforms and importers: Online marketplaces facilitating sale of EEE, as well as importers, carry producer-equivalent responsibilities for products sold through their channels.
  • Mid-sized and large enterprises: While smaller producers may have phased targets, medium-to-large companies with significant market volumes face the highest absolute collection obligations and are likely the primary audience for this enforcement notice.
  • Registered Dismantlers and Recyclers: Although the notice targets producers, recyclers and dismantlers in the EPR certificate ecosystem must ensure their documentation and certificate issuance processes are audit-ready to support producer compliance.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • EPR Portal Registration (Immediate): Producers not yet registered on the CPCB EPR portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in) must complete registration without delay. Operating without EPR authorisation is a direct violation of the 2022 Rules.
  • Annual EPR Target Setting: Producers must log collection targets for each product category on the portal for the current financial year (FY 2024-25), calculated as a percentage of quantity of EEE placed on the market in the preceding years as per the Rules' prescribed trajectory.
  • EPR Certificate Procurement: Producers must purchase EPR certificates from CPCB-authorised recyclers/dismantlers equivalent to their collection targets. Certificates must be linked to the producer's EPR account on the portal.
  • Annual Return Filing: Producers are required to file annual returns on the EPR portal detailing EEE placed on the market, e-waste collected, and EPR certificates purchased. Returns for FY 2023-24 must be filed as per the prescribed schedule — companies should verify their specific filing deadline on the CPCB portal.
  • Response to CPCB Notice (Urgent): Any producer who has received a direct notice from CPCB must respond within the stipulated timeframe mentioned in the notice — typically 15–30 days — with documentary evidence of compliance steps taken or an action plan for achieving compliance.
  • Deposit to Environmental Compensation Fund: Producers unable to meet their EPR targets must deposit the prescribed Environmental Compensation amount, as notified by CPCB, to avoid further penal action.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste compliance is not merely a domestic regulatory checkbox — it sits at the intersection of India's circular economy ambitions, global supply chain due diligence requirements, and climate transition commitments. Electronic waste is one of the fastest-growing waste streams globally, and improper disposal releases toxic substances and greenhouse gases, while responsible recycling recovers critical minerals such as cobalt, lithium, copper, and rare earth elements that are indispensable for the clean energy transition — electric vehicles, wind turbines, and solar panels all depend on these recovered materials. For Indian companies with export operations or multinational investors, EPR compliance is increasingly a supply chain transparency requirement. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standards both require companies to account for material resource use and waste across the value chain. A failure to demonstrate responsible end-of-life product management could surface as a material gap in sustainability disclosures, affecting access to green finance and export market eligibility. India's own BRSR Core framework, mandated by SEBI for the top 150 listed companies, explicitly covers waste management and circular economy metrics — making EPR performance directly reportable to investors. Beyond compliance, producers who build robust EPR programmes gain early access to secondary raw material streams, reduce supply chain exposure to volatile virgin material prices, and align with the global narrative of responsible electronics stewardship that institutional ESG investors increasingly scrutinise.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct an immediate EPR compliance audit: Assign your legal, sustainability, or EHS team to verify your company's registration status on the CPCB EPR portal, confirm all product categories are correctly listed, and identify any gaps in EPR certificate procurement versus targets for FY 2023-24 and FY 2024-25.
  • Engage authorised recyclers and secure EPR certificates: If certificate shortfalls exist, initiate procurement immediately from CPCB-authorised dismantlers and recyclers. Maintain a diversified panel of recycler partners to avoid supply-side constraints and ensure audit-ready documentation for all transactions.
  • File pending annual returns: Identify any unfiled or incorrectly filed annual returns and submit them on the EPR portal at the earliest. Consider appointing a dedicated compliance officer or engaging a specialist EPR consultant if internal capacity is limited.
  • Integrate EPR metrics into BRSR and investor disclosures: Ensure that e-waste collection volumes, EPR certificate data, and recycler partner credentials are captured in your BRSR Core reporting under the 'Resource and Waste Management' principle, and flag material EPR liabilities in sustainability risk registers shared with your board and investors.
  • Establish a forward-looking EPR governance structure: Embed EPR target-setting into your annual product planning and sales forecasting cycle. As EEE volumes grow, EPR obligations scale proportionally — proactive planning avoids the last-minute certificate scramble that is currently exposing many producers to regulatory risk.

Our Take

CPCB's urgent notice is a clear sign that India's e-waste regulator has shifted gears from education to enforcement. The 2022 Rules were designed with intent — the market-based EPR certificate mechanism was crafted to create genuine economic incentives for formalising India's largely informal e-waste recycling sector, which currently handles an estimated 95% of the country's e-waste. Non-compliance by producers not only creates legal exposure for those companies but structurally undermines the entire ecosystem the Rules are trying to build. We expect CPCB to escalate enforcement over the next 12–18 months, including public disclosure of non-compliant producers, suspension of EPR authorisations, and potentially linking EPR compliance status to import licensing and BIS product approvals. For sustainability leaders and CFOs, the message is unambiguous: EPR compliance for e-waste is no longer a back-office environmental obligation — it is a business continuity, investor relations, and supply chain resilience issue that deserves board-level attention.
