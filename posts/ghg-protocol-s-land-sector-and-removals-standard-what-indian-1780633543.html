Executive Summary

The GHG Protocol's Land Sector and Removals Standard establishes the first comprehensive global framework for how companies must account for greenhouse gas emissions and carbon removals from land use, land-use change, forestry, and nature-based solutions. For Indian businesses — particularly those in agriculture, food and beverages, plantations, real estate, and infrastructure — this standard fundamentally changes how carbon sequestration claims and land-related emissions must be measured, reported, and verified. Companies that fail to align with this standard risk having their net-zero commitments and carbon offset claims dismissed by investors, regulators, and global supply chain partners.

What Changed

Until now, the GHG Protocol's existing corporate accounting standards — most notably the Corporate Standard (2001) and the Land Use, Land-Use Change and Forestry (LULUCF) guidance — provided only partial and inconsistent direction on how companies should account for carbon stored in, or released from, land. This left a significant gap: companies could make sweeping claims about forest conservation, agroforestry, or mangrove restoration as carbon 'removals' without a rigorous, standardised methodology underpinning those claims. The Land Sector and Removals Standard closes that gap. It provides detailed accounting rules for biogenic carbon (carbon stored in living biomass, soil, and organic matter), land-based carbon removal activities (such as afforestation, soil carbon sequestration, and wetland restoration), and the treatment of carbon dioxide removals (CDRs) in corporate inventories and net-zero targets. Critically, the standard distinguishes between emission reductions (doing less harm) and actual removals (actively drawing down CO2), a distinction that has enormous implications for corporate net-zero integrity. It also introduces rules on how to treat permanence risk — the possibility that stored carbon is re-released due to fire, disease, or land conversion — and how to handle reversals in corporate accounts.

Who Is Affected

  • Agribusiness and food companies with supply chains involving crops, livestock, or forest commodities (e.g., tea, coffee, palm oil, timber, rubber)
  • Plantation and forestry companies managing large tracts of land for commercial or conservation purposes
  • Fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) companies sourcing agricultural raw materials and making 'net-zero' or 'carbon neutral' product claims
  • Real estate and infrastructure developers undertaking land-use change, especially in peri-urban or ecologically sensitive zones
  • Energy companies investing in biomass, biogas, or biofuel feedstocks tied to land use
  • Financial institutions and ESG-focused investors financing land-sector projects or companies with significant land-related emission profiles
  • Companies purchasing voluntary carbon offsets from nature-based solutions (NbS) projects — such as REDD+, afforestation, or blue carbon — to meet climate targets
  • Any company with a Science Based Target (SBTi) or net-zero commitment that relies on land-sector removals to achieve balance

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Separate accounting for emissions and removals: Companies must now disaggregate land-sector emissions from removals in their GHG inventories rather than netting them out — ensuring transparency and preventing double-counting.
  • Biogenic carbon boundary setting: Organisations must clearly define the boundary of biogenic carbon flows in their inventory, including CO2 absorbed by crops, forests, or soils within their operational or value chain scope.
  • Permanence and reversal accounting: Companies must assess and disclose the permanence risk of any carbon stored through land-based activities and establish protocols for how reversals (e.g., a forest fire releasing stored carbon) will be reflected in future inventories.
  • Distinction between CDR and emission reductions in net-zero targets: Net-zero claims must separately quantify how much of the 'balance' comes from genuine removals versus avoided emissions — aligning with the Science Based Targets initiative's (SBTi) Corporate Net-Zero Standard.
  • Supply chain (Scope 3) land emissions: Companies must account for land-use change emissions embedded in their supply chains — for instance, deforestation linked to soy, palm oil, or cattle — under Scope 3, Category 1 (purchased goods and services).
  • Voluntary carbon market alignment: Offsets from nature-based solutions used in corporate claims must meet the standard's requirements for additionality, permanence, and accurate quantification — raising the bar for which credits qualify.
  • Formal deadline for adoption: The GHG Protocol has not yet mandated a universal compliance deadline; however, alignment is expected to be required by major frameworks (SBTi, ISSB, CDP) as they update their guidance. Companies should treat 2025–2026 as the de facto adoption window for leading practice.

Climate Transition Angle

India's land sector is both a major source of emissions and one of the country's most significant potential carbon sinks. India's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) under the Paris Agreement explicitly commits to creating an additional carbon sink of 2.5 to 3 billion tonnes of CO2 equivalent through forest and tree cover by 2030. The Land Sector and Removals Standard directly intersects with this national ambition — and with the growing scrutiny that Indian companies face from global investors and supply chain partners on how they account for nature. From a physical risk perspective, Indian land assets are acutely vulnerable. Changing monsoon patterns, rising temperatures, and increasing frequency of extreme weather events threaten the carbon stored in forests, wetlands, and agricultural soils. A forest that sequesters carbon today may release it through drought-induced dieback or wildfire tomorrow. The standard's permanence and reversal accounting rules force companies to internalise this physical climate risk into their financial and non-financial reporting — a critical step toward honest climate resilience planning. Globally, this standard is highly complementary to the International Sustainability Standards Board's (ISSB) IFRS S2 Climate Disclosures standard and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), both of which require disclosure of Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions including land-use change. Indian companies listed on global exchanges or supplying into European value chains will face direct pressure to apply GHG Protocol land sector accounting as CSRD due diligence requirements cascade through supply chains from 2025 onwards. The standard also underpins the integrity requirements being developed under Article 6 of the Paris Agreement for internationally transferred mitigation outcomes (ITMOs) — relevant for Indian companies participating in carbon credit markets.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a land-sector emissions materiality assessment: Map all operations and supply chain activities involving land use, land-use change, or biological carbon stocks. Prioritise sectors where land-related emissions or removals are material — even if they have previously been excluded from your GHG inventory.
  • Audit existing carbon offset and NbS claims: Review any voluntary carbon credits or nature-based solution investments currently used in net-zero or carbon neutrality claims. Assess whether the underlying projects meet the standard's requirements for additionality, permanence, and reversal risk management. Retire or replace credits that do not qualify.
  • Update your GHG inventory methodology: Work with your sustainability team or external consultants to integrate land-sector emission and removal categories into your corporate GHG inventory, ensuring biogenic carbon flows are separately disclosed and Scope 3 land-use change emissions from supply chains are captured.
  • Engage your supply chain on deforestation and land-use data: Begin collecting primary or verified secondary data from Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers on land-use practices, particularly for high-risk commodities. This is essential both for accurate Scope 3 reporting and for meeting emerging EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) requirements.
  • Align net-zero target architecture with the new standard: If your company has an SBTi target or a self-declared net-zero commitment, review whether your land-sector removal claims are properly ring-fenced from emission reduction targets, and whether your accounting methodology will withstand scrutiny under the new GHG Protocol rules. Engage your SBTi account manager or target-validation body proactively.

Our Take

The GHG Protocol's Land Sector and Removals Standard is arguably the most consequential update to corporate carbon accounting in two decades. For too long, the land sector has been a refuge for vague, unverifiable, and sometimes misleading sustainability claims — from 'carbon neutral' product labels backed by low-quality forestry offsets to net-zero commitments that paper over Scope 3 deforestation with tree-planting programmes of questionable permanence. This standard ends that era. For Indian companies, the timing is particularly significant: as SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) framework matures, as the carbon credit market in India develops under the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS), and as European supply chain due diligence laws begin to bite, having a robust, internationally recognised methodology for land-sector accounting is no longer optional — it is a competitive and reputational necessity. Companies that move early to align will be better positioned to defend their climate credentials, access green finance, and retain access to premium export markets. Those that wait will find themselves scrambling to retrofit their disclosures under investor and regulatory pressure. The land beneath your feet is now firmly on the balance sheet.
