Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent interim notice governing the generation and transfer of Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) end-product credits — commonly known as EPR certificates — by registered e-waste recyclers in India. This interim arrangement is designed to bridge operational gaps in the EPR portal system and ensure that producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) can continue to meet their annual e-waste recycling targets without disruption. Companies that rely on EPR certificates to demonstrate compliance must immediately assess how this interim mechanism affects their credit procurement and reconciliation processes.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, India operates a market-based EPR framework where producers of electrical and electronic equipment are legally obligated to ensure a prescribed quantity of e-waste is collected and recycled each year. Registered recyclers generate EPR certificates upon processing e-waste, and these certificates are then transferred to PIBOs through the CPCB's centralised EPR portal to offset their compliance obligations. CPCB's latest notice introduces an interim arrangement for how recyclers generate and transfer these EPR end-product credits. While the full PDF content was not available for extraction, such interim notices from CPCB typically arise when the digital EPR portal faces technical limitations, certificate reconciliation backlogs, or when rule amendments require a transitional operating procedure before permanent systems are updated. In practical terms, this means the standard portal-based workflow for credit generation and transfer may be temporarily modified — with recyclers and producers required to follow alternative documentation, reporting, or approval steps as directed by CPCB during the interim period. This is not a relaxation of compliance obligations. Producers are still expected to meet their EPR targets, and recyclers must continue to process e-waste. The interim arrangement simply prescribes how credits are recorded, validated, and transferred until the permanent system is fully operational.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) of electrical and electronic equipment registered under CPCB's EPR portal — including consumer electronics, IT equipment, large and small household appliances, and lighting equipment manufacturers
  • CPCB-registered e-waste recyclers and dismantlers who generate and issue EPR certificates to PIBOs
  • Extended Producer Responsibility — Producer Responsibility Organisations (EPR-PROs) that facilitate bulk credit procurement and transfer on behalf of multiple producers
  • Large and mid-sized Indian manufacturers of electronics with annual EPR targets under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022
  • Multinational companies with India operations that import electronic goods and are registered as PIBOs with CPCB
  • Sustainability and compliance teams at companies whose EPR reconciliation cycles overlap with the period covered by the interim arrangement

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Monitor CPCB's official EPR portal and website (cpcb.nic.in) immediately for the specific procedural steps outlined in the interim arrangement — including any revised formats, manual submission requirements, or temporary approval workflows
  • E-waste recyclers must ensure that all e-waste processed during the interim period is documented with verifiable records (weight slips, processing logs, invoices) that can support credit generation once the permanent system is restored or updated
  • PIBOs must not assume that credits will be automatically reflected in their portal accounts during the interim period — proactively contact your registered recycler or PRO to confirm that credits are being tracked and will be formally transferred once the interim mechanism is resolved
  • Companies with EPR target deadlines falling in the near term (typically March 31 of the compliance year) should flag this interim notice to legal and compliance teams and document that they made good-faith efforts to transfer credits within the available system
  • PROs acting on behalf of multiple producers should consolidate credit positions across their client portfolios and communicate clearly with all clients about any potential delays in certificate issuance or portal reflection
  • Submit any grievances or clarification requests to CPCB in writing through official channels — retain copies of all correspondence as evidence of compliance diligence during the interim period

Climate Transition Angle

India's e-waste EPR framework is a cornerstone of the country's circular economy strategy — and circular economy performance is increasingly scrutinised under global climate disclosure and ESG reporting standards. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB), under IFRS S2, and the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) both require companies to disclose material risks and opportunities related to resource use, waste management, and supply chain sustainability. For Indian exporters and companies with global investors, EPR compliance is no longer just a domestic regulatory checkbox — it is a data point in Scope 3 emissions accounting and supply chain due diligence. E-waste contains critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, copper, and rare earth elements — whose extraction is carbon-intensive and geopolitically sensitive. A robust domestic recycling ecosystem, underpinned by a functioning EPR credit market, directly reduces India's dependence on virgin material extraction and supports the low-carbon transition of the electronics and clean energy sectors. Disruptions to EPR credit flows — even temporary interim arrangements — can create gaps in recycler cash flows, disincentivising formal recycling and pushing material back into the informal sector, which has significantly higher environmental and social risks. For companies disclosing under the Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework, EPR compliance and e-waste recycling volumes are directly relevant to Principle 2 (sustainable and safe products) and Principle 6 (environmental responsibility). Interim disruptions should be disclosed if material, and compliance teams should be prepared to explain the regulatory context to auditors and rating agencies.

What To Do Now

  • Immediately download and review the full CPCB interim notice from cpcb.nic.in and circulate it to your EPR compliance lead, legal team, and your registered recycler or PRO — do not wait for a portal notification
  • Conduct a rapid internal audit of your current EPR credit position: how many credits do you need for the current compliance year, how many have been confirmed by your recycler, and how many are pending transfer on the portal — identify any gaps that the interim arrangement may widen
  • Establish a direct communication protocol with your registered e-waste recycler(s) to receive weekly updates on credit generation volumes and the status of transfers under the interim mechanism — get this in writing
  • Brief your BRSR and ESG reporting teams on the interim arrangement so that any disclosure related to e-waste EPR compliance for the current financial year accurately reflects the regulatory context and your company's documented compliance efforts
  • Engage your industry association (such as ICEA, MAIT, or CEAMA) to seek collective clarification from CPCB on timelines for resolution of the interim arrangement and to advocate for a clearly communicated transition back to the standard portal workflow

Our Take

CPCB's interim arrangement notice is a reminder that India's EPR ecosystem — while ambitious in design — is still maturing in its operational infrastructure. The e-waste EPR portal has been a significant step forward, but the need for interim credit transfer mechanisms signals that the digital backbone supporting this market must be strengthened urgently. For producers and recyclers, the real risk here is not the interim arrangement itself, but the compliance ambiguity it creates: credits not reflected in the system are credits that cannot be used for target reconciliation, and enforcement actions for shortfalls have been escalating. Looking ahead, we expect CPCB to progressively tighten EPR enforcement across all waste streams — plastics, batteries, tyres, and e-waste — as India positions itself to meet its climate and circular economy commitments. Companies that invest now in robust EPR tracking systems, diversified recycler partnerships, and proactive CPCB engagement will be best placed not just for this interim disruption, but for the more demanding compliance environment that is clearly on the horizon.
