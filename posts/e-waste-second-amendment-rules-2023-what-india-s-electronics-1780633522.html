Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) notified the E-Waste (Management) Second Amendment Rules, 2023 on July 25, 2023, amending the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022. The amendment introduces three critical changes: mandatory safe management of refrigerants generated during the manufacture and dismantling of air-conditioning and cooling equipment; revised timelines for phasing out hazardous substances (RoHS compliance) across newly classified equipment schedules; and new conversion factors for Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) certificates covering multiple recycling end-products. Companies across electronics manufacturing, HVAC, healthcare equipment, and IT hardware sectors must immediately assess their compliance posture against these updated obligations.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Second Amendment Rules, 2023 amend the parent E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 in four substantive areas, effective from the date of gazette notification (July 25, 2023). First, refrigerant management obligations have been inserted into three separate rules — Rule 5 (manufacturers), Rule 7 (dealers/refurbishers), and Rule 9 (dismantlers/recyclers) — requiring that refrigerants generated during the manufacturing process as well as during the dismantling of old air-conditioning and cooling equipment be managed in a safe, leak-proof, and sustainable manner, using only CPCB-approved destruction technologies and following CPCB-issued guidelines. Second, the hazardous substance (RoHS) phase-out timeline has been restructured. Equipment placed on the market before May 1, 2014 (listed under the new Schedule II-B) remains exempt from RoHS restrictions where compliant spare parts are unavailable. Critically, a new Schedule II-C has been created for a broader category of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) — including advanced telecom, IT, and consumer electronics — placed on the market on or before April 1, 2025, with a further exemption for components and spare parts required for this equipment extending until April 1, 2028. Third, for recyclers generating multiple end-products from a single waste stream, conversion factors will now be determined by CPCB (with Steering Committee approval) to enable proportionate EPR certificate generation across all or some end-products — removing a previous ambiguity that disadvantaged recyclers handling complex material streams. Fourth, Schedule II-A has been introduced to carve out specific exemptions for medical and laboratory monitoring and control equipment, listing 24 categories of applications where lead, cadmium, and mercury are permitted in tightly defined technical uses — such as X-ray tubes, MRI superconductors, pH electrodes, and ultrasonic transducers — aligning India's approach with international RoHS exemption frameworks.

Who Is Affected

  • HVAC and Refrigeration Equipment Manufacturers: Companies manufacturing air conditioners, chillers, cold-storage units, and refrigeration systems — now obligated to manage refrigerants generated during production using CPCB-approved destruction technologies.
  • E-Waste Dismantlers and Recyclers: Facilities dismantling end-of-life air-conditioning and cooling equipment must ensure refrigerant recovery and safe disposal as per CPCB guidelines; recyclers generating multiple end-products gain clarity on EPR certificate conversion factors.
  • Consumer Electronics and IT Hardware Manufacturers and Importers: Companies placing desktops, laptops, notebooks, notepads, printers, copiers, telephones, and televisions on the Indian market — particularly those in the transition window to Schedule II-C — must track their RoHS compliance timelines.
  • Telecom Equipment Manufacturers and Importers: Products transmitting voice, image, or data via telecommunications (newly classified under Schedule II-C, Code ITEW17) face the April 1, 2025 market-placement threshold for RoHS exemption.
  • Medical Device and Laboratory Equipment Manufacturers: Companies producing X-ray systems, MRI machines, ultrasonic diagnostic equipment, pH meters, and portable defibrillators benefit from the new Schedule II-A exemptions but must document and justify their use of restricted substances against the listed categories.
  • Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) Portal Registrants: All producers registered under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 need to understand how the new conversion factor provisions affect their EPR certificate calculations and annual compliance filings.
  • Large Format Retailers and E-Commerce Platforms: As facilitators of EEE sales, they must ensure producer partners are compliant with the revised schedules applicable to products listed on their platforms.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Effective Immediately (July 25, 2023): Manufacturers of refrigeration and air-conditioning equipment must ensure that refrigerants generated during the manufacturing process are managed safely, in a leak-proof and sustainable manner, using CPCB-approved destruction technologies — as per new Rule 5(4).
  • Effective Immediately (July 25, 2023): Dismantlers and recyclers of old refrigeration and air-conditioning equipment must comply with new Rule 7(5) and Rule 9(11), mandating safe, leak-proof, and sustainable management of refrigerants from end-of-life equipment using CPCB-approved processes.
  • Effective Immediately (July 25, 2023): Recyclers handling e-waste that generates multiple end-products must engage with CPCB to understand the new conversion factor framework under Rule 14(1)(ii)(c) and adjust their EPR certificate generation methodology accordingly.
  • By April 1, 2025: Manufacturers and importers of Schedule II-C EEE (including advanced telecom and IT equipment) must note that products placed on the market after this date will no longer benefit from the RoHS exemption applicable to Schedule II-C equipment — full RoHS compliance will be required.
  • By April 1, 2028: Even for Schedule II-C equipment placed on market on or before April 1, 2025, all components, consumables, and spare parts must meet RoHS hazardous substance restrictions by this date, unless compliant alternatives remain unavailable.
  • Ongoing: Medical and laboratory equipment manufacturers must maintain documentation demonstrating that use of restricted substances (lead, cadmium, mercury) falls within the specific Schedule II-A exemption categories — this is not a blanket exemption and each application must be individually justified.
  • Ongoing: All regulated entities must monitor CPCB for issuance of specific guidelines on approved refrigerant destruction technologies, as the rules reference CPCB guidelines that are yet to be formally published in their final form.

Climate Transition Angle

The refrigerant management provisions in this amendment are far more consequential for India's climate commitments than they might first appear. Refrigerants used in air-conditioning and cooling equipment — particularly hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) — are among the most potent greenhouse gases, with global warming potentials (GWPs) hundreds to thousands of times higher than CO2. India is a signatory to the Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol, which mandates a phased reduction of HFC consumption. The amendment's requirement for safe, leak-proof, and sustainable refrigerant management directly supports India's Kigali compliance obligations and its Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) under the Paris Agreement. From a physical risk perspective, India's rapidly expanding cooling demand — driven by rising temperatures, urbanisation, and income growth — means that refrigerant leakage during manufacturing and end-of-life dismantling represents a growing emissions source. The International Energy Agency estimates that the cooling sector could account for 45% of India's electricity demand by 2050. Poorly managed refrigerant recovery today creates a significant and measurable Scope 1 emissions liability for manufacturers, and a Scope 3 liability for companies throughout the cooling equipment value chain. On the disclosure front, global reporting frameworks are converging on refrigerant management as a material climate issue. The ISSB's IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures) requires companies to disclose material Scope 1 emissions, which include direct refrigerant releases. The EU's CSRD, which Indian exporters and subsidiaries of European multinationals must increasingly navigate, requires detailed value-chain emissions reporting under ESRS E1. India's own SEBI BRSR Core framework for top-listed companies is evolving in the same direction. Companies that treat this amendment merely as a waste management compliance issue — rather than as a climate and ESG disclosure issue — will find themselves underprepared for the next wave of mandatory climate reporting. The RoHS schedule restructuring also has supply chain implications. As India integrates more deeply into global electronics supply chains — particularly under the Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes — adherence to hazardous substance restrictions is a baseline market access requirement for exports to the EU, UK, and increasingly Southeast Asian markets. The new Schedule II-C timelines give Indian manufacturers a transition window, but global buyers and EPR scheme administrators in export markets will not extend the same flexibility.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a Refrigerant Management Audit: HVAC and refrigeration manufacturers and dismantlers should immediately audit their current refrigerant handling procedures during both production and end-of-life processing. Map all points of refrigerant release, quantify volumes, and assess whether current recovery and destruction methods align with CPCB's forthcoming guidelines. Engage a certified environmental consultant to identify gaps and implement a corrective action plan.
  • Review and Reclassify Your Product Portfolio Against Updated Schedules: Legal, compliance, and product management teams should jointly review all EEE product lines against the revised Schedule II-B and the new Schedule II-C. Identify which products fall under which schedule, note the applicable RoHS exemption windows (April 1, 2025 and April 1, 2028), and create a product-level compliance roadmap to eliminate restricted substances from components and spare parts before deadlines lapse.
  • Update EPR Registrations and Certificate Calculations: Recyclers and producers registered on the CPCB EPR portal should review the implications of the new conversion factor provisions under Rule 14(1)(ii)(c). Engage with CPCB to clarify how conversion factors will be determined for your specific waste streams and end-products, and update your EPR compliance model accordingly to avoid certificate shortfalls or over-reporting.
  • Build Refrigerant Management into Climate and ESG Disclosures: Sustainability and CFO teams should begin quantifying refrigerant-related GHG emissions (particularly HFC releases) as part of their Scope 1 inventory, in preparation for BRSR Core disclosures and any voluntary ISSB/TCFD-aligned reporting. This positions the company ahead of mandatory climate disclosure requirements rather than scrambling to comply retrospectively.
  • Monitor CPCB for Implementing Guidelines: The amendment makes several provisions conditional on CPCB issuing specific guidelines — particularly on approved refrigerant destruction technologies. Assign a regulatory affairs team member to actively track CPCB notifications and circulars, and ensure that once guidelines are published, internal SOPs are updated within 60 days.

Our Take

The E-Waste Second Amendment Rules, 2023 are deceptively significant. On the surface, they look like a technical amendment — tweaking schedules, inserting sub-rules, and adjusting deadlines. But taken together, these changes signal that India's regulatory architecture around electronic and electrical waste is maturing rapidly, and is beginning to align with both the Kigali Protocol's refrigerant phase-down agenda and the global convergence on RoHS-equivalent hazardous substance restrictions. The refrigerant management clauses in particular deserve board-level attention in the HVAC and cooling sector — not because they impose immediate penalties, but because they establish the legal foundation for what will almost certainly become a more stringent, monitored, and reported regime as India's climate commitments deepen. The companies that treat these provisions as an opportunity to build best-in-class refrigerant recovery infrastructure today will gain a durable competitive advantage in both domestic and export markets. Those that wait for CPCB enforcement action will find themselves managing a compliance crisis and a reputational risk simultaneously. The window to get ahead of this is open — but it is narrowing.
