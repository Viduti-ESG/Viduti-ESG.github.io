Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice extending the timeline for filing annual returns on the E-Waste EPR Portal, providing temporary relief to producers, importers, brand owners, and recyclers struggling with compliance deadlines. While the extension offers breathing room, it does not reduce the underlying obligations — companies must use this window to ensure accurate data collection, reconcile EPR certificates, and close any compliance gaps. Failure to file within the revised timeline will attract regulatory action under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022.

What Changed

The CPCB has formally notified stakeholders registered on the E-Waste EPR Portal that the deadline for submitting annual returns has been extended beyond the originally prescribed date. This notice, classified as urgent, signals that the regulator acknowledged practical difficulties faced by obligated entities — likely including technical issues on the portal, delays in EPR certificate transactions, or challenges in aggregating collection and recycling data across complex supply chains. Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, all Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (PEEEs), importers, and authorised recyclers/refurbishers are required to file annual returns detailing quantities of e-waste generated, collected, channelised, and recycled. These returns are filed through the centralised CPCB E-Waste EPR Portal, which also tracks EPR certificate generation and redemption — the core mechanism by which companies demonstrate they have met their Extended Producer Responsibility targets. The extension modifies the filing deadline but does not alter the scope of what must be reported. All data covering the relevant compliance year — production volumes, e-waste collection, recycler linkages, and EPR certificate balances — must still be submitted in full. Stakeholders should monitor the CPCB portal and official communications closely for the revised specific date, as the extracted notice did not confirm a singular new deadline at the time of publication.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (PEEEs) — including manufacturers of consumer electronics, IT equipment, telecom devices, lighting equipment, and large/small household appliances
  • Importers of EEE products sold in India, regardless of company size or origin country
  • Brand Owners whose products are manufactured under licence or on a contract basis in India
  • Authorised E-Waste Recyclers and Refurbishers registered on the CPCB EPR Portal
  • Bulk consumers (large corporates, government bodies) who generate e-waste and have reporting obligations under the Rules
  • Dealers and refurbishers who have taken on EPR obligations by agreement with producers
  • Any entity that has already registered on the E-Waste EPR Portal and received a unique EPR Registration Number

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • FILE ANNUAL RETURNS: All obligated entities must submit annual returns on the CPCB E-Waste EPR Portal covering production/import volumes, e-waste collected, channelised, recycled, and EPR certificates purchased and redeemed — within the newly extended timeline announced by CPCB
  • MONITOR PORTAL FOR REVISED DATE: The specific extended deadline must be confirmed directly on the CPCB E-Waste EPR Portal (https://eprnew.cpcbccr.com) or via CPCB official circulars — do not rely on third-party sources alone
  • RECONCILE EPR CERTIFICATES: Before filing, ensure all EPR certificates procured from registered recyclers are uploaded, linked, and reconciled against your annual EPR target obligations to avoid shortfall penalties
  • MEET EPR TARGETS: Producers must demonstrate they have met the prescribed EPR collection targets (as per Schedule I of the E-Waste Rules, 2022) — extension of the filing window does not extend the period for actually meeting collection targets
  • AVOID LAPSE IN REGISTRATION: EPR registrations must remain active; companies that have not renewed or updated their registration details should do so immediately alongside filing returns
  • DOCUMENT RECYCLER LINKAGES: Maintain auditable records of agreements with CPCB-authorised recyclers, collection agencies, and PROs (Producer Responsibility Organisations) — these may be called for during scrutiny
  • PENALTY RISK: Non-compliance with return filing obligations can attract suspension of EPR registration, financial penalties, and reputational risk under the Environment Protection Act, 1986 and E-Waste Rules, 2022

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste EPR compliance is not merely a regulatory checkbox — it sits at the intersection of India's circular economy ambitions and the global climate transition agenda. Electronic and electrical equipment manufacturing is resource- and energy-intensive, and the improper disposal of e-waste releases toxic substances (lead, mercury, cadmium, brominated flame retardants) that contaminate soil and water systems, compounding physical climate risks in already stressed ecosystems. From a supply chain perspective, the recovery and recycling of critical minerals — lithium, cobalt, copper, rare earth elements — embedded in e-waste is increasingly central to India's strategy for energy transition. The batteries, solar inverters, and power electronics that underpin India's renewable energy buildout rely heavily on these same materials. A robust EPR ecosystem for e-waste is therefore a prerequisite for closing the loop on clean energy supply chains. Globally, frameworks such as the ISSB's IFRS S2 (climate-related disclosures) and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) are pushing companies to report on resource use, circularity, and waste across their value chains. Indian companies exporting to EU markets or seeking foreign investment must increasingly demonstrate that their e-waste management practices are aligned with international expectations — making CPCB EPR compliance a de facto element of ESG due diligence. The Securities and Exchange Board of India's (SEBI) Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework also requires disclosure of hazardous waste including e-waste for listed companies, creating a direct regulatory link between EPR compliance and capital market disclosures.

What To Do Now

  • CONFIRM THE NEW DEADLINE IMMEDIATELY: Assign a compliance officer to check the CPCB E-Waste EPR Portal and the official CPCB website today. Subscribe to portal notifications to receive real-time updates on the revised filing date and any further extensions or technical advisories
  • CONDUCT AN INTERNAL EPR DATA AUDIT: Pull together production/import data, e-waste collection records, recycler certificates, and PRO agreements for the relevant compliance year. Identify and close any data gaps before the extended deadline — last-minute scrambles lead to inaccurate filings and potential penalties
  • VERIFY RECYCLER AUTHORISATIONS: Confirm that all recyclers and refurbishers you have transacted with are currently authorised by CPCB and that the EPR certificates they have issued to you are valid and uploaded on the portal. Certificates from de-registered recyclers will not count toward your EPR target
  • ALIGN BRSR AND EPR DISCLOSURES: If your company is a listed entity, use the EPR filing process as an opportunity to reconcile e-waste data with your BRSR disclosures (Principle 2 — sustainable and safe products). Inconsistencies between regulatory filings and public disclosures create reputational and governance risks
  • ENGAGE YOUR PRO OR COMPLIANCE PARTNER: If you work with a Producer Responsibility Organisation (PRO) or third-party EPR compliance partner, convene an urgent call to review filing readiness, certificate balances, and any portal technical issues that need to be escalated to CPCB before the deadline

Our Take

CPCB's decision to extend the e-waste EPR return filing timeline reflects a recurring tension in India's EPR ecosystem: regulatory ambition outpacing operational readiness, particularly the technological and administrative capacity of the centralised portal to handle hundreds of thousands of registered entities simultaneously. While extensions are a practical necessity, they also risk normalising non-compliance for entities that treat each extension as an indefinite delay rather than a firm new boundary. The more important signal here is directional — CPCB is actively enforcing EPR return filing requirements, and the window for voluntary regularisation is finite. We expect the regulator to tighten enforcement significantly in the coming compliance cycles, with greater scrutiny on EPR certificate authenticity (a known area of fraud), recycler authorisation status, and the alignment between declared production volumes and actual collection targets. Companies that invest now in robust EPR data systems, credible recycler partnerships, and integrated sustainability reporting will not only avoid regulatory risk — they will be better positioned as India moves toward a more comprehensive circular economy policy framework, including potential lifecycle assessment requirements and e-waste targets linked to critical mineral recovery.
