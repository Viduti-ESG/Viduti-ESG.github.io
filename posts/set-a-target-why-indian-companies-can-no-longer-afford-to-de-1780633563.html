Set a Target: Why Indian Companies Can No Longer Afford to Delay SBTi Validation

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) provides a globally recognised framework for companies to set emissions reduction targets aligned with limiting global warming to 1.5°C — and validation through SBTi is rapidly becoming a baseline expectation from investors, global buyers, and regulators.

Executive Summary

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) provides a globally recognised framework for companies to set emissions reduction targets aligned with limiting global warming to 1.5°C — and validation through SBTi is rapidly becoming a baseline expectation from investors, global buyers, and regulators. For Indian companies embedded in export supply chains or seeking international capital, committing to and validating an SBTi target is no longer a voluntary 'nice-to-have' — it is a strategic imperative. Companies that act now will be positioned ahead of the compliance curve; those that delay risk losing market access, investor confidence, and social licence to operate.

What Changed

The Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) is an independent body that validates corporate greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction targets to ensure they are consistent with the pace of decarbonisation required by climate science. SBTi's Corporate Validation Services allow companies of all sizes — from large corporates to SMEs — to submit their emissions targets for independent assessment and earn the right to publicly claim their targets are 'science-based.' In recent years, SBTi has significantly scaled its validation infrastructure, introduced the Net-Zero Standard (requiring companies to address both near-term and long-term emissions across Scopes 1, 2, and 3), and launched the SME route to lower barriers for smaller businesses. Critically, SBTi is also developing sector-specific guidance — including for steel, cement, apparel, and financial institutions — making target-setting more rigorous and sector-relevant than ever before. The validation process requires companies to first commit publicly (signing the SBTi commitment letter), then submit targets within 24 months for validation against the latest SBTi criteria. Once validated, companies must report progress annually and recertify targets as standards evolve. The bar is rising: vague or incremental pledges are no longer sufficient — targets must be grounded in measurable, time-bound science.

Who Is Affected

  • Large Indian corporates in export-oriented sectors: textiles, automotive components, IT services, pharmaceuticals, steel, and cement — where global customers are mandating Scope 3 supplier targets
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational companies, where parent companies are already SBTi-committed and are cascading requirements down their supply chains
  • Companies seeking to raise capital from ESG-focused international investors, green bond markets, or sustainability-linked loans
  • Businesses subject to SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) Core framework, particularly the 9 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) on climate
  • SMEs supplying to large domestic or international buyers who are themselves SBTi-committed and under pressure to address Scope 3 emissions
  • Financial institutions and banks, for whom SBTi's Financial Institutions Net-Zero Standard is directly applicable to their lending and investment portfolios

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • COMMIT: Sign the SBTi Commitment Letter via the SBTi website — this is the formal, public declaration of intent and starts the 24-month clock for target submission
  • DEVELOP TARGETS (within 24 months of commitment): Set near-term targets (5–10 year horizon, aligned with 1.5°C) and long-term net-zero targets (by no later than 2050), covering Scopes 1, 2, and material Scope 3 categories
  • SCOPE 3 ASSESSMENT: Conduct a Scope 3 emissions inventory to identify material categories — for most Indian manufacturers, this will include purchased goods and services, use of sold products, and logistics
  • SUBMIT FOR VALIDATION: Submit target documentation to SBTi for independent review; expect a validation turnaround of several months and budget for potential target revisions based on SBTi feedback
  • ANNUAL PROGRESS REPORTING: Once validated, disclose annual emissions performance against targets in public sustainability reports, BRSR filings, and CDP disclosures
  • RECERTIFICATION: Monitor SBTi criteria updates (particularly sector-specific pathways as they are released) and recertify targets if business activities or SBTi standards change materially
  • SME ROUTE: Smaller companies can use the streamlined SBTi SME pathway, which requires commitment to a set of standardised actions rather than a full bespoke target submission — reducing the resource burden significantly

Climate Transition Angle

SBTi targets sit at the very heart of the global corporate climate transition. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) — whose standards India is actively considering adopting through SEBI — requires companies to disclose their transition plans and the emissions targets underpinning them. An SBTi-validated target is widely regarded as the gold standard for demonstrating that a company's transition plan is credible, not greenwashed. From a supply chain perspective, the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) and the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) are compelling European companies to account for Scope 3 emissions across their value chains. Indian suppliers to European buyers — in sectors from apparel to auto components to chemicals — will face increasing contractual and reputational pressure to demonstrate science-based commitments or risk deselection. Physical climate risks compound the urgency. Indian businesses face material exposure to heat stress, water scarcity, and extreme weather events. Investors and lenders using climate scenario analysis (aligned with TCFD, now integrated into ISSB standards) will reward companies with credible decarbonisation trajectories and penalise those perceived as stranded asset risks. Setting an SBTi target signals strategic awareness of both transition and physical risk — and the intent to manage both.

What To Do Now

  • CONDUCT A READINESS ASSESSMENT: Map your current GHG emissions inventory (Scopes 1, 2, and 3) against SBTi criteria. Identify data gaps — particularly in Scope 3 — and prioritise filling them before commitment. Engage a qualified sustainability consultant if needed.
  • ENGAGE LEADERSHIP: Present the business case for SBTi commitment to your Board and CFO — frame it around investor expectations, customer retention, access to green finance, and alignment with BRSR Core disclosures rather than purely compliance cost.
  • SIGN THE COMMITMENT LETTER: Do not wait for perfect data. Sign the SBTi commitment letter now to signal intent publicly — this also provides a 24-month runway to develop robust targets. Early movers gain reputational advantage in their sector.
  • DEVELOP AN INTERNAL ROADMAP: Assign cross-functional ownership (Finance, Operations, Procurement, Sustainability) for target development. Set internal milestones for Scope 3 data collection, target modelling, and submission — do not let the 24-month window slip by default.
  • ALIGN WITH BROADER REPORTING FRAMEWORKS: Ensure your SBTi journey is integrated with your BRSR Core disclosures, CDP submission, and any ISSB-aligned reporting you are preparing — creating a single, consistent climate narrative for all stakeholders.

Our Take

Setting a science-based target is no longer an act of corporate voluntarism — it is the entry ticket to the next decade of global commerce and capital markets. For Indian companies, the window to act proactively is narrowing fast: SEBI's BRSR Core framework, incoming ISSB-aligned standards, and the Scope 3 ambitions of multinational buyers are converging to make SBTi validation a de facto market requirement across sectors. What makes this moment particularly significant is that SBTi is simultaneously raising its standards — sector pathways are becoming more stringent, net-zero criteria are tightening, and greenwashing scrutiny is intensifying globally. Companies that commit and validate their targets now will not only avoid future compliance scrambles but will build the internal data systems, supplier engagement capabilities, and credibility with investors that define resilient, transition-ready businesses. The question is no longer whether to set a science-based target — it is how quickly you can get started.
