Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has extended the deadline for filing quarterly and annual EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) returns for FY 2023-24 under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, to 31st December 2024. This is a critical window for producers, manufacturers, importers, and authorised recyclers and refurbishers who have not yet completed their compliance filings on the CPCB EPR portal. Companies that miss this extended deadline risk regulatory action, suspension of EPR authorisation, and reputational exposure — treat this as a hard stop, not another soft extension.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, all registered entities — including Producers (brand owners, manufacturers, importers), and downstream partners such as refurbishers and recyclers — are required to file quarterly and annual returns on the centralised CPCB EPR portal. These returns capture data on e-waste generation, collection, channelisation, recycling, and EPR certificate transactions for a given financial year. The original deadlines for FY 2023-24 returns had already lapsed, and CPCB has now issued a formal notice providing a last-chance extension until 31st December 2024. This extension applies specifically to quarterly returns (Q1 through Q4 of FY 2023-24) as well as the consolidated annual return for the same financial year. The move signals that CPCB is aware of widespread non-compliance in the ecosystem but is unlikely to grant further leniency beyond this date, given the increasing scrutiny on EPR frameworks across all waste streams in India.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) — including Indian manufacturers and brand owners across consumer electronics, IT hardware, telecom equipment, large and small appliances, lighting equipment, and medical devices falling under the 21 categories listed in the Rules
  • Importers of EEE — companies importing finished goods or components that result in e-waste at end-of-life
  • Refurbishers — entities registered under the Rules for extending the life of used electronic equipment
  • Authorised E-Waste Recyclers — dismantlers and processors who are EPR partners and must report volumes processed and certificates issued
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) — intermediaries managing EPR obligations on behalf of producers, who must reconcile and report channelised volumes
  • Large enterprises and SMEs alike — there is no size exemption; all registered entities on the CPCB EPR portal for e-waste are covered

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • HARD DEADLINE — 31st December 2024: File all pending quarterly returns (Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4) for FY 2023-24 on the CPCB EPR portal (epro.cpcb.gov.in)
  • HARD DEADLINE — 31st December 2024: File the consolidated Annual Return for FY 2023-24 on the CPCB EPR portal
  • Producers must report: actual e-waste collected (in metric tonnes), EPR certificates purchased/redeemed, and shortfall or surplus against annual EPR targets set at registration
  • Recyclers must report: volumes of e-waste received from producers/PROs, processed, and certificates generated — with reconciliation against CPCB-issued EPR certificates
  • PROs must report: volumes channelised on behalf of each registered producer, with itemised category-wise breakdowns
  • Entities with pending EPR certificate reconciliations or unresolved mismatches on the portal must resolve discrepancies before submitting returns — do not submit with open errors
  • Post-December 2024: CPCB is expected to initiate show-cause notices, suspend EPR authorisations, or impose penalties under Rule 22 for entities remaining non-compliant

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste EPR is not merely a domestic compliance checkbox — it sits at the intersection of India's circular economy ambitions and its obligations under the global climate transition. Electronic equipment contains critical minerals such as cobalt, lithium, copper, and rare earth elements whose responsible recovery directly reduces the carbon footprint and environmental damage associated with virgin mining. By ensuring formal, traceable recycling through EPR compliance, companies are effectively contributing to a domestic critical mineral supply chain that supports the energy transition — including batteries for EVs and renewable energy storage. From a global standards perspective, the ISSB's IFRS S2 and the EU's CSRD both require companies to disclose material risks and opportunities related to resource use, waste, and circular economy practices. For multinational producers or Indian companies supplying to global value chains, failure to maintain clean EPR records creates a data gap that will increasingly surface in supply chain due diligence requests from European and global buyers. India's e-waste rules are also benchmarked against EU WEEE Directive principles, meaning non-compliance here can raise red flags in cross-border ESG assessments. Physical climate risk is also relevant: informal e-waste recycling — which grows when formal EPR systems are weak — causes severe soil and groundwater contamination, increasing climate-related vulnerability in communities near informal recycling clusters. Strong EPR compliance strengthens the formal sector and reduces these localised environmental and social risks.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your portal status immediately: Log into epro.cpcb.gov.in, identify all pending quarterly and annual returns for FY 2023-24, and assign a responsible team member to complete each filing no later than 20th December 2024 — build in buffer before the 31st deadline
  • Reconcile EPR certificate data: Cross-check certificates purchased from recyclers/PROs against your annual EPR target (as registered with CPCB). Resolve any mismatches, duplicate entries, or unverified certificates with your recycler or PRO before filing — incorrect data in returns can trigger scrutiny even if filed on time
  • Engage your PRO or recyclers proactively: If you rely on a PRO for EPR fulfilment, confirm with them in writing that their filings on your behalf are complete and that you have received the required certificate documentation. Do not assume it has been handled
  • Document everything: Maintain offline records of all return submissions, acknowledgement receipts from the CPCB portal, and supporting data (collection records, recycler invoices, EPR certificates). These will be essential if CPCB initiates verification or audit proceedings post-deadline
  • Begin FY 2024-25 compliance planning now: Use this filing exercise as a trigger to set up quarterly data collection systems so you are not in a similar position next year. Consider integrating EPR reporting into your broader ESG data management platform and aligning it with your BRSR disclosures on resource management

Our Take

This extension is a regulatory lifeline, but it should be read as the last one. CPCB has demonstrated consistent intent to tighten the e-waste EPR framework since the 2022 Rules replaced the earlier 2016 regime — introducing EPR certificates as a tradeable instrument, mandating portal-based tracking, and progressively raising collection targets. The granting of an extension to 31st December 2024 reflects the reality that portal adoption and data quality across the ecosystem remain uneven, but the direction of travel is unmistakable: India is moving toward real-time, verifiable EPR compliance, not self-reported declarations. Companies that treat this extension as an opportunity to build robust internal systems — rather than simply filing to clear the backlog — will be far better positioned as CPCB introduces stricter enforcement, third-party audits, and potentially links EPR compliance status to broader environmental clearances and green procurement eligibility. The circular economy is becoming a core pillar of India's climate strategy, and e-waste EPR is one of its most operationally mature expressions. Get compliant by December 31st — then get strategic.
