Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice to producers — including manufacturers, importers, and brand owners of electrical and electronic equipment — directing them to comply with their obligations under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022. Companies that have not yet registered on the EPR portal, set collection targets, or filed returns are now at heightened regulatory risk. Non-compliance can attract penalties, production stoppages, and reputational damage under India's strengthening extended producer responsibility (EPR) framework.

What Changed

The E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 replaced the earlier 2016 rules and introduced a significantly more structured Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework for electrical and electronic equipment (EEE). Under EPR, producers — defined broadly to include manufacturers, importers, and brand owners selling EEE in India — are legally required to take back and channel end-of-life products through registered recyclers and dismantlers. The 2022 rules also introduced EPR certificates as a market-based compliance instrument, allowing producers to meet targets by purchasing certificates from registered recyclers. The CPCB's urgent notice signals that the regulator has identified a significant gap in compliance — many producers have either not registered on the centralised EPR portal, have not set or met their annual collection targets, or have failed to submit the mandatory annual returns. This notice is a formal warning that enforcement action is imminent for defaulting entities. It also reflects CPCB's broader push to operationalise EPR across waste streams (plastic, battery, tyre, and e-waste) as a cornerstone of India's circular economy agenda.

Who Is Affected

  • Manufacturers of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) with operations or sales in India — including consumer electronics, IT and telecom equipment, large and small household appliances, lighting equipment, and medical devices covered under Schedule I of the Rules
  • Importers of EEE — any company bringing finished electronic goods or components into India for sale
  • Brand owners — companies that place EEE products in the Indian market under their own brand, even if manufactured by a third party
  • E-commerce platforms and producers selling directly to consumers online who may have obligations as brand owners or importers
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) managing EPR compliance on behalf of producers
  • Small and medium enterprises (SMEs) in the electronics sector — the Rules apply regardless of company size if products fall under the notified categories

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • REGISTRATION (Immediate): All producers, importers, and brand owners must register on the CPCB's centralised EPR portal (https://eprewaste.cpcb.gov.in). Unregistered entities are in direct violation of Rule 5 of the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022.
  • EPR TARGETS: Producers must set and fulfil annual EPR targets for e-waste collection, channelisation, and recycling as prescribed under Schedule II of the Rules. Targets are based on sales volumes from previous years.
  • EPR CERTIFICATE PROCUREMENT: Where producers cannot meet collection targets through their own systems, they must procure EPR certificates from CPCB-registered recyclers or dismantlers within the compliance year.
  • ANNUAL RETURNS: Producers are required to file annual returns on the EPR portal detailing quantities of EEE placed on the market, e-waste collected, and EPR certificates held. Returns for the previous financial year must be filed promptly — check the portal for the current deadline.
  • TAKE-BACK SYSTEMS: Producers must establish or participate in a system for collecting e-waste from consumers, including through collection centres, reverse logistics, or PRO partnerships. Evidence of operational take-back mechanisms must be maintained.
  • RECORD-KEEPING: Maintain records of all e-waste generated, collected, transported, and channelised to authorised dismantlers/recyclers for a minimum of 5 years and make these available to CPCB/State PCBs on demand.
  • NON-COMPLIANCE RISK: CPCB has indicated enforcement action including cancellation of EPR registration, financial penalties under the Environment Protection Act, 1986, and potential suspension of production or import licences for persistent defaulters.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste compliance is not merely a regulatory checkbox — it sits at the intersection of climate risk, supply chain resilience, and global sustainability standards. The extraction of virgin raw materials for electronics (rare earth metals, cobalt, lithium, copper) is energy-intensive and carbon-heavy. A well-functioning EPR system for e-waste creates the infrastructure for a domestic circular economy, reducing dependence on imported virgin materials and cutting embodied carbon in the electronics value chain. For companies aligned with or preparing for global frameworks — including the ISSB's IFRS S2 (climate-related disclosures), the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) for entities with European exposure, and India's own BRSR Core mandatory disclosures for listed companies — e-waste management is increasingly a material topic. Supply chain Scope 3 emissions, responsible sourcing, and circular economy practices are now standard expectations from institutional investors and multinational buyers. Physical climate risks also intersect here: flooding, heat stress, and extreme weather events disrupt e-waste collection logistics and informal recycling clusters concentrated in cities like Delhi, Mumbai, and Bengaluru. Producers who build robust, formalised take-back systems now are better insulated against supply chain shocks as climate volatility intensifies. Furthermore, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CS3D) and similar regulations will soon require Indian exporters and suppliers to demonstrate circular economy compliance — making CPCB EPR compliance a de facto export-readiness requirement.

What To Do Now

  • AUDIT YOUR EPR STATUS IMMEDIATELY: Conduct an internal compliance audit to confirm your registration status on the CPCB EPR portal, verify that your product categories and sales volumes are correctly declared, and assess whether your EPR targets for the current financial year are on track to be met.
  • APPOINT OR REVIEW YOUR PRO ARRANGEMENT: If you are using a Producer Responsibility Organisation (PRO) to manage EPR obligations, obtain a written status report confirming collection activities, recycler partnerships, and certificate balances. If you do not have a PRO, engage one or build an in-house take-back system without delay.
  • PROCURE EPR CERTIFICATES TO BRIDGE GAPS: If your collection targets cannot be met through physical take-back before the year-end, immediately engage CPCB-registered recyclers to procure EPR certificates. Delays in certificate procurement are a common compliance gap — act before the financial year closes.
  • INTEGRATE E-WASTE KPIs INTO BRSR AND SUSTAINABILITY REPORTING: Map your e-waste collection and recycling data to BRSR Core Principle 2 (Sustainable and Safe Products) disclosures and to ISSB/TCFD-aligned climate reporting on circular economy and Scope 3 value chain impacts — this strengthens investor and buyer confidence.
  • ESTABLISH A REGULATORY MONITORING PROCESS: Assign a dedicated compliance owner to track CPCB portal notifications, EPR rule amendments, and State PCB directives. Given the pace of regulatory activity across EPR categories (plastic, battery, tyre, e-waste), a fragmented monitoring approach creates avoidable risk.

Our Take

CPCB's urgent notice is a clear signal that India's EPR enforcement era has well and truly begun. For years, e-waste rules existed largely on paper, with low registration rates and minimal enforcement action. That era is ending. The CPCB has invested significantly in its digital EPR portal infrastructure, and the issuance of formal urgent notices to producers — with implied threats of production and import licence consequences — marks a qualitative shift in regulatory intent. We expect this to be followed by show-cause notices to specific defaulters, and potentially publicised enforcement actions, as CPCB seeks to establish credibility ahead of EPR frameworks for additional waste categories. For business leaders, the message is unambiguous: e-waste EPR compliance is now a board-level risk topic, not an operational afterthought. Companies that treat this as a compliance-first, strategy-second opportunity — building circular supply chains, engaging certified recyclers, and transparently reporting performance — will be better positioned for the regulatory, investor, and customer scrutiny that is coming at pace.
