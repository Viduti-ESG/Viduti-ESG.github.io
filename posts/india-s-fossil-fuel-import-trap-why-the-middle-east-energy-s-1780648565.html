Executive Summary

India's structural dependence on imported fossil fuels — 85–90% of crude oil, 45–50% of gas, and over 240 million tonnes of imported coal annually — is no longer a geopolitical footnote but a balance sheet risk that Indian CFOs and board risk committees must price into long-term capital planning. The Middle East crisis, as documented by Climate Action Tracker (CAT) in its analysis 'Breaking the cycle of energy shocks: India's renewable opportunity' (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/breaking-the-cycle-of-energy-shocks-indias-renewable-opportunity/), exposes a systemic contradiction: India is simultaneously accelerating renewable energy deployment and doubling down on imported coal and gas infrastructure, creating a stranded asset liability that will surface in BRSR Core disclosures and ISSB S2-aligned climate risk reporting within the next 24 months. For listed Indian companies — particularly energy-intensive sectors under BEE's PAT scheme, exporters facing EU CBAM scrutiny, and companies with international institutional investors applying ISSB or TCFD lenses — this is not an abstract energy policy debate. It is a live, quantifiable financial risk that is heading directly toward mandatory disclosure.

What Changed

Reporting by Dr. Nandini Das, CAT's India country lead, published at https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/breaking-the-cycle-of-energy-shocks-indias-renewable-opportunity/, documents what three decades of energy shocks have repeatedly confirmed: India's energy system vulnerability is structural, not cyclical. The current Middle East disruption — affecting approximately 20% of global energy supply — has triggered two simultaneous and contradictory Indian policy responses: emergency clearances for wind and battery storage acceleration, and the invocation of emergency clauses to run imported-coal plants at full capacity. This is not a temporary tactical tension; it represents a fundamental strategic incoherence in India's energy transition pathway. What has materially changed is the speed at which these contradictions are becoming financially visible. India's coal import bill and crude oil import dependency create a persistent current account vulnerability — each oil price spike of USD 10/barrel adds approximately USD 12–15 billion to India's annual import bill, directly affecting the INR, inflation, and fiscal headroom. For Indian corporates, this translates into input cost volatility, working capital stress, and — critically — scope 2 and scope 3 emissions exposure that is now subject to mandatory disclosure under SEBI's BRSR Core framework for the top 150 listed companies (FY2023-24) and will extend to the top 1,000 listed companies by FY2026-27. The CAT analysis makes explicit what Indian boards have been slow to acknowledge: expanding gas infrastructure — LNG terminals, city gas distribution networks, cross-country pipelines — risks locking India into a second fossil fuel dependency trap at precisely the moment when solar and wind are cost-competitive and storage technology is scaling rapidly. The India-specific regulatory consequence is sharp. Under SEBI's BRSR Core (mandated for top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24), companies must now disclose Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions with reasonable assurance, and energy intensity metrics tied to their business activity. For companies in sectors such as steel, cement, aluminium, petrochemicals, and power — all of which sit within BEE's PAT Scheme Cycle III — exposure to imported fossil fuel price volatility is not just an operational risk; it is a disclosed, auditable financial risk. Simultaneously, Indian exporters to the EU face the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), which is now in its transitional reporting phase (October 2023–December 2025) and enters full financial liability from January 2026, with embedded carbon costs in steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, and hydrogen exports directly affecting trade competitiveness.

Who Is Affected

  • Energy-intensive industrial sectors under BEE PAT Scheme Cycle III: steel, cement, aluminium, chlor-alkali, pulp and paper, textiles, fertilisers — all face rising input cost volatility from imported coal and gas price shocks
  • Top 150 and progressively top 1,000 BSE/NSE-listed companies subject to SEBI BRSR Core mandatory GHG and energy disclosures — fossil fuel exposure is now an auditable line item
  • Indian exporters to the EU in CBAM-covered sectors (steel, aluminium, cement, fertilisers, hydrogen) — embedded carbon from coal-based power and process heat is a direct financial liability from January 2026
  • CFOs and Treasury heads at energy-intensive corporates: fuel import cost volatility is a working capital and hedging strategy issue requiring board-level risk quantification
  • Chief Sustainability Officers and ESG heads at listed companies: must now map fossil fuel dependency into BRSR Core KPIs, and anticipate ISSB IFRS S2 scenario analysis disclosure requirements
  • Board Risk Committees: stranded asset risk from long-lived coal and gas infrastructure investments (20–30 year asset life vs. accelerating energy transition) needs explicit treatment in risk registers
  • Institutional investors — domestic (LIC, SBI MF, HDFC AMC) and foreign (GIC, Norges, BlackRock) — applying TCFD or ISSB lenses to Indian energy and industrial sector holdings
  • Project finance lenders and infrastructure funds exposed to Indian coal-based power or gas distribution capex — stranded asset timelines are compressing as renewables + storage cost curves fall

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • SEBI BRSR Core — Scope 1, 2, and 3 GHG emissions with reasonable assurance: mandatory for top 150 listed entities from FY2023-24 (reporting due in annual reports filed by September 2024); extends to top 1,000 listed entities by FY2026-27
  • BEE PAT Scheme Cycle III — energy consumption and intensity reduction targets for designated consumers (DCs) across 13 industrial sectors: compliance and trading period active; DCs must meet specific energy consumption targets or purchase Energy Saving Certificates (ESCerts)
  • EU CBAM transitional phase — Indian exporters in covered sectors must submit quarterly embedded carbon reports to EU importers: reporting obligation active from October 2023 through December 2025; full financial carbon cost liability from January 1, 2026
  • ISSB IFRS S2 Climate Disclosures — scenario analysis and climate-related financial risk disclosure: adopted by IOSCO; SEBI is expected to align BRSR evolution with ISSB S1/S2 architecture — Indian listed companies should treat scenario analysis as a near-term disclosure expectation, likely mandatory within 2–3 years
  • MoEFCC Environment Impact Assessment (EIA) Notification — any new coal or gas infrastructure requiring environmental clearance must demonstrate alignment with India's NDC pathways; boards approving such capex face increasing regulatory and investor scrutiny
  • India's Updated NDC (2022) — 500 GW non-fossil capacity by 2030, 50% electricity from non-fossil sources by 2030: all corporate energy procurement strategies and PPAs must be stress-tested against this policy trajectory to assess stranded asset risk in existing fossil fuel offtake contracts

Climate Transition Angle

The CAT analysis crystallises a transition risk that Indian boards have been underpricing: the physical and transition risk vectors are now converging simultaneously. On the physical side, Middle East supply disruptions — which CAT estimates are affecting ~20% of global energy supply — directly raise India's energy import costs and inflation, a macroeconomic transmission mechanism that ultimately affects corporate earnings, consumer demand, and sovereign fiscal capacity. On the transition risk side, India's accelerating renewable buildout (solar module prices have fallen over 90% in a decade; utility-scale solar is now the cheapest form of new electricity generation in India at INR 2–3/kWh) is rapidly making new coal and gas assets economically non-competitive, even before carbon pricing mechanisms fully activate. For Indian companies, the BRSR Core's Scope 3 disclosure requirement is particularly consequential in this context. Supply chain emissions — Category 1 (purchased goods and services) and Category 4 (upstream transportation) — are directly exposed to fossil fuel import price volatility and embedded carbon costs. A steel company using imported coking coal, or a cement company running captive coal-fired power, is carrying both a financial risk (fuel cost volatility) and a disclosure risk (rising Scope 1 and 2 emissions intensity) that will increasingly affect access to green finance, export market eligibility, and ESG ratings. The SBTi's requirement that corporate net-zero targets cover Scope 1, 2, and material Scope 3 emissions means that companies with heavy fossil fuel dependency in their value chains face a structural barrier to credible target-setting — which in turn affects their eligibility for green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, and institutional ESG mandates. India's carbon market, under development via the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) notified by MoEFCC in June 2023, will over time put an explicit price on this embedded fossil fuel dependency, further compressing the economics of coal and gas-intensive operations. The renewable energy acceleration pathway — if executed with storage integration, grid flexibility, and demand-side management — represents India's most credible hedge against both physical energy security risk and transition-related capital market risk. For corporate energy buyers, this translates into a clear strategic imperative: accelerate long-term renewable PPAs, invest in captive solar and wind, and where feasible, pursue green hydrogen for hard-to-abate process heat. These are not just ESG commitments — they are balance sheet risk management decisions that CFOs should be driving alongside CSOs.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Treasury: Within Q3 FY2025, commission a quantitative stress test mapping the company's direct and indirect fossil fuel import cost exposure against three oil/gas price scenarios (base, +30%, +60%) — this feeds directly into BRSR Core's financial risk disclosure and ISSB S2 scenario analysis expectations
  • CSO / Sustainability team: Map all energy inputs — grid electricity, captive generation, process fuels — against BRSR Core Scope 1/2/3 boundaries by December 2024; identify which Scope 3 categories carry fossil fuel import price risk and disclose accordingly in the FY2025 annual report
  • Board Risk Committee: Add 'fossil fuel import dependency and stranded asset risk' as a named line item in the enterprise risk register, with quantified financial exposure and a transition timeline; link this to the company's capital allocation review for any planned coal or gas infrastructure investments over a 5-year horizon
  • Strategy / Capex Committee: Any new capex proposals involving coal-based power, gas distribution, or LNG offtake contracts must include a stranded asset assessment against India's 2030 NDC targets and IEA's Net Zero 2050 scenario — board approval should require explicit sign-off on transition risk
  • Procurement / Supply Chain: For EU-facing exporters, initiate CBAM embedded carbon calculation for all exported products in covered categories immediately — the transitional reporting period is live, and errors or gaps will create compliance liability from January 2026
  • Investor Relations: Proactively brief top 10–15 institutional investors (both domestic and FII) on the company's fossil fuel transition strategy, renewable energy procurement roadmap, and BRSR Core GHG disclosure trajectory — investors applying ISSB or TCFD frameworks are asking these questions now, and silence is being read as unpreparedness
  • Legal / Compliance: Review all long-term fossil fuel offtake contracts and PPAs for force majeure, price re-opener, and termination provisions in light of accelerating energy transition policy; escalate any contracts with tenors beyond 2030 to board risk committee for stress-testing

Our Take

The CAT analysis by Dr. Nandini Das is a rigorous, data-grounded articulation of what India's most sophisticated energy economists have been saying privately for years: India's energy transition is not a climate policy choice — it is an economic and national security imperative. What makes the current moment strategically different from previous energy shock cycles is the simultaneous maturation of three forces that did not exist together before: renewable energy is now cheaper than new fossil fuel capacity in India; mandatory ESG disclosure frameworks (BRSR Core, EU CBAM, incoming ISSB alignment) are making fossil fuel dependency financially legible to capital markets; and India's carbon market architecture (CCTS + PAT scheme convergence) is beginning to put a price signal on carbon intensity for the first time. For boards and institutional investors, the 12–36 month outlook is unambiguous: the cost of inaction on fossil fuel transition is rising faster than the cost of transition itself. We expect SEBI to progressively align BRSR with ISSB S1 and S2 over the next two annual reporting cycles, which will introduce mandatory climate scenario analysis for listed companies — a disclosure that will make stranded asset risk in coal and gas infrastructure directly visible to markets. We also expect MoEFCC's CCTS to begin its first compliance cycle for obligated entities by FY2026-27, creating an explicit carbon cost that will further erode the economics of coal-intensive operations. Companies that treat this as a compliance exercise will be perpetually behind; companies that treat it as a capital reallocation signal — accelerating renewable procurement, retiring or repurposing fossil fuel assets, and embedding carbon pricing into investment hurdle rates — will build a structural competitive advantage in export markets, green finance access, and institutional investor engagement. The renewable opportunity that CAT identifies is real. The question for Indian boards is whether they will capture it proactively or be forced into it reactively, at higher cost and with greater reputational and regulatory exposure.
