Executive Summary

In October 2025, the IMO failed to adopt the Net Zero Framework — its most ambitious shipping decarbonisation mechanism to date — postponing the decision to MEPC 84 in April 2026 under pressure from the US and Saudi Arabia. Climate Action Tracker (CAT) warns this delay risks locking international shipping onto a 4°C warming trajectory through 2050, compared to a near-2°C pathway if the NZF is adopted and strengthened. For India, the stakes are existential on multiple fronts: as the world's second-largest maritime nation by cargo volume, home to major shipbuilding ambitions under the Sagarmala programme, and as an exporter whose supply chain competitiveness is directly contingent on how quickly green shipping corridors, alternative fuel infrastructure, and carbon-adjusted freight costs become the global standard. Indian boards and CSOs must not treat MEPC 84 as a distant diplomatic event. The NZF's fuel emissions standard and market-based mechanism will directly determine freight cost structures, green corridor eligibility, EU FuelEU Maritime compliance exposure for Indian-flagged or Indian-chartered vessels, and the commercial viability of India's nascent green hydrogen and methanol export ambitions.

What Changed

Reporting by Climate Action Tracker (https://climateactiontracker.org/blog/rescuing-shippings-net-zero-framework/), the NZF — agreed at MEPC 82 earlier in 2025 — was expected to be formally adopted at the IMO's October 2025 session. It was not. The US and Saudi Arabia applied sufficient pressure to defer the decision to MEPC 84, scheduled for April 2026. The NZF, in its current form, combines a global fuel emissions intensity standard (the GFS — Greenhouse Gas Fuel Standard) with a market-based mechanism (MBM) that would price carbon in shipping through a combination of compliance units and a dedicated IMO Fund designed to accelerate zero-emission fuel (ZEF) uptake and support a just transition for developing nations including Small Island Developing States (SIDS) and LDCs. CAT's modelling is unambiguous and alarming: without the NZF, shipping emissions will continue rising through 2050. With it, the sector moves from 'Highly Insufficient' (consistent with 3–4°C) to 'Insufficient' (consistent with 2–3°C) by 2030 — still far from Paris-aligned but a material step change. Critically, CAT finds that if stronger fuel standard targets are set post-2035, the NZF framework architecture could theoretically be made 1.5°C-compatible by 2050. The delay to April 2026 is therefore not merely procedural — every month of inaction extends the window during which LNG lock-in, biofuel dependency, and unpriced carbon in shipping freight rates continue to distort the green transition economics. For India, this matters in five specific ways. First, the Directorate General of Shipping (DGS) and the Ministry of Ports, Shipping and Waterways (MoPSW) are active IMO member-state participants — India's diplomatic position at MEPC 84 will signal its alignment with the SIDS/LDC bloc supporting adoption or with the obstructionist bloc. Second, India's National Green Hydrogen Mission (NGHM) — which targets 5 MMTPA of green hydrogen production by 2030 — is premised partly on export via green ammonia and methanol shipping routes; the NZF's fuel standard directly prices the competitiveness of these fuels versus LNG. Third, Indian exporters in sectors including textiles, chemicals, auto components, and pharmaceuticals face mounting Scope 3 freight emissions disclosure requirements under BRSR Core (for the top 150 listed companies from FY2024-25) and will face direct exposure if EU counterparties begin requiring FuelEU Maritime-compliant shipping as a supply chain condition. Fourth, India's major ports — JNPT, Mundra, Chennai, Vishakhapatnam — are negotiating green shipping corridor MOUs under the Clydebank Declaration framework; the NZF's adoption is the enabling policy instrument for those corridors to achieve commercial credibility. Fifth, Indian shipyards under the Sagarmala and Shipbuilding Financial Assistance Policy are positioning for alternative-fuel vessel orders — a weakened or delayed NZF materially reduces the order pipeline and technology investment rationale for those yards.

Who Is Affected

  • Indian exporters in merchandise-heavy sectors: textiles, pharma, chemicals, engineering goods, auto components — whose Scope 3 Category 4 (upstream transportation) and Category 9 (downstream transportation) emissions are directly tied to the carbon intensity of the vessels carrying their goods
  • BSE 500 and NSE-listed companies required to report BRSR Core disclosures including Scope 3 emissions — especially those with export revenues exceeding 20% of total revenue where shipping freight is a material emissions source
  • India's major port authorities and private terminal operators (Adani Ports, JSW Infrastructure, DP World India operations) whose capex planning for onshore green fuel bunkering infrastructure (green methanol, green ammonia, LNG) is contingent on IMO fuel standard timelines
  • Indian shipbuilding companies (Cochin Shipyard, Mazagon Dock, Garden Reach Shipbuilders) seeking alternative-fuel vessel contracts from domestic and international shipowners — the NZF delay reduces near-term order certainty
  • Green hydrogen and green ammonia producers and developers targeting maritime export corridors — ACME, Greenko, L&T, NTPC Green Energy, ReNew Power — whose project economics rest partly on shipping fuel demand curves shaped by the NZF
  • Institutional investors and ESG-focused funds with exposure to Indian shipping, port, logistics, and energy transition stocks — including foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) subject to SFDR Article 8/9 requirements who must assess Principal Adverse Impact (PAI) indicators including shipping emissions
  • CFOs and treasury heads at Indian conglomerates with captive shipping fleets or long-term freight charter agreements, where carbon pricing in shipping will become a balance sheet liability post-NZF adoption
  • Chief Sustainability Officers at Indian companies preparing CDP Climate disclosures or ISSB IFRS S2-aligned climate risk assessments where shipping sector transition risk must be quantified as a material financial risk

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • MEPC 84, April 2026: IMO member states vote on NZF adoption — India's DGS and MoPSW must finalise India's diplomatic position by Q1 2026; CSOs at Indian shipping-exposed companies should engage MoPSW on India's stance now
  • EU FuelEU Maritime: enters into force January 2025 for vessels >5,000 GT calling at EU ports — Indian-flagged or Indian-chartered vessels on EU trade routes must already comply; non-compliance triggers surcharges that Indian exporters may absorb indirectly through freight cost pass-through
  • BRSR Core Scope 3 disclosures: top 150 listed companies from FY2024-25, expanding to top 250 from FY2025-26 — Category 4 and 9 freight emissions must be disclosed; shipping carbon intensity of contracted carriers becomes a material data requirement
  • IMO CII (Carbon Intensity Indicator) and EEXI (Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index): already mandatory from January 2023 — Indian shipowners and operators must already be rated; CII ratings directly affect vessel charterability and insurance pricing
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (effective for reporting periods beginning January 2024): Indian companies voluntarily adopting or those subject to investor demands must assess climate-related financial risks including shipping sector transition risk in their value chain — the NZF delay extends the period of regulatory uncertainty that must be disclosed as a risk
  • India's National Green Hydrogen Mission milestones: 1 MMTPA production target by 2026-27 — project developers must model NZF adoption/non-adoption scenarios into offtake and export logistics assumptions by mid-2025 planning cycles
  • SBTi FLAG and Transport sector guidance: companies with significant freight emissions seeking SBTi validation must account for Scope 3 shipping emissions reduction pathways — the NZF's trajectory affects the baseline and reduction curve used in target-setting

Climate Transition Angle

CAT's central finding — that the NZF determines whether shipping drifts to 4°C or converges toward 2°C — has profound implications for Indian climate transition strategy, which cannot be understood in isolation from the sector. International shipping accounts for approximately 2.9% of global GHG emissions, but for India, maritime trade carries over 70% of the country's merchandise exports by volume. The embedded carbon in that freight is increasingly being scrutinised by European buyers under the EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) and CSRD supply chain emissions disclosure requirements — meaning the NZF is not just an IMO issue but a direct supply chain competitiveness and market access issue for Indian exporters. Under India's Updated NDC (submitted to UNFCCC in 2022), India committed to reducing emissions intensity of GDP by 45% by 2030 from 2005 levels and achieving 50% cumulative electric power capacity from non-fossil sources. Shipping decarbonisation, while not explicitly named, sits within India's broader industrial and export sector transition pathway. More concretely, India's NGHM positions green ammonia and green methanol as priority export commodities — and the IMO's fuel standard is the single most important demand-side policy instrument for those fuels. A weakened NZF, or one that locks in LNG (as CAT explicitly warns is a design risk), directly undermines the NGHM's export revenue assumptions and the capital mobilisation rationale for the $100 billion+ green hydrogen investment pipeline India is targeting. The just transition dimension — highlighted by SIDS and LDC support for the NZF's IMO Fund — resonates directly with India's G20 presidency legacy and its positioning at COP29 and beyond as a voice for climate equity. India voting against or abstaining on NZF adoption at MEPC 84 would create a diplomatic contradiction with its stated climate leadership posture. Conversely, India actively championing NZF adoption with strengthened fuel standards positions it as a beneficiary of the MBM's technology transfer and fund provisions, potentially directing IMO Fund resources toward Indian port green infrastructure. The BRSR and ISSB S2 frameworks increasingly require companies to disclose physical and transition climate risks across the value chain — for Indian manufacturers with maritime supply chains, the NZF's fate is a named, quantifiable transition risk that must appear in FY2026 disclosures.

What To Do Now

  • Sustainability/Government Affairs teams at Indian exporters and port operators: engage MoPSW and DGS by January 2026 to understand India's planned position at MEPC 84 and assess alignment with your company's green corridor and BRSR Scope 3 commitments — this is a six-week window
  • CFOs at BSE 500 companies with >15% export revenues: commission a freight carbon intensity audit of your top five shipping lanes by Q1 FY2026 to baseline Scope 3 Category 4/9 emissions ahead of BRSR Core expansion to top 250 companies — use IMO DCS (Data Collection System) records if available from carriers
  • CSOs at companies preparing ISSB IFRS S2-aligned or CDP Climate disclosures: explicitly model two NZF scenarios (adopted April 2026 vs. further delayed/weakened) as transition risk scenarios in your climate risk assessment — quantify the freight cost delta and Scope 3 emissions trajectory difference; this is now material enough to require board-level disclosure
  • Green hydrogen and green ammonia project developers (ACME, Greenko, NTPC Green Energy, L&T Hydrocarbon): update financial models and offtake term sheets to include NZF sensitivity analysis — if the NZF is weakened to permit LNG flexibility through 2035, green ammonia bunkering demand curves shift materially; stress-test IRR at both NZF-adopted and NZF-weakened scenarios before finalising project FIDs in 2025-26
  • Port authorities and private terminal operators (Adani Ports, JSW Infrastructure): accelerate green bunkering infrastructure feasibility studies under Clydebank Declaration corridor commitments — specifically for green methanol and green ammonia — independent of NZF timing, since EU FuelEU Maritime is already live and creating demand from vessels on Europe-India routes
  • Investor relations and board risk committees at listed Indian companies: include IMO NZF delay as a named transition risk in the FY2025-26 Annual Report's Management Discussion & Analysis section under 'Climate and Sustainability Risks' — citing BRSR Principle 6 and ISSB S2 risk identification requirements; do not wait for MEPC 84 outcome to begin disclosure

Our Take

The IMO NZF postponement is a microcosm of the broader geopolitical fracturing of multilateral climate governance — and Indian boards should read it as a structural signal, not a temporary hiccup. The US withdrawal from climate multilateralism under the current administration, combined with Saudi Arabia's strategic interest in protecting fossil-fuel shipping revenue streams, has now demonstrably delayed the single most consequential sectoral decarbonisation mechanism outside of power and transport. CAT's finding that the NZF in its current form only moves shipping from 'Highly Insufficient' to 'Insufficient' should itself be alarming — this is the best case, not the benchmark. What is most consequential for India in the next 12 to 36 months is not whether MEPC 84 adopts the NZF — it probably will, in some form, given the coalition of SIDS, LDCs, and EU member states in support — but whether the MBM and IMO Fund survive intact or are gutted through the 'flexibility' provisions that CAT explicitly identifies as risks for LNG lock-in and ZEF delay. India's strategic posture at MEPC 84 will be one of its most consequential climate diplomacy decisions of 2026. A India that votes for a strong NZF — with the IMO Fund intact and fuel standard targets escalating post-2035 — is an India that positions itself as a green fuel exporter, a Clydebank corridor leader, and a just transition champion consistent with its NDC and NGHM commitments. An India that hedges, abstains, or aligns with the weakening bloc to protect short-term LNG bunkering revenues at ports like JNPT and Mundra is an India that undermines its own green hydrogen investment narrative and hands a reputational gift to competitors like South Korea, Japan, and the Netherlands who are already positioning as green shipping leaders. For institutional investors, the NZF delay extends uncertainty over the shipping sector's carbon liability — which means Scope 3 freight emissions remain harder to price and harder to reduce in portfolio company value chains. ESG-focused FPIs with SFDR Article 8/9 mandates should be pressing Indian investee companies on their freight decarbonisation roadmaps now, not after MEPC 84. The companies that have already mapped their maritime Scope 3 exposure, engaged their freight forwarders and shipping lines on fuel transition timelines, and built NZF scenario analysis into their ISSB S2 disclosures will be materially better positioned for capital access, export market credibility, and audit committee scrutiny in the 2026-2030 window. The NZF's fate is not a shipping industry problem — it is a board-level ESG risk for every Indian company that moves goods across an ocean.
