Executive Summary

On 30 June 2023, the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) issued a formal letter to Customs Authorities directing the release of imported consignments belonging to producers of 106 Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) items covered under India's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework. This directive signals that CPCB is actively coordinating with border agencies to ensure EPR-compliant producers face no undue trade disruption, while simultaneously reinforcing that EPR registration and compliance are non-negotiable prerequisites for importing covered EEE goods. Companies operating in the electronics and electrical equipment space must treat this as a clear signal that EPR compliance is now a supply chain and customs-clearance issue — not merely a regulatory checkbox.

What Changed

The CPCB's letter to Customs Authorities on 30 June 2023 is a critical operational intervention under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, which mandate Extended Producer Responsibility for producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) of 106 categories of Electrical and Electronic Equipment listed in Schedule I of the Rules. Under EPR, producers are legally obligated to register on the CPCB EPR portal, obtain EPR certificates, and meet annual e-waste collection and channelisation targets. The significance of this letter lies at the intersection of trade and environmental regulation: Customs Authorities at Indian ports and airports serve as a practical enforcement point for EPR compliance. When consignments of covered EEE items are held at the border — either because the importer lacks EPR registration or because there is ambiguity about their compliance status — it creates immediate business disruption. CPCB's directive to facilitate the release of consignments for producers who are registered and compliant is both a relief measure and a compliance signal. It confirms that Customs is now an active enforcement partner in the EPR ecosystem, and that unregistered or non-compliant importers risk having their goods held at the border indefinitely. In plain terms: if you import any of the 106 covered EEE items and you are not registered on the CPCB EPR portal with a valid EPR authorisation, your shipments are at risk of being detained at customs. This letter resolves the immediate bottleneck for compliant producers — but it draws a sharper line for those who have not yet registered.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers and importers of any of the 106 EEE item categories listed under Schedule I of the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — including consumer electronics, IT and telecom equipment, lighting equipment, electrical and electronic tools, and large/small household appliances.
  • Brand owners who place EEE products on the Indian market under their own label, even if manufactured by a third party domestically or abroad.
  • Foreign Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) with Indian subsidiaries or authorised importers who bring in covered electronic goods.
  • Customs brokers and logistics providers handling EEE shipments at Indian ports, ICDs (Inland Container Depots), and airports — as they must now verify EPR compliance status before clearance.
  • Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) in the electronics import and distribution segment who may have assumed EPR registration was only applicable to large corporations — this notice dispels that assumption.
  • E-commerce companies and marketplace operators importing electronics inventory directly.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • EPR Registration (Immediate): All producers, importers, and brand owners of covered EEE items must register on the CPCB EPR Portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in) and obtain a valid EPR authorisation before importing any covered goods. This is a prerequisite — not an optional step.
  • Annual EPR Target Compliance: Registered PIBOs must meet annual e-waste collection targets as prescribed under the E-Waste Rules, 2022. Targets are set as a percentage of quantity of EEE placed on the market in previous years (Phase I: 60% for Year 1, scaling upward).
  • EPR Certificate Procurement: Producers must procure EPR certificates from CPCB-registered recyclers and dismantlers to demonstrate that e-waste equivalent to their obligation has been responsibly processed. These certificates must be uploaded on the CPCB portal.
  • Customs Documentation: With immediate effect following this notice, companies should ensure that EPR authorisation letters and registration numbers are included in import documentation packages presented to Customs Authorities to facilitate smooth clearance.
  • Annual Filing: PIBOs are required to file annual returns on the CPCB portal detailing quantities of EEE placed on the market and e-waste collected/recycled, within prescribed timelines under the Rules.
  • Deadline for FY 2022-23 Compliance: Producers should have already met their EPR targets for the financial year ending 31 March 2023. Any shortfall must be addressed through extended EPR certificate procurement and reported to CPCB promptly to avoid penalties.
  • Penalty Exposure: Non-compliance with EPR obligations can attract Environmental Compensation (EC) as prescribed under the Rules, in addition to potential detention of imported consignments at Customs.

Climate Transition Angle

India's EPR framework for e-waste is not merely a domestic compliance exercise — it sits squarely within the global push for circular economy principles that are central to credible climate transition strategies. The 106 EEE categories covered under these Rules encompass products whose manufacturing is highly resource- and energy-intensive, involving rare earth metals, hazardous materials, and long, complex global supply chains. By mandating that producers take end-of-life responsibility for these products, India is embedding circular economy logic into its industrial policy in a manner increasingly aligned with international frameworks. From a physical risk perspective, informal e-waste recycling — which EPR is designed to displace — contributes directly to soil and water contamination, creating localised environmental degradation that affects community health and, over time, erodes the social licence of companies operating in affected areas. EPR-compliant, formal recycling reduces these risks and builds supply chain resilience by enabling recovery of critical minerals (cobalt, lithium, copper, rare earths) that are essential for the energy transition itself — including batteries for electric vehicles and renewable energy storage. Global ESG reporting standards are increasingly demanding supply chain transparency in this domain. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) under IFRS S2 requires companies to disclose material climate-related risks across their value chains. Extended producer responsibility obligations — and the risk of supply chain disruption from customs detention — are precisely the kind of regulatory and operational risks that must be disclosed under these standards. Similarly, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its sector-specific standards are expanding obligations for European companies with Indian suppliers or subsidiaries. Indian exporters of electronics components who are not EPR-compliant may find themselves unable to meet the due diligence requirements of their European buyers. This customs-level enforcement by CPCB is, therefore, a preview of the kind of supply chain accountability that global standards will require comprehensively within this decade.

What To Do Now

  • Audit Your Product Portfolio Immediately: Map all EEE products you produce, import, or brand against the 106 categories in Schedule I of the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022. Identify which products are covered and quantify the volumes placed on the Indian market in the preceding financial years — this forms the basis for your EPR obligation calculation.
  • Register or Verify Registration on the CPCB EPR Portal: If your organisation is not yet registered, initiate the registration process on the CPCB EPR portal without delay. If already registered, verify that your authorisation is current, your annual targets are correctly filed, and your EPR certificates are uploaded. Assign a dedicated compliance officer with accountability for this portal.
  • Brief Your Customs and Logistics Teams: Share the CPCB Customs Authority letter reference with your customs brokers, freight forwarders, and import teams. Instruct them to include EPR authorisation documentation in all import clearance packages for covered EEE consignments to avoid detention at ports and ICDs.
  • Engage CPCB-Registered Recyclers and Dismantlers: Establish or review agreements with authorised e-waste processors to ensure you can generate sufficient EPR certificates to meet your annual obligations. Build this into procurement and vendor management processes — do not treat it as an afterthought at year-end.
  • Integrate EPR Compliance into ESG Reporting and Due Diligence: Include your EPR registration status, annual targets, and achievement rates in your BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report) disclosures and any voluntary ESG reporting frameworks you follow. Proactively communicate compliance status to investors, buyers, and global partners who are increasingly scrutinising supply chain sustainability credentials.

Our Take

CPCB's decision to issue a formal letter to Customs Authorities is a strategic escalation that should not be read narrowly as an administrative facilitation measure. It is, in effect, the regulator making trade access conditional on EPR compliance — a powerful lever that transforms what many companies treated as a back-office environmental obligation into a front-line business continuity issue. The companies that will be most affected are not the large, well-resourced electronics majors who have already built EPR compliance into their operations, but the mid-tier importers, distributors, and brand owners who have delayed registration assuming enforcement would remain lax. That assumption is now definitively invalidated. Looking ahead, we expect CPCB to deepen its coordination with Customs, the Directorate General of Foreign Trade (DGFT), and potentially the Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) to create a multi-agency enforcement ecosystem around covered EEE imports. Companies that invest now in robust EPR compliance infrastructure — including digital tracking of product placement volumes, formal agreements with certified recyclers, and integration of EPR data into sustainability reporting — will not only avoid costly supply chain disruptions but will build the kind of ESG credibility that increasingly influences both market access and capital allocation decisions in a transitioning global economy.
