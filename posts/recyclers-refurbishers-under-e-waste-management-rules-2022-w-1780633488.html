Recyclers & Refurbishers under E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022: What the Latest CPCB Notice Means for Your Business

Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice targeting recyclers and refurbishers operating under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, signalling heightened enforcement attention on Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations across the electronics value chain. Recyclers and refurbishers who have not yet registered on the EPR portal, filed returns, or met channelisation targets face immediate compliance risk, including potential suspension of authorisations. Companies across the electronics, IT hardware, consumer durables, and telecom sectors — both as producers and as their downstream recycling partners — must treat this notice as a call to audit their EPR ecosystems without delay.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — which replaced the 2016 framework — significantly expanded the Extended Producer Responsibility architecture in India. Under the new rules, producers, manufacturers, refurbishers, and recyclers are each assigned distinct legal obligations: producers must meet annual EPR targets by procuring EPR certificates from registered recyclers; recyclers must obtain CPCB authorisation, operate within approved capacities, and generate EPR certificates only against verifiable e-waste processed; and refurbishers must register, report refurbished volumes, and channel residual waste to authorised recyclers. The CPCB's latest urgent notice is directed specifically at recyclers and refurbishers, suggesting that the Board has identified widespread non-compliance in this segment — whether through missed registration deadlines, underreporting of processed volumes, unauthorised capacity expansion, or failure to upload EPR certificates on the centralised portal. This type of urgent notice from CPCB typically precedes show-cause notices, cancellation of authorisations, and in serious cases, referral to State Pollution Control Boards for on-ground enforcement action. The message is unambiguous: the era of passive compliance in e-waste recycling is over.

Who Is Affected

  • Authorised E-Waste Recyclers: Entities processing end-of-life electronics and generating EPR certificates for producers — directly in the crosshairs of this notice.
  • Refurbishers: Companies that repair, refurbish, and resell used electronic and electrical equipment (EEE) and are required to register under the 2022 Rules and manage residual waste responsibly.
  • Electronics & IT Hardware Producers: OEMs, importers, and brand owners whose EPR compliance depends on the validity and volume of certificates procured from recyclers — if your recycler is non-compliant, your EPR targets are at risk.
  • Consumer Durables & Telecom Equipment Manufacturers: Companies selling products listed under Schedule I of the E-Waste Rules, obligated to channel e-waste through authorised recyclers.
  • Bulk Consumers & Dismantlers: Large institutional users of EEE (IT companies, banks, government entities) and dismantlers in the informal-to-formal transition pipeline who interact with the recycling ecosystem.
  • EPR Compliance Aggregators & PROs: Producer Responsibility Organisations facilitating EPR certificate procurement on behalf of multiple producers — whose entire service model depends on recycler authorisation status.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Registration on CPCB EPR Portal (Immediate): All recyclers and refurbishers must be registered on the centralised EPR portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in). Unregistered entities must apply immediately and halt operations pending authorisation.
  • Annual Returns Filing: Recyclers and refurbishers are required to file annual returns on the portal detailing volumes of e-waste collected, processed, and EPR certificates generated. Entities that have missed filings for FY 2022-23 or FY 2023-24 must rectify this without delay.
  • EPR Certificate Generation & Reconciliation: Recyclers must ensure EPR certificates are generated only against actual e-waste processed and reconciled with producer accounts on the portal. Mismatches or inflated certificate issuance are a key CPCB enforcement focus.
  • Capacity Compliance: Recyclers must operate strictly within CPCB-authorised processing capacities. Any expansion requires prior amendment of authorisation — operating beyond approved capacity is a violation.
  • Channelisation Documentation: Refurbishers must maintain records of all e-waste channelled to authorised recyclers and upload proof of disposal on the EPR portal. Residual waste cannot be handed to informal/unauthorised handlers.
  • Response to CPCB Notice (Urgent): Any entity that has received a direct communication from CPCB must respond within the stipulated timeframe (typically 15–30 days) with documentary evidence of compliance. Failure to respond is treated as admission of non-compliance.
  • State-Level Authorisation Renewal: Recyclers must also ensure their State Pollution Control Board (SPCB) authorisations under the Hazardous and Other Wastes Rules are current — CPCB and SPCB actions are often coordinated.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste regulation sits at the intersection of circular economy principles and climate transition strategy — and this is increasingly recognised by global investors and standard-setters. The ISSB's IFRS S2 standard and the EU's CSRD both require companies to disclose how their value chains — including end-of-life product management — contribute to or mitigate climate risk. India's own SEBI Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) framework, mandatory for the top 1,000 listed companies, requires disclosure of waste management practices including e-waste under Principle 2 (Sustainable & Safe Products and Processes). From a physical risk standpoint, the informal e-waste sector — which handles an estimated 80–90% of India's e-waste — releases toxic materials and greenhouse gases, contributing to localised environmental degradation and long-term supply chain risk for critical minerals like lithium, cobalt, and rare earths. Formalising the recycling ecosystem through rules like these is therefore not just a compliance exercise — it is foundational to building the domestic circular material flows that India's clean energy transition (batteries, solar panels, EVs) will depend on. For multinational companies sourcing from or selling into India, due diligence requirements under the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) will increasingly require evidence of responsible end-of-life management in supply chains. Indian recyclers and producers who cannot demonstrate CPCB-compliant operations risk being excluded from global supply chains as these requirements come into force between 2026 and 2028.

What To Do Now

  • Audit Your Recycler & Refurbisher Network Immediately: Producers and PROs should pull the current authorisation status of every recycler in their EPR chain from the CPCB portal. Any recycler with lapsed, suspended, or missing authorisation puts your EPR certificate holdings at legal risk — act before CPCB does.
  • Verify Portal Compliance End-to-End: Log into the CPCB EPR portal and confirm that all annual returns are filed, EPR certificates are reconciled, and no discrepancies exist between declared processing volumes and certificates issued. Engage your portal administrator or compliance consultant to conduct a full data audit.
  • Respond to Any CPCB Communications Proactively: If your organisation has received any notice, advisory, or query from CPCB — even a routine one — treat it as urgent. Prepare a structured compliance response with documentary evidence of authorisation, processing records, and return filings. Legal counsel with environmental regulatory experience should review all responses.
  • Assess BRSR & ESG Disclosure Implications: For listed companies, check whether your FY 2024-25 BRSR disclosures accurately reflect your e-waste EPR status. Gaps between reported compliance and actual portal status are a growing area of ESG investor scrutiny and could attract regulatory attention.
  • Engage with Informal-to-Formal Transition Planning: If your supply chain relies on informal recyclers or dismantlers, begin a structured transition plan to channel e-waste exclusively through CPCB-authorised entities. Document this transition roadmap as part of your climate and circular economy commitments under ISSB/CSRD-aligned reporting.

Our Take

This notice is a clear indicator that CPCB is moving from framework-building to active enforcement in India's e-waste sector — and the timing is deliberate. With EPR portals now operational, data on recycler performance is visible to regulators in near real-time, removing the ambiguity that allowed non-compliance to persist under the old paper-based system. What we are witnessing is the natural maturation of India's EPR architecture: the rules are in place, the digital infrastructure exists, and regulators are now using it. Companies — both producers dependent on recycler certificates and recyclers themselves — that treated EPR as a box-ticking exercise are now exposed. More importantly, this enforcement push is a preview of things to come: India is expected to progressively tighten e-waste targets, expand the product scope of the rules, and align its framework more closely with global circular economy standards as pressure from BRSR, ISSB, and trade partners intensifies. The companies that invest now in building genuinely compliant, transparent, and resilient EPR ecosystems will not just avoid regulatory penalties — they will be positioned to capture the commercial opportunity of a formalised, growing circular economy in one of the world's largest electronics markets.
