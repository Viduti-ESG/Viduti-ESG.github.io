EFRAG & EU CSRD: What Indian Companies Exporting to Europe Must Know Now

Executive Summary

The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), underpinned by standards developed by EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group), is rapidly becoming the most consequential sustainability disclosure regime in the world — and its reach extends well beyond European borders. Indian companies supplying goods or services to EU-based corporations, or those listed on EU-regulated markets, are already within scope of its value chain disclosure requirements. Companies that treat CSRD as a purely European problem do so at significant commercial risk.

What Changed

The CSRD replaced the earlier Non-Financial Reporting Directive (NFRD) and dramatically expanded both the scope of companies required to report and the depth of information they must disclose. The European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), developed by EFRAG and adopted by the European Commission, form the technical backbone of CSRD reporting. These standards cover a sweeping range of topics — climate change (physical and transition risks), biodiversity, water, pollution, workforce conditions, supply chain due diligence, and governance. Crucially, ESRS E1 (Climate) requires large EU companies to disclose their full value chain emissions — Scope 1, 2, and 3 — along with transition plans, scenario analyses, and climate-related financial risks. This means EU buyers will increasingly demand verified emissions data, sustainability performance metrics, and evidence of credible decarbonisation commitments from their Indian suppliers. EFRAG continues to refine sector-specific standards and guidance for Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs), signalling that the regulatory framework is still evolving and tightening.

Who Is Affected

  • Large Indian exporters supplying directly to EU-listed or EU-headquartered companies in sectors such as textiles, pharmaceuticals, automotive components, IT services, chemicals, and agri-commodities
  • Indian subsidiaries or joint ventures of European parent companies, which may be required to provide consolidated sustainability data
  • Indian companies with securities listed on EU-regulated markets or those raising capital from European institutional investors
  • Mid-sized Indian manufacturers embedded in EU company supply chains — even if not directly reporting under CSRD, they will face contractual data requests from EU customers
  • Indian IT and professional services firms providing ESG data management, assurance, or consulting services to European clients
  • Indian banks and financial institutions with significant exposure to EU-linked corporates or green bond markets

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • January 2025 (FY2024 data): Large EU public-interest entities (>500 employees, already under NFRD) must publish first CSRD-compliant reports — their Scope 3 data requests to Indian suppliers begin immediately
  • January 2026 (FY2025 data): All large EU companies (meeting two of three thresholds: >250 employees, >€40M turnover, >€20M total assets) must report under CSRD — significantly expanding the pool of EU buyers requesting value chain ESG data from Indian partners
  • January 2027 (FY2026 data): Listed SMEs on EU-regulated markets come into scope — relevant for Indian companies cross-listed in Europe
  • Ongoing — EFRAG Sector Standards: Sector-specific ESRS (covering oil & gas, mining, textiles, food & agriculture, and others) are under development; Indian companies in these sectors should monitor EFRAG's publication calendar closely
  • Ongoing — Double Materiality Assessments: EU companies must conduct 'double materiality' assessments identifying both how sustainability issues affect the business AND how the business impacts society and environment — Indian suppliers will be asked to contribute data to these assessments
  • Immediate — Contractual ESG clauses: EU buyers are already embedding CSRD-aligned data-sharing requirements into supplier contracts and RFP processes; Indian companies should review new and renewal contracts carefully

Climate Transition Angle

CSRD and the EFRAG standards are not merely a reporting exercise — they are a structural mechanism to align European corporate behaviour with the EU's 2050 net-zero target and the Paris Agreement's 1.5°C pathway. For Indian companies, this creates a dual imperative. First, physical climate risk: ESRS E1 requires EU companies to assess and disclose climate-related physical risks across their supply chains. Indian manufacturing hubs — particularly in coastal regions, river basins, and heat-stressed geographies — face material physical risks from extreme weather, water stress, and sea-level rise. EU buyers will scrutinise whether their Indian suppliers have assessed and are managing these risks, as supply chain disruptions become a financial disclosure item for European companies. Second, transition risk and global standard convergence: CSRD's architecture closely mirrors the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) framework — specifically IFRS S2 on climate — and has significant overlap with the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD). India's own SEBI Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) Core framework, and RBI's emerging climate risk guidance, are also converging toward these global standards. Indian companies that build CSRD-compatible reporting capabilities will simultaneously be better positioned for domestic BRSR compliance and ISSB-aligned disclosures as India's regulatory expectations rise.

What To Do Now

  • Map your EU exposure immediately: Identify all EU-domiciled customers, investors, and business partners. Determine which of them fall under CSRD's first and second waves of reporting — these entities will begin requesting supplier ESG data in 2025 and 2026 respectively
  • Establish a Scope 3 emissions baseline: Begin measuring your Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions across all three scopes using the GHG Protocol. EU buyers will require credible, ideally third-party verified emissions data. Engage a reputable assurance provider familiar with both GHG Protocol and ESRS requirements
  • Conduct a gap assessment against ESRS E1 and ESRS S1/S2: Review your current sustainability disclosures against the European Sustainability Reporting Standards — particularly on climate (E1), your own workforce (S1), and workers in your value chain (S2). Identify material gaps in data collection, governance, and reporting infrastructure
  • Engage legal and procurement teams on contract review: Ensure your legal and procurement teams are reviewing new EU customer contracts for sustainability data-sharing clauses, audit rights, and representations. Negotiate reasonable timelines and data specifications before signing
  • Align internal reporting systems with ISSB/ESRS architecture: Invest in ESG data management systems that can produce structured, auditable sustainability data aligned with both ESRS and ISSB IFRS S1/S2. This dual-standard compatibility will future-proof your reporting for both EU market access and domestic SEBI BRSR Core requirements

Our Take

CSRD is not on the horizon for Indian businesses — it is already at the door. The first wave of EU companies began reporting on FY2024 data in early 2025, meaning their Scope 3 supply chain data requests are live today. What makes CSRD particularly significant compared to previous sustainability initiatives is its legal enforceability: non-compliant EU companies face penalties, and they will pass that compliance pressure directly down their supply chains through contractual requirements, procurement scoring, and supplier audits. For Indian companies, the window to treat sustainability as a voluntary communications exercise has closed. Those who move now to build credible, data-driven sustainability reporting capabilities will gain a durable competitive advantage in EU markets — and will find themselves well-prepared for the tightening domestic regulatory environment that SEBI, RBI, and MCA are clearly signalling. Those who wait will face contract losses, market access barriers, and a far steeper — and more expensive — remediation curve.
