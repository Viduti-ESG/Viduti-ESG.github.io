Executive Summary

Carbon Tracker's January 2024 Climate Accounting and Audit Assessment (CAAA) for Climate Action 100+ companies — covering oil & gas and electric utilities — has established that the overwhelming majority of large listed emitters continue to produce financial statements that are materially inconsistent with their own stated climate commitments, violating the internal coherence test that auditors are now being pressed to apply. For Indian CFOs and audit committees at BSE 500 companies, this is not a distant benchmark: the same logical inconsistency — net zero pledges sitting entirely outside the financial statements — now sits at the centre of SEBI's BRSR Core assurance framework, ISSB IFRS S2 adoption discussions at MCA, and the expectations of every major foreign institutional investor with a CA100+ or Net Zero mandate voting at Indian AGMs.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker (https://carbontracker.org/reports/climate-accounting-and-audit-2023-assessments-to-climate-action-100/), published 26 January 2024, presents the third annual iteration of its CAAA methodology applied to CA100+ focus companies across oil & gas and electric utilities. The core finding — consistent with their prior 'Still Flying Blind' report — is that companies continue to use climate assumptions in their financial statements (asset useful life, impairment discount rates, capital expenditure schedules, reserve valuations) that are incompatible with a 1.5°C or even 2°C scenario. Auditors are signing off on accounts where a company simultaneously pledges net zero by 2050 in its sustainability report and then uses a business-as-usual fossil fuel demand curve to justify full asset carrying values under IAS 36. Carbon Tracker's methodology tests precisely this internal contradiction — it is not asking companies to adopt SBTi targets; it is asking whether the numbers in the financial statements are consistent with the narrative in the same annual report. This has direct and escalating relevance to Indian-listed companies for three reasons. First, SEBI's BRSR Core framework (mandatory for the top 150 listed companies by market cap from FY2023-24, expanding to top 250 from FY2024-25) now requires 'reasonable assurance' on nine KPIs including Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG emissions — this is the first time an Indian regulator has mandated external assurance on a climate metric that feeds directly into financial risk assessment. Second, ISSB IFRS S1 and S2 have been endorsed as the global baseline by IOSCO, of which SEBI is a member, and MCA's Company Law Committee is actively considering their adoption into Indian accounting standards — once that happens, the same 'climate-financial consistency' test that Carbon Tracker applies will become an audit obligation under Indian GAAP and Ind AS. Third, Reliance Industries, ONGC, NTPC, Adani Ports, JSW Steel, and Tata Steel are all CA100+ focus companies; their climate accounting practices are being scrutinised by the same institutional investors who hold significant FII positions on Indian exchanges.

Who Is Affected

  • Oil & gas producers and refiners: ONGC, Reliance Industries, Oil India, BPCL, HPCL — all holding long-dated fossil fuel assets whose carrying values depend on demand and price assumptions that must now be stress-tested against IEA NZE scenarios
  • Power utilities and coal-dependent generators: NTPC, Adani Power, Tata Power, NHPC — particularly those with coal plant assets whose useful life assumptions embedded in depreciation schedules may not survive a 1.5°C demand pathway
  • Statutory auditors and Big Four audit partners signing off accounts of BSE 500 energy and heavy industry companies — the CAAA methodology is now being cited by audit regulators globally as the standard for what 'climate-aware audit' looks like
  • CFOs and Group Finance Controllers at companies with net zero pledges or science-based targets — they carry personal accountability under Ind AS 36 (impairment) and Schedule III Companies Act disclosures if climate assumptions in accounts diverge materially from stated commitments
  • ESG heads and Chief Sustainability Officers preparing BRSR Core submissions who are not yet coordinating with their CFO and statutory auditor on the financial statement implications of their climate narratives
  • Foreign institutional investors with CA100+ or Net Zero Asset Managers Initiative (NZAMI) mandates — they are required by their own commitments to vote against auditor reappointment and against accounts where this inconsistency persists
  • Credit rating agencies and lenders under RBI's draft climate risk disclosure framework — the same asset impairment logic applies to collateral valuations and covenant assessments in green and sustainability-linked bonds

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core — Reasonable Assurance on GHG emissions (Scope 1 & 2): Mandatory for top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24 disclosures (filed by September 2024); expanding to top 250 from FY2024-25 — CFOs must ensure GHG figures are audit-ready and consistent with climate assumptions in financial statements
  • SEBI Circular SEBI/HO/CFD/CFD-SEC-2/P/CIR/2023/122 (July 2023): Mandates BRSR Core KPI assurance and introduces the 'glide path' for broader ESG disclosure — companies must align narrative climate commitments with financial reporting assumptions or face investor challenge at AGM
  • Ind AS 36 (Impairment of Assets): Already in force — requires companies to use assumptions consistent with their best estimate of future economic conditions; climate transition scenarios now constitute a quantifiable input; auditors who ignore this are exposed to NFRA scrutiny
  • IEA Net Zero Emissions by 2050 Scenario: Adopted as the reference scenario by CA100+ benchmark — Indian CA100+ focus companies must be able to demonstrate their capex plans, asset lives, and reserve valuations survive a stress test against this pathway or disclose why they do not
  • MCA / ICAI Ind AS convergence with ISSB IFRS S2: No formal deadline yet, but ICAI's Sustainability Reporting Standards Board (SRSB) is actively mapping ISSB to Indian context — CFOs should plan for adoption readiness within 24-36 months
  • CA100+ Net Zero Company Benchmark Phase 2: Assessment reports published January 2024 — Indian companies named as focus companies should review their individual company profiles (behind login at carbontracker.org) and prepare investor response briefs before the next proxy season (April-July 2024 AGMs)

Climate Transition Angle

The Carbon Tracker CAAA framework operationalises what regulators have been signalling for three years: climate transition risk is not a sustainability narrative — it is a balance sheet question. For Indian companies, the transition pathway is particularly acute because India has committed to net zero by 2070 under its NDC, and the BEE PAT scheme is already forcing energy intensity reductions that affect the productive life assumptions of industrial assets. NTPC's coal fleet, for example, carries assets with accounting lives extending to 2045-2060; if India's coal phase-down trajectory accelerates under pressure from the Global Stocktake or bilateral climate finance agreements, those asset lives — and the depreciation and impairment treatment flowing from them — become materially misstated. The same logic applies to ONGC's upstream reserve valuations under Ind AS 106 (Exploration for and Evaluation of Mineral Resources), where proved developed reserves are discounted at rates that embed an oil demand curve inconsistent with even a 2°C scenario. ISSB IFRS S2's requirement to disclose the financial effects of climate-related risks — including transition risks on asset values, revenue assumptions, and capex plans — will, once adopted in India, make this inconsistency an explicit audit finding rather than an activist campaign point. The capital market consequence is already playing out: FIIs with NZAMI commitments are required to escalate to voting action against companies that fail the climate accounting consistency test, and proxy advisors IiAS and SES are beginning to incorporate climate accounting quality into their audit committee assessments.

What To Do Now

  • CFO and statutory auditor (immediate — before FY2024 accounts sign-off): Conduct an internal 'climate accounting consistency review' — map every climate commitment made in the sustainability/BRSR report against the assumptions embedded in IAS 36 impairment models, asset useful life schedules, and capex authorisation papers; document where divergences exist and whether they are material
  • ESG/Sustainability team and CFO (within 60 days): Cross-reference your company's CA100+ company profile on carbontracker.org — if your company is a CA100+ focus company, your climate accounting score is visible to every signatory investor; prepare a formal response brief for the investor relations team before the AGM season
  • Audit Committee (next scheduled meeting): Place 'climate assumptions in financial statements' as a standing agenda item; request that the statutory auditor brief the committee on how they are applying Ind AS 36 in the context of the company's stated net zero or emission reduction targets — document this in the audit committee report
  • Company Secretary and Board (before next AGM): Review proxy advisor guidelines from IiAS and SES on climate-related voting — anticipate shareholder resolutions or vote-against recommendations on auditor reappointment if the climate accounting gap is visible and unaddressed
  • Sustainability Reporting team (within 90 days): Align BRSR Core GHG emission figures with the same emission trajectory implied by your net zero or SBTi commitment — if Scope 1 emissions in your BRSR are growing while your sustainability narrative claims a 2030 peak, that inconsistency is now audit-relevant under BRSR Core reasonable assurance
  • Treasury and Capital Markets team: If your company has issued green bonds or sustainability-linked bonds referencing climate targets, assess whether the financial statement assumptions are consistent with the KPI targets in the bond framework — misalignment creates covenant and reputational risk with international bond investors

Our Take

Carbon Tracker's CAAA work is quietly becoming the most consequential ESG analytical framework that most Indian CFOs have never read — and that is a board governance failure. The shift from 'disclose your climate risk' to 'prove your financial statements are internally consistent with your climate commitments' is the single most important transition happening in global sustainability regulation right now, and India is not insulated from it. NFRA, India's audit regulator, has been steadily raising the bar on audit quality and auditor independence since 2018; it is only a matter of time before a climate accounting inconsistency at a major listed company becomes the subject of an NFRA inspection finding or enforcement action — particularly as ISSB standards get embedded into Indian accounting and audit standards through the ICAI-SRSB process. The CA100+ universe is also expanding its India footprint: as more Indian companies enter global sustainability indices and attract NZAMI-mandated capital, the number of Indian companies subject to this level of climate accounting scrutiny will grow from the current handful to several dozen within three to five years. Boards should understand that the 'climate accounting gap' — the distance between the net zero narrative and the financial statement assumptions — is no longer a communications problem or an ESG ratings problem; it is an audit opinion problem, a director liability problem, and, in the context of SEBI's expanding BRSR assurance requirements, a regulatory compliance problem. The companies that get ahead of this — by genuinely aligning capex plans, asset life assumptions, and impairment models with their stated transition pathways — will not only reduce audit exposure but will find it materially easier to access green capital, retain FII ownership, and command a lower cost of debt on sustainability-linked instruments. The ones that do not will face an increasingly uncomfortable AGM season from 2025 onwards.
