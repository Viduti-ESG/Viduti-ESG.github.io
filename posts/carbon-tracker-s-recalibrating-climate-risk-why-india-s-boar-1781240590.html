Executive Summary

A major new report from Carbon Tracker and the University of Exeter's Green Futures Solutions, published 5 February 2026 and drawing on structured expert judgement from climate scientists across 12 countries, establishes with rigour what many Indian risk officers have quietly suspected: the economic damage models underpinning climate scenario analysis used by governments, central banks, and institutional investors are systematically understating physical climate risk as warming approaches 2°C — and the gap is widening, not closing. For Indian listed companies, this is not an abstract modelling debate. India is one of the world's highest-exposure geographies to physical climate impacts — heatwaves, monsoon disruption, coastal flooding, and agricultural stress — and the SEBI BRSR Core framework, ISSB IFRS S2, and the Reserve Bank of India's emerging climate risk supervisory guidance all require scenario-based physical risk disclosure. If the scenarios being used are structurally flawed, so is the disclosure, the capital allocation, and the board's risk assurance. The central finding is that economic damage functions used in integrated assessment models (IAMs) — including those embedded in the NGFS climate scenarios widely used by Indian banks and SEBI-regulated entities for BRSR and IFRS S2 reporting — undercount losses from mortality, ecosystem degradation, inequality, and regional extremes, while GDP-reconstruction effects can mask welfare destruction after disasters. For Indian CFOs and audit committees, this means the climate scenario analysis they are filing or preparing is not just incomplete — it may be creating a false sense of quantified certainty that will expose them to regulatory and investor challenge as physical impacts materialise.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker (https://carbontracker.org/reports/recalibrating-climate-risk/), the report 'Recalibrating Climate Risk' published 5 February 2026, represents a significant escalation in the credibility of the critique against standard climate economic modelling. This is not a fringe academic argument — it builds on Carbon Tracker's 'Loading the DICE Against Pensions' (2023) and the Institute and Faculty of Actuaries/University of Exeter 'Emperor's New Climate Scenarios' (2023), and now has structured expert input from climate scientists across 12 countries. The University of Exeter's Green Futures Solutions — whose researchers have directly shaped UK, EU, and UN climate risk thinking — leads the analysis. The report's four structural findings each have direct Indian disclosure and investment implications. First, physical climate damages are structural and compounding: at higher warming levels, cascade effects across sectors and geographies undermine stable economic growth. India's economic geography makes it acutely vulnerable to this dynamic — a severe monsoon failure simultaneously stresses agriculture (22% of employment), hydropower generation, inland logistics, and rural consumption. Single-variable IAM models miss this compounding entirely. Second, economic damage models focus on global mean temperature, but India's actual risk is driven by regional and local extremes — the wet bulb temperature thresholds that determine outdoor labour productivity loss in Rajasthan and Odisha, the specific flood return periods in Mumbai's coastal industrial zones, the drought frequency in Vidarbha's cotton belt. BRSR Core's principal adverse impact disclosures and IFRS S2's physical risk quantification requirements both implicitly demand regional-level granularity that current models do not provide. Third, GDP metrics mask welfare losses: reconstruction spending after cyclones in Odisha or Andhra Pradesh can show as GDP-positive while communities and ecosystems suffer multi-year productivity losses. Fourth, and most critically for risk governance: as warming approaches 2°C, tail risks and tipping points become dominant features of the risk landscape, yet models present smooth, precise-looking point estimates — exactly the kind of output that audit committees sign off on as 'robust' climate scenario analysis. Carbon Tracker's CEO Mark Campanale is direct: 'financial institutions will continue to chronically under-price climate risks — meaning that pension funds and taxpayers will remain dangerously exposed.'

Who Is Affected

  • BSE 500 and NSE-listed companies in the top 1,000 by market capitalisation subject to SEBI BRSR Core mandatory disclosure — particularly those with material physical climate exposure: agriculture-linked FMCG, cement, steel, textiles, chemicals, real estate, and infrastructure
  • Indian scheduled commercial banks and NBFCs preparing for RBI's climate risk and scenario analysis supervisory guidelines, whose NGFS scenario-based stress tests embed the same flawed damage functions this report critiques
  • Indian insurance and reinsurance companies — GIC Re, New India Assurance, private sector general insurers — whose catastrophe modelling and premium pricing face material mis-calibration if physical damage functions understate tail risks
  • Domestic institutional investors: LIC, EPFO, NPS Trust, and mutual funds with long-duration equity and infrastructure exposure in climate-vulnerable sectors and geographies across India
  • CFOs and Chief Risk Officers at Indian infrastructure developers — ports, highways, renewable energy, urban water utilities — where project finance covenants increasingly require ISSB S2-aligned physical risk disclosure
  • ESG analysts and stewardship teams at foreign institutional investors (FIIs) with India equity allocations, who use scenario analysis to underpin engagement letters and proxy voting on climate risk management
  • Company secretaries and audit committee chairs at BRSR Core-filing entities who are responsible for assuring the integrity of climate-related financial disclosures under SEBI's Listing Obligations and Disclosure Requirements (LODR)

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core — SEBI mandatory disclosure for top 150 listed entities (FY2023-24 onwards) and top 250 (FY2024-25 onwards): Principle 6 and Leadership Indicators require disclosure of physical and transition risk exposure and scenario analysis. The Carbon Tracker findings directly challenge whether NGFS-based scenarios used in these filings meet the 'decision-useful' standard SEBI expects
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (effective for Indian companies voluntarily adopting or required by lenders/investors): Paragraphs 10-12 require disclosure of physical risk scenario analysis using recognised scenarios — but IFRS S2 does not mandate specific scenarios, leaving companies exposed to challenge if chosen scenarios systematically understate damages
  • RBI Draft Guidelines on Climate Risk and Scenario Analysis (expected to be finalised in 2025-26 cycle): Indian banks using NGFS scenarios for supervisory stress tests must assess whether damage functions in those scenarios are fit for India's regional climate exposure profile
  • SEBI ESG Rating Providers (ERPs) Regulations (2023): ERPs rating Indian companies on physical risk are implicitly calibrated against the same damage models — a systemic mis-calibration of the underlying science flows directly into ESG ratings and index inclusion/exclusion decisions
  • Companies Act 2013 / LODR — Board fiduciary duty: Directors have an ongoing duty to ensure material risk disclosures are accurate. If physical climate risk is structurally mispriced in scenario analysis, audit committees need to assess whether current disclosures satisfy the materiality standard under Schedule III amendments requiring climate-related financial information
  • CDP 2025 disclosure cycle (questionnaire responses due mid-2025): Physical risk scoring in CDP's Climate Change questionnaire C2 requires scenario analysis; companies selecting flawed scenarios risk lower scores and investor confidence gaps

Climate Transition Angle

The Carbon Tracker report lands at a moment when India's physical climate risk exposure is moving from theoretical to balance-sheet real. The 2024 monsoon saw deficits in key agricultural states, the 2023 Manipur floods disrupted supply chains across the Northeast, and summer 2024 heatwaves in Rajasthan and UP resulted in documented labour productivity losses in construction and agriculture — the sectors that structurally underpin Indian GDP growth. Yet when Indian companies file BRSR Core disclosures citing NGFS 'Orderly 2°C' or 'Hot House World' scenarios, the damage functions embedded in those scenarios — derived from DICE, PAGE, or FUND model variants — will, per this report's expert-consensus findings, systematically undercount exactly the regional extreme-weather losses that are already appearing in Indian P&L statements. The transition finance dimension is equally sharp. India's sovereign green bond programme, the green infrastructure pipeline under the National Infrastructure Pipeline, and the nascent domestic carbon market under the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) all depend on investor confidence in climate risk pricing. If the physical risk anchor is miscalibrated, the risk-adjusted return calculations underpinning green bond pricing and infrastructure equity valuations are wrong. This matters for India's ability to mobilise the USD 2.5 trillion in climate finance it needs to 2030 under its Nationally Determined Contribution. The Paris Agreement's Article 2.1(c) goal of making financial flows consistent with a low-carbon, climate-resilient pathway requires not just transition finance but accurate physical risk pricing — and this report says the latter is broken. For Indian issuers of sustainability-linked bonds and green loans under SEBI's green bond framework, lenders are beginning to ask harder questions about physical risk scenario quality, and the Carbon Tracker findings give them specific grounds to do so.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Chief Risk Officer — within 90 days: Commission a gap assessment of the climate scenarios currently used in BRSR Core and IFRS S2 disclosures against the specific model limitations identified in the Carbon Tracker report — particularly whether your scenario source (NGFS, IEA, IPCC AR6 RCPs) uses global mean temperature damage functions or regional extreme-event calibrations relevant to your Indian asset footprint
  • Sustainability / ESG team — within 60 days: Engage your scenario analysis provider or internal modelling team to identify whether physical risk quantification covers the four specific gaps flagged: cascade effects, regional extremes, non-GDP welfare losses (mortality, morbidity, ecosystem), and tail risk/tipping point representation. Document this assessment for audit committee review
  • Audit Committee — at next quarterly meeting: Add a standing agenda item: 'Integrity of Climate Scenario Assumptions.' Request management to confirm which economic damage models underpin physical risk disclosures, and seek external expert assurance on model fitness-for-purpose in line with SEBI LODR Regulation 17 (board risk oversight) and Schedule III climate disclosure requirements
  • Investor Relations / Board — before next AGM: Prepare an enhanced narrative in the Annual Report and BRSR that explicitly acknowledges the limitations of current physical risk models and describes the precautionary and robustness measures the company is taking in its risk management — this positions ahead of investor questions and demonstrates the disclosure sophistication that FIIs and proxy advisors like Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) and Glass Lewis increasingly reward
  • Treasury / Green Finance team — linked to next debt issuance: If issuing green bonds or sustainability-linked instruments under SEBI's green bond circular, proactively engage lead arrangers and rating agencies on the physical risk scenario methodology question — document your approach to model uncertainty, as international bond investors post this report will treat NGFS-only scenario reliance as a disclosure risk flag
  • Board Risk Committee — 12-month horizon: Commission scenario planning that goes beyond NGFS standard scenarios to incorporate India-specific regional physical risk pathways, using IPCC AR6 Working Group II regional chapters on South Asia as the scientific anchor, and stress-test key assets (manufacturing sites, supply chain nodes, water-intensive operations) against 1.5°C, 2°C, and 3°C regional warming trajectories with cascade and tail risk overlays

Our Take

The Carbon Tracker 'Recalibrating Climate Risk' report is, in our reading, a watershed document — not because its critique is new, but because it has now reached the threshold of structured, multi-country expert consensus that regulators, central banks, and institutional investors can no longer dismiss as minority scientific opinion. The trajectory from here is clear: within 24-36 months, NGFS will be compelled to update its scenario damage functions, ISSB will face pressure to specify minimum standards for physical risk model quality in IFRS S2 implementation guidance, and the EU's European Supervisory Authorities will likely require banks and insurers under CSRD and Pillar 3 to disclose the specific damage model assumptions underlying their physical risk quantification. India will not be insulated from this wave. SEBI, whose BRSR Core framework explicitly references ISSB alignment, will need to respond — and we expect SEBI's sustainability disclosure advisory committee to take up the model integrity question in its 2026-27 review cycle. For Indian companies, the risk is a two-stage exposure: first, the scenario analysis they are currently filing will be retrospectively seen as having understated physical risk — creating reputational and potential legal exposure as climate impacts materialise; second, the recalibration of damage models will, when it comes, produce a step-change upward in disclosed physical risk that will re-price insurance premiums, infrastructure valuations, and credit spreads for Indian climate-exposed assets. Boards that have treated climate scenario analysis as a compliance checkbox — exactly the 'tick-box disclosure exercise' Campanale names — will find themselves managing a sudden credibility gap with investors, lenders, and regulators simultaneously. The companies that invest now in model quality, regional granularity, and honest uncertainty communication will be structurally better positioned for that reckoning. This is not a 2050 problem. The physical damages are accruing now, the model gap is documented, and the regulatory response clock is running.
