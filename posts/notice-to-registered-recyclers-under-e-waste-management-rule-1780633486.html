Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice directed at registered recyclers operating under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, signalling heightened regulatory scrutiny of India's formal e-waste recycling ecosystem. This notice is a clear indicator that CPCB is moving to tighten enforcement of Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations across the e-waste value chain — from producers and importers to brand owners and their authorised recycling partners. Companies that have not yet aligned their EPR compliance programmes with the 2022 Rules should treat this as an urgent call to action.

What Changed

The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 replaced the earlier 2016 framework and introduced a significantly more structured EPR regime, including the creation of a centralised EPR portal managed by CPCB, mandatory annual EPR targets for producers and importers, and stricter registration and reporting requirements for recyclers and refurbishers. Under the 2022 Rules, registered recyclers are not passive participants — they are accountable entities required to maintain detailed records of e-waste collected, processed, and channelled, and to report this data accurately on the CPCB EPR portal. The issuance of this urgent notice to registered recyclers suggests that CPCB has identified compliance gaps — likely including underreporting, non-submission of quarterly or annual returns, discrepancies between declared processing capacities and actual throughput, or failure to renew registrations on time. While the full text of the notice was not publicly extractable at the time of writing, urgent notices of this nature from CPCB typically serve one or more of the following purposes: directing recyclers to update or verify registration details on the EPR portal, warning of cancellation of registration for non-compliant entities, demanding submission of overdue processing certificates or annual returns, or flagging audits of facility capacities and environmental compliance.

Who Is Affected

  • Registered e-waste recyclers and dismantlers authorised under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022
  • Producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) of electrical and electronic equipment who have EPR obligations and rely on registered recyclers to meet their annual collection and recycling targets
  • Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) that facilitate EPR compliance on behalf of multiple producers
  • Refurbishers of electrical and electronic equipment registered under the 2022 Rules
  • Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) in sectors including consumer electronics, IT hardware, telecom equipment, white goods, and industrial electronics
  • E-commerce platforms and retailers with obligations under the rules as importers or brand owners
  • Environmental compliance and legal teams at any company with products listed under Schedule I of the E-Waste Rules

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Registered recyclers must ensure their CPCB EPR portal registration is current, accurate, and reflects actual facility capacity — update any outdated information immediately
  • Recyclers are required to submit quarterly processing reports on the CPCB EPR portal; verify that all pending submissions for FY 2023-24 and FY 2024-25 are completed without further delay
  • Annual EPR target fulfilment certificates must be issued to PIBOs and PROs in a timely manner — recyclers should audit all outstanding certificates and issue them promptly
  • Producers and importers (PIBOs) must verify that their empanelled recyclers remain validly registered; relying on a recycler whose registration has lapsed or is under scrutiny creates direct EPR compliance risk for the PIBO
  • PROs should immediately cross-check the registration status of all recyclers in their network against the CPCB portal and flag any anomalies to their PIBO clients
  • All recycling facilities should ensure they are audit-ready — physical infrastructure, pollution control equipment, and record-keeping must align with declared capacity and the conditions of their authorisation
  • Watch for any individual show-cause notices or directions that may follow this broader urgent notice; response timelines in such cases are typically 15–30 days and must not be missed

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste compliance is far more than a domestic regulatory checkbox — it sits at the intersection of India's circular economy ambitions, global supply chain transparency requirements, and climate transition strategy. The extraction of virgin metals and rare earth elements used in electronics is among the most carbon- and water-intensive industrial processes on earth. Effective e-waste recycling — when done through verified, formal channels — directly reduces demand for primary resource extraction, lowering lifecycle emissions of electronic products. For multinational companies operating in India or sourcing from Indian suppliers, the integrity of the domestic recycling ecosystem is increasingly relevant to their own climate and ESG disclosures. Frameworks such as the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) IFRS S2 and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) require companies to assess and disclose Scope 3 emissions and supply chain environmental risks — which explicitly includes end-of-life product management. If recyclers in a company's EPR network are found to be non-compliant or operating below environmental standards, this creates both a disclosure risk and a reputational liability in global markets. India's own SEBI Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework, now mandatory for the top 1,000 listed companies, requires disclosure of waste management practices including e-waste. The CPCB's tightening of recycler oversight directly supports the credibility of the data that companies will need to report under BRSR Core from FY 2024-25 onwards. Companies that have built their EPR compliance on informal or under-documented recycling relationships are exposed to data quality risks that will surface in sustainability reporting cycles.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your recycler network immediately: If you are a PIBO or PRO, log into the CPCB EPR portal today and verify the registration status, capacity authorisation, and compliance history of every recycler in your network. Remove or replace any entity whose registration is lapsed, suspended, or flagged.
  • Reconcile your EPR target fulfilment records: Cross-check the volume of e-waste channelled to recyclers against the EPR certificates received. Any gaps between claimed and documented recycling volumes must be resolved before they attract regulatory attention or invalidate your annual EPR filings.
  • Engage your recycling partners directly: Contact your empanelled recyclers to confirm they have received and are responding to the CPCB notice. A proactive conversation now is far less costly than discovering mid-year that a key recycling partner has had their registration cancelled.
  • Review your BRSR and sustainability disclosure pipeline: Ensure that e-waste data being prepared for BRSR, CDP, or CSRD disclosures is sourced from CPCB-verified, compliant recyclers. Sustainability reports that cite EPR fulfilment through recyclers later found to be non-compliant carry restatement and reputational risk.
  • Consult legal and environmental counsel: If your organisation has received this notice directly as a registered recycler, do not delay in seeking expert guidance on the appropriate response. Frame your reply carefully, provide complete documentation, and treat the notice as an opportunity to demonstrate full compliance rather than as a routine administrative matter.

Our Take

This notice is a meaningful signal that CPCB is graduating from rule-setting to rule-enforcing in the e-waste space — and that the era of passive registration without active compliance is over. India generates over 1.6 million metric tonnes of e-waste annually and ranks among the top five e-waste generating nations globally, yet the proportion channelled through formal, environmentally sound recycling remains stubbornly low. The 2022 Rules were designed to change this, and CPCB's escalating enforcement posture — of which this notice is one data point — reflects both the urgency of the problem and growing institutional capacity to act on it. For businesses, the message is clear: EPR compliance in e-waste can no longer be delegated and forgotten. It requires ongoing due diligence of recycling partners, real-time data management on the EPR portal, and integration with broader ESG reporting systems. Companies that invest in building robust, auditable EPR programmes now will be far better positioned as regulatory enforcement intensifies and as global customers and investors increasingly demand credible, verified sustainability data from their Indian counterparts.
