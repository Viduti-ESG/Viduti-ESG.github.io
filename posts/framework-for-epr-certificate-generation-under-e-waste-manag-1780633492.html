Framework for EPR Certificate Generation Under E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022: What Producers Must Know

Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has released a detailed operational framework for generating Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) certificates under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, tying certificate issuance directly to the recovery of specific key metals — Gold, Copper, Aluminium, and Iron — from end-of-life electrical and electronic equipment. Producers of electronics must now track metal-specific recycling obligations tied to item codes, with a phased gold recovery ramp-up starting at 20% of total obligation in FY 2023-24 and scaling to 100% by FY 2028-29. Failure to meet these obligations triggers automatic liability carry-overs or compensatory obligations in non-ferrous metal certificates, making compliance strategy a financial and operational priority.

What Changed

India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 established an EPR regime that requires producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) of electrical and electronic equipment (EEE) to ensure their products are responsibly recycled at end-of-life. What this new CPCB framework adds is a granular, metal-by-metal certificate system — moving EPR from a broad weight-based recycling target to a chemically-specific accountability model. Under this framework, EPR certificates are no longer generic proof of recycling. They are now issued based on the actual recovery of three classified groups of metals: (1) Precious Metals — specifically Gold (Au); (2) Non-Ferrous Metals — Copper (Cu) and Aluminium (Al); and (3) Ferrous Metals — Iron (Fe), including steel and galvanized iron. Each category of electronic equipment — from mainframes and laptops to mobile phones, refrigerators, and solar panels — has been assigned a specific metal composition percentage in Annexure I, which forms the mathematical basis for calculating a producer's EPR obligation. Critically, because India's current gold recovery infrastructure is limited — CPCB verified that the combined annual gold recovery capacity of 11 certified e-waste recyclers falls short of total producer obligations — the framework introduces a phased ramp-up specifically for gold EPR targets. For copper, aluminium, and iron, the obligation is immediate and set at 100% from the outset. Additionally, the framework provides a sophisticated offset mechanism: producers who over-perform on gold can convert that surplus into reduced non-ferrous metal obligations, and those who under-perform on gold must either carry the deficit forward or purchase additional non-ferrous certificates — creating a dynamic, interconnected compliance calculus.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) of all 45+ categories of electrical and electronic equipment listed under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 — including IT equipment (ITEW codes), consumer electronics (CEEW codes), and solar panels
  • Manufacturers of smartphones, laptops, tablets, and personal computers — these carry gold (Au) EPR obligations and face the phased ramp-up schedule
  • Producers of telecom infrastructure equipment such as BTS towers (ITEW18), which carry one of the highest gold composition ratios at 0.0035% by weight
  • Consumer electronics brands selling TVs, refrigerators, washing machines, and air conditioners — subject to copper, aluminium, and iron EPR obligations at 100% from day one
  • E-waste recyclers and dismantlers registered with CPCB — they are the certificate-generating entities whose verified metal recovery capacity directly determines market supply of EPR certificates
  • EPR compliance service providers and recycling aggregators who facilitate certificate trading between recyclers and producers
  • Solar panel manufacturers and importers (CEEW14) — now formally within the EPR certificate framework for iron and aluminium recovery
  • Corporate sustainability, legal, and procurement teams responsible for EPR registration, target-setting, and annual reporting to CPCB's centralized portal

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE — Register on CPCB's EPR portal and establish item-wise EPR liability calculations using the metal composition percentages in Annexure I for every EEE category you produce or import
  • FY 2023-24 (Year 1) — Meet gold (Au) EPR obligation at 20% of total gold liability; meet 100% of copper (Cu), aluminium (Al), and iron (Fe) obligations for all applicable EEE items
  • FY 2024-25 (Year 2) — Gold EPR target increases to 30% of total gold obligation; any gold deficit from FY 2023-24 carries forward as additional gold liability or triggers a proportional increase in non-ferrous certificate purchases
  • FY 2025-26 (Year 3) — Gold EPR target increases to 45% of total gold obligation
  • FY 2026-27 (Year 4) — Gold EPR target increases to 60% of total gold obligation
  • FY 2027-28 (Year 5) — Gold EPR target increases to 80% of total gold obligation
  • FY 2028-29 (Year 6) — Full 100% gold EPR obligation must be met; complete phase-in of gold recovery infrastructure expected by this date
  • ONGOING — For each financial year, calculate and exercise offset options (surplus gold → reduce non-ferrous liability; deficit gold → increase non-ferrous liability or carry forward) before annual compliance filing
  • ONGOING — Procure EPR certificates only from CPCB-verified recyclers with documented metal recovery capabilities; maintain item-code-level (ITEW/CEEW) certificate records for audit purposes
  • ONGOING — Monitor CPCB's periodic updates on Rare Earth and other precious material EPR obligations, which are flagged for future inclusion in the certificate regime

Climate Transition Angle

This EPR framework is far more than a domestic compliance exercise — it is India's entry point into the global circular economy transition for critical minerals, and its implications for climate strategy are substantial. Gold, copper, aluminium, and the rare earth elements flagged for future inclusion in this framework are all classified as critical minerals essential to the clean energy transition. Copper is the backbone of electric vehicle motors, wind turbines, and power grids. Aluminium is central to lightweight EV design and solar panel frames. Rare earths are indispensable for permanent magnets in wind turbines and EV drivetrains. By building a formal, traceable recycling certificate system for these metals, India is creating the infrastructure for a domestic secondary materials market that reduces dependence on virgin mining — itself a major source of Scope 3 emissions in electronics and clean energy supply chains. From a supply chain risk perspective, companies that master EPR compliance early will also be building the data systems needed to respond to emerging global disclosure requirements. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) under IFRS S2 requires companies to disclose climate-related risks across their value chains — and physical risks to mining regions (from water stress, extreme heat, and flooding) increasingly affect the availability and price of these exact metals. Similarly, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act will soon require Indian exporters supplying European markets to demonstrate responsible sourcing and circular material flows. The metal-specific EPR certificate system being built now will provide precisely the kind of granular, auditable data that these global frameworks demand. There is also a physical risk dimension domestically: as climate change intensifies monsoon variability and urban flooding in Indian cities, informal e-waste processing — which currently dominates — becomes both a toxic hazard and an untracked loss of recoverable materials. Formalizing the recycling chain through this certificate framework is therefore also a climate adaptation measure, reducing community exposure to hazardous substances while securing domestic material stocks.

What To Do Now

  • CONDUCT A METAL LIABILITY AUDIT: Map every EEE product category you produce or import to its ITEW or CEEW code, apply the Annexure I metal composition percentages to your annual quantity placed on market, and calculate your exact gold, copper, aluminium, and iron EPR obligations for FY 2023-24 — this is the foundation of all compliance planning
  • BUILD YOUR RECYCLER NETWORK STRATEGICALLY: Not all CPCB-registered recyclers can generate gold EPR certificates — only 11 have verified gold recovery capacity. Identify and contract with these verified facilities now, before certificate demand outstrips supply as obligations scale up; simultaneously, secure capacity with copper, aluminium, and iron recyclers for 100% obligation fulfillment
  • MODEL YOUR OFFSET SCENARIOS: Build a financial model that simulates both over-performance and under-performance on gold obligations and calculates the downstream impact on non-ferrous certificate requirements — this is essential for procurement budgeting and should be reviewed quarterly, not just at year-end
  • INTEGRATE EPR DATA INTO ESG REPORTING SYSTEMS: Establish item-code-level tracking of EPR certificate procurement and retirement in a format that can feed directly into your BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report) disclosures, ISSB-aligned climate reports, and any future CSRD-compliant value chain disclosures — avoid siloing EPR compliance in a legal team spreadsheet
  • MONITOR RARE EARTH AND PRECIOUS MATERIAL DEVELOPMENTS: CPCB has explicitly signaled that Rare Earth Elements and other precious materials will be brought into the EPR certificate regime; companies in sectors like EV batteries, wind energy, and advanced electronics should begin inventorying these materials in their products now, so they are not caught off-guard when obligations are formalized

Our Take

This framework represents a meaningful maturation of India's e-waste governance — moving from aspirational recycling targets to a chemically-specific, auditable accountability system that mirrors the direction of global circular economy policy. The phased gold ramp-up is pragmatic and reflects an honest acknowledgment of where India's recycling infrastructure actually stands today, rather than where regulations wish it were. However, the compounding nature of the offset mechanism — where gold deficits automatically inflate non-ferrous obligations — means that producers who treat EPR as a year-end box-checking exercise will face escalating financial exposure. The smarter play is to treat this framework as a five-year transition program: invest in verified recycler relationships now, build internal data systems capable of tracking metal-level obligations by product category, and position your company to meet the 100% gold target by FY 2028-29 without scrambling for certificates in a supply-constrained market. Looking ahead, we expect CPCB to progressively tighten the framework — adding rare earth obligations, increasing scrutiny of recycler verification, and potentially linking EPR certificate compliance to product registration or import clearances. Companies that build robust systems today will not only avoid penalties; they will have a competitive advantage as global buyers and investors increasingly demand proof of responsible material stewardship throughout the electronics value chain.
