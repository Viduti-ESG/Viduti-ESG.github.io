Executive Summary

Today's digest carries a high-urgency regulatory signal: India's Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) is actively enforcing its Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) ecosystem across e-waste, battery waste, and plastic waste simultaneously — a coordinated compliance pressure that affects thousands of producers, importers, brand owners, and recyclers. The issuance of urgent notices to e-waste recyclers alongside interim credit-transfer arrangements signals that the CPCB is closing loopholes in the EPR certificate ecosystem before the financial year-end compliance cycle. Companies that have treated EPR as a paperwork exercise are now facing real enforcement risk, and those without audited recycler partnerships or verified credit trails should treat this week as a red-alert moment.

What Changed

TODAY'S HEADLINE: CPCB Issues Dual Urgent Notices on E-Waste EPR — The most consequential development today is the CPCB's release of two simultaneous urgent notices targeting the e-waste recycling chain. The first notice puts recyclers on formal notice regarding compliance obligations under the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 — signalling that CPCB is auditing whether registered recyclers are meeting their processing targets, maintaining required documentation, and operating within approved capacities. Non-compliant recyclers risk de-registration, which would immediately invalidate EPR credits purchased by producers linked to them. The second urgent notice addresses an interim arrangement for the generation and transfer of EPR End Product Credits (certificates) by e-waste recyclers. This is technically significant: it means the CPCB has identified gaps or disputes in how recyclers generate and transfer certificates on the EPR portal, and has issued a temporary procedural fix. For producers and brand owners, this creates a short-term uncertainty window — credits already purchased or in transit may need re-verification under the new interim process. Legal and compliance teams should immediately check the status of pending certificate transfers on the CPCB EPR portal. PLASTIC WASTE: Draft Rules and EPR Notification Recirculated — The Draft Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2022 and the accompanying Draft Notification on Extended Producer Responsibility for plastics have re-entered the regulatory spotlight today, likely ahead of a final notification or amendment cycle. These drafts establish the architecture for plastic EPR obligations — covering producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) dealing in plastic packaging across four categories. The recirculation suggests a revision or stakeholder consultation round may be imminent. Companies in FMCG, retail, e-commerce, and food & beverage must treat this as a preparatory signal to audit their plastic packaging inventory and EPR registration status. E-WASTE RULES CONSOLIDATED: 2022 Base Rules + 2023 First Amendment — The E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022 form the foundational legal framework, establishing EPR obligations for producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE), targets for collection and recycling, and a digital certificate-based compliance mechanism. The E-Waste First Amendment Rules, 2023 refined these obligations — notably adjusting EPR targets, refining definitions of covered equipment categories, and strengthening the role of the CPCB-operated EPR portal. Together, these rules create a binding, target-driven system where producers must demonstrate verified recycling of a percentage of the EEE they placed on the market in prior years. BATTERY WASTE: Dual-Year Framework Now Fully Active — The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 and their 2023 successor rules establish India's first comprehensive EPR framework for batteries — covering portable, automotive, industrial, and EV battery packs. The 2023 rules increased ambition: higher collection targets, mandatory extended producer responsibility registration, and specific provisions for lithium-ion batteries that reflect the explosive growth of the EV sector. With EV adoption accelerating in India, the battery waste rules are transitioning from future-concern to present-obligation for automotive OEMs, EV manufacturers, battery importers, and consumer electronics brands.

Who Is Affected

  • E-Waste EPR Notices: All CPCB-registered e-waste recyclers; producers and importers of electrical and electronic equipment (laptops, phones, appliances, IT equipment, medical devices); brand owners who have purchased EPR certificates from recyclers now under CPCB scrutiny
  • Plastic Waste EPR: Producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) dealing in plastic packaging — specifically FMCG companies, e-commerce platforms, food & beverage manufacturers, retail chains, and packaging converters
  • E-Waste Rules (2022 + 2023 Amendment): Any company manufacturing or importing EEE covered under Schedule I of the rules — including consumer electronics, IT and telecom equipment, large and small household appliances, lighting equipment, power tools, and medical devices
  • Battery Waste Rules (2022 + 2023): Automotive OEMs (including EV manufacturers), two-wheeler and three-wheeler producers, battery importers, consumer electronics brands with battery-embedded products, industrial equipment manufacturers, and energy storage system providers
  • Recyclers and Dismantlers: All entities registered with CPCB under any of these three waste streams — e-waste, plastic, or battery — face direct compliance obligations and are subject to the enforcement actions signalled today
  • Investors and Lenders: Financial institutions with exposure to consumer goods, electronics, automotive, and packaging sectors face material ESG risk from portfolio companies with unresolved EPR liabilities

  • IMMEDIATE (This Week): E-waste producers and brand owners must log into the CPCB EPR portal and verify the status of all pending EPR End Product Credit transfers — the interim arrangement notice may require re-submission or re-validation of credits generated by recyclers now under scrutiny
  • IMMEDIATE: If your EPR certificates were sourced from recyclers named in or potentially covered by the CPCB urgent compliance notice, engage your legal and compliance team today to assess certificate validity and explore alternative verified recycler partnerships
  • BY 31 MAY 2026: Cross-check your EPR certificate inventory against FY2025–26 collection and recycling targets for e-waste. Any shortfall must be addressed before annual return filing windows open — penalties under the rules include financial liability and potential suspension of EPR registration
  • JUNE 2026 (Anticipated): Watch for CPCB's finalisation of the interim credit-transfer arrangement — the current notice is explicitly temporary, and a revised process is expected within weeks. Assign a portal monitoring responsibility within your team now
  • ONGOING — Plastic Waste: Ensure your company is registered on the CPCB EPR portal for plastic packaging if annual turnover exceeds the threshold. Begin compiling plastic packaging data by category (rigid, flexible, multi-layer, compostable) ahead of any final rule notification
  • ONGOING — Battery Waste: EV manufacturers and battery importers must confirm registration under Battery Waste Management Rules, 2023 and verify that collection targets for FY2025–26 are being met through registered recycling partners. Lithium-ion battery EPR targets are non-negotiable given CPCB's stated enforcement focus
  • ANNUAL RETURN FILING: E-waste and battery waste annual returns for FY2025–26 are expected to fall due in the July–September 2026 window — begin data consolidation now to avoid last-minute scrambles with recycler documentation

Climate Transition Angle

Today's regulatory cluster is not just a compliance story — it is a material signal about where India's industrial economy is heading. The simultaneous enforcement of EPR frameworks across three waste streams (e-waste, plastic, battery) represents India operationalising the circular economy as climate policy. Extended Producer Responsibility, when functioning as intended, internalises the end-of-life cost of products into business models — exactly what the global transition to sustainable production requires. The battery waste framework deserves special attention from a climate lens. India's EV transition is accelerating, driven by government targets, falling battery costs, and rising fuel prices. The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2023 are the regulatory guardrail ensuring that the climate benefits of EVs are not undermined by an unmanaged waste stream. Companies in the EV value chain — OEMs, battery manufacturers, charging infrastructure providers — face a dual transition obligation: decarbonise their products AND close the loop on the materials that make decarbonisation possible. From a global standards perspective, the EPR enforcement wave aligns directly with what the International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) under IFRS S2, and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), are demanding from multinational companies: disclosure of circular economy risks, supply chain material flows, and waste management liabilities. Indian subsidiaries of global companies, and Indian exporters supplying EU or UK markets, face a convergence of domestic EPR enforcement and international disclosure expectations. Failure to maintain clean EPR compliance records will increasingly translate into supply chain disqualification, investor scrutiny, and reputational risk at the global level. The physical risk dimension is equally relevant: India's informal recycling sector — which the EPR framework is designed to formalise — operates in conditions of high climate vulnerability. Extreme heat events, flooding in industrial clusters, and water stress affect recycler operations and throughput capacity. Companies relying on a thin network of recycling partners should stress-test their EPR compliance chains against climate disruption scenarios — a supply chain resilience question that is rapidly becoming a board-level concern.

Cross-reference the issuing recyclers against any CPCB compliance notice lists. If any certificates originate from recyclers under enforcement action, flag this immediately to your General Counsel and explore procurement from alternative CPCB-verified recyclers to fill potential gaps
  • ASSIGN A CPCB PORTAL MONITOR: Designate a specific team member — within EHS, Legal, or Sustainability — as the daily monitor for the CPCB EPR portal across all three waste streams your company is registered under. The interim arrangement for e-waste credit transfers will be updated, and missing the process change could invalidate compliance filings
  • CONDUCT A THREE-STREAM EPR READINESS REVIEW: Map your company's obligations under all three frameworks — e-waste, plastic waste, and battery waste — against current registration status, annual targets, collected quantities, and verified recycler contracts. This is the week to find gaps before enforcement pressure intensifies
  • BRIEF YOUR BOARD AND INVESTORS: The CPCB's coordinated enforcement action is a material ESG development. Sustainability officers should prepare a short brief for the board or ESG committee covering current EPR compliance status, any certificate validity risks, and the financial exposure from potential non-compliance — including penalties, reputational risk, and supply chain disruption
  • PREPARE FOR PLASTIC WASTE RULE FINALISATION: If your company uses plastic packaging and has not yet built an internal plastic packaging data system (volumes by category, supplier data, recyclability classification), start now. The recirculation of draft rules today suggests finalisation is approaching — companies caught without data infrastructure will face acute compliance pressure at short notice

Our Take

Today's digest represents something qualitatively different from the routine regulatory updates we track: it signals that India's EPR enforcement machinery has shifted from framework-building to active accountability. The CPCB's decision to issue two urgent notices on e-waste in a single day — one targeting recyclers, one addressing systemic gaps in the certificate ecosystem — tells us that regulators have identified material weaknesses in the EPR credit market and are moving to close them before they undermine the entire compliance architecture. This is the moment companies discover whether their EPR programmes are genuinely robust or merely paper-compliant. Looking ahead, we expect CPCB to introduce stricter third-party audit requirements for recyclers, tighter certificate traceability mechanisms, and potentially a tiered penalty structure that makes EPR non-compliance financially prohibitive rather than merely inconvenient. The convergence of e-waste, plastic, and battery waste enforcement in a single news cycle also previews a future in which India's EPR regimes are managed under a unified digital compliance infrastructure — making real-time monitoring, and real-time enforcement, the new normal. Companies that invest now in robust EPR data systems, diversified recycler networks, and board-level sustainability governance will find themselves ahead of a regulatory curve that is only going to steepen.
