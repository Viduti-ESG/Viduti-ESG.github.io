Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent compliance notice directed at registered e-waste recyclers, signalling a tightening of enforcement under India's E-Waste Management Rules, 2022. Recyclers who fail to meet registration, reporting, and Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) certificate issuance obligations face the risk of licence suspension, penalties, and removal from the CPCB's authorised recycler portal. This notice is a clear signal that India's e-waste regulatory framework is moving from policy articulation to active enforcement — and the entire EPR value chain, from producers to recyclers, must take notice.

What Changed

India's E-Waste Management Rules, 2022 replaced the earlier 2016 rules and introduced a significantly revamped Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework. Under this system, Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) of electrical and electronic equipment are required to meet annual e-waste collection and recycling targets. To fulfil these targets, PIBOs purchase EPR certificates from CPCB-authorised recyclers and refurbishers who process end-of-life electronics. The CPCB's latest notice to recyclers indicates that a substantial number of authorised recyclers are not meeting their core compliance obligations — including filing annual returns, updating processing capacity data on the centralised EPR portal, issuing EPR certificates accurately, and maintaining documentation of e-waste received and processed. The notice serves as a formal warning: non-compliant recyclers risk having their authorisation cancelled, which would directly disrupt the EPR certificate supply that producers depend upon to demonstrate their own compliance. In plain terms, this is the CPCB pulling on a critical thread in the e-waste compliance ecosystem. If recyclers are not compliant, the entire EPR market — including the producers who rely on them — is exposed to regulatory risk.

Who Is Affected

  • CPCB-authorised e-waste recyclers and dismantlers across all states, particularly those operating in major e-waste clusters such as Delhi-NCR, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Chennai, Hyderabad, and Pune.
  • Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) of electrical and electronic equipment — including consumer electronics, IT equipment, telecom gear, and large/small appliances — who depend on recycler-issued EPR certificates to meet annual compliance targets.
  • E-waste aggregators and collection point operators whose downstream recycling partners may be at risk of losing authorisation.
  • Electronics manufacturers with extended supply chains who have entered into EPR fulfilment agreements with third-party recycling agencies.
  • EPR compliance management platforms and intermediaries facilitating certificate trading on the CPCB portal.
  • State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) responsible for issuing and monitoring recycler authorisations at the state level.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • RECYCLERS — Portal Registration & Profile Update: Ensure your facility profile on the CPCB Centralised EPR Portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in) is current, including actual processing capacity, infrastructure details, and valid authorisation certificates from the relevant SPCB.
  • RECYCLERS — Annual Returns Filing: File annual returns for the Financial Year 2023-24 immediately if not already submitted. Returns must include total e-waste received (quantity and category), processed quantities, EPR certificates issued, and residual waste disposal records.
  • RECYCLERS — EPR Certificate Issuance Integrity: Issue EPR certificates only against verified and documented quantities of e-waste actually processed. Inflated or fraudulent certificate issuance is a primary trigger for CPCB enforcement action and can attract criminal liability under the Environment Protection Act.
  • RECYCLERS — Traceability Documentation: Maintain an unbroken chain of custody — from collection/receipt of e-waste through dismantling, shredding, and downstream processing or safe disposal of hazardous residues. This documentation must be available for CPCB/SPCB inspection at any time.
  • PRODUCERS/PIBOs — Recycler Due Diligence: Audit the compliance status of all recyclers from whom you are purchasing EPR certificates. A recycler whose authorisation is cancelled retroactively invalidates certificates already purchased — creating a compliance gap for your organisation.
  • ALL PARTIES — Respond to CPCB Show-Cause Notices: If your organisation has received a specific show-cause notice as part of this enforcement action, a written response must be submitted within the timeframe specified in the notice (typically 15–30 days from receipt). Failure to respond is treated as admission of non-compliance.
  • ONGOING — Quarterly Reporting: Under the 2022 Rules, recyclers are expected to update processing data on the portal on a quarterly basis. Ensure Q3 and Q4 FY2024-25 data is uploaded on schedule.

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste recycling sits at the intersection of the circular economy and climate transition — yet this compliance notice exposes a critical gap between India's regulatory ambitions and ground-level implementation. The formal e-waste recycling sector is a cornerstone of India's resource efficiency strategy: responsible recycling of lithium, cobalt, copper, and rare earth elements from end-of-life electronics is essential for the domestic supply chains that will power the energy transition — from electric vehicles to solar panels and grid storage batteries. From a physical risk and supply chain perspective, the failure of the recycler ecosystem has cascading consequences. If authorised recyclers are non-compliant and lose their licences, e-waste flows back into the informal sector, where hazardous materials such as lead, mercury, cadmium, and brominated flame retardants are processed using methods that contaminate soil and groundwater — creating long-term physical risks for communities and ecosystems near informal processing clusters. For companies reporting under global frameworks, this matters significantly. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) under IFRS S2 requires disclosure of climate-related risks and opportunities across the value chain. E-waste management is increasingly being flagged as a Scope 3 end-of-life treatment issue. Similarly, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) require disclosure on circular economy practices and waste. Indian companies with EU market exposure or EU-based investors will face questions about whether their EPR obligations are being met through genuinely compliant, traceable recycling — not certificate arbitrage with non-compliant processors. The CPCB's enforcement action is therefore not just a domestic compliance matter — it is directly relevant to the integrity of ESG disclosures that Indian businesses are making to global investors and regulators.

What To Do Now

  • IMMEDIATE RECYCLER AUDIT: If you are a PIBO, conduct an emergency compliance audit of every recycler in your EPR fulfilment network. Verify their current authorisation status on the CPCB portal, check their annual return filing status, and request copies of their processing logs. Do not wait for a CPCB notice to discover that your EPR certificates are backed by a non-compliant recycler.
  • PORTAL RECONCILIATION: Log into the CPCB Centralised EPR Portal and reconcile all EPR certificates purchased against the recycler's reported processing quantities. Any discrepancies between certificates purchased and quantities processed should be flagged to your legal and compliance team immediately — this is where regulatory exposure concentrates.
  • CONTRACTUAL PROTECTIONS: Review and strengthen your EPR fulfilment agreements with recyclers to include compliance warranties, audit rights, and indemnification clauses in the event of recycler authorisation cancellation. Ensure contracts require recyclers to notify you within 48 hours of any CPCB/SPCB show-cause notice or enforcement action.
  • INTERNAL ESG DISCLOSURE REVIEW: If your company reports under BRSR (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting), GRI, or is preparing for ISSB-aligned disclosures, review how e-waste EPR compliance is being characterised in your sustainability reports. Ensure disclosures accurately reflect the compliance status of your recycler network — not just your own certificate procurement.
  • ENGAGE A COMPLIANCE SPECIALIST: Given the complexity of the EPR portal, annual return requirements, and the risk of retroactive certificate invalidation, companies without dedicated e-waste compliance resources should immediately engage a specialist consultant or legal advisor with CPCB e-waste experience to conduct a gap assessment and remediation plan.

Our Take

This CPCB notice is more significant than it may appear at first glance. For years, India's e-waste EPR framework has been criticised for creating a compliance-on-paper ecosystem — where certificates are traded without corresponding real-world recycling activity, and where the informal sector continues to process the vast majority of India's 1.6 million metric tonnes of annual e-waste generation. The CPCB's direct targeting of recyclers is a meaningful escalation: it attacks the supply side of the EPR certificate market, which, if enforced rigorously, will force a reckoning across the entire value chain. Producers who have been treating EPR as a certificate-buying exercise rather than a genuine material recovery programme will find themselves exposed. We expect this enforcement wave to intensify through 2025, with the CPCB likely publishing a list of non-compliant recyclers and potentially suspending portal access for defaulters. The companies that act now — by auditing their recycler networks, strengthening contracts, and building genuine traceability into their EPR programmes — will not only avoid regulatory risk but will be best positioned as India moves toward stricter e-waste targets and as global ESG reporting standards demand credible evidence of circular economy practices.
