Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change notified the Plastic Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2021 on 12 August 2021, introducing a phased ban on identified single-use plastic (SUP) items effective 1 July 2022, raising minimum thickness thresholds for plastic carry bags, and expanding Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations to brand owners and plastic waste processors. Companies across manufacturing, retail, food and beverage, FMCG, e-commerce, and packaging supply chains face hard legal deadlines — non-compliance carries significant regulatory and reputational risk. Acting now to audit plastic use, redesign packaging, and register under EPR frameworks is no longer optional; it is a board-level imperative.

What Changed

The Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2016 were originally notified to regulate the manufacture, use, collection, and recycling of plastic waste in India. The 2018 amendment tightened certain provisions, but the landmark 2021 amendment — drafted in March 2021 and finalised in August 2021 after public consultation — is the most sweeping revision to date. The amendment does four major things. First, it prohibits the manufacture, import, stocking, distribution, sale, and use of a defined list of single-use plastic items from 1 July 2022. The banned items include plastic sticks in ear buds, balloons, and flags; candy and ice-cream sticks; polystyrene (thermocol) decoration; cutlery such as plates, cups, glasses, forks, spoons, knives, straws, and trays; wrapping films around sweet boxes; PVC banners below 100 microns; and stirrers. Compostable plastic alternatives are explicitly exempted from some of these restrictions. Second, the rules raise the minimum thickness of plastic carry bags in two steps: to 75 microns from 30 September 2021, and further to 120 microns from 31 December 2022. Non-woven plastic carry bags must meet a minimum weight of 60 grams per square metre (GSM) from 30 September 2021. Third, the amendment broadens the scope of Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) to explicitly include brand owners and plastic waste processors (recyclers, co-processors) alongside manufacturers and importers. This means brand owners — including FMCG companies and retailers who use plastic packaging — now carry direct legal accountability for end-of-life plastic management, not just the manufacturer of the packaging material. Fourth, the rules add new definitions for key terms: 'single-use plastic item,' 'non-woven plastic bag,' 'plastic waste processing,' 'thermoplastic,' and 'thermoset plastic.' These definitions matter because they set the legal boundaries of what is regulated.

Who Is Affected

  • FMCG and Consumer Goods Companies: Any brand owner using plastic packaging — pouches, wrappers, multi-layer packaging — now falls directly under EPR obligations.
  • Food & Beverage Industry: Restaurants, quick-service chains, packaged food manufacturers, and beverage companies that use cups, straws, cutlery, stirrers, or thermocol packaging face the SUP ban directly.
  • Retail and E-Commerce: Businesses using plastic carry bags, wrapping films, or SUP items in customer-facing or last-mile operations must transition their packaging and logistics.
  • Plastic Manufacturers and Importers: Must comply with revised thickness and GSM standards for carry bags and are now explicitly named in the EPR framework.
  • Plastic Waste Processors — Recyclers and Co-Processors: Newly brought within the regulatory ambit; must ensure compliance with amended rules and EPR guidelines.
  • Advertising and Event Management: PVC banners below 100 microns are banned; industries reliant on large-format outdoor advertising must switch materials.
  • Pharmaceutical and Healthcare Packaging: While primary pharma packaging has specific exemptions, secondary packaging using affected SUP items must be reviewed.
  • Importers of Any Category: Import of banned SUP items is prohibited alongside domestic manufacture, making customs compliance and supply chain sourcing a critical issue.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • 30 September 2021 (PAST — remediation required if not done): Plastic carry bags must be at minimum 75 microns thick. Non-woven plastic carry bags must weigh at least 60 GSM. Companies still using sub-standard bags are in violation and must rectify immediately.
  • 1 July 2022 (PAST — compliance audit required): Complete ban on manufacture, import, stocking, distribution, sale, and use of all identified SUP items (ear bud sticks, balloon sticks, plastic flags, candy sticks, ice-cream sticks, thermocol decoration, plastic cutlery, plates, cups, glasses, straws, trays, wrapping films around sweet boxes, invitation cards with plastic wrapping, cigarette packets with plastic wrapping, PVC banners below 100 microns, and stirrers). Companies must audit all operations to confirm no banned items remain in the supply chain.
  • 31 December 2022 (PAST — compliance verification required): Plastic carry bags must be at minimum 120 microns thick. All non-compliant inventory must have been phased out by this date.
  • Ongoing — EPR Registration and Compliance: Brand owners must register with the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and relevant State Pollution Control Boards under the EPR framework, submit annual returns on plastic packaging used and waste collected, and demonstrate fulfilment of EPR targets as notified by CPCB.
  • Ongoing — Labelling Requirements: Manufacturers, producers, and brand owners must ensure plastic carry bags and packaging materials bear the required markings including producer name, registration number, and thickness/GSM details as prescribed.
  • Ongoing — Plastic Waste Processors: Recyclers and co-processors must register under the amended rules and comply with guidelines issued by CPCB from time to time.
  • 10-Year Sunset Clause: Any future notifications banning additional categories of carry bags, plastic sheets, covers made from plastic sheets, multi-layer packaging, or polystyrene and expanded polystyrene items will come into force 10 years from the date of this notification (i.e., 12 August 2031), giving industry a long-term transition horizon for more complex packaging formats.

Climate Transition Angle

Plastic waste is not merely a waste management issue — it is a climate issue. The production of virgin plastic is a fossil fuel-intensive process, and single-use plastics that escape waste streams become a source of microplastic pollution with cascading effects on ecosystems, agriculture, and water security, all of which are physical climate risks that affect business continuity. India generates approximately 3.5 million tonnes of plastic waste annually, and a significant portion remains uncollected and unprocessed, contributing to soil degradation and water contamination that undermine agricultural supply chains. For companies aligning with global reporting frameworks, this regulation is directly material. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) IFRS S1 and S2 standards require companies to disclose material sustainability and climate risks — plastic waste management practices, EPR compliance, and exposure to single-use plastic bans are increasingly considered material risks for consumer goods, retail, and packaging companies. Similarly, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), which affects Indian subsidiaries and supply chain partners of European companies, requires disclosure on pollution, resource use, and circular economy practices, all of which are implicated by plastic waste obligations. The 2021 amendment also accelerates India's alignment with global momentum: over 60 countries have enacted SUP bans or restrictions, and the UN Global Plastics Treaty — currently under negotiation with India as a signatory participant — is expected to impose even more stringent obligations on plastic producers and brand owners globally. Companies that treat the 2021 amendment as a floor, not a ceiling, and invest in alternative materials, circular packaging design, and robust EPR infrastructure will be better positioned to manage tightening global requirements and access international markets where plastic-free credentials are increasingly a trade prerequisite.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a Full Plastic Inventory Audit: Map every plastic item used across your operations — product packaging, in-store consumables, logistics packaging, and marketing materials. Classify each item against the SUP ban list and thickness/GSM requirements. Identify gaps and non-compliant items that require immediate substitution or elimination.
  • Register Under the EPR Framework Immediately: If your company is a brand owner, manufacturer, importer, or plastic waste processor and has not yet registered with CPCB under the EPR portal (https://eprplastic.cpcb.gov.in), do so without delay. Assign a dedicated compliance officer responsible for annual EPR filings, target fulfilment, and documentation.
  • Engage Your Supply Chain: Issue formal compliance notices to all packaging suppliers, contract manufacturers, and logistics partners requiring them to confirm adherence to the minimum thickness (120 microns for carry bags) and GSM standards (60 GSM for non-woven bags). Include compliance clauses in procurement contracts and conduct supplier audits.
  • Accelerate Packaging Redesign and Alternative Sourcing: Work with your R&D and procurement teams to identify and qualify alternative materials for any banned SUP items still in use. Prioritise compostable, reusable, or recyclable alternatives. Build a 12–18 month transition roadmap with clear milestones, factoring in consumer communication and any necessary product reformulation.
  • Integrate Plastic Compliance into ESG Reporting: Ensure your annual report, Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR), and any ISSB- or GRI-aligned disclosures include quantitative data on plastic packaging used, EPR targets met, recycled content percentages, and progress toward eliminating SUP items. This positions your company credibly with investors, customers, and regulators as the UN Plastics Treaty tightens global norms.

Our Take

The Plastic Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2021 represent a structural shift in how India regulates plastic — moving from a purely downstream, waste-collection model to upstream producer accountability through EPR, and from voluntary action to hard prohibitions on specific product categories. The SUP ban is not a symbolic gesture; enforcement actions by CPCB and State Pollution Control Boards have intensified since July 2022, and penalties under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 — including imprisonment and fines — apply to violations. What makes this amendment particularly significant for businesses is the explicit inclusion of brand owners in the EPR net: this means that even if your company does not manufacture plastic, if your brand appears on plastic packaging, you are legally responsible for its end-of-life management. Looking ahead, the 10-year sunset clause provides a transition window for complex packaging categories, but given the pace of the UN Plastics Treaty negotiations and India's stated ambition to eliminate single-use plastics entirely, companies that wait for the deadline before acting will face a costly, compressed transition. The businesses that will emerge strongest are those that treat this regulation as the catalyst for a circular economy transformation — redesigning products, building reverse logistics, and investing in recycled content — rather than a compliance checkbox to be minimally satisfied.
