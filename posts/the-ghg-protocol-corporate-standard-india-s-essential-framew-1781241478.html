Executive Summary

The GHG Protocol Corporate Accounting and Reporting Standard is the globally accepted foundation for measuring and disclosing a company's greenhouse gas emissions — and it underpins virtually every major climate disclosure framework adopted or referenced in India today, including SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR), the ISSB's IFRS S2, and the EU's CSRD. Indian companies that have not yet structured their emissions inventory around GHG Protocol principles are carrying significant regulatory, reputational, and investor-relations risk as mandatory disclosure requirements tighten. Understanding and implementing this standard is no longer optional — it is the price of participation in global capital markets and supply chains.

What Changed

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard, originally published in 2001 and updated in 2004 by the World Resources Institute (WRI) and the World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD), establishes the rules of the road for how companies identify, calculate, and report their greenhouse gas emissions. It defines the three-scope framework that has become universal: Scope 1 (direct emissions from owned or controlled sources), Scope 2 (indirect emissions from purchased energy), and Scope 3 (all other indirect emissions across the value chain). While the standard itself has not been recently revised, its global relevance has never been greater. The ISSB's IFRS S2 Climate-related Disclosures standard — which India's Institute of Chartered Accountants (ICAI) and SEBI are actively monitoring for adoption — explicitly requires GHG Protocol-aligned accounting. SEBI's BRSR Core framework, mandatory for the top 150 listed companies from FY2023-24 and expanding further, requires Scope 1 and Scope 2 disclosures and encourages Scope 3 reporting. In short, the Corporate Standard is the technical bedrock beneath all of these obligations, and any gaps in how a company applies it will cascade into non-compliance across multiple frameworks simultaneously.

Who Is Affected

  • Top 1,000 listed companies in India subject to SEBI's BRSR reporting requirements, particularly the top 150 for whom BRSR Core assurance is already mandatory
  • Large unlisted companies preparing for voluntary ESG disclosures or anticipating future mandatory requirements under Companies Act amendments
  • Exporters and manufacturers supplying to EU customers subject to CSRD supply chain due diligence, including textiles, chemicals, steel, cement, and auto-component sectors
  • Indian subsidiaries of multinational corporations whose parent companies report under CDP, TCFD, or ISSB-aligned frameworks
  • Financial institutions — banks, insurers, and asset managers — required to disclose financed emissions (Scope 3, Category 15) under emerging RBI and SEBI guidance
  • Infrastructure and energy companies seeking green finance, sustainability-linked loans, or green bond certification, where third-party GHG verification is a prerequisite

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core (FY2023-24 onwards): Top 150 NSE/BSE-listed companies must disclose assured Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions — these must be calculated using GHG Protocol-consistent methodologies to withstand third-party assurance scrutiny
  • BRSR Core (FY2024-25 onwards): Mandatory scope expands to top 250 listed companies; Scope 3 disclosures are encouraged and expected to become mandatory in subsequent phases — begin Scope 3 screening now
  • CDP Reporting Cycles (annual, typically April–July submission window): CDP questionnaires for Climate, Water, and Forests are explicitly built on GHG Protocol standards; companies with institutional investor or customer requests must align fully
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (anticipated Indian adoption horizon 2025-2027): Requires disclosure of gross Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions with GHG Protocol as the default methodology; companies should treat current BRSR compliance as a stepping stone, not a ceiling
  • EU CSRD Supply Chain Obligations (phased from 2024-2026): Indian suppliers to EU-based companies will face contractual data requests for Scope 1, 2, and relevant Scope 3 categories — non-compliant suppliers risk losing contracts
  • Establish or audit your organisational boundary: Choose consolidation approach (equity share, financial control, or operational control) and document the rationale — this decision affects the entire emissions inventory
  • Set a base year: Select a representative historical year for emissions benchmarking, following GHG Protocol's base year recalculation policy, to enable credible year-on-year progress tracking

Climate Transition Angle

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard is not merely a compliance tool — it is the measurement infrastructure of the entire climate transition economy. Without accurate, comparable, and assured emissions data structured around its three-scope framework, it is impossible to set credible Science-Based Targets (SBTs), participate meaningfully in carbon markets, or demonstrate alignment with a 1.5°C pathway to investors and customers. For Indian companies, the transition angle is particularly acute on two dimensions. First, physical risk: as climate impacts intensify — extreme heat, monsoon disruption, water stress — investors and lenders are demanding emissions data as a proxy for transition readiness. A company that cannot account for its own carbon footprint signals broader strategic vulnerability. Second, supply chain pressure: India's export-oriented sectors face mounting due diligence requirements from global buyers in the EU, UK, and US, all of whom are under their own disclosure mandates. The GHG Protocol is the common language that makes cross-border supply chain emissions reporting legible. Global frameworks including ISSB IFRS S2, the EU's ESRS E1 under CSRD, and the SEC's climate disclosure rules (for US-listed entities) all converge on GHG Protocol as the accounting standard. Indian companies that build robust GHG Protocol-aligned systems now are not just meeting today's compliance bar — they are building the data infrastructure for net-zero target-setting, internal carbon pricing, and climate-integrated capital allocation decisions that will define competitive advantage over the next decade.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a GHG Protocol Readiness Assessment: Audit your current emissions inventory against the Corporate Standard's requirements — check organisational boundary definition, scope categorisation, emission factor sources, and data quality. Identify gaps before your next BRSR or CDP submission cycle.
  • Build a Scope 3 Screening Inventory: Even if Scope 3 is not yet mandatory for your company, conduct a high-level screening of all 15 Scope 3 categories using the GHG Protocol Scope 3 Standard. Identify your top three to five material categories — for most Indian manufacturers, this will include purchased goods and services (Category 1), use of sold products (Category 11), and business travel or employee commuting.
  • Engage Your CFO and Internal Audit Function: Emissions data quality and boundary-setting decisions have financial materiality implications. Bring financial control teams into the GHG accounting governance structure now, ahead of mandatory assurance requirements expanding under BRSR Core.
  • Select and Document Emission Factors Transparently: Use credible, jurisdiction-appropriate emission factors — the Central Electricity Authority (CEA) grid emission factor for Indian electricity, IPCC factors for fuels, and supplier-specific data where available. Document sources and update annually. Inconsistent emission factor use is the most common reason for assurance qualifications.
  • Develop a Multi-Year Disclosure Roadmap: Map your current GHG accounting maturity against the requirements of BRSR Core, ISSB IFRS S2, and any customer-driven CSRD obligations. Set internal milestones for achieving Scope 3 readiness, third-party verification, and ultimately Science-Based Target submission within a 24-36 month horizon.

Our Take

The GHG Protocol Corporate Standard occupies a unique position in the ESG landscape: it is simultaneously the most foundational and most under-implemented standard in Indian corporate sustainability practice. Dozens of companies publish BRSR reports with Scope 1 and Scope 2 numbers, yet when examined closely, the organisational boundaries are poorly defined, emission factors are outdated or misapplied, and Scope 3 is either absent or superficially disclosed. This is not a minor technical deficiency — it is a strategic liability. As SEBI expands BRSR Core assurance requirements, as institutional investors deploy GHG data in portfolio-level climate risk modelling, and as EU customers activate supply chain due diligence provisions, the cost of inadequate GHG accounting will shift from reputational to financial with remarkable speed. Our strong view is that Indian companies should treat GHG Protocol implementation not as a compliance checkbox but as the foundation of a broader climate intelligence system — one that informs capital allocation, supplier engagement, product strategy, and investor communication. The companies that invest in this infrastructure today will not just be compliant; they will be positioned to lead.
