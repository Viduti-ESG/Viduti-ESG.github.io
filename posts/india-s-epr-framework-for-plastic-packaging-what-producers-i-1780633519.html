Executive Summary

India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) has embedded legally binding Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) guidelines for plastic packaging directly into the Plastic Waste Management (Amendment) Rules, 2022, effective February 16, 2022. Producers, importers, and brand owners are now required to collect, channel, and ensure the recycling or end-of-life processing of both pre-consumer and post-consumer plastic packaging waste — with specific roles, targets, and accountability mechanisms enforced by the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB). Non-compliance carries significant regulatory, reputational, and supply-chain risk, particularly as global ESG disclosure standards increasingly demand transparency on plastic footprints.

What Changed

Prior to this amendment, EPR obligations for plastic packaging under the Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2016 were governed by guidelines issued on an ad hoc basis — meaning the rules could shift without formal gazette notification, creating ambiguity for businesses. The 2022 amendment changes this fundamentally: the EPR guidelines are now codified as Schedule II of the PWM Rules, giving them the full force of law. In plain terms, this means that any company that manufactures, imports, or sells products using plastic packaging — from carry bags to multi-layer packaging to plastic sheets — can no longer treat EPR as a voluntary or loosely monitored obligation. The guidelines define precisely who is responsible (producers, importers, brand owners), what they must do (collect and channel pre- and post-consumer plastic packaging waste for recycling or end-of-life processing), and which authority oversees compliance (CPCB at the national level, State Pollution Control Boards and Pollution Control Committees at the state level). The amendment also introduces critical definitions that determine who falls within scope. 'Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)' is defined as a producer's responsibility for environmentally sound management of a product until its end of life. 'Brand Owner' covers any individual or company selling commodities under a registered brand, label, or trademark. 'Importer' includes anyone importing plastic packaging products, carry bags, multilayer packaging, or plastic sheets. Together, these definitions cast a wide net across India's manufacturing and consumer goods ecosystem.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers: Manufacturers of carry bags, multilayer packaging, plastic sheets, and similar products — including industries that use plastic sheets or multilayer packaging to wrap or package goods for sale.
  • Importers: Any entity importing plastic packaging products, carry bags, multilayer packaging, plastic sheets, or similar materials into India.
  • Brand Owners: Any individual or company that sells commodities under a registered brand, label, or trademark — this includes FMCG, food & beverage, pharmaceuticals, personal care, e-commerce, and retail companies whose products reach consumers in plastic packaging.
  • Recyclers and Plastic Waste Processors: Entities engaged in recycling plastic waste or converting it into energy (waste-to-energy), oil (waste-to-oil), or industrial composting — they are assigned specific roles in the EPR ecosystem.
  • Sectors with highest exposure: Fast-Moving Consumer Goods (FMCG), food processing, beverages, pharmaceuticals, personal care and cosmetics, e-commerce and logistics, agriculture (plastic mulch films), and retail.
  • Company size: The rules apply regardless of company size — small manufacturers and importers are not exempt, though compliance pathways (such as Producer Responsibility Organisations, or PROs) can ease the burden for smaller entities.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Immediate effect (February 16, 2022): The EPR guidelines under Schedule II came into force on the date of gazette notification. All ongoing EPR processes must be aligned with these guidelines without delay.
  • EPR Registration: Producers, importers, and brand owners must register on the CPCB's centralised EPR portal to obtain EPR certificates. Operating without registration exposes companies to enforcement action.
  • Collection and Channelisation Targets: Entities must meet prescribed annual targets for collection and channelisation of plastic packaging waste — both pre-consumer waste (manufacturing rejects and trimmings) and post-consumer waste (packaging discarded by end consumers). Targets are category-specific and increase progressively year on year.
  • Plastic Packaging Categories: Companies must classify their plastic packaging under the prescribed categories (e.g., Category I: rigid plastic packaging; Category II: flexible plastic packaging including carry bags and multilayer packaging; Category III: multilayer plastic; Category IV: plastic sheets and like products) as targets and obligations vary by category.
  • Use of Recycled Plastic Content: Companies are required to progressively incorporate recycled plastic content into their packaging as mandated — this is a supply-side obligation that requires early engagement with certified recyclers.
  • EPR Certificate Procurement: Where companies cannot meet collection targets through their own systems, they must procure EPR certificates from registered recyclers or plastic waste processors through the CPCB portal — this is the compliance offset mechanism.
  • Annual Reporting: Entities must file annual returns with CPCB/SPCBs detailing plastic packaging placed on the market, waste collected, recycled, and processed, along with EPR certificates held.
  • End-of-Life Processing: Plastic waste that cannot be recycled must be directed to approved end-of-life processing routes — waste-to-energy, co-processing in cement kilns, waste-to-oil, or road construction as per Indian Roads Congress guidelines. Open burning and landfilling are not compliant disposal routes.
  • Non-compliance: Failure to meet EPR targets or register attracts penalties under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, including fines, production stoppages, and public disclosure of non-compliance by CPCB.

Climate Transition Angle

India's EPR framework for plastic packaging is not just a waste management regulation — it sits at the intersection of the circular economy, supply chain decarbonisation, and global climate disclosure requirements. From a physical risk perspective, plastic pollution degrades ecosystems, contaminates water bodies, and contributes to microplastic accumulation in soil and water — risks that directly impair agricultural productivity and water security, both of which are material climate adaptation concerns for India. By mandating collection and recycling, the EPR rules reduce the volume of plastic entering natural systems. On the transition risk and opportunity side, the shift to recycled plastic content in packaging aligns directly with the global push for circular economy principles embedded in frameworks like the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosure standard. Multinational brand owners sourcing from or selling into India are already subject to supply chain due diligence requirements that include plastic footprint disclosure. India's EPR rules create the domestic infrastructure — registered recyclers, certified EPR certificates, traceable collection chains — that makes this disclosure credible and auditable. For investors, EPR compliance is increasingly a proxy for broader ESG governance quality. Companies that build robust EPR systems now are better positioned to respond to Scope 3 value chain disclosures, ESG ratings scrutiny, and export market sustainability requirements — particularly as the EU's Green Deal tightens import standards on packaging sustainability. The SEBI Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework already requires India's top listed companies to disclose plastic waste management practices, making EPR compliance a direct input to mandatory ESG reporting.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a plastic packaging audit immediately: Map every SKU and product line to identify the type, category, and volume of plastic packaging placed on the market annually — this baseline is the foundation of your EPR target calculation and BRSR disclosure.
  • Register on the CPCB EPR portal without delay: If your company has not already registered as a producer, importer, or brand owner, initiate registration now. Retroactive compliance gaps increase regulatory exposure, especially as CPCB ramps up enforcement.
  • Engage certified recyclers and PROs: Build or formalise relationships with CPCB-registered recyclers or Producer Responsibility Organisations (PROs) who can help meet collection targets and supply EPR certificates. Do not wait until year-end — recycler capacity is finite and early movers secure better terms.
  • Embed recycled content targets into procurement and packaging design: Work with packaging R&D and procurement teams to incorporate recycled plastic content into future packaging specifications. This is both a regulatory requirement and a competitive differentiator for export markets and sustainability-conscious buyers.
  • Integrate EPR obligations into BRSR and ESG disclosures: Align your EPR data — tonnes of plastic placed on market, collected, recycled, EPR certificates held — with your SEBI BRSR reporting and any voluntary ISSB/GRI disclosures. Investors and ESG raters are increasingly scrutinising this data for accuracy and completeness.

Our Take

The codification of EPR guidelines into Schedule II of the Plastic Waste Management Rules is a watershed moment for India's plastic governance landscape. By elevating what were previously administrative guidelines to statutory rules, the government has removed ambiguity and significantly raised the compliance bar. What this signals clearly is that India is moving from a framework of stated intent to one of measurable accountability — with CPCB's centralised digital portal creating a real-time audit trail that makes enforcement far more credible than it was under the pre-2022 regime. For business leaders, the message is unambiguous: EPR is no longer a checkbox exercise managed by the environment team in isolation. It demands cross-functional coordination across procurement, packaging design, supply chain, finance, and ESG reporting. Companies that invest now in building traceable, certified EPR systems will not only stay ahead of domestic enforcement but will also be better prepared for the next wave of obligations — which is likely to include category-specific recycled content mandates, EPR for categories beyond packaging, and integration with India's broader green taxonomy and sustainable finance frameworks. The question is not whether to comply, but how quickly and how strategically you build the capability to do so.
