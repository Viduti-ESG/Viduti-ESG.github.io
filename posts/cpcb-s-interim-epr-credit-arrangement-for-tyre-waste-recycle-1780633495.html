Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent interim notice governing how tyre waste recyclers generate and transfer EPR end-product credits (certificates) under India's Extended Producer Responsibility framework for waste tyres. This interim arrangement signals that the formal digital EPR portal mechanism for tyres is still being operationalised, and both recyclers and tyre producers must act under transitional rules to remain compliant. Companies that ignore this notice risk gaps in their EPR obligation fulfilment, exposing them to regulatory penalties and reputational risk.

What Changed

India's EPR framework for waste tyres, notified under the Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules and subsequently operationalised through CPCB circulars, requires tyre producers, importers, and brand owners (PIBOs) to ensure the collection and processing of a specified tonnage of end-of-life tyres proportional to their sales volumes. Recyclers who process this waste generate EPR credits — certificates that PIBOs purchase or claim to demonstrate compliance with their annual targets. However, the full digital infrastructure for seamless credit generation and transfer on the centralised EPR portal for tyres has not yet been fully deployed. In response, CPCB has issued this urgent notice to establish an interim arrangement — a transitional mechanism that defines how recyclers can formally generate credits and how those credits can be transferred to PIBOs while the permanent system is being finalised. This is essentially a bridging protocol: it legitimises credit transactions during the gap period, preventing a compliance vacuum for all parties in the tyre waste value chain. The interim arrangement likely prescribes specific documentation requirements, formats for credit certificates, a defined process for submission to CPCB or the relevant State Pollution Control Board (SPCB), and timelines within which these interim credits will be recognised. Recyclers and PIBOs should treat this notice as legally binding and operationally urgent — interim does not mean optional.

Who Is Affected

  • Tyre Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs): All entities with EPR obligations under the waste tyre rules who need to procure or account for EPR credits to meet their annual targets.
  • Registered Tyre Waste Recyclers: Facilities registered with CPCB/SPCBs that process end-of-life tyres and generate EPR credits for sale or transfer to PIBOs.
  • Retreaders and Co-processors: Entities involved in alternative end-of-life tyre processing who may also be eligible for credit generation under the framework.
  • EPR Compliance and Sustainability Teams: In-house and outsourced compliance functions at tyre manufacturers (e.g., MRF, Apollo, CEAT, JK Tyre, Bridgestone India) and importers of tyres.
  • EPR Aggregators and Intermediaries: Third-party organisations that facilitate credit transactions between recyclers and PIBOs.
  • Automobile OEMs with tyre import obligations: Vehicle manufacturers who import tyres as part of CKD/SKD kits and carry associated EPR liability.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Immediate Review: All tyre waste recyclers must review the full CPCB interim notice to understand the prescribed format for generating EPR end-product credit certificates under the transitional arrangement.
  • Documentation Compliance: Recyclers must ensure all interim credit certificates are generated using the exact format and data fields specified by CPCB — deviations may render credits invalid for PIBO compliance purposes.
  • Credit Transfer Protocol: Recyclers and PIBOs must follow the interim transfer mechanism (likely involving physical or email-based submission to CPCB/SPCB) until the digital portal is fully operational.
  • Record-Keeping: Both recyclers and PIBOs must maintain complete records of all interim credit transactions — quantities, dates, certificate numbers, and counterparty details — as these will need to be reconciled once the permanent portal goes live.
  • Annual EPR Target Tracking: PIBOs must map interim credits received against their annual EPR obligation targets and flag any shortfalls to their compliance teams immediately, given that year-end reconciliation windows may be tight.
  • Deadline Monitoring: While the notice does not appear to specify a fixed end-date for the interim arrangement, companies should monitor CPCB's portal (cpcb.nic.in) and official communications closely for the announcement of the permanent system go-live, which will trigger new obligations.
  • SPCB Coordination: Recyclers should confirm with their respective SPCBs whether state-level endorsement or acknowledgement of interim credits is required in their jurisdiction.

Climate Transition Angle

The EPR framework for waste tyres is far more than a compliance checkbox — it is a foundational pillar of India's circular economy transition and directly relevant to corporate climate strategies. End-of-life tyres, if not properly managed, are a significant source of toxic black carbon and microplastic pollution, contributing to both air quality degradation and long-term soil and water contamination — compounding physical climate risks for communities and ecosystems. From a supply chain perspective, large automotive and tyre companies with global investors and customers are increasingly expected to demonstrate circular material flows as part of their Scope 3 emissions management and broader ESG commitments. The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) frameworks — IFRS S1 and S2 — require companies to disclose material sustainability-related risks and opportunities, including those in their value chains. A tyre producer that cannot demonstrate credible EPR compliance faces not only regulatory risk but also ESG disclosure gaps that institutional investors are beginning to scrutinise. The EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and its supply chain due diligence requirements mean that Indian tyre exporters supplying European automakers must align with global circular economy expectations. Robust EPR credit systems — even in interim form — provide the evidentiary basis for these disclosures. Companies that build strong EPR compliance infrastructure now will be better positioned to meet CSRD-aligned supply chain audits from European customers in the near term. Furthermore, the transition to sustainable tyre materials, retreading, and pyrolysis-based recycling aligns with India's Net Zero and Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) goals. The CPCB's push to operationalise a digital EPR credit market for tyres mirrors global trends toward traceable, technology-enabled environmental accounting — a direction that only accelerates as climate disclosure regulation tightens.

What To Do Now

  • Obtain and Review the Full Notice Immediately: Access the CPCB notice directly at the source URL (cpcb.nic.in) and circulate it to your legal, compliance, and sustainability teams. Do not rely on secondhand summaries for operational decisions.
  • Audit Your Current EPR Credit Position: PIBOs should run an immediate audit of credits already received, credits pending from recycler partners, and the gap versus annual EPR obligations — then assess whether the interim mechanism can bridge any shortfall before the compliance year closes.
  • Brief Your Recycler Partners: If you are a PIBO, contact your empanelled tyre waste recyclers to confirm they are aware of the interim arrangement and are generating credits in the prescribed interim format. A recycler generating credits in a non-compliant format could invalidate your claimed compliance.
  • Establish an Interim Documentation Repository: Set up a dedicated, auditable record-keeping system (even a structured shared drive with version control) for all interim EPR credit certificates — organised by recycler, date, tonne-equivalent, and certificate reference number — to facilitate seamless migration to the permanent portal.
  • Engage Legal Counsel on Contractual Implications: Review existing EPR procurement contracts with recyclers and aggregators to determine whether the interim arrangement affects delivery timelines, penalty clauses, or credit validity warranties — and issue contract amendments or side letters as needed.

Our Take

This interim notice is a revealing signal about where India's EPR infrastructure stands — ambitious in regulatory design but still catching up in digital execution. The fact that CPCB has issued an urgent bridging arrangement for tyre waste credits suggests that the formal portal is facing operationalisation delays, which is not uncommon in large-scale environmental registry builds. What is significant, however, is that CPCB is not letting the infrastructure gap become a compliance gap — it is proactively establishing rules of the road for the interim period, which reflects a maturing regulatory posture. For companies, the lesson is clear: EPR for waste tyres is no longer a future obligation to prepare for — it is a present compliance reality with active regulatory oversight. As CPCB moves toward a fully digital, traceable EPR credit market (analogous to what is already functioning for plastic EPR), companies that have invested in robust recycler networks, clean documentation practices, and internal EPR accounting systems will have a decisive advantage. Those treating EPR as a paperwork exercise are taking on growing regulatory, financial, and reputational risk — and the window to course-correct is narrowing.
