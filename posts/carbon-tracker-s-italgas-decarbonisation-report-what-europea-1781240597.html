Executive Summary

Carbon Tracker's February 2024 report on Italgas — the third in a trilogy covering European gas network operators Snam, Italgas, and Enagas — delivers a stark warning to institutional investors: the energy transition is materially undermining the future cash flow certainty of gas transport and distribution assets, and decarbonisation credibility is now a direct determinant of equity and debt returns. For Indian CFOs and CSOs, this is not a European story. It is a direct preview of the stranded asset, regulatory, and capital market dynamics now accelerating toward India's own listed gas distribution companies — GAIL, Gujarat Gas, Indraprastha Gas, Mahanagar Gas, and Gujarat State Petronet — all of which face analogous structural questions about asset life, hydrogen viability, and Paris alignment that their European peers are being forced to answer today. The India-specific urgency is sharpened by SEBI's BRSR Core framework, which mandates climate-related financial disclosures aligned with ISSB IFRS S2 principles for the top 150 listed entities from FY2024-25, with expansion to the top 1,000 imminent. Gas network operators and city gas distribution (CGD) licensees with long-dated pipeline infrastructure now face a convergence of regulatory disclosure pressure, investor engagement escalation, and asset impairment risk that Carbon Tracker's Italgas analysis maps in precise, replicable detail.

What Changed

Reporting by Carbon Tracker (https://carbontracker.org/reports/decarbonising-gas-networks-part-3-italgas/), authored by Senior Corporate Research Analyst Chris Moore and published 8 February 2024, provides institutional investors with a structured analytical framework to assess whether Italgas — and by extension all European gas network operators — is Paris-aligned, whether hydrogen decarbonisation pathways are financially realistic, whether asset economic lives have been appropriately shortened in response to lower long-term gas demand, and whether national regulators are creating sufficient incentive structures for accelerated decarbonisation. The report explicitly calls on investors to engage with investee companies, regulators, and policymakers to push three concrete actions: further reduction of methane and gas leaks, shortening of gas network asset economic lives on balance sheets, and improvement of national regulatory frameworks to incentivise faster decarbonisation. What this report overturns — or more precisely, challenges — is the long-standing investment thesis that gas network infrastructure is a 'regulated asset base' business with predictable, bond-like cash flows protected by national regulators who treat pipelines as strategic assets. Carbon Tracker's analysis shows that this thesis is eroding rapidly: declining gas throughput, accelerating electrification, uncertain hydrogen economics, and the physics of methane leakage combine to create a fundamental question about whether current asset lives embedded in regulated returns are financially honest. For Indian listed gas companies and their investors, this framework is immediately applicable. India's CGD network — now covering over 90% of India's geographical area across 307 GAs (Geographical Areas) licensed by PNGRB — was built on a similar regulated-return model. As India's own net zero 2070 pathway and its Nationally Determined Contribution targets translate into declining long-term gas demand scenarios, the same asset life, stranded cost, and hydrogen viability questions will arrive at PNGRB, MoPNG, and the boards of India's listed gas distributors. The question is not if — it is when, and whether Indian boards are getting ahead of it.

Who Is Affected

  • Listed Indian city gas distribution companies: Indraprastha Gas Ltd (IGL), Mahanagar Gas Ltd (MGL), Gujarat Gas Ltd, GAIL (India) Ltd, Gujarat State Petronet Ltd (GSPL) — all carrying long-dated pipeline and distribution infrastructure on their balance sheets
  • Infrastructure-focused institutional investors and debt capital market participants with exposure to Indian gas sector bonds, NCDs, or green/sustainability-linked bonds issued by gas utilities
  • BRSR Core-reporting entities (top 150 listed companies by market cap from FY2024-25) in the energy sector, where ISSB IFRS S2-aligned climate scenario analysis and transition risk disclosure is now a compliance requirement
  • ESG analysts and fund managers at domestic mutual funds (SBI MF, HDFC MF, Nippon India MF) and FPIs with significant India infrastructure or energy holdings who use Carbon Tracker-style frameworks for engagement
  • CFOs and treasury teams at gas sector companies preparing sustainability-linked financing, where lenders are increasingly requiring Paris-alignment evidence and asset-life review as covenant conditions
  • Chief Sustainability Officers and BRSR reporting teams at gas utilities, who must now address Scope 1 methane emissions, transition risk scenarios, and asset impairment disclosures with far greater specificity than in prior voluntary reports
  • PNGRB (Petroleum and Natural Gas Regulatory Board) and MoPNG officials, whose tariff-setting and asset-life assumptions are directly analogous to the European regulatory frameworks Carbon Tracker critiques
  • Board risk committees at gas sector companies, who are now exposed to investor engagement on Paris alignment and stranded asset risk in terms directly mirroring the Italgas, Snam, and Enagas engagement campaigns Carbon Tracker is driving

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core — FY2024-25 mandatory: Top 150 NSE/BSE-listed entities (by market capitalisation) must provide BRSR Core disclosures including climate-related financial risk indicators aligned with ISSB IFRS S2; gas utilities in this cohort must disclose transition risks, Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG emissions, and climate scenario analysis — Carbon Tracker's three-point framework (Paris alignment, asset lives, hydrogen viability) maps directly onto these required disclosure categories
  • ISSB IFRS S2 (effective globally January 2024): Requires disclosure of climate-related risks and opportunities, including physical and transition risks, scenario analysis using 1.5°C and 2°C pathways, and industry-specific metrics — gas network operators must assess whether their asset base is consistent with each scenario; Indian companies voluntarily adopting ISSB or facing ISSB-aligned BRSR requirements must begin this work now
  • GHG Protocol / Scope 1 methane emissions: Carbon Tracker's explicit call for 'further reduction of gas leaks' maps to Scope 1 fugitive emissions reporting — BRSR Core requires Scope 1 and Scope 2 GHG data with third-party assurance for top 150 listed entities from FY2024-25; Indian gas companies must now ensure methane leak quantification methodology meets GHG Protocol standards, not just CPCB general air quality reporting
  • SBTi Net-Zero Standard: As European gas peers face SBTi validation pressure, Indian gas utilities seeking to issue green or sustainability-linked bonds will face lender and rating agency scrutiny on whether their capex plans and asset retirement schedules are SBTi-consistent; no hard deadline, but capital market access is the effective enforcement mechanism
  • PNGRB Tariff Review Cycles: Asset economic lives are embedded in PNGRB's tariff determination methodology; Carbon Tracker's call for 'shortening of gas network economic asset lives' is a direct signal that Indian regulators will face similar pressure from investors and civil society to revisit 25-30 year asset life assumptions in CGD tariff models — boards should anticipate this as a 3-5 year regulatory risk horizon
  • EU CBAM (Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism) — Phase-in 2024-2026, full enforcement 2026: While primarily affecting steel, cement, and fertiliser exports, CBAM signals the direction of EU trade policy; Indian gas-intensive manufacturing exporters supplying EU markets must understand that Scope 1 and Scope 2 emissions intensity is now a trade cost, not just a disclosure item

Climate Transition Angle

The Carbon Tracker Italgas report crystallises a transition risk dynamic that is structurally identical to the challenge facing India's gas infrastructure sector, even if the regulatory and market timeline differs by 5-10 years. The core mechanism is this: gas networks are capital-intensive, long-lived assets whose economic viability depends on throughput volume and regulated returns over 20-30 year time horizons. Under any credible 1.5°C or even 2°C Paris-aligned scenario — including India's own NDC commitment to achieve net zero by 2070 and reduce emissions intensity of GDP by 45% from 2005 levels by 2030 — long-term gas demand in distribution networks will peak and decline. When that happens, the regulated asset base supporting current tariffs and investor returns becomes partially stranded. The timing and magnitude of this stranding depends on three variables Carbon Tracker explicitly tests: how fast electrification displaces gas in end-use sectors (cooking, heating, industrial process heat), how viable hydrogen repurposing of gas networks proves to be at commercial scale, and how quickly national regulators adjust asset life assumptions in tariff frameworks. In the Indian context, the hydrogen question is particularly acute. India's National Green Hydrogen Mission (NGHM), launched in January 2023 with a target of 5 MMT of green hydrogen production by 2030, explicitly contemplates blending hydrogen into existing gas networks and repurposing CGD infrastructure. This is precisely the decarbonisation pathway Carbon Tracker interrogates for Italgas — and finds wanting at current cost and readiness levels. Indian gas companies and their investors should treat this finding as a direct caution: the 'hydrogen will save the gas network' narrative, which is already appearing in Indian company sustainability reports and investor presentations, needs to be tested against the same rigorous Paris-alignment and financial viability scrutiny that Carbon Tracker applies to European peers. BRSR Core's climate scenario analysis requirement creates the disclosure architecture to force this testing into the public domain. Investors who read Carbon Tracker's Italgas report and then look at the BRSR disclosures of IGL, MGL, or Gujarat Gas in FY2025 will be asking exactly these questions — and the quality of the answers will increasingly determine cost of capital. The methane leakage dimension adds a further layer of urgency specific to India's context. India's gas distribution network, particularly older city distribution infrastructure in Tier 1 cities, has significant methane fugitive emission exposure. Methane's global warming potential over 20 years (GWP-20) is 80 times that of CO2, and the IPCC's AR6 report has reinforced that rapid methane reduction is one of the highest-leverage near-term climate interventions available. BRSR Core's Scope 1 emissions disclosure requirement, combined with growing investor expectations for GHG Protocol-compliant methane quantification, means that Indian gas utilities can no longer treat fugitive emissions as an operational footnote. Carbon Tracker's call for 'further reduction of gas leaks' as a primary investor engagement priority for European gas networks will translate directly into Indian investor engagement agendas within 24-36 months.

What To Do Now

  • CFO / Treasury (immediate — Q1 FY2025-26): Commission an independent asset life review of all gas pipeline and distribution infrastructure on your balance sheet, stress-tested against 1.5°C and 2°C IEA NZE scenarios; understand the impairment exposure before your auditors or investors surface it in annual accounts or engagement letters
  • Chief Sustainability Officer / BRSR Reporting Team (by FY2024-25 annual report filing): Ensure BRSR Core climate disclosures explicitly address transition risk scenarios for gas demand decline, including quantified Scope 1 fugitive/methane emissions using GHG Protocol methodology with third-party assurance — do not rely on CPCB air quality data as a proxy; align disclosure language with ISSB IFRS S2 industry-specific guidance for oil and gas
  • Board Risk Committee (next board cycle): Place 'gas network stranded asset and Paris alignment risk' formally on the board risk register, referencing Carbon Tracker's analytical framework as the investor engagement template now being applied to European peers; brief the board that BRSR Core disclosures will expose these risks to public investor scrutiny from FY2025 onward
  • Strategy / Regulatory Affairs (6-12 months): Engage proactively with PNGRB on the trajectory of asset life assumptions in tariff determinations and the regulatory treatment of stranded costs under accelerated energy transition scenarios; Carbon Tracker's critique of European regulators for being 'insufficiently focused on decarbonisation' is a direct signal that PNGRB will face similar pressure — better to shape that conversation than react to it
  • Investor Relations (before next earnings cycle): Prepare an explicit Paris-alignment narrative for analyst and investor briefings that addresses the hydrogen viability question with financial rigour, not aspirational language; FPIs and ESG-focused domestic funds are reading Carbon Tracker reports and will ask whether your hydrogen transition strategy meets the same standard applied to Italgas and Snam
  • Procurement / Operations (12-18 months): Initiate a gas leak detection and repair (LDAR) programme benchmarked to international best practice (EPA Method 21 or OGI camera standards) and capable of generating GHG Protocol-compliant methane emissions data; this is both a Scope 1 emissions reduction opportunity and a BRSR Core assurance requirement

Our Take

Carbon Tracker's Italgas report is not primarily about an Italian gas utility — it is a methodological template for how institutional investors will engage with gas infrastructure companies globally as the energy transition accelerates. The three-part series on Snam, Italgas, and Enagas should be read by every board member and CFO of a listed Indian gas company as a preview of the investor engagement letters, analyst downgrades, and regulatory challenge they will face within the next 3-7 years. The European gas network sector is approximately one regulatory cycle ahead of India: CSRD-mandated double materiality assessments, ISSB-aligned climate scenario analysis, and SBTi validation pressure are already forcing European utilities to confront the questions Carbon Tracker raises. India's BRSR Core framework, while less prescriptive than CSRD, is converging rapidly on the same disclosure architecture — and SEBI has made clear that the top 1,000 listed companies will be within its scope within the next reporting cycle. The deeper signal here is about the future of regulated infrastructure as an asset class. For decades, gas networks have been prized by institutional investors — pension funds, infrastructure funds, insurance companies — for their bond-like, inflation-linked returns underpinned by regulatory compacts. Carbon Tracker's analysis challenges whether that regulatory compact can survive the energy transition intact, or whether the stranded cost risk is being systematically underpriced in both equity valuations and bond spreads. In India, this question will arrive at PNGRB and the MoPNG with increasing force as India's NDC-aligned policies, the BEE's energy efficiency standards, and the emerging national carbon market (under the Carbon Credit Trading Scheme notified in 2023) create explicit financial penalties for high-carbon operating models. Indian gas utilities that get ahead of this — by shortening asset lives in their financial models, quantifying methane exposure with rigour, and building credible Paris-aligned transition plans — will have a structural advantage in capital market access, sustainability-linked financing terms, and regulatory goodwill. Those that do not will find themselves facing the same investor engagement pressure, credit rating scrutiny, and potential impairment charges that Carbon Tracker is now systematically building the case for across European gas networks. The window to act on Indian terms, rather than react to external pressure, is open — but it is not open indefinitely.
