CPCB Puts Recyclers on Notice: What the E-Waste Management Rules 2022 Compliance Crackdown Means for Your Business

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice to registered e-waste recyclers across India, signalling heightened regulatory scrutiny over compliance with the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022. Recyclers who have not met their Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations

Executive Summary

The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) has issued an urgent notice to registered e-waste recyclers across India, signalling heightened regulatory scrutiny over compliance with the E-Waste Management Rules, 2022. Recyclers who have not met their Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) obligations — including collection targets, channelisation records, and certificate issuance — now face potential suspension or cancellation of registration. This notice is a clear signal that India's e-waste enforcement regime is tightening, and the entire EPR value chain — from producers to dismantlers to recyclers — must treat compliance as a board-level priority.

What Changed

The E-Waste Management Rules, 2022, which came into force replacing the earlier 2016 rules, introduced a significantly more structured EPR framework. Under this framework, Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) are required to meet annual e-waste collection and recycling targets, and they must do so by sourcing EPR certificates from CPCB-registered recyclers and dismantlers. The entire system is operationalised through a centralised EPR portal managed by CPCB. The CPCB's latest urgent notice targets registered recyclers who appear to be falling short of their obligations under these rules. Non-compliances likely include failure to upload accurate processing data on the EPR portal, inability to issue valid EPR certificates to producers, inadequate record-keeping of material channelisation, and operating below permitted capacities. By issuing this notice, CPCB is formally putting recyclers on record — non-response or continued non-compliance could trigger enforcement action including show-cause notices, financial penalties, and deregistration. This is not a routine advisory. An 'Urgent Notice' classification from CPCB indicates that the regulator has already identified specific gaps and is demanding corrective action within a defined timeframe. Recyclers receiving this notice must treat it as a legal document requiring an immediate, documented response.

Who Is Affected

  • CPCB-registered e-waste recyclers and dismantlers across all states, particularly those with pending or incomplete EPR portal submissions
  • Producers, Importers, and Brand Owners (PIBOs) of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) — including consumer electronics, IT equipment, white goods, and telecom hardware — who rely on recyclers to fulfil their own EPR targets
  • E-waste aggregators and collection point operators whose downstream recycling partners are affected
  • Companies in the electronics manufacturing sector — including contract manufacturers and component suppliers — with EPR obligations under Schedule I of the 2022 Rules
  • Investors and lenders with exposure to e-waste recycling businesses, where regulatory non-compliance creates material financial and reputational risk
  • Sustainability and ESG teams at large corporates whose Scope 3 or product stewardship disclosures depend on verified e-waste recycling data

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • Registered recyclers must ensure all e-waste processing data — quantity received, processed, and channelised — is accurately uploaded and up to date on the CPCB EPR portal (cpcbepr.parivesh.nic.in)
  • EPR certificates must be generated and issued to PIBOs in a timely manner upon verified processing; backlog in certificate issuance must be cleared immediately
  • Recyclers must maintain and be prepared to submit physical records of inward and outward material movement, including weight slips, invoices, and Form 3/Form 4 records as applicable under the 2022 Rules
  • Any discrepancy between declared capacity and actual throughput must be explained in a written response to CPCB, with supporting documentation
  • PIBOs should immediately audit their recycler partners' compliance status on the EPR portal — reliance on non-compliant recyclers puts the producer's own EPR target fulfilment at risk
  • Recyclers who have received the notice must respond within the timeframe specified in the notice (typically 15–30 days); failure to respond is treated as admission of non-compliance
  • Annual EPR targets for FY 2023–24 and FY 2024–25 must be reconciled; shortfalls may require additional collection or payment into the EPR fund as per CPCB guidelines
  • All recyclers should verify their registration validity and renewal status — lapsed registrations must be renewed before resuming operations or issuing certificates

Climate Transition Angle

E-waste is not merely a compliance issue — it sits at the intersection of circular economy imperatives, critical mineral supply chains, and India's broader climate transition goals. Electronic devices contain recoverable quantities of gold, silver, copper, cobalt, lithium, and rare earth elements. Informal or unverified recycling not only creates toxic pollution but also means these materials are lost from the formal circular economy, increasing demand for virgin mining — a carbon-intensive process with significant biodiversity and water risks. From a global standards perspective, the ISSB's IFRS S2 climate disclosures and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) both require companies to assess and disclose Scope 3 emissions, which include end-of-life treatment of sold products. Indian companies exporting to Europe or listed on global exchanges must be able to demonstrate that their e-waste is processed by verified, compliant recyclers — otherwise, their sustainability disclosures lack credibility and their supply chains face audit risk. India's National Action Plan on Climate Change and the country's commitment to net zero by 2070 both implicitly depend on building robust secondary materials markets. A well-functioning EPR system for e-waste is foundational infrastructure for this transition. CPCB's enforcement action, therefore, is not just about rule compliance — it is about building the institutional trust that makes India's circular economy investable and credible on the global stage. Physical climate risks are also relevant here: rising temperatures increase the rate at which electronic equipment fails and is discarded, and extreme weather events (floods, cyclones) can destroy unprocessed e-waste stockpiles, creating acute pollution incidents. Recyclers operating in climate-vulnerable geographies must factor these physical risks into their storage and operational continuity planning.

What To Do Now

  • RECYCLERS — Conduct an immediate internal audit: Log into the CPCB EPR portal today, verify all submitted data for accuracy, identify any gaps in processing records or certificate issuance, and prepare a compliance rectification plan with clear owner accountability and a 2-week completion target
  • PRODUCERS & BRAND OWNERS (PIBOs) — Run a partner compliance check: Pull the list of all recyclers you have sourced EPR certificates from in FY 2023–24 and FY 2024–25 and cross-verify their active registration and portal submission status; if your recycler partners are flagged by CPCB, your own EPR fulfilment is at legal risk and you need contingency sourcing arrangements immediately
  • LEGAL & COMPLIANCE TEAMS — Review your notice response protocol: If your organisation has received the CPCB urgent notice, engage environmental law counsel within 48 hours, prepare a structured written response addressing each point of alleged non-compliance, and document all corrective actions taken — this response becomes part of your regulatory record
  • SUSTAINABILITY TEAMS — Align EPR data with ESG disclosures: Ensure that e-waste collection and recycling volumes reported in your Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR), GRI disclosures, or CDP submissions are consistent with data filed on the CPCB EPR portal; any discrepancy is a red flag in third-party audits
  • CFOs & RISK OFFICERS — Quantify EPR liability: Calculate the financial exposure from any shortfall in EPR targets, including potential penalties under the Environment Protection Act and the cost of purchasing additional EPR certificates in the secondary market; provision for this in your Q4 risk register if not already done

Our Take

This notice marks a meaningful inflection point in India's e-waste governance. For years, the EPR framework existed largely on paper — registration was obtained, targets were notionally assigned, but enforcement was inconsistent and data quality on the portal was poor. CPCB's decision to issue an urgent, public-facing compliance notice to recyclers signals that the regulator is moving from a registration-and-report model to an active enforcement posture. We expect this to be followed by physical inspections, show-cause notices to persistent non-compliers, and potentially a public blacklist of deregistered recyclers — similar to enforcement patterns we have seen in the plastic EPR space. For producers, the message is equally clear: you cannot outsource your EPR responsibility to a recycler and walk away. Due diligence on your recycling partners is now a compliance obligation, not just a best practice. Companies that build robust, auditable EPR supply chains now will be better positioned as India's environmental disclosure requirements — including SEBI's BRSR Core — become more stringent, and as global customers and investors demand verified circular economy credentials.
