CPCB Directs Customs Release for 106 EEE Items: What EPR-Registered Producers Must Know

Executive Summary

On 31 August 2023, the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) issued a formal letter to Customs Authorities facilitating the release of imported consignments belonging to producers of 106 categories of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) listed under India's E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022. This intervention signals that EPR registration and compliance status are now actively linked to import clearance — a significant escalation in regulatory enforcement. Producers who are not registered on the CPCB EPR portal or who have lapsed obligations risk customs-level disruption to their supply chains.

What Changed

Under the E-Waste (Management) Rules, 2022, producers of Electrical and Electronic Equipment (EEE) are legally required to register with CPCB, set annual EPR targets, and ensure end-of-life collection and recycling of their products through authorised dismantlers and recyclers. The 106 EEE item categories covered span a wide range — from consumer electronics and IT equipment to large household appliances and medical devices. The CPCB letter dated 31 August 2023 directed Customs Authorities to release imported consignments specifically for producers who are compliant with EPR obligations. In plain terms: CPCB has formally communicated to Customs that EPR registration and compliance is a condition that must be verified at the point of import. Consignments from non-compliant or unregistered producers face the risk of being held at ports. This move effectively transforms EPR compliance from a back-office regulatory requirement into a real-time, supply-chain-critical obligation.

Who Is Affected

  • Producers and brand owners of any of the 106 EEE categories who import finished goods, components, or sub-assemblies into India
  • Importers acting as 'producers' under the E-Waste Rules — including subsidiaries of multinational electronics, appliance, and technology companies
  • Consumer electronics companies (smartphones, laptops, TVs, audio equipment)
  • White goods manufacturers and importers (refrigerators, washing machines, air conditioners)
  • IT and office equipment producers (printers, servers, networking equipment)
  • Medical device companies whose products fall under the notified EEE schedule
  • Lighting equipment producers and importers (including LED systems)
  • Customs brokers and freight forwarders handling EEE consignments on behalf of producers

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE: Verify that your organisation is registered as a Producer on the CPCB EPR Portal (https://eprnewportal.cpcb.gov.in) for all applicable EEE product categories
  • IMMEDIATE: Confirm that your EPR Certificate is current, valid, and reflects accurate product categories matching your import consignments
  • BY NEXT IMPORT CYCLE: Ensure your Customs Broker has a copy of your valid CPCB EPR registration/certificate to present at the time of import clearance
  • ONGOING (Annual): Meet your EPR targets for e-waste collection and channelisation through CPCB-authorised recyclers — non-fulfilment of targets can trigger non-compliance status affecting future imports
  • ONGOING: File quarterly and annual returns on the CPCB EPR portal accurately and on time; gaps in filings can trigger compliance flags
  • REVIEW: If your consignment was recently held or delayed at customs, engage with CPCB's regional office immediately to resolve compliance status and obtain clearance documentation
  • AUDIT: Conduct an internal review of all product SKUs imported against the 106 EEE categories to ensure no category is inadvertently unregistered

Climate Transition Angle

While EPR for e-waste is primarily framed as a waste management regulation, its climate dimensions are significant and increasingly recognised in global sustainability frameworks. E-waste is one of the fastest-growing solid waste streams globally, and improper disposal leads to toxic emissions, soil contamination, and loss of critical minerals — including lithium, cobalt, and rare earth elements — that are essential for the clean energy transition (EV batteries, solar panels, wind turbines). From a climate transition strategy perspective, robust EPR compliance directly supports circular economy goals — a key pillar of India's Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) and the National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC). Recovering critical minerals through formal e-waste recycling reduces dependence on primary mining, which is both carbon-intensive and geopolitically risky. Internationally, frameworks such as the ISSB's IFRS S2 (climate-related disclosures) and the EU's CSRD increasingly require companies to account for Scope 3 emissions and circular economy performance across their value chains. Indian subsidiaries of global companies reporting under CSRD or preparing for BRSR Core disclosures will find that EPR compliance data — collection rates, recycling volumes, certified recycler partnerships — feeds directly into resource use and circular economy metrics demanded by these standards. Non-compliance is not just a regulatory risk; it is an ESG disclosure gap.

What To Do Now

  • REGISTER OR RENEW: Log into the CPCB EPR Portal today and confirm your Producer registration is active, covers all 106 applicable EEE categories you manufacture or import, and that your EPR certificate has not lapsed
  • BRIEF YOUR CUSTOMS TEAM: Share your current EPR compliance documentation (registration certificate, target fulfilment records) with your customs broker and import/logistics team — make this a standard document in your import checklist
  • MAP YOUR EPR TARGETS: Work with your sustainability or compliance team to calculate your mandatory collection targets under the 2022 Rules and verify you have sufficient agreements in place with CPCB-authorised PROs (Producer Responsibility Organisations) or recyclers to meet them
  • INTEGRATE EPR INTO SUPPLY CHAIN RISK MANAGEMENT: Flag EPR compliance status as a supply chain risk indicator — a lapsed registration can now cause port-level delays, directly impacting revenue and customer commitments
  • ALIGN WITH ESG REPORTING: Begin capturing EPR performance data (tonnes collected, recycling certificates, PRO agreements) in a format compatible with BRSR Core and ISSB/CSRD disclosures to future-proof your sustainability reporting

Our Take

This CPCB letter is a watershed moment in India's EPR enforcement journey. For years, EPR for e-waste existed largely as a compliance-on-paper obligation — producers registered, filed returns, and moved on with limited real-world consequence for non-fulfilment. By formally looping in Customs Authorities, CPCB has introduced a hard, operational consequence: your goods don't move if your EPR house is not in order. This is exactly the kind of multi-agency enforcement coordination that gives regulatory teeth to environmental rules, and it mirrors how the EU has long linked market access to environmental compliance. We expect this approach to be extended further — to plastics EPR, battery EPR, and tyre EPR — as CPCB builds on this precedent. For business leaders, the message is clear: EPR compliance is no longer a sustainability team checkbox. It is a supply chain continuity issue that belongs on the CFO's and COO's radar today.
