Executive Summary

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) notified the Battery Waste Management Amendment Rules, 2025 on 24 February 2025, introducing three targeted but operationally significant changes to Schedule I of the 2022 Rules. Producers of batteries and battery-integrated equipment must now print their Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) registration number as a barcode or QR code on products, packaging, or accompanying brochures — and must notify the Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) in writing before doing so. Simultaneously, the amendment clarifies that mandatory hazardous-metal labelling for Cadmium ('Cd') and Lead ('Pb') does not apply where concentrations fall below defined trace thresholds, reducing compliance burden for manufacturers of modern low-hazard battery chemistries.

What Changed

The Battery Waste Management Rules, 2022 established India's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework for batteries — requiring producers, importers, and brand owners to register with CPCB, meet collection and recycling targets, and label their batteries with specific information. The 2025 Amendment, the fifth modification to the 2022 Rules, makes three precise changes to Schedule I, Paragraph 2. First, an exemption has been carved out for labelling obligations under Clause (ia): packaging that is already regulated under Rule 26 of the Legal Metrology (Packaged Commodities) Rules, 2011 — which governs mandatory declarations on pre-packaged goods — will not need to duplicate those requirements under the Battery Waste Rules. This eliminates regulatory overlap for consumer-facing packaged battery products. Second, a new Clause (ib) has been inserted, offering producers a flexible pathway to fulfil EPR labelling requirements. Instead of a single mandated format, producers may now embed their EPR registration number into a barcode or QR code and place it on any one of five specified locations: the battery or battery pack itself, the equipment containing the battery, the battery's packaging, the equipment's packaging, or bulk packaging for wholesale (non-retail) batteries. Alternatively, the EPR registration number may be printed on the product information brochure. The critical procedural requirement: producers must inform CPCB in writing before opting for this alternative route. CPCB will in turn publish and quarterly-update a consolidated public list of producers who have done so on its centralised online portal. Third, the amendment brings welcome clarity on hazardous metal marking. Previously, all batteries containing Cadmium or Lead were required to display the chemical symbols 'Cd' or 'Pb'. The new proviso under Clause (v) specifies that this marking is not required when Cadmium concentration is at or below 0.002% by weight (20 parts per million) or Lead concentration is at or below 0.004% by weight (40 parts per million). This threshold-based exemption aligns India's approach more closely with international practice and reflects the reality that trace amounts of these metals in modern battery chemistries pose a categorically different risk profile than batteries where these metals are a primary component.

Who Is Affected

  • Battery producers and importers: All entities registered or required to register under the EPR framework for batteries — including manufacturers of portable, automotive, industrial, EV traction, and stationary storage batteries.
  • Brand owners and OEMs: Companies that sell equipment with built-in batteries (consumer electronics, power tools, electric vehicles, UPS systems) under their own brand name and hold EPR obligations.
  • Bulk/wholesale battery suppliers: Entities supplying batteries or battery packs in bulk for industrial or commercial use (not for direct retail sale) must comply with QR/barcode labelling on bulk packaging.
  • Battery recyclers and extended supply chain: While the direct obligations are on producers, recyclers and downstream handlers benefit from improved traceability enabled by mandatory EPR registration codes on products.
  • Compliance and legal teams in the EV sector: Given India's rapidly scaling electric vehicle ecosystem, EV OEMs and their battery suppliers face the most material operational impact given high production volumes and complex battery pack configurations.
  • Consumer electronics manufacturers: Companies producing smartphones, laptops, wearables, and other devices with integrated or replaceable batteries must assess labelling on both the battery and the device/packaging.
  • Legal Metrology-regulated packagers: Companies whose packaging already complies with Legal Metrology (Packaged Commodities) Rules, 2011, Rule 26 gain direct relief from duplicative battery labelling requirements.

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE (effective 24 February 2025): The amendment came into force on the date of publication in the Official Gazette. All new obligations and exemptions are legally operative from this date.
  • EPR QR/Barcode Labelling — Prior Written Notice to CPCB: Before a producer adopts the barcode/QR code route under new Clause (ib), they must submit written intimation to the Central Pollution Control Board. This is a precondition, not a post-facto filing. Producers must not implement this labelling approach without completing this step.
  • EPR Registration Number Placement: Producers must print a barcode or QR code containing their CPCB EPR registration number on at least one of the following: (a) the battery or battery pack; (b) the equipment containing the battery; (c) packaging of the battery/battery pack; (d) packaging of the equipment; or (e) bulk packaging of batteries not for retail sale. Alternatively, the EPR registration number must appear on the product information brochure.
  • Quarterly Portal Updates: Producers who opt for the QR/barcode route must keep their details current on CPCB's centralised online portal, as CPCB will publish a consolidated and quarterly-updated list of such producers. Non-updation may result in removal from the published list, creating compliance visibility risk.
  • Cadmium and Lead Marking Exemption — Internal Chemical Testing: Producers seeking to avail the 'Cd'/'Pb' marking exemption must verify and document that their battery's Cadmium concentration is ≤ 0.002% by weight (20 ppm) and/or Lead concentration is ≤ 0.004% by weight (40 ppm). Internal test records must be maintained as evidence for potential regulatory scrutiny.
  • Legal Metrology Overlap Check: Producers whose packaging falls under Rule 26 of the Legal Metrology (Packaged Commodities) Rules, 2011 should formally document that determination to substantiate their exemption from the overlapping Battery Waste Rules labelling requirement under Clause (ia).
  • Review Existing EPR Registrations: Ensure your CPCB EPR registration number is current, valid, and accurately reflected in all labelling systems and product information materials before deploying QR/barcode-based compliance.

Climate Transition Angle

At first glance, labelling and marking amendments may appear to be administrative housekeeping. In the context of India's climate transition, however, they carry deeper strategic significance. Batteries are the foundational technology enabling the shift from fossil fuels to clean energy — in electric vehicles, in grid-scale storage balancing intermittent solar and wind generation, and in distributed energy systems for homes and businesses. The integrity of the battery value chain — from raw material sourcing through manufacturing, use, collection, and recycling — is a critical climate infrastructure question. The QR/barcode EPR registration requirement is a traceability mechanism. When every battery in the market carries a machine-readable link to its producer's EPR registration, it becomes far easier to enforce collection and recycling targets, close leakages in the reverse logistics chain, and generate auditable data on battery material flows. This kind of product-level digital traceability is precisely what global frameworks are beginning to mandate: the European Union's Battery Regulation (EU) 2023/1542, for instance, requires a 'battery passport' — a digital record of a battery's composition, carbon footprint, supply chain provenance, and end-of-life data — for EV, industrial, and LMT batteries by 2027. India's EPR QR code requirement is an embryonic version of this concept. Companies that treat it as a checkbox exercise will find themselves unprepared when global buyers, institutional investors, and export market regulators ask for richer data. Companies that build robust digital product identity systems now — linked to bill-of-materials data, carbon footprint per cell, and recycled content claims — will be positioned to meet both domestic EPR obligations and emerging international disclosure requirements simultaneously. On physical risk: the amendment's trace-threshold exemptions for Cadmium and Lead reflect the shift in dominant battery chemistries — Lithium Iron Phosphate (LFP), Nickel Manganese Cobalt (NMC), and solid-state batteries — away from historically hazardous chemistries. This chemistry transition is itself climate-driven, as energy density, cycle life, and thermal safety improvements accelerate electrification. Compliance teams should track chemical composition as a live variable, not a static product specification, given how rapidly battery technology is evolving. From a supply chain disclosure perspective, SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) Core framework — mandatory for the top 150 listed companies from FY 2023-24 — requires value chain disclosures on environmental metrics. Battery producers and their upstream mineral suppliers (Lithium, Cobalt, Manganese) face growing pressure to disclose Scope 3 emissions and hazardous waste management practices. ISSB's IFRS S2 Climate Disclosures and the EU's CSRD (applicable to Indian subsidiaries of European companies and Indian companies accessing EU capital markets) both require disclosure of climate-related physical and transition risks that are directly relevant to battery manufacturers operating in a rapidly decarbonising economy.

What To Do Now

  • Audit your current EPR registration status and labelling: Immediately verify that your CPCB EPR registration number is valid and active. Map all current product labels, packaging designs, and brochures to identify where the EPR number currently appears — and whether a barcode/QR code integration is feasible in your next production cycle.
  • Submit written intimation to CPCB before switching to QR/barcode labelling: If your organisation chooses to adopt the new QR/barcode pathway under Clause (ib), draft and dispatch formal written notice to CPCB before any product bearing the new label format reaches the market. Work with your legal and regulatory affairs team to ensure this notification is filed correctly and acknowledged.
  • Conduct battery chemistry testing and documentation for Cd/Pb exemption: Commission or review existing test reports for Cadmium and Lead concentration in your battery products. If concentrations are at or below the defined thresholds (Cd ≤ 20 ppm, Pb ≤ 40 ppm), formally document this finding and instruct your labelling team to apply the exemption — while retaining test evidence for at least the duration of your regulatory record-keeping obligations.
  • Establish a quarterly CPCB portal update process: Assign ownership within your compliance or regulatory function to ensure your producer details on CPCB's centralised portal are reviewed and updated every quarter. Treat this as a recurring calendar obligation, not a one-time registration activity, to remain on CPCB's published consolidated producer list.
  • Begin building a battery digital traceability roadmap: Use this amendment as the trigger to initiate a broader conversation internally about digital product identity for batteries. Evaluate whether your QR code can be enriched over time to carry carbon footprint data, recycled content claims, and end-of-life instructions — positioning your organisation ahead of the EU Battery Regulation passport requirements and future BRSR value chain disclosure expectations.

Our Take

The Battery Waste Management Amendment Rules, 2025 are incremental in legal scope but directionally significant. India's battery EPR framework is still relatively young, and each amendment is systematically closing gaps — reducing regulatory duplication with Legal Metrology rules, providing operational flexibility on labelling formats, and calibrating hazardous-substance marking to reflect the chemistry realities of modern batteries. What stands out is the emphasis on digital traceability: mandating EPR registration numbers in machine-readable QR/barcode format is a meaningful step toward a data-driven circular economy for batteries. For compliance teams, the immediate priorities are clear — CPCB notification before label changes, chemistry testing for metal thresholds, and portal hygiene. But the strategic message is larger: the global battery regulatory environment is tightening rapidly, driven by the EU Battery Regulation, ISSB climate disclosure standards, and the supply chain scrutiny of institutional investors. Indian battery producers and OEMs that invest now in robust product identity, traceability infrastructure, and chemistry transparency will not merely be compliant — they will be competitive in the markets and supply chains that matter most over the next decade.
