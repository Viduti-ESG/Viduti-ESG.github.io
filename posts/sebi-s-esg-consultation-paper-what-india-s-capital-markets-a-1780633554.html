Executive Summary

In February 2023, SEBI released a landmark Consultation Paper on ESG Disclosures, Ratings and Investing, signalling a comprehensive effort to strengthen the architecture of sustainable finance in India's capital markets. The paper proposes reforms across three interconnected pillars — corporate ESG disclosures under BRSR, the regulation of ESG Rating Providers (ERPs), and standards for ESG-themed investment products such as mutual funds. For listed companies, institutional investors, and rating agencies, this consultation marks the beginning of a more rigorous, accountable, and globally comparable ESG regime in India.

What Changed

SEBI's February 2023 Consultation Paper does not itself enact new rules — rather, it invites public comment on a suite of proposed reforms that are expected to translate into binding regulations. This is an important distinction: companies and investors should treat it as a preview of the regulatory direction, not merely an academic exercise. The paper addresses three major areas. First, it proposes enhancements to the Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) framework, including the introduction of a 'BRSR Core' — a shorter, high-priority set of Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) subject to reasonable assurance by third parties. This would make a subset of ESG disclosures verifiable and auditable, raising the bar significantly from the current self-reported model. Second, the paper proposes a regulatory framework for ESG Rating Providers (ERPs) operating in India. Currently, ESG ratings are largely unregulated, creating risks of inconsistency, conflicts of interest, and opacity. SEBI proposes that ERPs be registered, follow defined methodologies, and ensure transparency in how ratings are derived — a move that mirrors regulatory trends in the EU and the UK. Third, the paper scrutinises ESG-themed mutual funds and other investment products, proposing guardrails to prevent greenwashing — the practice of marketing financial products as sustainable without substantive underlying criteria. This includes minimum thresholds for ESG-aligned investments within such funds and stronger disclosure obligations for asset managers.

Who Is Affected

  • Top 1,000 listed companies (by market capitalisation) — already subject to BRSR; the proposed 'BRSR Core' with assured KPIs will directly impact their disclosure and audit processes
  • All listed companies — broader BRSR enhancements may progressively extend to a wider universe of listed entities
  • ESG Rating Providers (ERPs) — domestic and foreign agencies providing ESG ratings on Indian companies will face mandatory registration and conduct requirements
  • Asset Management Companies (AMCs) — mutual funds offering ESG-labelled schemes will need to meet new portfolio composition, disclosure, and anti-greenwashing standards
  • Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) — those investing in Indian markets may face new ESG-related disclosure or engagement obligations
  • Sustainability consultants and ESG assurance providers — demand for third-party assurance of BRSR Core KPIs will create both opportunity and responsibility for assurance professionals

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • BRSR Core KPIs: SEBI proposed a set of ~50 high-priority ESG indicators spanning environment, social, and governance themes, with mandatory reasonable assurance — companies should begin gap assessments against these indicators immediately
  • Third-Party Assurance: The 'BRSR Core' framework proposes that disclosures be subject to third-party reasonable assurance, likely phased in starting with the top 150 listed companies before expanding further — identify and onboard qualified assurance providers now
  • ESG Rating Provider Registration: ERPs will be required to register with SEBI, disclose their methodologies, manage conflicts of interest, and publish ratings with explanatory rationale — ERPs should prepare compliance frameworks ahead of formal rule notification
  • ESG Mutual Fund Guardrails: AMCs offering ESG schemes must ensure a minimum proportion of assets are invested in companies meeting defined ESG criteria, with enhanced scheme-level disclosures — AMCs should review existing fund mandates for alignment
  • Consultation Response Deadline: The public comment period for this consultation paper closed in early 2023; formal regulatory circulars translating these proposals into rules are expected to follow — monitor SEBI notifications closely for binding implementation dates
  • Value Chain Disclosures: SEBI proposed that large listed companies disclose ESG performance of their top suppliers and customers (Scope 3 equivalent) — companies should begin supplier ESG data collection programmes

Climate Transition Angle

SEBI's consultation paper is best understood not in isolation, but as India's contribution to a global convergence of sustainability disclosure standards. The proposed BRSR Core KPIs include climate-specific metrics — greenhouse gas emissions (Scope 1, 2, and progressively Scope 3), energy consumption, water intensity, and waste generation — that closely mirror the climate-related disclosure requirements under the ISSB's IFRS S2 standard and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). Indian companies with international operations, foreign investors, or global supply chain relationships are increasingly expected to report in a manner consistent with these frameworks. The push for assured disclosures is particularly significant from a climate transition perspective. Investors and lenders globally are using ESG data to make capital allocation decisions aligned with net-zero pathways. Unverified, inconsistent data undermines this process. By introducing assurance requirements, SEBI is laying the groundwork for Indian capital markets to support credible climate transition finance — including green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, and transition finance instruments. The value chain disclosure proposals also carry material implications for physical and transition climate risks. Companies that map their supplier ESG performance will, by extension, begin to understand where climate-related vulnerabilities sit in their supply chains — whether that is water-stressed agricultural suppliers, carbon-intensive raw material producers, or geographically exposed logistics partners. This is precisely the kind of systemic risk visibility that regulators, investors, and boards need as India's climate transition accelerates.

What To Do Now

  • Conduct a BRSR Core Gap Assessment: Map your current disclosures against the proposed ~50 BRSR Core KPIs. Identify data gaps, especially for GHG emissions (Scope 1 & 2 as a minimum), water withdrawal, and employee safety metrics — these are most likely to be prioritised in the final rules
  • Appoint an Assurance Provider Early: The market for BRSR assurance services is nascent and will become competitive quickly. Engage with a qualified assurance provider (typically a Big 4 or specialist sustainability assurance firm) to understand the readiness requirements and timeline for reasonable assurance
  • Build Supplier ESG Data Infrastructure: Begin collecting basic ESG data from your top 20–30 suppliers in preparation for value chain disclosure obligations. A simple supplier ESG questionnaire aligned to BRSR metrics is a practical starting point
  • Review ESG-Labelled Financial Products: If you are an AMC or advise on ESG fund structuring, conduct a portfolio review against SEBI's proposed minimum ESG investment thresholds and update fund disclosures to pre-empt greenwashing scrutiny
  • Monitor SEBI Notifications and Engage Proactively: Track SEBI's regulatory calendar for formal circulars following this consultation. Consider engaging through industry associations (CII, FICCI, AMFI) to provide feedback that shapes implementation timelines and definitions favourably for your sector

Our Take

SEBI's February 2023 Consultation Paper is one of the most significant regulatory signals in India's ESG history — not because it changes rules today, but because it maps out a coherent, ambitious architecture for sustainable finance that will define the next decade of corporate disclosure and investment practice. The introduction of assured BRSR Core disclosures is a genuine step-change: it moves India from a disclosure culture based on self-reporting and box-ticking toward one grounded in accountability and comparability. The regulation of ESG Rating Providers addresses a long-standing market integrity gap that has undermined investor confidence in ESG data quality. What comes next will matter enormously — the specificity of the final KPI definitions, the pace of the assurance roll-out, and the stringency of anti-greenwashing rules for funds will determine whether India's ESG market matures into a credible force for capital reallocation, or remains a compliance exercise. Companies that start building data systems, assurance relationships, and supply chain visibility now will not only be compliant — they will be competitively positioned as global investors increasingly scrutinise India's listed universe through a sustainability lens.
