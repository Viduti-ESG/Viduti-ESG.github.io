Decoding ISSB Support Mechanisms: What Indian Companies Need to Know About Consistent Standards Application

The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) has established a structured framework to support consistent global application of its landmark sustainability disclosure standards — IFRS S1 (General Requirements) and IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures). For Indian companies navigating SEBI

Executive Summary

The International Sustainability Standards Board (ISSB) has established a structured framework to support consistent global application of its landmark sustainability disclosure standards — IFRS S1 (General Requirements) and IFRS S2 (Climate-related Disclosures). For Indian companies navigating SEBI's Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) framework and eyeing access to global capital markets, understanding how the ISSB supports implementation is no longer optional — it is a strategic imperative. Companies that align early with ISSB guidance will be better positioned to meet converging regulatory expectations from both Indian and international regulators.

What Changed

The ISSB, operating under the IFRS Foundation, has published and continuously updates a suite of implementation support resources designed to help preparers, auditors, regulators, and investors apply IFRS S1 and IFRS S2 in a consistent and comparable manner across jurisdictions. These resources include educational materials, illustrative examples, frequently asked questions (FAQs), and capacity-building initiatives targeted at both developed and emerging markets. Critically, the ISSB's support framework addresses one of the most common barriers to adoption: the perception that these standards are too complex or too resource-intensive for companies outside large Western markets. The ISSB has acknowledged the varied starting points of different jurisdictions — including India — and has built transition reliefs and phased implementation pathways into its guidance. This signals a clear intention: the ISSB wants broad, global adoption, and it is actively removing friction to get there. The ISSB also works in close coordination with the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO), which has endorsed IFRS S1 and S2 for use by its 130+ member regulators — including SEBI. This regulatory endorsement chain means that ISSB standards are increasingly likely to inform future mandatory disclosure requirements in India.

Who Is Affected

  • Top 1,000 listed companies in India currently subject to SEBI's mandatory BRSR Core disclosures
  • Companies with international listings, cross-border financing, or global investor bases seeking capital from ESG-focused funds
  • Indian subsidiaries and supply chain partners of multinational corporations subject to CSRD (EU) or SEC climate rules (US)
  • CFOs, Chief Sustainability Officers, and Investor Relations teams responsible for sustainability reporting strategy
  • Statutory auditors and assurance providers preparing to provide limited or reasonable assurance on sustainability disclosures
  • Financial institutions and banks assessing climate-related risks in their lending and investment portfolios
  • Mid-sized companies aspiring to join global supply chains where sustainability disclosure is becoming a procurement prerequisite

Key Obligations & Deadlines

  • IMMEDIATE: Familiarise your reporting team with ISSB's free implementation support portal at ifrs.org — including the IFRS S1 and S2 Basis for Conclusions documents, which explain the intent behind each requirement
  • IMMEDIATE: Review the ISSB's FAQ library and educational materials to identify gaps between your current BRSR disclosures and IFRS S2 climate-related disclosure requirements (especially Scope 1, 2 & 3 emissions, scenario analysis, and governance disclosures)
  • Q1 2025 ONWARDS: Monitor SEBI communications for formal guidance on ISSB alignment — SEBI's BRSR Core framework is already conceptually aligned with TCFD (which ISSB S2 supersedes), and a formal convergence announcement is anticipated
  • ONGOING: Track ISSB's 'IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Taxonomy' updates, which facilitate digital, machine-readable reporting — a likely future SEBI requirement for structured data submissions
  • ONGOING: Engage with the ISSB's capacity-building programmes and regional workshops, particularly those conducted through the IFRS Foundation's Emerging Economies Group, in which India participates
  • 2025–2026: Begin voluntary parallel reporting against IFRS S2 alongside mandatory BRSR submissions — this builds internal capability and signals disclosure quality to global investors

Climate Transition Angle

The ISSB's implementation support framework is not merely a technical compliance exercise — it sits at the heart of the global architecture being built to finance the climate transition. IFRS S2 requires companies to disclose material climate-related risks and opportunities across both physical risks (extreme weather, water stress, sea-level rise) and transition risks (carbon pricing, stranded assets, changing consumer preferences). For Indian companies, both dimensions are acutely relevant: India faces some of the world's highest physical climate risk exposure, while simultaneously undertaking one of the most ambitious energy transitions of any major economy. The convergence of ISSB standards with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — through the ongoing interoperability project between ISSB and EFRAG — means that Indian exporters and supply chain participants serving European markets will face a dual disclosure expectation. Similarly, the US SEC's climate disclosure rules, though currently under legal challenge, signal the same direction of travel. Companies that use the ISSB's support resources to build robust climate disclosure capabilities now are essentially future-proofing themselves against a cascade of regulatory requirements from multiple directions. Perhaps most significantly, consistent application of ISSB standards globally enables apples-to-apples comparison of climate risk across portfolios — which is precisely what institutional investors, green bond issuers, and development finance institutions require to allocate transition capital efficiently. Indian companies that speak the ISSB language will have a measurable advantage in accessing this growing pool of sustainability-linked finance.

What To Do Now

  • CONDUCT A GAP ASSESSMENT: Map your current BRSR Core disclosures against the specific requirements of IFRS S1 and IFRS S2 — pay particular attention to climate scenario analysis, Scope 3 value chain emissions, and the 'significant judgements' disclosure requirements that most Indian companies currently omit
  • BUILD INTERNAL CAPABILITY: Designate a cross-functional ISSB working group (Finance, Legal, Sustainability, Operations, IR) and use the ISSB's free educational modules to upskill the team — this is foundational, not optional
  • ENGAGE YOUR AUDITORS EARLY: Discuss with your statutory auditor and any sustainability assurance provider how they are preparing for IFRS S2-aligned assurance engagements — the assurance landscape in India is evolving rapidly and early dialogue prevents last-minute surprises
  • LEVERAGE TRANSITION RELIEFS STRATEGICALLY: ISSB has built in a one-year relief from Scope 3 and some comparative reporting requirements for first-time adopters — understand these reliefs and plan your adoption roadmap to use them intelligently rather than deferring preparation
  • COMMUNICATE PROACTIVELY WITH INVESTORS: If you have foreign institutional investors (FIIs) or are seeking ESG-labelled debt, proactively communicate your ISSB alignment roadmap — investors increasingly distinguish between companies that are reactive to disclosure mandates and those that are building genuine climate governance

Our Take

The ISSB's commitment to supporting consistent application of its standards is a signal that global sustainability disclosure is moving from aspiration to infrastructure. For Indian business leaders, the temptation may be to treat ISSB standards as a 'foreign' requirement relevant only to multinationals — but this view is increasingly untenable. SEBI's BRSR framework is converging with ISSB principles by design, not coincidence, and the trajectory points clearly toward mandatory ISSB-aligned disclosures for listed Indian companies within the next three to five years. The companies that will navigate this transition smoothly are those investing now in data systems, governance structures, and internal capabilities — not those scrambling to comply at the deadline. The ISSB's implementation support framework is, in effect, a public good: free, authoritative, and specifically designed to help companies like yours succeed. The question is not whether to engage with it, but how quickly.
